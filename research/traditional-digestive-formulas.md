# Traditional Herbal Formulas for Digestion

A comprehensive research compilation of traditional digestive formulas from Chinese Medicine, Ayurveda, Western Herbalism, and Japanese Kampo traditions.

---

## Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) Formulas

### Liu Jun Zi Tang (六君子汤) - Six Gentlemen Decoction

**Tradition:** Traditional Chinese Medicine (Yuan Dynasty)

**Herb Components:**
- Ren Shen (Panax ginseng) - Ginseng
- Fu Ling (Poria cocos) - Hoelen/Poria mushroom
- Bai Zhu (Atractylodis Macrocephalae Rhizoma) - Atractylodes macrocephala
- Gan Cao (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) - Licorice root
- Ban Xia (Pinellia ternata Rhizoma) - Pinellia tuber
- Chen Pi (Citri Reticulatae Pericarpium) - Aged tangerine peel
- Sheng Jiang (Zingiber officinale) - Fresh ginger
- Da Zao (Ziziphus jujuba) - Jujube/Chinese date

**Variant: Xiang Sha Liu Jun Zi Tang** adds:
- Mu Xiang (Aucklandia lappa Radix) - Costus root
- Sha Ren (Amomum villosum) - Amomum/Cardamom

**Traditional Indications:**
- Functional dyspepsia
- Chronic digestive weakness
- Gastric discomfort with fatigue
- Poor appetite
- "Spleen Qi deficiency" in TCM terms

**Modern Clinical Studies:**
- A 2024 study examined Xiangsha-Liujunzi-Tang's function in enhancing duodenal mucosal barrier by inhibiting MC/Tryptase/PAR-2 signaling pathway in functional dyspepsia rats (J Ethnopharmacol, 2024)
- Systematic review and meta-analysis on efficacy and safety for functional dyspepsia (Front Pharmacol, June 2024)
- Meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials showed significantly better gastric emptying than placebo in functional dyspepsia patients
- Used for irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) with significant reductions in diarrhea
- 2023 randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial for decreased digestive function in convalescent COVID-19 patients

**Mechanisms of Action:**
- Improves gastric emptying
- Reduces chronic dyspepsia symptoms
- Enhances duodenal mucosal barrier function
- Modulates gastrointestinal motility

**Safety Profile:**
Satisfactory safety profile both in clinical studies and after hundreds of years of traditional use in China.

### Bao He Wan (保和丸) - Preserve Harmony Pill

**Tradition:** Traditional Chinese Medicine (Yuan Dynasty, created by physician Zhu Danxi)

**Herb Components:**
- Shan Zha (Crataegus pinnatifida) - Chinese hawthorn fruit
- Shen Qu - Medicated leaven (fermented combination of wheat flour, Artemisia annua, Xanthium, Polygonum hydropiper)
- Lai Fu Zi (Raphanus sativus) - Radish seed
- Chen Pi (Citrus reticulata) - Aged tangerine peel
- Zhi Ban Xia (Pinellia ternata) - Prepared pinellia corm
- Fu Ling (Poria cocos) - Poria mushroom
- Lian Qiao (Forsythia suspensa) - Forsythia fruit
- Mai Ya (Hordeum vulgare) - Barley sprouts

**Traditional Indications:**
- Food stagnation
- Indigestion from overeating
- Abdominal bloating
- Gas and distension
- Post-meal discomfort

**Modern Clinical Studies:**
- Found to be among commonly prescribed herbal formulas for gastrointestinal disorders in post-surgery colon cancer patients
- In mice fed high-protein and high-fat diet, BHW conferred anti-diarrheal effect by modulating intestinal lactase-producing bacteria composition
- Inhibited growth of conditionally pathogenic bacteria Ralstonia in intestinal mucosa

**Mechanisms of Action:**
- Promotes gastric emptying
- Increases gastric acid secretion
- Exerts anti-ulcer effects
- Modulates intestinal microbiome composition
- Reduces pathogenic bacteria

### Xiao Yao San (逍遥散) - Free Wanderer Powder

**Tradition:** Traditional Chinese Medicine (Song Dynasty, 1078-1085 AD, from Tai Ping Hui Min He Ji Ju Fang)

**Herb Components:**
- Chai Hu (Bupleurum chinense) - Bupleurum root
- Dang Gui (Angelica sinensis) - Chinese angelica root
- Bai Shao (Paeonia lactiflora) - White peony root
- Bai Zhu (Atractylodes macrocephala) - White atractylodes
- Fu Ling (Poria cocos) - Poria mushroom
- Sheng Jiang (Zingiber officinale) - Fresh ginger
- Bo He (Mentha haplocalyx) - Peppermint
- Gan Cao (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) - Licorice

**Active Constituents:**
Paeoniflorin, ferulic acid, glycyrrhizic acid, liquiritin, and atractylenolide I

**Traditional Indications:**
- Digestive disorders related to "liver stagnation and spleen deficiency"
- Irritable bowel syndrome
- Functional dyspepsia with stress component
- Indigestion with emotional factors
- Constipation

**Modern Clinical Studies:**
- 2022 meta-analysis (Biomedicine and Pharmacotherapy) concluded XYS may reduce symptoms and recurrence rates in IBS, constipation, and indigestion
- Effective for abdominal pain, abdominal distention, and diarrhea
- Improves IBS symptom severity scores and quality of life
- Studies show antidepressant effects through modulation of inflammation-related receptors

**Mechanisms of Action:**
- Improves gastrointestinal motility by increasing motilin and 5-hydroxytryptamine levels
- Decreases somatostatin levels
- Strengthens spleen and stomach function
- Protects digestive system
- Modulates advanced glycation end products receptors
- Addresses liver-spleen connection in TCM theory

### Ping Wei San (平胃散) - Calm the Stomach Powder

**Tradition:** Traditional Chinese Medicine

**Herb Components:**
- Cang Zhu (Atractylodes lancea) - Atractylodes rhizome
- Hou Po (Magnolia officinalis) - Magnolia bark
- Chen Pi (Citrus reticulata) - Tangerine peel
- Gan Cao (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) - Licorice root
- Sheng Jiang (Zingiber officinale) - Fresh ginger
- Da Zao (Ziziphus jujuba) - Jujube

**Traditional Indications:**
- Dyspepsia
- Nausea
- Abdominal pain
- Anorexia
- Dampness and stagnation in stomach and spleen

**Modern Clinical Studies:**
- Notably improved gastric emptying and intestinal transit
- Significantly reduced gastric potential of hydrogen
- Increased pepsin and lipase gene expression
- Demonstrated anti-inflammatory and immune-boosting effects
- Protects gut barrier and regulates water balance

**Mechanisms of Action:**
- Produces membrane depolarization in interstitial cells of Cajal (pacemaker cells of GI tract)
- Enhances gastric motility
- Protects intestinal barrier integrity
- Prevents intestinal damage
- Aids tissue repair in digestive disorders

## Ayurvedic Formulas

### Triphala (त्रिफला) - Three Fruits

**Tradition:** Ayurveda (ancient India)

**Herb Components (1:1:1 ratio):**
- Haritaki (Terminalia chebula) - Chebulic myrobalan
- Bibhitaki (Terminalia bellerica) - Belleric myrobalan
- Amalaki (Phyllanthus emblica/Emblica officinalis) - Indian gooseberry/Amla

**Key Bioactive Compounds:**
- Polyphenols (gallic acid, chebulagic acid, chebulinic acid)
- Vitamin C
- Flavonoids
- Tannins
- Phenolic acids

**Traditional Indications:**
- Digestive disorders
- Constipation
- Detoxification
- General health tonic
- "Rasayana" (rejuvenative) in Ayurveda

**Modern Clinical Studies:**
- Clinical trial in patients 16-52 years with GI disorders: reduced mucous in stool and flatulence (p < 0.05), improved stool frequency (p < 0.05), yield (p < 0.001), and consistency
- Clinical study in non-insulin-dependent diabetes: 5g Triphala powder for 45 days significantly lowered blood glucose
- Validated for free radical scavenging, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, immunomodulating effects

**Mechanisms of Action:**
- Restores epithelium lining of digestive tract
- Exhibits mild laxative properties facilitating colonic transit
- Inhibits pancreatic glycolytic enzymes (alpha-amylase and alpha-glucosidase)
- Modulates gut microbiome, promoting Bifidobacteria and Lactobacillus growth
- Inhibits undesirable gut microbes
- Inhibits LPS-induced inflammatory responses
- Suppresses NFκB transcription factor
- Provides cytoprotective and antifungal properties

**Additional Therapeutic Properties:**
Appetite stimulation, gastric hyperacidity reduction, dental caries prevention, antipyretic, analgesic, antibacterial, antimutagenic, wound healing, anticariogenic, antistress, adaptogenic, hypoglycemic, anticancer, hepatoprotective, chemoprotective, and radioprotective effects.

### Hingvastak Churna - Eight-Ingredient Asafoetida Powder

**Tradition:** Ayurveda (described by scholars Sharangdhar and Vaidya Vagbhata)

**Herb Components:**
- Hing (Ferula asafoetida) - Asafoetida
- Jeeraka (Cuminum cyminum) - Cumin seeds
- Ajamoda (Apium graveolens) - Wild celery/Ajwain seeds
- Maricha (Piper nigrum) - Black pepper
- Krishnajeeraka (Nigella sativa) - Black cumin/Nigella seeds
- Shunthi (Zingiber officinale) - Dried ginger
- Pippali (Piper longum) - Long pepper
- Saindhava Lavana - Mineral/Himalayan salt
- Some formulations include Methi (Trigonella foenum-graecum) - Fenugreek seed

**Traditional Indications:**
- Excess flatulence
- Abdominal colic
- Anorexia
- Indigestion
- Vata imbalance
- Bloating
- Joint diseases (Vata-related)

**Modern Clinical Studies:**
- Open-label study (International Journal of Ayurveda Research, 2021): 15 IBS patients showed improved stool consistency and reduced abdominal pain over 28 days
- Limited clinical evidence overall; more research needed

**Mechanisms of Action:**
- Soothes vata in GI tract
- Warming blend supporting healthy appetite
- Ignites digestive fire (agni)
- Promotes optimal nutrient absorption
- Balances vata and kapha doshas

**Dosage:**
1-2 grams (¼ to ½ teaspoon) up to twice daily post-meals, with ghee or warm water

### Trikatu Churna - Three Peppers/Spices

**Tradition:** Ayurveda

**Herb Components (equal proportions):**
- Shunthi (Zingiber officinale) - Dried ginger rhizome
- Maricha (Piper nigrum) - Black pepper (dried fruits)
- Pippali (Piper longum) - Long pepper (dried fruits)

**Key Bioactive Compounds:**
- Piperine (3-5% in P. longum and P. nigrum)
- Gingerols
- Gingiberene
- Shagols

**Traditional Indications:**
- Tastelessness
- Digestive impairment
- Chronic rhinitis/sinusitis
- Respiratory conditions (asthma, cough)
- Obesity
- Skin diseases

**Modern Clinical Studies:**
- Himalaya clinical studies: effective support for indigestion, bloating, belching, and flatulence
- Experimentally proven liver protection activity
- Black pepper as bio-enhancer boosted turmeric absorption by 200-2000%

**Mechanisms of Action:**
- Stimulates 'Agni' (digestive fire)
- Improves nutrient assimilation
- Increases digestive juices in stomach
- Influences liver to increase bile salt production
- Natural bioavailability enhancers for other herbs/medications
- Improves metabolic transformation
- Prevents fat accumulation and burns accumulated fats
- Rectifies fat metabolism

## Japanese Kampo Formulas

### Rikkunshito (六君子湯, TJ-43) - Six Gentlemen Decoction

**Tradition:** Kampo (Japanese adaptation of Traditional Chinese Medicine)

**Herb Components:**
- Ninjin (Panax ginseng) - Ginseng
- Bukuryo (Poria cocos) - Poria sclerotium
- Byakujutsu (Atractylodes lancea/macrocephala) - Atractylodes rhizome
- Chinpi (Citrus unshiu) - Citrus peel
- Hange (Pinellia ternata) - Pinellia tuber
- Kanzo (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) - Licorice
- Taisou (Ziziphus jujuba) - Jujube
- Shokyo (Zingiber officinale) - Ginger

**Traditional Indications:**
- Functional dyspepsia in patients with "kyo-sho" (deficiency pattern)
- Nausea
- Indigestion
- Anorexia
- Upper gastrointestinal symptoms

**Modern Clinical Studies:**
- Four randomized controlled trials suggested usefulness in relieving subjective symptoms of functional dyspepsia
- Significantly improved epigastric discomfort and extra-gastric symptoms like general fatigue compared to controls
- Combined with PPI therapy useful for PPI-refractory GERD patients
- Ameliorates anorexia induced by anticancer drugs
- Improves quality of life scores
- May prolong survival when combined with cancer therapy

**Mechanisms of Action:**
- Improves gastric motility dysfunction (impaired adaptive relaxation, delayed gastric emptying)
- Reduces gastric hypersensitivity
- Facilitates ghrelin secretion to improve anorexia
- Exhibits anti-stress effects
- Attenuates stress-induced exacerbation of gastric sensation

### Hange-shashin-to (半夏瀉心湯, HST, TJ-14)

**Tradition:** Kampo (Japanese traditional medicine)

**Herb Components:**
- Hange (Pinellia ternata) - Pinellia
- Ogon (Scutellaria baicalensis) - Scutellaria/Skullcap
- Ninjin (Panax ginseng) - Ginseng
- Kanzo (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) - Licorice
- Daisou (Ziziphus jujuba) - Jujube
- Kankyo (Zingiber officinale) - Dried ginger
- Oren (Coptis japonica) - Coptis/Goldthread

**Traditional Indications:**
- Diarrhea
- Gastrointestinal inflammation
- Functional dyspepsia
- Chemotherapy-induced diarrhea

**Modern Clinical Studies:**
- Prevented CPT-11-induced diarrhea, anorexia, and body weight loss in rats without loss of antitumor effects
- Clinical study confirmed preventive effect toward CPT-11-induced diarrhea in patients with advanced non-small-cell lung cancer
- Significantly suppressed increases in plasma ACTH and cortisol levels under stress

**Mechanisms of Action:**
- Attenuates increase in prostaglandin E2 (PGE2)
- Promotes colonic water absorption
- Anti-diarrheal through multiple pathways
- Modulates stress hormone response

### Dai-kenchu-to (大建中湯, DKT, TJ-100)

**Tradition:** Kampo (Japanese traditional medicine)

**Herb Components:**
- Kankyo (Zingiber officinale) - Processed/dried ginger
- Sansho (Zanthoxylum piperitum) - Japanese pepper/Zanthoxylum fruit
- Ninjin (Panax ginseng) - Ginseng root
- Koi (maltose powder)

**Traditional Indications:**
- Adhesive bowel obstruction
- Chronic constipation
- Post-operative ileus
- Impaired intestinal motility

**Modern Clinical Studies:**
- Clinical trials showed DKT enhanced intestinal transit in patients after abdominal surgeries and during mechanical ventilation
- Used as prokinetic agent in multiple clinical settings
- Prevents bacterial translocation by reducing inflammatory reaction and maintaining intestinal integrity

**Mechanisms of Action:**
- Stimulates upper GI motility through cholinergic and 5-HT3 receptors
- Releases acetylcholine from cholinergic nerve endings
- Involves 5-HT4 receptors in contractile response
- Produces membrane depolarization in interstitial cells of Cajal
- Induces calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP) production
- CGRP provides microvascular vasodilation, anti-inflammatory, and trophic actions
- Acts as complementary medicine with Western drugs for postoperative bowel motility

### Saikokeishito (柴胡桂枝湯, TJ-10)

**Tradition:** Kampo (Japanese traditional medicine)

**Herb Components:**
- Saiko (Bupleurum falcatum) - Bupleurum root
- Ogon (Scutellaria baicalensis) - Scutellaria
- Ninjin (Panax ginseng) - Ginseng
- Hange (Pinellia ternata) - Pinellia
- Kanzo (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) - Licorice
- Keishi (Cinnamomum cassia) - Cinnamon bark
- Shakuyaku (Paeonia lactiflora) - Peony
- Taisou (Ziziphus jujuba) - Jujube
- Shokyo (Zingiber officinale) - Fresh ginger

**Traditional Indications:**
- Duodenal ulcers
- Pancreatitis
- Chronic liver diseases
- Subacute post-infectious cold-like conditions
- Gastrointestinal symptoms with systemic symptoms
- Headache with epigastralgia
- Irritable bowel syndrome
- Anorexia

**Modern Clinical Studies:**
- 2025 prospective observational study: Most frequently prescribed Kampo for long COVID
- Combination with kamikihito associated with greater improvements in health-related quality of life (odds ratio 5.4)
- Particularly effective for brain fog in long COVID patients
- Successfully used in clinical case reports for headache, IBS, and Behcet's disease-related anorexia

**Mechanisms of Action:**
- Exerts therapeutic effect on liver injury through attenuation of neutrophil infiltration
- Reduces oxidative stress in liver tissue
- Protects against liver injury by reducing hepatic lipid peroxide levels
- Nitric oxide-mediated inhibition of neutrophil infiltration
- Anti-inflammatory actions (weakly suppressed by anti-glucocorticoids)
- Antioxidant action by inhibiting lipid peroxidation
- Scavenges reactive oxygen species (superoxide and hydroxyl radicals)
- Inhibits production of transforming growth factor-β1 (TGF-β1)
- Prevents development of pancreatic fibrosis
- Baicalin component exhibits anti-SARS-CoV-2 activity and suppresses microglial activation

**Safety Considerations:**
Occasionally causes Kampo medicine-induced liver injury (KMILI), usually mild and not fatal, as it contains Scutellaria Radix

### Carminative Aromatic Formulas

**Tradition:** Western herbalism (also present in Ayurveda and TCM)

**Definition:**
Carminative herbs are aromatic herbs rich in volatile/essential oils that reduce flatulence, nausea, intestinal gas discomfort, and dyspepsia.

**Common Carminative Herbs:**
- Foeniculum vulgare - Fennel seed
- Elettaria cardamomum - Cardamom
- Mentha × piperita - Peppermint
- Zingiber officinale - Ginger
- Melissa officinalis - Lemon balm
- Cuminum cyminum - Cumin
- Coriandrum sativum - Coriander
- Illicium verum - Star anise
- Matricaria chamomilla - Chamomile
- Rosmarinus officinalis - Rosemary
- Cinnamomum verum - Cinnamon

**Specific Herb Properties:**

**Fennel (Foeniculum vulgare):**
- Benefits ongoing stomach conditions like IBS
- Relieves gas, bloating, constipation, and diarrhea
- Traditionally chewed before/after meals in Indian culture

**Cardamom (Elettaria cardamomum):**
- Warm, spicy, and sweet
- Carminative and antispasmodic qualities
- Addresses stomach cramps, heartburn, gas, bloating, and nausea
- Also has bitter properties (dual action)

**Peppermint (Mentha × piperita):**
- Classic cooling and refreshing herb
- One of most effective herbs for digestive issues
- Anti-inflammatory, analgesic, antispasmodic, carminative
- Digestive stimulant

**Traditional Formula Examples:**
- Carminative Compound: Fennel seed + Chamomile + Peppermint + Wild Yam + Ginger
- Spiced Bitters: Gentian + Artichoke + Ginger + Cinnamon + Orange peel + Cardamom

**Mechanisms of Action:**
- Prevent or relieve gas and bloating
- Rich in volatile oils that relax digestive smooth muscle
- Antispasmodic effects
- Stimulate digestive secretions
- Reduce intestinal gas discomfort

## Cross-Cultural Patterns and Insights

### Common Herbs Across Traditions

Several herbs appear in multiple traditional systems:

1. **Ginger (Zingiber officinale)**
   - TCM: Liu Jun Zi Tang, Bao He Wan, Ping Wei San
   - Ayurveda: Trikatu, Hingvastak, Lavanbhaskar
   - Kampo: Rikkunshito, Hange-shashin-to, Dai-kenchu-to, Saikokeishito
   - Western: Bitters, Carminative formulas

2. **Licorice (Glycyrrhiza species)**
   - TCM: Liu Jun Zi Tang, Xiao Yao San, Ping Wei San
   - Kampo: Rikkunshito, Hange-shashin-to, Saikokeishito
   - Western: Bitters formulas

3. **Poria (Poria cocos)**
   - TCM: Liu Jun Zi Tang, Bao He Wan, Xiao Yao San
   - Kampo: Rikkunshito

4. **Black Pepper/Long Pepper (Piper species)**
   - Ayurveda: Trikatu, Hingvastak, Lavanbhaskar

5. **Cardamom (Elettaria cardamomum)**
   - Ayurveda: Lavanbhaskar
   - Western: Swedish Bitters, Carminative formulas

### Common Therapeutic Approaches

1. **Warming Digestive Fire**
   - Ayurveda: "Agni" stimulation (Trikatu, Hingvastak)
   - TCM: Strengthening spleen Yang
   - Western: Warming aromatics in bitters

2. **Carminative Action**
   - Present in all traditions
   - Aromatic herbs for gas and bloating
   - Essential oil-rich plants

3. **Bitter Stimulation**
   - Western: Gentian, dandelion, artichoke
   - TCM: Chen Pi (tangerine peel)
   - Ayurveda: Some components of Triphala

4. **Prokinetic Effects**
   - TCM: Improving Qi flow
   - Kampo: Cholinergic and serotonergic stimulation
   - Western: Digestive stimulants

5. **Stress-Digestion Connection**
   - TCM: Xiao Yao San for liver-spleen harmony
   - Kampo: Rikkunshito and Hange-shashin-to for stress modulation
   - Ayurveda: Adaptogenic aspects of formulas

### Modern Research Validation

Common mechanisms validated by modern research across traditions:

1. **Gastric Motility Enhancement**
   - Cholinergic pathways
   - Serotonergic modulation (5-HT3, 5-HT4 receptors)
   - Ghrelin secretion
   - Motilin modulation

2. **Anti-inflammatory Effects**
   - NFκB pathway suppression
   - Prostaglandin modulation
   - Cytokine regulation
   - Oxidative stress reduction

3. **Microbiome Modulation**
   - Promotion of beneficial bacteria (Bifidobacteria, Lactobacillus)
   - Inhibition of pathogenic species
   - Prebiotic effects

4. **Digestive Secretion Stimulation**
   - Bile production
   - Gastric acid secretion
   - Digestive enzyme release
   - Saliva production

5. **Gut Barrier Protection**
   - Epithelial integrity maintenance
   - Tight junction enhancement
   - Mucosal barrier strengthening
   - Prevention of bacterial translocation
