# Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses: Herbal Treatments for Allergies

Research compiled: 2026-01-15

## Executive Summary

This document summarizes systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and clinical practice guidelines on herbal treatments for allergic conditions, particularly allergic rhinitis (hay fever) and seasonal allergies. The evidence base includes multiple recent systematic reviews (2023-2025) analyzing randomized controlled trials of various herbal interventions.

**Overall Quality of Evidence:** Predominantly very low to low certainty (GRADE assessment), with some moderate-certainty evidence for specific comparisons.

**Key Finding:** While several herbs show promise and appear well-tolerated, clear definitive evidence supporting routine clinical use remains limited due to methodological limitations in primary studies.

## Major Systematic Reviews & Meta-Analyses

### 1. Medicinal Plants for Allergic Rhinitis (PLOS One, 2024)

**Citation:** Published April 11, 2024 in PLOS One
**Source:** [Medicinal plants for allergic rhinitis: A systematic review and meta-analysis](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0297839)

**Scope:**
- 29 randomized controlled trials (n=1,879 participants)
- 27 studies (n=1,769) contributed to meta-analyses
- Focus on single medicinal plants
- Databases: MEDLINE, CENTRAL, Web of Science

**Most Studied Herb:** *Petasites hybridus* (butterbur) - 5 trials

**Other Notable Plants:**
- *Urtica dioica* (stinging nettle) - 2 studies
- *Cinnamomum zeylanicum* (cinnamon) - 2 studies
- Single studies: ginger, ginseng, spirulina, broccoli sprout extract, *Tinospora cordifolia*, *Perilla frutescens*

**Evidence Quality (GRADE):**
- Very low to low certainty for most outcomes
- Main downgrading factors:
  - Unclear risk of bias affecting subjective outcomes
  - Wide confidence intervals and small sample sizes
  - High heterogeneity (substantial I² values)
- Moderate certainty for comparison with antihistamines

**Key Findings:**

*Versus Placebo (very low-to-low certainty):*
- Total nasal symptoms: Small improvement (SMD -0.31, 95% CI -0.59 to -0.02)
- Rhinoconjunctivitis quality of life (RQLQ): Modest benefit (MD -0.46, 95% CI -0.84 to -0.07)
- Better response rates for rhinorrhea and nasal itching
- Nasal congestion and sneezing also improved
- **Conclusion:** "No clear evidence that medicinal plants can substantially improve most of the commonly reported clinical outcome measures"

*Versus Antihistamines (moderate certainty):*
- No significant differences in overall nasal symptoms (SMD -0.14)
- Medicinal plants comparable in effectiveness to conventional antihistamines

*As Adjunctive Therapy (moderate certainty):*
- SNOT-22 scores improved when given intranasally (MD -7.47, 95% CI -10.75 to -4.18)
- Limited number of studies available

**Safety:**
- Medicinal plants "reasonably well tolerated" up to 8 weeks
- Mild to moderate adverse events comparable to controls
- Common side effects: headache, dizziness, gastrointestinal disturbances
- *Zingiber officinale* (ginger) showed higher eructation rates vs loratadine

**Authors' Conclusion:** "Clear beneficial evidence of medicinal plants for allergic rhinitis is still lacking." Future trials require improved reporting standards and quality documentation of herbal preparations.

### 2. Efficacy and Safety of Phytotherapy and Anthroposophic Medicine in Seasonal Allergic Rhinitis (2024-2025)

**Citation:** Published online July 2024, International Archives of Allergy and Immunology 2025
**Source:** [Efficacy and Safety of Phytotherapy and Anthroposophic Medicine in Seasonal Allergic Rhinitis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39084196/)

**Scope:**
- Systematic review of European/Western phytotherapy and anthroposophic medicine
- Database searches: January 1990 - March 2023
- 14 studies included: 11 European/Western phytotherapy, 3 anthroposophic medicine
- Approximately half met sufficient quality standards

**Primary Herb Investigated:**
**Butterbur (*Petasites hybridus*):**
- Most frequently studied plant
- Beneficial effects on:
  - Immunological parameters
  - Subjective symptoms
  - Nasal airflow measurements

**Other Promising Preparations:**
- Herbal combination: *Citrus limonis* (lemon) + *Cydonia oblonga* (quince)
  - Showed beneficial immunological and clinical effects

**Safety:**
"The medications examined by studies of sufficient quality were judged to be safe"

**Authors' Conclusion:** Two herbal preparations merit further investigation (one from conventional Western phytotherapy, one from anthroposophic medicine), representing promising rather than definitively proven options.

### 3. Herbal Medicines for the Treatment of Allergic Rhinitis (2008)

**Citation:** Annals of Allergy, Asthma & Immunology, 2008
**Source:** [Herbal medicines for the treatment of allergic rhinitis: a systematic review](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18219828/)

**Scope:**
- 16 eligible randomized controlled trials
- Testing 10 different herbal products
- Focus on intermittent allergic rhinitis

**Key Findings by Herb:**

**Butterbur (*Petasites hybridus*):**
- 6 RCTs examined butterbur extract
- Superior to placebo
- Similarly effective compared with non-sedative antihistamines
- **Important safety note:** Unpurified butterbur contains hepatotoxic pyrrolizidine alkaloids (UPAs)

**Stinging Nettle (*Urtica dioica*):**
- One randomized, double-blind study
- 57% of patients rated nettles effective in relieving symptoms
- 48% said it equaled or surpassed previously used allergy medications

**Other Notable Findings:**
- Encouraging evidence for butterbur for treating allergic rhinitis
- *Tinospora cordifolia* and *Perilla frutescens* showed statistically significant effects for quality of life
- Confirmation in larger, more rigorously designed trials warranted

## Individual Herbs: Detailed Evidence

### Butterbur (*Petasites hybridus*)

**Evidence Level:** Most extensively studied herb for allergic rhinitis

**Clinical Trial Evidence:**

1. **Randomized controlled trial vs. cetirizine:**
   - Butterbur (ZE 339 extract tablets) showed similar efficacy to cetirizine
   - Did NOT produce sedative effects associated with antihistamines
   - [Source](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC64514/)

2. **Systematic review conclusion (2008):**
   - 6 RCTs support butterbur for allergic rhinitis
   - Superior to placebo
   - Similar effectiveness to second-generation antihistamines
   - Relief of symptoms over 1-2 weeks

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Active compounds (petasins) suppress mast cell degranulation
- Reduces anti-inflammatory mediators (histamine, LTB4, cysteinyl-LT)
- Dose-dependent symptom improvements

**CRITICAL SAFETY CONCERN:**
- **Pyrrolizidine alkaloids (UPAs)** in unpurified butterbur are:
  - Hepatotoxic
  - Nephrotoxic
  - Mutagenic
  - Carcinogenic
  - Associated with venoocclusive disease
- **Only use PA-free (pyrrolizidine alkaloid-free) preparations**
- Long-term safety and efficacy data lacking

**Current Status:**
- Some jurisdictions have removed butterbur from market due to safety concerns
- PA-free formulations theoretically safer but require verification

---

### Stinging Nettle (*Urtica dioica*)

**Evidence Level:** Moderate evidence from limited studies

**Clinical Trial Evidence:**

1. **Randomized, double-blind study (Mittman P., Planta Med 1990):**
   - Freeze-dried *Urtica dioica* for allergic rhinitis
   - 58% of participants reported nettle worked better than placebo
   - 57% rated it effective in relieving symptoms
   - 48% said it equaled or surpassed previously used medications

2. **Meta-analysis findings:**
   - Herbal supplements including nettle improved nasal symptoms vs placebo
   - Low quality of studies limits confidence

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Inhibits proinflammatory pathways related to allergic rhinitis
- Antagonizes histamine H1 receptor
- Inhibits prostaglandin formation
- Blocks mast cell degranulation

**Dosage:**
- Recommended: 600 mg dried nettle leaf per day
- Typically divided into three doses

**Safety:**
Generally well-tolerated

**Prevalence:**
- One of the most common herbal supplements used for allergic rhinitis
- Turkish study: 12.6% of allergy patients using nettle

### Quercetin (Flavonoid in Various Herbs)

**Evidence Level:** Mechanistic evidence strong; clinical trial data limited

**Sources:**
Quercetin is not a single herb but a flavonoid found in:
- Apples
- Onions
- Tea
- Berries
- Many other fruits and vegetables
- Available as isolated supplement

**Mechanisms of Action:**

1. **Mast Cell Stabilization:**
   - Inhibits histamine release by stabilizing mast cell membranes
   - Blocks calcium influx
   - At 100 µM concentration:
     - Suppresses IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α release by 82%
     - Reduces tryptase release by 79-96%
     - Reduces histamine by 52-77%

2. **Immune Balance:**
   - Induces Th1-derived interferon (IFN)-γ expression
   - Downregulates Th2-derived IL-4 production
   - Restores healthy Th1/Th2 ratios (typically skewed toward Th2 in allergies)

3. **Inflammatory Enzyme Inhibition:**
   - Inhibits lipoxygenase and phosphodiesterase enzymes
   - Suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokines and leukotrienes

**Clinical Evidence:**

1. **Japanese study - Allergic Rhinitis:**
   - 66 adults with allergic rhinitis
   - 200 mg quercetin daily for 4 weeks
   - Significantly improved vs placebo:
     - Eye itching
     - Sneezing
     - Nasal discharge
     - Sleep disturbances
   - Improved symptom and quality of life scores

2. **Asthma studies:**
   - 250-500 mg daily as adjunct to standard therapy
   - Less frequent daytime and nighttime symptoms
   - Improved pulmonary function tests
   - Reduced need for rescue inhalers

3. **Epidemiological data:**
   - Finnish population: Higher quercetin intake associated with lower asthma incidence
   - UK research: Apple consumption (quercetin-rich) showed stronger inverse relationship with asthma than total fruit intake

4. **Animal studies - Food allergies:**
   - Entirely restricted peanut-induced anaphylactic reactions in rats
   - Substantially decreased plasma histamine levels

**Mechanism Comparison:**
Similar to cromolyn sodium - stabilizes mast cells preventing histamine release

**Dosage:**
- For allergy support: 500-1,000 mg per day in divided doses
- Research doses: 200-500 mg daily

**Safety Considerations:**
- Consult physician before use
- Moderate interactions with at least 60 different drugs
- No significant side effects demonstrated in studies
- Should never replace emergency treatment for acute reactions like anaphylaxis

**Clinical Use:**
- Taken consistently over weeks or months for mild hay fever and allergy symptoms
- Not a replacement for standard treatment
- Preventive rather than acute use

### Spirulina (*Arthrospira platensis*)

**Evidence Level:** Limited but positive RCT data

**Systematic Review (2021):**
- Only 2 placebo-controlled trials identified
- Total: 215 patients
- Differences between studies prevented meta-analysis
- Conclusion: Positive effects but very low level of evidence; larger trials needed

**Key Randomized Controlled Trials:**

1. **Mao et al. (2005):**
   - Randomized, double-blind, crossover study
   - Spirulina 1,000 mg or 2,000 mg daily for 12 weeks
   - 2,000 mg/day significantly reduced IL-4 levels by 32%

2. **Cingi et al. (2008):**
   - Randomized controlled trial
   - 2,000 mg spirulina daily for 6 months
   - Significant efficacy vs placebo (p < 0.001)
   - Improved symptoms:
     - Nasal discharge
     - Sneezing
     - Nasal congestion
     - Itching

3. **Iranian study (2020) - Spirulina vs. Cetirizine:**
   - **Design:** Single-blind RCT, 2015-2016
   - **Sample:** 53 patients completed (26 spirulina, 27 cetirizine)
   - **Dosing:**
     - Spirulina: 2 g daily for 2 months
     - Cetirizine: 10 mg daily for 2 months

   **Results - Spirulina SUPERIOR for:**
   - Rhinorrhea (P = 0.021)
   - Nasal obstruction (P = 0.039)
   - Smell reduction (P = 0.030)
   - Sleep quality (P < 0.05)
   - Daily work (P < 0.05)
   - Social activity (P < 0.05)

   **Laboratory markers (spirulina group):**
   - Significantly reduced: IL-1α, IL-1β, IL-4 (P < 0.001-0.008)
   - Significantly elevated: IL-10 (P = 0.049)

   **Safety:** FDA-approved, no adverse events reported

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Modulates Th cell profiles by suppressing Th2 differentiation
- Reduces IL-4 production
- Decreases histamine and IgE levels

**Prevalence:**
Turkish study: 5.7% of allergy patients using spirulina

**Dosage:**
2,000 mg (2 g) daily appears to be effective dose

### Tinospora cordifolia (Guduchi)

**Evidence Level:** Strong single RCT

**Randomized Double-Blind Placebo-Controlled Trial:**
- **Sample:** 75 patients
- **Duration:** 8 weeks
- **Intervention:** *Tinospora cordifolia* (TC) extract vs placebo

**Results - Impressive Symptom Relief:**
With TC treatment, **100% relief** reported in:
- 83% of patients: sneezing
- 69% of patients: nasal discharge
- 61% of patients: nasal obstruction
- 71% of patients: nasal pruritus

**Laboratory Findings:**
- After TC: eosinophil and neutrophil count decreased
- Goblet cells absent in nasal smear
- After placebo: marginal decrease in counts, goblet cells still present

**Safety:**
Well tolerated

**Systematic Review Conclusion:**
Promising results; confirmation in larger, more rigorously designed trials warranted

---

### Perilla frutescens (Perilla/Shiso)

**Evidence Level:** Positive RCT data, enriched extract

**Key Trial:**
- **Design:** 21-day, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled parallel group study
- **Population:** Mild seasonal allergic rhinoconjunctivitis
- **Intervention:** Extract enriched for rosmarinic acid
  - High dose: 200 mg (n=10)
  - Low dose: 50 mg (n=9)
  - Placebo (n=10)

**Results:**
Supplementation resulted in significantly increased responder rates (P < 0.05) for:
- Itchy nose
- Watery eyes
- Itchy eyes
- Total symptoms

**Active Component:**
Rosmarinic acid (polyphenolic phytochemical)

## Evidence Quality Assessment

### GRADE Certainty Ratings Summary

**Very Low to Low Certainty:**
- Most outcomes in single herb meta-analyses
- Comparisons with placebo for symptom improvement

**Moderate Certainty:**
- Single herbs vs antihistamines comparison
- Intranasal herbal medicines as adjunctive therapy
- Spirulina findings (some outcomes)

**Main Reasons for Downgrading:**
1. **Risk of bias:** Unclear methodology in primary studies
2. **Imprecision:** Wide confidence intervals, small sample sizes
3. **Inconsistency:** High heterogeneity between studies (substantial I² values)
4. **Indirectness:** Subjective outcome measures vulnerable to bias
5. **Publication bias:** Not systematically assessed in all reviews

## Safety Considerations

### General Safety Profile

**Overall Finding:** Most herbal medicines for allergic rhinitis appear well-tolerated for up to 8 weeks of use.

**Common Adverse Events:**
- Headache
- Dizziness
- Gastrointestinal disturbances
- Generally mild to moderate
- Comparable rates to placebo/control groups

### Specific Safety Concerns

**Butterbur (*Petasites hybridus*):**
- ⚠️ **CRITICAL:** Unpurified preparations contain pyrrolizidine alkaloids (UPAs)
- UPAs are hepatotoxic, nephrotoxic, mutagenic, carcinogenic
- Associated with venoocclusive disease
- **Only use certified PA-free preparations**
- Long-term safety data lacking
- Some jurisdictions have removed from market

**Stinging Nettle (*Urtica dioica*):**
- Generally well-tolerated
- No significant safety signals in reviewed studies

**Spirulina:**
- FDA-approved safety
- No adverse events in clinical trials
- Well-tolerated at 2 g daily for up to 6 months

**Quercetin:**
- No significant side effects in studies
- Moderate drug interactions (60+ medications)
- Physician consultation recommended before use

**Traditional Chinese Medicine Formulas:**
- Generally well-tolerated in trials
- Individual herb safety profiles vary
- Quality control of preparations important

### Drug Interactions

**Important Considerations:**
- Herbal therapies have pharmacological actions
- Potential for adverse effects and drug interactions
- Recommendation: Discontinue 2-3 weeks before surgery
- Quercetin: Known interactions with 60+ drugs
- Always inform healthcare providers of herbal use

## Gaps in Evidence and Research Needs

### Identified Limitations

1. **Study Quality:**
   - Unclear risk of bias in many primary studies
   - Small sample sizes
   - Short duration (most ≤8 weeks)
   - Subjective outcome measures vulnerable to bias

2. **Standardization Issues:**
   - Poor documentation of herbal preparations
   - Variable extraction methods
   - Inconsistent dosing
   - Lack of active constituent quantification

3. **Long-term Data:**
   - Most studies 8-12 weeks duration
   - Long-term efficacy unknown
   - Long-term safety data lacking (especially butterbur)

4. **Comparative Effectiveness:**
   - Limited head-to-head comparisons between herbs
   - Few studies comparing combination vs single herbs
   - Insufficient data on optimal treatment duration

5. **Mechanistic Understanding:**
   - Limited human studies on mechanisms
   - Mostly in vitro and animal data
   - Individual variation in response not well studied

### Future Research Priorities

**Systematic Reviews Recommend:**

1. **Improved Trial Design:**
   - Larger sample sizes
   - Longer follow-up periods
   - Rigorous blinding and randomization
   - Objective outcome measures alongside subjective
   - Standardized outcome reporting

2. **Herbal Product Characterization:**
   - Better documentation of preparations
   - Standardized extraction methods
   - Quantification of active constituents
   - Quality control measures
   - Batch-to-batch consistency

3. **Safety Studies:**
   - Long-term safety monitoring
   - Drug interaction studies
   - Dose-finding studies
   - Special populations (children, pregnancy, elderly)

4. **Mechanistic Studies:**
   - Human mechanistic trials
   - Biomarker validation
   - Pharmacokinetic studies
   - Individual response prediction

5. **Comparative Studies:**
   - Head-to-head herb comparisons
   - Combination therapies
   - Integration with conventional treatments
   - Cost-effectiveness analyses

## Clinical Implications

### Current State of Evidence

**What We Know:**
1. Several herbs show promise for allergic rhinitis symptom relief
2. Some herbs appear comparable to antihistamines in effectiveness
3. Most herbs appear well-tolerated for short-term use (≤8 weeks)
4. Safety profiles generally favorable with important exceptions (butterbur)

**What Remains Uncertain:**
1. Definitive clinical efficacy for most herbs
2. Optimal dosing and duration
3. Long-term safety and effectiveness
4. Which patients are most likely to benefit
5. How herbs compare to each other

### Considerations for Clinical Use

**Promising Options Based on Evidence:**

1. **Butterbur (PA-free only):**
   - Most extensively studied
   - Moderate evidence of efficacy
   - ⚠️ Critical: Only certified PA-free preparations
   - Short-term use only until long-term safety established

2. **Stinging Nettle:**
   - Moderate evidence
   - Good safety profile
   - Well-tolerated
   - May be worth trial for mild-moderate symptoms

3. **Spirulina:**
   - Limited but positive RCT data
   - Excellent safety profile
   - May be superior to antihistamines (needs confirmation)
   - Dose: 2 g daily

4. **Quercetin:**
   - Strong mechanistic rationale
   - Limited clinical trial data
   - Excellent safety (with drug interaction awareness)
   - Preventive use (weeks to months)
   - Dose: 500-1,000 mg daily divided

**Traditional Chinese Medicine:**
- International guidelines now support TCM approaches
- Specific formulas (Biminne, Shi-Bi-Lin) have RCT support
- Consider for patients interested in TCM
- Quality of preparations critical

### Integration with Conventional Care

**Not Replacements For:**
- Emergency treatment of acute allergic reactions
- Severe allergic rhinitis requiring conventional therapy
- Allergic asthma management

**Potential Roles:**
- Complementary therapy for mild-moderate symptoms
- Patients preferring natural approaches
- Adjunctive therapy to reduce medication burden
- When conventional treatments poorly tolerated

**Essential Practices:**
1. Inform healthcare providers of all herbal use
2. Use only quality-verified preparations
3. Be aware of drug interactions
4. Monitor for adverse effects
5. Discontinue before surgery
6. Start one herb at a time to identify reactions

## Conclusions

The evidence base for herbal treatments in allergic rhinitis is evolving, with increasing recognition in international clinical practice guidelines, particularly for Traditional Chinese Medicine approaches. While several herbs show promise—particularly butterbur (PA-free), stinging nettle, and spirulina—the overall quality of evidence remains predominantly low to very low.

**Key Takeaways:**

1. **Efficacy:** Some herbs appear comparable to antihistamines but with very low to low certainty evidence
2. **Safety:** Generally well-tolerated short-term (≤8 weeks) with critical exceptions (unpurified butterbur)
3. **Clinical role:** Potential complementary therapy for mild-moderate symptoms; not replacement for standard care
4. **Research needs:** Larger, better-designed trials with standardized preparations and longer follow-up
5. **Patient care:** Careful selection of quality-verified products; awareness of drug interactions; integration with conventional care

The field would benefit significantly from larger, more rigorously designed trials with better characterization of herbal preparations and longer-term safety monitoring.
