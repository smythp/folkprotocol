# Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses on Herbal Treatments for Digestive Disorders

Compiled: 2026-01-15

This document contains findings from systematic reviews and meta-analyses examining herbal treatments for various digestive conditions including IBS, functional dyspepsia, nausea, bloating, and other gastrointestinal disorders.

---

## 1. Complementary and Alternative Medicine for IBS (2020)

**Authors:** Wade Billings, Karan Mathur, Hannah J Craven, Huiping Xu, Andrea Shin

**Journal:** Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology, 19(8):1538–1553.e14

**Year:** 2020 (published September 19, 2020)

**Study Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis

**Number of Trials:** 66 randomized controlled trials (6,764 participants; from 2,825 screened articles)

### Herbs/Supplements Studied

**Herbal Therapies:**
- Curcuma (with peppermint oil, caraway oil, fennel)
- Tong-Xie-Yao-Fang (Chinese herbal formulas)
- Chinese herbal medicine blends
- Boswellia carterii
- Korean herbal medicines
- St. John's Wort
- Peppermint oil
- Padma Lax (Tibetan formula)

**Dietary Supplements:**
- Aloe vera
- Berberine hydrochloride
- Melatonin
- Ginger
- Cod protein hydrolysate
- Prebiotics (short-chain fructooligosaccharides)

### Conditions Treated
Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) across all subtypes

### Key Effectiveness Findings

**Abdominal Pain Relief:**
- Herbal therapy: SMD=0.47, 95% CI: 0.20-0.75, demonstrated significant benefit over placebo
- Mind-body interventions: SMD=0.29 (borderline significance)
- Dietary supplements and body-based therapies: no significant benefit

**Overall Response Rates:**
- Herbal therapy: RR=1.57 (NNT=5)
- Dietary supplements: RR=1.95 (NNT=4)
- Mind-body therapy: RR=1.67 (NNT=5)

### Quality Assessment (GRADE)
- Overall evidence: **Low to Very Low confidence**
- Herbal therapies for abdominal pain: Low confidence
- High heterogeneity between studies, risk of bias present

### Main Conclusions
CAM therapies such as herbal or dietary supplements and mind-body based approaches may be beneficial for IBS symptoms, but overall quality of evidence is low. Authors call for rigorous, high quality clinical trials with appropriately designed placebo or sham controls.

**Reference:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8112831/

## 2. Herbal Medicine for Functional Gastrointestinal Disorders (2020)

**Authors:** Niandi Tan, Kok Ann Gwee, Jan Tack, Mengyu Zhang, Yuwen Li, Minhu Chen, Yinglian Xiao

**Journal:** Journal of Gastroenterology and Hepatology, 35(4):544-556

**Year:** 2020 (online November 7, 2019)

**Study Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis

**Number of Trials:** 49 randomized controlled trials (7,396 participants)

**Herbal Products Tested:** Over 33 different herbal formulas

### Conditions Treated
- Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS): 23 trials
- Functional Dyspepsia (FD): 19 trials
- Functional Constipation (FC): 7 trials

### Key Effectiveness Findings

**Versus Placebo:**
- Overall: RR = 1.67 (95% CI 1.48-1.88) - statistically significant benefits for symptom improvement
- Functional Dyspepsia: RR = 1.50
- IBS: RR = 1.62
- Functional Constipation: RR = 3.83 (strongest effect)

**Versus Conventional Drugs:**
- Herbal medicines non-inferior in 5 comparative trials: RR = 1.10

### Quality Assessment
- Low risk of bias: 9 trials
- Unclear risk: 36 trials
- High risk: 4 trials

### Main Conclusions
Findings provide a positive signal for herbal medicines as potentially well-tolerated and effective treatment warranting further high-quality research. Safety profiles resembled conventional pharmacological treatments.

**Reference:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31674057/

## 3. Herbs and Spices for Functional Gastrointestinal Disorders (2018)

**Authors:** Amanda C Fifi, Cara Hannah Axelrod, Partha Chakraborty, Miguel Saps

**Journal:** Nutrients, 10(11):1715

**Year:** 2018

**Study Type:** Comprehensive clinical review

### Herbs and Spices Studied
1. Peppermint Oil
2. STW 5 (Iberogast)
3. Turmeric/Curcumin
4. Cannabis/Dronabinol
5. Aloe Vera
6. Ginger

### Conditions Treated
- Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)
- Functional Abdominal Pain (FAP)
- Functional Dyspepsia (FD)
- Abdominal migraine

### Key Effectiveness Findings by Herb

**Peppermint Oil** ✓ (Most Promising)
- Evidence Quality: 6 pediatric/adult RCTs
- Kline et al.: 75% reduction in pain severity (p < 0.001) in children
- Asgarshirazi et al.: Significantly reduced pain duration, frequency, severity (p = 0.0001)
- Adult studies: 40-75% symptom reduction vs. placebo
- Contraindication: Avoid in GERD/hiatal hernia patients

**STW 5/Iberogast** ✓ (Strong Evidence)
- Components: Bitter candytuft, angelica root, milk thistle, celandine, caraway, licorice, peppermint, balm leaf, chamomile
- Pediatric data: 96.84% treatment efficacy, 97.99% tolerability in 1,042 children
- Rapid onset: Improvements within 5 minutes, >90% maximum effect by 1 hour
- Adult RCTs: significance for IBS symptoms (p = 0.001) and pain (p = 0.0009)

**Turmeric/Curcumin** ✗ (Limited/Mixed)
- Evidence Quality: 3 adult studies only; NO pediatric data
- Inconsistent results; insufficient evidence
- No significant differences vs. placebo in most studies

**Cannabis/Dronabinol** ✗ (Ineffective)
- Evidence Quality: 2 adult RCTs only
- No significant effect on sensory threshold for discomfort
- Did not impact sensation for gas (p = 0.39) or pain (p = 0.43)

**Aloe Vera** ✗ (Ineffective)
- Evidence Quality: 3 adult RCTs; NO pediatric data
- Not superior to placebo for quality of life
- High dropout rates (29-57%) due to adverse effects
- Safety concern: Case reports of toxic hepatitis

**Ginger** ✗ (Ineffective for Pain)
- Evidence Quality: 3 adult studies; NO pediatric data
- No better than placebo for IBS (p > 0.05)
- 57.1% placebo response vs. 33.3-46.7% ginger

### Study Quality Assessment
**Limitations:**
- Majority of research in adults (limited pediatric data)
- Small sample sizes common
- Short treatment duration (2-8 weeks typically)
- Limited long-term follow-up

### Main Conclusions
Large, randomized, placebo-controlled studies needed to assess effectiveness and safety. Evidence supports use of peppermint oil and STW 5; insufficient evidence for turmeric, ginger, aloe vera, and cannabis.

**Reference:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6266883/

## 10. STW 5 (Iberogast) for Functional Dyspepsia and IBS

**Study Type:** Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses

**Clinical Trials:** 12 clinical trials since 1990; 6 RCTs; 3 placebo-controlled studies included in meta-analysis

### Components
Bitter candytuft, angelica root, milk thistle, celandine, caraway, licorice, peppermint, balm leaf, chamomile

### Key Effectiveness Findings

**Functional Dyspepsia:**
- Verum (n=138) more effective than placebo (n=135)
- Severity of most bothersome GI symptom: P-value: 0.001, OR: 0.22, 95% CI: 0.11-0.47
- Clear overall therapeutic effect demonstrated in meta-analysis

**Irritable Bowel Syndrome:**
- STW 5 reduced IBS symptom score by 1.5 points more than placebo
- Clinically relevant and statistically significant superiority (p < 0.0004)

**Comparative Studies:**
- No significant difference between STW 5 and cisapride in RCT

### Safety Profile
- Adverse drug reaction incidence: 0.04%
- Adverse events similar with verum and placebo
- No serious adverse events occurred

### Mechanism of Action
Multi-target action: Different extracts exert different pharmacological effects on different regions of the GI tract, addressing the whole symptom complex

**References:**
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3580135/
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7075703/
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15606389/

## 13. Ginger for Nausea and Vomiting (Multiple Reviews)

**Study Type:** Overview of systematic reviews and meta-analyses

**Year:** 2023 (published December 2023)

### Conditions Studied
- Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV)
- Postoperative nausea and vomiting (PONV)
- Pregnancy-related nausea and vomiting

### Key Effectiveness Findings

**Chemotherapy-Induced Nausea/Vomiting:**
- Effective in managing CINV in cancer patients
- Ginger reduced acute CINV, particularly acute vomiting
- Likelihood of acute vomiting reduced by 60% with ginger ≤1 g/day for >3 days
- Ginger supplements did NOT significantly decrease nausea, delayed nausea, or delayed vomiting

**Postoperative Nausea/Vomiting:**
- Ginger superior to placebo
- Equally effective as metoclopramide
- Summary relative risks: 0.69 for nausea, 0.61 for vomiting

**Pregnancy-Related:**
- Evidence shows promise for alleviating pregnancy-related nausea and vomiting

### Overall Assessment
Pooled evidence suggests ginger is a safe botanical option for managing nausea and vomiting, though scientific quality of published meta-analyses needs improvement. Evidence most consistent for acute vomiting; effects on nausea remain less clear.

**References:**
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38072785/
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10793599/
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34700257/

## 14. Turmeric/Curcumin for Digestive Disorders (2024)

**Year:** 2024

**Study Type:** Systematic review

**Screening:** 1,136 citations screened; 26 eligible studies retained

### Key Findings by Condition

**Irritable Bowel Syndrome:**
- 4 out of 7 studies showed beneficial effect

**Ulcerative Colitis:**
- 6 out of 7 studies showed positive effects
- Adjuvant curcumin produced significant beneficial effect on clinical remission vs placebo
- No significant effects on clinical improvement, endoscopic remission, or endoscopic improvement

**Peptic Ulcer Disease:**
- Both studies showed positive effects

**Crohn's Disease:**
- 2 out of 4 studies demonstrated positive effects

**H. pylori Infection:**
- 2 out of 4 studies highlighted potential role in eradication

### Quality Assessment
- Meta-analysis not conducted due to high heterogeneity
- Most studies assessed to have high risk of bias
- Low quality of evidence base: clinical/methodological heterogeneity, high risks of bias, poor reporting, statistical issues

### Main Conclusions
Turmeric appears safe and could be used as nutraceutical option or add-on therapy in treatment of digestive disorders, particularly in patients with IBD or IBS. However, findings should be interpreted with caution given low quality of evidence.

**Reference:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38503513/

## 15. Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra) for Peptic Ulcer (2024)

**Year:** 2024 (searched databases up to August 2024)

**Study Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis

**Number of Studies:** 9 studies (618 participants)

### Key Effectiveness Findings
- **Pain improvement:** Pooled OR = 0.52 (95% CI: 0.42–1.45) - NOT statistically significant
- **Complete ulcer healing:** Pooled OR = 0.60 (95% CI: 0.24–1.43) - NOT statistically significant

### Individual Trial Results

**Negative Results:**
- Trial with 96 gastric ulcer patients: No differences between DGL and placebo after 4 weeks in complete healing, ulcer area reduction, or clinical improvement
- Caved-S study: Unable to demonstrate healing effect on gastric ulcer

**Positive Results:**
- Trial with 33 patients (760 mg DGL 3x daily for 1 month):
  - DGL recipients: 78% reduction in ulcer size vs 34% placebo
  - Complete healing: 44% vs 6% placebo
- Licorice vs bismuth comparison:
  - Peptic ulcer healing: 70% (licorice) vs 95% (bismuth)
  - H. pylori eradication: 45% (licorice) vs 70% (bismuth)

### Overall Assessment
Clinical trial support for DGL is weak. Data suggest DGL may have role in protecting mucosal erosion and decreasing relapse, but has yet to meet criteria for inclusion in evidence-based conventional medicine.

**Reference:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2212958825000448

## 16. Artichoke Extract for Digestive Disorders

**Study Type:** Multiple clinical trials (not full systematic review)

### Conditions Studied
- Functional Dyspepsia
- Irritable Bowel Syndrome

### Key Clinical Trial Findings

**Functional Dyspepsia:**
- Double-blind RCT: 247 patients
- Treatment: Artichoke leaf extract 640 mg 3x daily vs placebo
- Overall symptom improvement over 6 weeks significantly greater with artichoke extract
- Combined ginger + artichoke extract: Significant improvement in FD symptoms after 14 days vs placebo

**Irritable Bowel Syndrome:**
- Subset analysis showed 26.4% fall in IBS incidence
- Significant shift in bowel pattern from "alternating constipation/diarrhea" toward "normal"
- Symptom score decreased by 41%
- Quality-of-life score improved by 20% after treatment

### Overall Assessment
Growing evidence for therapeutic properties, with dyspepsia as specific indication. Symptom overlap suggests potential for treating IBS as well.

## 18. Fennel for Digestive Disorders

**Study Type:** Multiple clinical trials and animal studies

### Key Findings

**Post-Surgical Recovery:**
- Heated fennel therapy accelerated recovery of GI function postoperatively
- Facilitated gastrointestinal motility in patients early postoperatively

- Fennel seed extract reduced histological and functional damage in mice with DSS-induced colitis

## Summary of Evidence Quality

### Strong Evidence (Multiple High-Quality Systematic Reviews/Meta-Analyses)
1. **Peppermint oil** for IBS - Consistent positive findings across multiple reviews
2. **STW 5 (Iberogast)** for functional dyspepsia and IBS - Well-established efficacy
3. **Ginger** for acute vomiting (chemotherapy, postoperative) - Strong evidence for vomiting, weaker for nausea

### Moderate Evidence (Positive Systematic Reviews with Quality Concerns)
1. **Chinese herbal medicine** for functional dyspepsia - Positive but variable quality
2. **Herbal medicines generally** for functional GI disorders - Positive signal but methodological limitations
3. **Turmeric/curcumin** for IBD and IBS - Mixed results, high heterogeneity

### Limited/Unclear Evidence
1. **Aloe vera** for IBS - Inconsistent results, safety concerns
2. **Licorice/DGL** for peptic ulcers - Mixed results, weak trial support
3. **Artichoke extract** for dyspepsia/IBS - Promising but limited systematic review data

### Insufficient Evidence
1. **Cannabis/dronabinol** for GI disorders - Ineffective in available trials
2. **Ginger** for IBS pain - Not superior to placebo

---

## Common Methodological Limitations Across Reviews

1. **Quality of Evidence:** Most reviews rate overall evidence quality as low to very low (GRADE assessments)
2. **Heterogeneity:** High heterogeneity between studies in terms of:
   - Herbal preparations and dosages
   - Study populations
   - Outcome measures
   - Treatment duration
3. **Risk of Bias:** Many included trials have unclear or high risk of bias
4. **Sample Sizes:** Often small sample sizes in individual trials
5. **Duration:** Short treatment and follow-up periods (typically 2-8 weeks)
6. **Reporting:** Poor reporting quality in many trials
7. **Pediatric Data:** Significant lack of pediatric-specific research

---

## Research Gaps Identified

1. Need for large, high-quality, randomized, placebo-controlled trials
2. Longer-term follow-up studies to assess sustained benefits
3. Standardization of herbal preparations and dosages
4. More pediatric-focused research
5. Mechanism of action studies
6. Head-to-head comparisons between different herbal therapies
7. Studies in diverse populations
8. Investigation of optimal treatment duration
9. Better adverse event monitoring and reporting

## Safety Findings

### Generally Safe with Low Adverse Event Rates
- STW 5 (Iberogast): 0.04% adverse drug reaction incidence
- Herbal medicines for FGIDs: Adverse events similar to or lower than conventional medicines
- Chamomile: Self-limiting, non-serious adverse events
- No serious adverse events reported for most herbal interventions in IBS/FD trials

### Herbs with Safety Concerns
- **Aloe vera:** High dropout rates (29-57%), case reports of toxic hepatitis
- **Peppermint oil:** Higher adverse event rate than placebo (RR=1.57), contraindicated in GERD
- **Chinese herbal medicine:** Higher adverse event rate than placebo for IBS

### Overall Safety Profile
Most systematic reviews conclude that herbal medicines for digestive disorders have acceptable safety profiles, with adverse events generally mild and self-limiting. However, the quality of adverse event reporting is often poor, limiting definitive safety conclusions.
