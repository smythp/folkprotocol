# Comparative Trials: Herbal Antiemetics for Nausea

## Overview

This document synthesizes head-to-head comparative clinical trials examining herbal antiemetics across different nausea contexts: motion sickness, pregnancy, chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV), and post-operative nausea and vomiting (PONV).

---

## Ginger vs Peppermint

### Chemotherapy-Induced Nausea (CINV)
A quasi-experimental study at Husada Hospital Jakarta compared peppermint and ginger aromatherapy in 28 breast cancer patients undergoing chemotherapy. Both significantly reduced nausea and vomiting (p < 0.05), but at 6 hours post-chemotherapy, **peppermint showed lower average nausea scores than ginger**.

Sources:
- [Peppermint and Ginger Aromatherapy for Managing Nausea and Vomiting During Chemotherapy](https://jurnal.globalhealthsciencegroup.com/index.php/IJGHR/article/view/6212)

### Gastrointestinal Disturbances in TB Patients
A randomized clinical trial (NCT06157034) with 200 participants compared ginger (1g/day), caraway (2g/day), peppermint (3mL/day), and placebo over 3 months. **Ginger showed superior results**: no vomiting recurrence in both intervention and washout phases, and lower postprandial distress intensity.

Sources:
- [Comparative Study on the Prokinetic Effects of Ginger, Caraway, and Peppermint in TB Patients](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12104075/)

### Post-Operative Nausea (PONV)
A single-blind RCT after oral surgery under general anesthesia found that aromatherapy with peppermint, ginger, and lavender all significantly reduced nausea severity (p < 0.001), with both ginger and peppermint groups showing significantly lower pain and nausea compared to controls. Patient satisfaction was significantly improved.

Sources:
- [Effect of aromatherapy with peppermint, ginger, and lavender on postoperative nausea](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40154579/)
- [Effect of Ginger and Mint on PONV in Thyroidectomy Patients](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40856681/)

### Meta-Analysis Summary
In meta-analyses, **ginger showed the most significant effect on nausea overall**, with peppermint and lavender also showing significant effects. Chamomile and ginger reduced nausea, while peppermint and ginger were promising for vomiting.

## Ginger vs Pharmaceutical Antiemetics

### Ginger + Ondansetron vs Ondansetron Alone
A prospective, double-blind RCT (March 2008–July 2010) with 100 adults randomized to either IV ondansetron (4mg) + placebo OR IV ondansetron (4mg) + ginger (1g) one hour before anesthesia. **The combination was significantly superior (p < 0.05)** for both frequency and severity of PONV.

Sources:
- [The efficacy of ginger added to ondansetron for preventing PONV](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3897009/)

### Ginger as Adjunct to Standard CINV Prophylaxis
In bone sarcoma patients receiving cisplatin/doxorubicin with standard ondansetron + dexamethasone:
- **Ginger group showed clinically meaningful lower delayed nausea**: Cycle 2 (53% vs 75%, p=0.020), Cycle 3 (49% vs 79%, p=0.002)
- **Delayed vomiting**: Cycle 2 (4% vs 27%, p=0.001), Cycle 3 (2% vs 23%, p=0.001)

Sources:
- [Anti-emetic effect of ginger powder as add-on therapy](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20842754/)
- [Effect of Standardized Ginger Root Powder on CINV](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2212267223015265)

### Ginger vs Metoclopramide

**PONV in Laparoscopic Surgery (n=120)**
- Nausea rates: Ginger 21% vs Metoclopramide 27% vs Placebo 41%
- Ginger significantly fewer nausea patients vs placebo (p=0.0006)
- **Ginger equally effective as metoclopramide**

**Cesarean Section (n=180)**
- Metoclopramide 10mg IV vs Ginger 1g oral before spinal anesthesia
- **Ginger reduced nausea and vomiting to the same extent as metoclopramide**
- Ginger has fewer side effects (no sedation, hypotension, or extrapyramidal effects)

**Pregnancy Nausea (Mixed Results)**
- One study: Ginger less effective than metoclopramide
- Another study: Ginger more effective than metoclopramide for severity relief

Sources:
- [Comparing effects of ginger and metoclopramide on pregnancy nausea](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22545357/)
- [Ginger and metoclopramide in cesarean section](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7073360/)

### Ginger vs Dimenhydrinate (Motion Sickness)

**Classic Mowrey & Clayson Study (1982)**
- 36 blindfolded, motion-sickness-prone subjects in rotating chair
- Ginger 940mg vs Dimenhydrinate 100mg vs Placebo
- **Ginger subjects averaged 5.5 min (half completed 6 min full test)**
- **Dimenhydrinate averaged 3.5 min (none completed 6 min)**
- Placebo averaged 1.5 min

**Mechanism Difference (Holtmann et al. 1989)**
- Ginger did NOT affect vestibular or oculomotor systems (no nystagmus changes)
- Dimenhydrinate DID reduce nystagmus response
- **Conclusion: Ginger works on gastric system, not vestibular**

**Gastric Mechanism Study**
Ginger (1,000–2,000mg) pretreatment reduced:
- Nausea scores
- Tachygastria (abnormal stomach contractions)
- Plasma vasopressin elevation

Sources:
- [Motion sickness, ginger, and psychophysics](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6121968/)
- [Anti-motion sickness mechanism of ginger](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2683568/)
- [Effects of ginger on motion sickness and gastric slow-wave dysrhythmias](https://journals.physiology.org/doi/full/10.1152/ajpgi.00164.2002)

## Ginger vs Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine) for Pregnancy Nausea

### Meta-Analyses

**2020 Meta-Analysis (n=1,174)**
- Ginger improved NVP symptoms vs placebo (significant)
- Ginger vs B6: No significant difference for nausea, vomiting, or general symptoms
- **Conclusion: Ginger more effective than B6, though not statistically significant**

**2022 Meta-Analysis (n=819)**
- No discernible difference between ginger and B6 for nausea or vomiting scores
- **Conclusion: Ginger equivalent to vitamin B6**

Sources:
- [Effect of ginger vs vitamin B6 and placebo: meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31937153/)

### Individual RCTs

**Australian Teaching Hospital (n=291)**
- 1.05g ginger vs 75mg B6 daily for 3 weeks
- **Ginger equivalent to B6** for nausea, retching, and vomiting

**Iranian Triple-Blind Trial**
- Ginger 500mg BID vs B6 40mg BID vs placebo for 4 days
- Ginger more effective than placebo
- **Ginger comparable to B6**

**Direct Comparison (n=70)**
- Ginger 1g/day vs B6 40mg/day for 4 days
- Visual analogue nausea scores decreased more in ginger group (p=0.024)
- **Ginger more effective for nausea severity**
- Equally effective for vomiting episodes

Sources:
- [Comparing Effectiveness of B6 and Ginger](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3819920/)
- [A randomized comparison of ginger and vitamin B6](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14649969/)
- [Comparison ginger vs B6: double-blind controlled trial](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17621727/)

## Ginger vs P6 Acupressure

### Pregnancy Nausea (n=159)
First direct comparison RCT of these two non-pharmacological therapies:
- Ginger reduced vomiting 52%, nausea 48%
- **Ginger more effective than acupressure** for mild-moderate NVP <16 weeks
- Acupressure also effective (p < 0.001) but less so than ginger

Sources:
- [Acupressure and Ginger for NVP: Randomized Study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3929824/)

### Chemotherapy-Induced Nausea (n=172)
Four groups: control, ginger, acupressure, combined
- Ginger improved delayed CINV and functional living index (p < 0.05)
- Ginger had no significant effect on acute CINV
- **Acupressure more effective for acute symptoms**
- Combined therapy improved both acute and delayed symptoms

Sources:
- [Effect of ginger and P6 acupressure on CINV](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38461442/)

## Ginger vs Chamomile

### CINV in Breast Cancer (n=65)
Double-blind RCT, ginger 500mg vs chamomile 500mg twice daily, 5 days before/after chemo:
- Both significantly reduced vomiting frequency (no difference between groups)
- **Ginger significantly reduced nausea frequency; chamomile did not**

Sources:
- [Effect of Ginger and Chamomile on CINV in Breast Cancer](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27644672/)

### Pregnancy Nausea
**Three-Way Comparison (n=105)**
- Chamomile vs ginger vs B6, oral capsules for 4 days
- **All three equally effective** with no significant superiority

**Direct Ginger vs Chamomile (n=unspecified, 2018)**
- 500mg twice daily for one week
- **Chamomile MORE effective than ginger** for reducing NVP

Sources:
- [Effect of Ginger and Chamomile on NVP](https://journal.waocp.org/article_33110.html)

## Chamomile vs Placebo

### Post-Operative Nausea - Middle Ear Surgery (n=110)
Triple-blind RCT after tympanoplasty/mastoidectomy:
- No significant difference immediately after or at 1, 2, 6 hours
- **At 4 hours: chamomile group had significantly lower nausea severity**
- No GI side effects reported

Sources:
- [Chamomile for PONV in Middle Ear Surgery](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11910166/)

### CINV
- Chamomile extract reduced nausea scores 2 hours post-chemo (p=0.034)
- No significant difference in vomiting

## Peppermint Aromatherapy vs Standard Antiemetics

### Meta-Analysis Results (PONV)
- Aromatherapy reduced nausea severity (SMD: -0.93, p=0.010)
- Vomiting reduction NOT statistically significant
- **Ginger essence most effective (SMD: -3.44)**
- Lavender (SMD: -0.64) and peppermint (SMD: -0.62) also significant

### Rescue Antiemetic Use
- Participants receiving aromatherapy less likely to require rescue antiemetics (RR 0.60, 95% CI 0.37-0.97, p=0.04)
- Aydin et al.: Peppermint group 3.7% required antiemetics vs 24.1% control
- Cetin et al.: Peppermint group 0% required antiemetics vs 18.4% control (p < 0.05)

### Limitations
- **No evidence aromatherapy can REPLACE pharmacological antiemetics**
- Blinding difficult due to distinctive scent
- Some benefit may be from inhalation/relaxation rather than peppermint specifically

Sources:
- [Aromatherapy for PONV prevention: systematic review and meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11236078/)
- [Effects of Peppermint Aromatherapy on PONV](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32782076/)

## Cannabis/Cannabinoids vs Pharmaceutical Antiemetics

### Dronabinol vs Ondansetron
One RCT found dronabinol similarly effective to ondansetron for delayed-onset CINV. Combination of both was significantly better than placebo for acute CINV.

**Limitations:**
- Baseline risk factors imbalanced in favor of dronabinol
- Most trials conducted 1975–1991, before modern 5-HT3 antagonists

### Cannabinoids vs Placebo
- 5x more likely to report complete absence of vomiting
- 3x more likely to report complete absence of both nausea and vomiting

### THC:CBD Combination
Phase II/III trial with THC 2.5mg + CBD 2.5mg capsules TID:
- **Effective adjunct despite standard antiemetic prophylaxis**
- Associated with additional adverse events

Sources:
- [Oral Cannabis Extract for Secondary Prevention of CINV](https://ascopubs.org/doi/10.1200/JCO.23.01836)
- [Cannabinoids for nausea and vomiting in adults with cancer](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6931414/)

## Cardamom Trials

### Cesarean Section PONV (n=70)
Single-blind RCT with cardamom aromatherapy:
- Episodes of nausea: **37.1% vs 65.7%** (p=0.006)
- Episodes of retching: **20% vs 45.7%** (p=0.028)
- Antiemetic use: **37.1% vs 65.7%** (p=0.009)

### Pregnancy Nausea (n=120)
Double-blind RCT, cardamom 500mg TID before meals:
- Significantly decreased nausea frequency, duration, and vomiting (p<0.0001)
- Reduced severity during and after intervention

Sources:
- [Cardamom Inhalation Therapy for PONV in Cesarean Section](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35581106/)
- [Cardamom powder effect on pregnancy nausea](https://cmja.arakmu.ac.ir/browse.php?a_id=279)

## Lemon and Spearmint

### Spearmint vs Peppermint for CINV
RCT at Mashhad University compared spearmint, peppermint, and placebo essential oils. Both mints were effective and **less expensive than standard antiemetics**, providing a safe therapeutic option.

Sources:
- [Antiemetic activity of spearmint and peppermint in CINV](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3562057/)

### Lemon Aromatherapy for Pregnancy Nausea (n=100)
Double-blind RCT found **lemon scent effective** in reducing NVP.

### Combined Lemon + Peppermint for Pregnancy (n=90)
Mean nausea/vomiting scores became significantly different on days 2, 3, and 4 of intervention. **Combined aromatherapy reduced mild-moderate NVP.**

### Peppermint-Lemon in Pediatric Leukemia (n=90)
Aromatherapy inhalation via diffuser **prevented nausea and vomiting** in children 2–12 years undergoing chemotherapy. Recommended as adjunct to antiemetics.

Sources:
- [Combined Lemon and Peppermint Aromatherapy for NVP](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33344211/)
- [Effect of peppermint-lemon aromatherapy in pediatric leukemia](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0882596323001793)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Ginger is the most studied herbal antiemetic** with consistent evidence across all contexts
2. **Ginger works via gastric mechanisms**, not vestibular—making it complementary to antihistamines for motion sickness
3. **For pregnancy**: Ginger ≈ Vitamin B6 ≈ Dimenhydrinate in effectiveness, but ginger causes less drowsiness
4. **Combination therapy** (ginger + standard antiemetics) outperforms either alone
5. **Peppermint aromatherapy** reduces need for rescue antiemetics but doesn't replace pharmacological prophylaxis
6. **Chamomile** reduces vomiting but is less effective than ginger for nausea specifically
7. **P6 acupressure** may be more effective for acute symptoms; ginger better for delayed symptoms
8. **Cardamom** shows promise, especially via aromatherapy for PONV
9. **Limited head-to-head trials** between different herbs—most compare to placebo or pharmaceuticals

---

## Research Gaps

- Few direct herb-vs-herb comparisons (most compare to placebo or drugs)
- No modern trials comparing cannabinoids to current 5-HT3 antagonists
- Optimal dosing and timing poorly characterized
- Long-term safety data limited for high-dose/prolonged use
- Blinding challenges with aromatic interventions
- Heterogeneous outcome measures across studies
