# Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses: Herbal Treatments for Nausea

*Research compilation for Folk Protocol*
*Last updated: 2026-01-15*

---

## Overview

This document summarizes the highest-quality evidence (systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and key RCTs) for herbal treatments of nausea. The evidence is strongest for ginger, with significant support for peppermint aromatherapy and emerging evidence for chamomile.

---

## 1. Ginger (Zingiber officinale)

### Evidence Summary

Ginger is the most extensively studied herbal antiemetic, with 15+ published meta-analyses examining its efficacy across multiple contexts.

**2024 Umbrella Review** ([Ginger for treating nausea and vomiting: an overview of systematic reviews and meta-analyses](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38072785/))
- Analyzed 15 meta-analyses meeting PRISMA 2009 guidelines
- Methodological quality (AMSTAR-2): critically low to low
- Conclusion: Supports ginger's effectiveness across multiple contexts despite quality limitations

### By Clinical Context

#### Pregnancy-Associated Nausea and Vomiting (NVP/Hyperemesis Gravidarum)

**Key Meta-Analysis** ([A systematic review and meta-analysis of the effect and safety of ginger in the treatment of pregnancy-associated nausea and vomiting](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3995184/))
- 10 high-quality RCTs included
- Forms studied: capsules, powders, biscuits
- Comparators: placebo, vitamin B6, metoclopramide
- **Pooled OR: 0.41 (95% CI: 0.22-0.79, P = 0.008)**
- Interpretation: 59% reduction in odds of nausea/vomiting symptoms

**Additional Finding**: Ginger equivalent to vitamin B6 and diphenhydramine for pregnancy NVP

**Regulatory Note**: Finland prohibits ginger supplements for pregnant women due to perceived insufficient evidence; most other regulatory bodies consider it safe

#### Chemotherapy-Induced Nausea and Vomiting (CINV)

**Systematic Review 2022** ([Effects of Ginger Intake on Chemotherapy-Induced Nausea and Vomiting: A Systematic Review of Randomized Clinical Trials](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9739555/))
- Supports ginger's effectiveness in managing CINV
- Reduces severity and decreases need for rescue antiemetics
- Recommendation: Initiate ginger before chemotherapy, alongside standard antiemetics

**Meta-Analysis 2024** ([Efficacy and Safety of Ginger on Chemotherapy-Induced Nausea and Vomiting: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38625733/))
- Acute vomiting reduced by 60% with ginger ≤1 g/day for >3 days
- **OR 0.4 (95% CI 0.17-0.81; P=0.01)**

**Important Caveat** ([2019 systematic review update](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31519467/)): Found no association between ginger and CINV outcomes due to substantial clinical heterogeneity (varying chemotherapy agents, cancer types, antiemetic protocols)

#### Postoperative Nausea and Vomiting (PONV)

**Meta-Analysis** ([Efficacy of Ginger in Preventing Postoperative Nausea and Vomiting](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34312974/))
- Significantly lower nausea severity: **MD = -0.71 (95% CI = -1.37 to -0.06, p = 0.03)**
- Reduced rescue antiemetic use: **RR = 0.71 (95% CI = 0.62-0.82, p < 0.001)**
- Conclusion: Ginger effective for PONV prevention

**Early Systematic Review** ([Efficacy of ginger for nausea and vomiting: a systematic review of randomized clinical trials](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10793599/))
- 2 of 3 PONV studies: ginger superior to placebo
- Ginger equally effective as metoclopramide
- Pooled absolute risk reduction for nausea incidence (ginger 1g pre-op): non-significant vs placebo

### Dosage Recommendations from Evidence

| Context | Optimal Dose | Duration | Form |
|---------|-------------|----------|------|
| General nausea | 1,000 mg/day | As needed | Capsule, powder |
| Pregnancy NVP | <1,500 mg/day | Throughout symptoms | Capsule, biscuit |
| CINV | 500-1,000 mg/day | 3+ days pre-chemo | Capsule |
| PONV | 1,000 mg | Single dose pre-op | Capsule |

**Standard Protocol**: 250 mg capsules × 4 daily (most commonly studied)

**Key Finding**: Subgroup analyses favor doses <1,500 mg/day; higher doses may not improve efficacy

**Source**: [Ginger in gastrointestinal disorders: A systematic review of clinical trials](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/fsn3.807)

### Safety Profile

**Pregnancy Safety** ([Systematic review and meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3995184/))
- No significant risk for spontaneous abortion vs placebo: RR 3.14 (95% CI 0.65-15.11, p = 0.15)
- No significant risk vs vitamin B6: RR 0.49 (95% CI 0.17-1.42, p = 0.19)
- No evidence of teratogenicity in human studies
- No associations with malformations

**Side Effects** (15 RCTs, 777 patients):
- 3.3% reported mild side effects not requiring treatment
- Common: mild gastrointestinal symptoms, heartburn, sleepiness
- Doses >6 g may cause gastric irritation

**Drug Interactions**:
- Major interaction: Nifedipine
- Moderate interactions: Metformin, insulin, aspirin
- Theoretical concern: Antiplatelet activity (dose-dependent inhibition of platelet thromboxane)

**FDA Status**: Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS)

### Comparative Effectiveness

**Ginger vs Standard Antiemetics** ([RCT comparison](https://journals.lww.com/iphr/abstract/2003/35010/ginger_as_an_antiemetic_in_nausea_and_vomiting.6.aspx))
- Complete nausea control: Ginger 62%, Metoclopramide 58%, Ondansetron 86%
- Complete vomiting control: Ginger 68%, Metoclopramide 64%, Ondansetron 86%
- Conclusion: Ginger ≈ metoclopramide; ondansetron superior to both

**Ginger + Ondansetron vs Ondansetron Alone** ([RCT](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3897009/))
- Combination significantly better for PONV prevention (P < 0.05)
- Reduced frequency and severity compared to ondansetron alone

## 2. Peppermint (Mentha × piperita)

### Evidence Summary

Peppermint aromatherapy (essential oil inhalation) has strong evidence for nausea, particularly postoperative and chemotherapy-induced.

#### Systematic Review 2025

**[Inhaling Peppermint Essential Oil as a Promising Complementary Therapy in the Treatment of Nausea and Vomiting](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12294836/)**
- Databases: Scopus, Embase, Cochrane, MEDLINE, Web of Science
- Updated December 2024
- Contexts: PONV, CINV, pregnancy NVP
- Finding: Peppermint group showed lower nausea/vomiting scores than control, especially at later time points

#### Chemotherapy-Induced Nausea (CINV)

**2025 Meta-Analysis** ([Journal of Advances in Medical and Biomedical Research](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/393885256_The_Effect_of_Peppermint_on_Chemotherapy_Induced_Nausea_and_Vomiting_CINV_A_Systematic_Review_and_Meta-Analysis))
- Random effects model analysis
- No significant publication bias (Egger P=0.125)
- Heterogeneity: relatively high (P=0.020, I²=60.1%)

**Aromatherapy for Cancer Meta-Analysis 2024** ([Effects of aromatherapy on nausea and vomiting in patients with cancer](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1744388124000112))
- 25 RCTs across 10 articles
- Aromatherapy reduced nausea/vomiting: **SMD = −0.81 (95% CI: −1.11 to −0.52)**
- Nausea alone: SMD = −0.85
- Combined nausea + vomiting: SMD = −1.08
- **Peppermint oil was the most effective** essential oil tested

#### Irritable Bowel Syndrome (with digestive symptoms)

**Cochrane-Style Review 2022** ([Systematic review and meta-analysis: efficacy of peppermint oil in irritable bowel syndrome](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35942669/))
- 10 studies, 1,030 participants
- Peppermint oil superior to placebo for:
  - Overall IBS symptoms
  - Abdominal pain
- Adverse events more frequent than placebo
- Quality of evidence: very low
- Note: More side effects than placebo observed

### Dosage and Administration

| Form | Application | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------|
| Essential oil inhalation | 1-3 drops on tissue/cotton ball | Most evidence for nausea |
| Aromatherapy diffuser | Per manufacturer directions | For ongoing symptom management |
| Enteric-coated capsules | 180-225 mg, 2-3× daily | For IBS/digestive symptoms |

**Note**: Oral peppermint oil should be enteric-coated to prevent gastric release

### Safety

- Generally well tolerated via inhalation
- Oral use: heartburn, gastric irritation possible
- Contraindications: GERD, hiatal hernia (oral use)
- May interact with cyclosporine, other CYP3A4 substrates

## 3. Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla)

### Evidence Summary

Chamomile shows promise for nausea, particularly via aromatherapy and in combination with other modalities.

**Systematic Review on Cancer Complications** ([Effect of Chamomile on the Complications of Cancer](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10108427/))
- 18 articles, 1,099 cancer patients
- 15 RCTs included
- Forms: mouthwash, topical, tea, capsule, syrup, aromatherapy massage
- Chamomile effectively reduced vomiting
- Improved appetite and quality of life

**Aromatherapy for CINV** ([Effectiveness of Inhaled Aromatherapy on Chemotherapy-Induced Nausea and Vomiting](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34283916/))
- 11 studies (9 adults, 2 children)
- Chamomile decreased nausea only (not vomiting) in 3/7 significant studies
- Peppermint and ginger more effective for combined nausea + vomiting

**Post-Cesarean Aromatherapy** ([Effects of aromatherapy on discomfort in mothers undergoing cesarean section](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39675059/))
- 17 studies, 1,490 participants
- Reduced nausea incidence: **RR = 0.52 (95% CI [0.33, 0.81], p = 0.004)**
- Chamomile oil particularly effective
- Low dosage (≤2 drops) optimal
- Sessions ≤20 minutes most effective

### Safety

**Systematic Review on Adverse Events** ([Chamomile: A systematic review of adverse events](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0965229925000676))
- 72 trials, 11 case reports
- 2,896 patients received chamomile
- 65 adverse events across 10 trials
- Most common: gastrointestinal complications, drowsiness
- All self-limiting and non-serious
- Conclusion: Generally safe in controlled dosages

## 4. Complementary Approaches: P6 Acupressure

While not herbal, P6 (Neiguan) acupressure is often used alongside or compared with herbal antiemetics.

### Cochrane Review Evidence

**[Stimulation of the wrist acupuncture point P6 for preventing postoperative nausea and vomiting](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD003281_what-are-benefits-and-risks-different-wrist-pc6-acupoint-stimulation-techniques-preventing-nausea)**
- Both invasive (acupuncture) and noninvasive (acupressure bands) effective
- Reduction with PC6 techniques: 135-247 fewer people per 1,000 experiencing PONV
- PC6 + antiemetics: 203-383 fewer per 1,000
- Combining noninvasive PC6 with antiemetics reduces need for rescue drugs

**Meta-Analysis** ([Acupressure the PC6 point for alleviating postoperative nausea and vomiting](https://journals.lww.com/md-journal/fulltext/2019/08160/acupressure_the_pc6_point_for_alleviating.73.aspx))
- 9 RCTs analyzed
- Reduced vomiting incidence: **RR=0.50 (95% CI: 0.37-0.66, P < 0.01)**
- Nausea incidence: no significant difference (RR=0.85, 95% CI: 0.72-1.00)

**CINV Acupressure Review** ([The Effectiveness of P6 and Auricular Acupressure as a Complimentary Therapy in Chemotherapy-Induced Nausea and Vomiting](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10943727/))
- P6 and auricular acupressure effective complementary therapy
- Applicable across cancer types and chemotherapy regimens

### Safety

- 12 studies (1,328 participants): no complaints of side effects
- Minor, self-limiting effects in some studies: redness, irritation, swollen wrists
- No serious or long-term complications reported

## 5. Other Herbs with Emerging Evidence

### Lemon Aromatherapy

**RCT Evidence** ([The effect of lemon inhalation aromatherapy on pain, nausea, as well as vomiting](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12906-023-04047-z))
- Lower extremity fracture surgery patients
- Reduced post-surgical nausea and vomiting
- Additional study (2014): pregnancy NVP, double-blind RCT

### Cardamom, Fennel, Spearmint

- Included in systematic reviews for pregnancy NVP
- Often used in traditional formulas
- Cardamom: RCT in aromatherapy blend (N=1,151)
- Less individual study evidence than ginger/peppermint

### Pomegranate

- Mentioned in systematic reviews as safe alternative for pregnancy NVP
- Limited individual trial data

## 6. Evidence Quality Assessment

### Methodological Limitations

1. **Heterogeneity**: Substantial variation in:
   - Ginger form (fresh, dried, extract, capsule, biscuit)
   - Dosing regimens
   - Chemotherapy agents (for CINV studies)
   - Antiemetic protocols
   - Patient populations

2. **Quality Ratings**:
   - Most meta-analyses: AMSTAR-2 critically low to low
   - Many individual RCTs: high risk of bias
   - Blinding challenges with aromatherapy

3. **Funding and Conflicts**:
   - Variable disclosure across studies
   - Some industry-sponsored trials

### Recommendations for Interpretation

- Ginger: Sufficient evidence for clinical use as adjunct therapy
- Peppermint aromatherapy: Strong evidence for PONV/CINV
- Chamomile: Promising but needs more large RCTs
- All herbs: Best evidence as adjunct to, not replacement for, standard antiemetics

## 7. Clinical Practice Implications

### Evidence-Based Recommendations

| Context | First Line | Dosage | Notes |
|---------|-----------|--------|-------|
| Pregnancy NVP | Ginger | 1,000 mg/day in divided doses | Vitamin B6 comparable alternative |
| Mild PONV | Ginger + P6 acupressure | 1,000 mg pre-op | Can combine with standard antiemetics |
| CINV | Ginger + peppermint aromatherapy | Ginger 500-1,000 mg/day; peppermint inhalation PRN | Always with standard antiemetics |
| General nausea | Ginger or peppermint tea | As tolerated | Safe self-care option |

### When to Use vs. Standard Antiemetics

**Use herbal approaches as:**
- First-line for mild, self-limiting nausea
- Adjunct to reduce rescue antiemetic needs
- Alternative when pharmaceutical options contraindicated
- Patient preference for natural options

**Standard antiemetics remain first-line for:**
- Severe vomiting with dehydration risk
- High-emetogenic chemotherapy
- Hyperemesis gravidarum requiring hospitalization
