# Clinical Treatment Guide for Nausea

This guide synthesizes evidence from systematic reviews, comparative trials, and mechanism studies to provide practical treatment recommendations for nausea and vomiting.

## Evidence Hierarchy: Which Herbs Work Best?

### Tier 1: Strongest Evidence
**Ginger (Zingiber officinale)**
- **Evidence level**: 15+ meta-analyses, highest quality evidence
- **Effectiveness**: OR 0.41 (95% CI: 0.22-0.79, P = 0.008) across all nausea contexts
- **Best for**: Pregnancy NVP, PONV, CINV, general digestive nausea
- **Mechanism**: Non-competitive 5-HT3 receptor antagonism (allosteric modulation), NK-1 antagonism, mitochondrial protection

**Peppermint (Mentha piperita)**
- **Evidence level**: Multiple systematic reviews, "most effective essential oil" in 2025 meta-analysis
- **Effectiveness**: SMD = -0.81 for cancer-related nausea (aromatherapy)
- **Best for**: PONV aromatherapy, CINV aromatherapy, IBS-related nausea
- **Mechanism**: Calcium channel blockade (IC50 15.2-25.9 μg/mL), 5-HT3 receptor antagonism, TRPM8 activation

### Tier 2: Good Supporting Evidence
**Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla)**
- **Evidence level**: Multiple RCTs, systematic reviews
- **Effectiveness**: RR = 0.52 for post-cesarean nausea; equal to ginger for CINV vomiting
- **Best for**: Nausea with anxiety/tension, CINV, post-surgical nausea
- **Mechanism**: Voltage-dependent calcium channel inhibition (apigenin), GABAergic modulation, anti-inflammatory

**Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine)**
- **Evidence level**: Multiple RCTs in pregnancy
- **Effectiveness**: Comparable to ginger for NVP in most trials
- **Best for**: Pregnancy NVP (first-line pharmaceutical option)
- **Dose**: 10-25 mg three times daily

### Tier 3: Traditional/Moderate Evidence
**Fennel (Foeniculum vulgare)** - Carminative, antispasmodic, best for gas/bloating-associated nausea

**Cardamom (Elettaria cardamomum)** - Gastroprotective, antispasmodic, studied in aromatherapy blends

**Lemon (Citrus limon)** - Aromatherapy for pregnancy NVP

**Lavender (Lavandula)** - Calming aromatherapy, studied in PONV protocols

## Comparative Effectiveness: Head-to-Head Results

### Ginger vs Peppermint
**Context-dependent effectiveness:**

1. **CINV aromatherapy** (6 hours post-chemo):
   - Peppermint: Mean score 6.57
   - Ginger: Mean score 10.29
   - **Winner**: Peppermint (p = 0.036)

2. **PONV after thyroidectomy** (Rhodes score):
   - Ginger: 0.48 ± 0.51
   - Peppermint: 1.11 ± 0.64
   - Control: 1.37 ± 0.49
   - **Winner**: Ginger (P < .001)

3. **TB patient GI symptoms** (3-month intervention):
   - Ginger 1g daily: No vomiting recurrence in intervention OR washout
   - Peppermint 3mL daily: Less effective
   - **Winner**: Ginger for sustained oral use

**Clinical takeaway**: Peppermint aromatherapy superior for acute CINV; ginger oral preparations superior for sustained use and PONV in most contexts.

### Ginger vs Chamomile (CINV Study)
**Intervention**: 500mg capsules twice daily × 5 days before/after chemo

- **Nausea frequency**: Ginger significantly better than chamomile
- **Vomiting frequency**: No significant difference (both effective)
- **Clinical takeaway**: Ginger superior for nausea; both equal for vomiting

### Ginger vs Vitamin B6 (Pregnancy NVP)
**Four RCTs with mixed results:**

1. **Smith 2004**: Comparable effectiveness
2. **Ensiyeh 2009**: Ginger more effective than B6
3. **Chittumma 2007**: Ginger more effective for nausea severity; equal for vomiting episodes
4. **Sharifzadeh 2018**: No significant difference in MPUQE score changes

**Clinical takeaway**: Ginger at least equivalent to B6, possibly superior for nausea severity. Both safe first-line options.

### Ginger vs Placebo (Pregnancy NVP)
**Meta-analysis of 7 studies (Ni 2020)**:
- All 7 studies: Ginger > placebo
- **Nausea severity**: Significant reduction
- **General NVP symptoms**: Significant improvement
- **Vomiting alone**: Not significant vs placebo

**Clinical takeaway**: Ginger effective for nausea and general symptoms, but vomiting reduction inconsistent.

## Symptom-Specific Treatment Selection

### Nausea-Predominant (without much vomiting)
**Primary herbs**:
1. **Ginger** - Strongest evidence for nausea reduction
   - Dose: 1-2g daily (divided doses)
   - Mechanism: 5-HT3 antagonism, gastric emptying acceleration

2. **Peppermint aromatherapy** - Rapid relief via inhalation
   - Mechanism: Serotonin receptor blockade, smooth muscle relaxation
   - Use: Inhale essential oil as needed

**Why these work**: Both target 5-HT3 receptors (primary trigger for nausea reflex) and enhance gastric motility.

### Vomiting-Predominant
**Primary herbs**:
1. **Ginger** - Effective for vomiting frequency
2. **Chamomile** - Equally effective as ginger for vomiting in CINV

**Traditional formulas**:
- **Banxia Houpo Tang** (TCM) - Specifically for "rebellious qi" causing vomiting
  - Contains: Pinellia (Ban Xia), Poria, Magnolia bark, Perilla, Ginger
  - Pattern: Phlegm + qi stagnation blocking downward flow

- **Xiao-Ban-Xia Decoction** (TCM) - Classical anti-emetic
  - Modern use: CINV
  - Mechanism: Inhibits ferroptosis via Nrf2/SLC7A11/GPX4 pathway

### Nausea with Anxiety/Tension Component
**Primary herbs**:
1. **Chamomile** - Dual action: antispasmodic + anxiolytic
   - Active: Apigenin (GABAergic modulation)
   - Dose: 1-4 cups tea daily OR 500mg extract twice daily

2. **Lavender aromatherapy** - Calming for anxiety-related nausea

**Traditional approach**:
- **Banxia Houpo Tang** - Addresses emotional component ("plum-pit qi" = sensation of throat obstruction with digestive symptoms)

### Nausea with Bloating/Gas
**Primary herbs** (all carminative + antispasmodic):
1. **Fennel** - Breaks down gas bubbles, enhances bile secretion
   - Dose: 1-2 tsp crushed seeds as tea
   - Active: Trans-anethole, fenchone

2. **Cardamom** - Gastroprotective, increases gastric mucus by 89.3%
   - Dose: 2-4 pods crushed in tea
   - Active: 1,8-cineole

3. **Peppermint** - Relaxes GI smooth muscle
   - Dose: 1-2 cups tea OR enteric-coated oil 0.2-0.4mL 3x/day

**Traditional formulas**:
- **CCF Tea** (Ayurveda) - Cumin, Coriander, Fennel
  - Actions: Digestive support, gentle cleansing, reduces bloating

- **Cardamom-Ginger-Fennel Tea** (Ayurveda)
  - Actions: Improves circulation, burns morning mucous, antiemetic

## Traditional Combinations: Why They Work

### TCM Formula: Banxia Houpo Tang (Pinellia-Magnolia Decoction)
**Composition**:
- Pinellia (Ban Xia) - Primary antiemetic
- Poria (Fu Ling) - Strengthens spleen, drains dampness
- Magnolia bark (Hou Po) - Moves qi, transforms phlegm
- Perilla (Zi Su Ye) - Regulates qi, harmonizes stomach
- Fresh Ginger (Sheng Jiang) - Warms stomach, stops vomiting

**Why it works**:
- **Synergistic targeting**: Addresses multiple pathogenic factors (phlegm, qi stagnation, dampness)
- **Directional correction**: Moves qi downward (counteracts "rebellious qi" that causes vomiting)
- **Emotional-physical integration**: Treats both GI symptoms and throat/emotional tension

**Modern applications**: Functional dyspepsia, GERD with anxiety, chronic nausea with phlegm

### TCM Formula: Xiao-Ban-Xia Decoction (Minor Pinellia)
**Modern clinical use**: CINV prevention/treatment

**Why it works**:
- **Anti-ferroptosis**: Activates Nrf2/SLC7A11/GPX4 pathway
- **Cellular protection**: Prevents mitochondrial dysfunction from chemotherapy
- **Multi-target**: Addresses chemotherapy-induced oxidative damage at cellular level

### Ayurvedic: CCF Tea (Cumin-Coriander-Fennel)
**Why it works**:
- **Agni stimulation**: All three herbs kindle digestive fire without aggravating Pitta
- **Balanced action**: Cumin (warming), Coriander (cooling), Fennel (neutral) = tridoshic
- **Gentle detox**: Supports ama (toxin) clearance without harsh purgation
- **Preventive approach**: Daily use prevents nausea rather than just treating it

### Western Aromatherapy Blend (2012 RCT, n=1151)
**Composition**: Ginger, Spearmint, Peppermint, Cardamom essential oils

**Results**:
- Statistically significant reduction in nausea
- Statistically significant reduction in antiemetic medication requests

**Why it works**:
- **Multiple 5-HT3 pathways**: All four oils antagonize serotonin receptors
- **Synergistic mechanisms**: Ginger (allosteric modulation) + Peppermint (calcium blockade) + Cardamom (gastroprotection)
- **Aromatherapy advantage**: Direct olfactory nerve stimulation bypasses digestive tract (useful when oral intake difficult)

## Dosing Guidelines

### Acute Nausea (immediate relief needed)

**Aromatherapy** (onset: minutes):
- **Peppermint essential oil**: Inhale from bottle or 2-3 drops on tissue, as needed
- **Ginger essential oil**: Inhale as needed
- **Blend**: Ginger + peppermint + spearmint + cardamom

**Oral - Fast-acting** (onset: 15-30 minutes):
- **Fresh ginger tea**: 2-4g fresh root steeped 5-10 min
- **Ginger capsules**: 500-1000mg with water
- **Peppermint tea**: 1-2 cups hot tea

### Chronic/Recurrent Nausea (ongoing management)

**Pregnancy NVP**:
- **Ginger**: 1g daily (divided doses, 250mg 4x/day)
  - Duration: Throughout symptomatic period (typically weeks 6-16)
  - Expected timeline: Improvement within 2-4 days, full effect 1-2 weeks

- **Vitamin B6**: 10-25mg three times daily
  - Often combined with ginger
  - Expected timeline: 1-2 weeks for full effect

**CINV** (chemotherapy):
- **Ginger**: 500-1000mg twice daily
  - Timing: Start 5 days before chemotherapy, continue 5 days after
  - Expected timeline: Reduced nausea within first 24-48 hours, cumulative benefit

- **Chamomile**: 500mg extract twice daily
  - Same timing as ginger
  - Alternative or complementary to ginger

**PONV** (post-operative):
- **Ginger**: 1g preoperatively (2 hours before surgery)
  - Expected timeline: Reduced PONV incidence in first 24 hours

- **Peppermint/Ginger aromatherapy**: Inhale immediately post-op and as needed
  - Expected timeline: Relief within 5-10 minutes

**General Digestive Nausea**:
- **Ginger**: 1-2g daily (tea, capsules, or fresh)
  - Continuous use for chronic conditions
  - Expected timeline: Acute relief 30-60 min; chronic improvement 1-2 weeks

- **Peppermint**: 1-2 cups tea daily OR enteric-coated oil 0.2-0.4mL 3x/day
  - Take between meals
  - Expected timeline: Acute relief 15-30 min; chronic improvement 2-4 weeks

- **CCF tea**: 1 cup 2-3x daily before meals
  - Preventive, long-term use
  - Expected timeline: Preventive benefits develop over 1-2 weeks

### Formulation-Specific Dosing

**Fresh ginger root**: 2-4g daily
**Dried ginger powder**: 250-1000mg daily
**Ginger tea**: 1-2g steeped 5-10 minutes

**Peppermint tea**: 1-2 cups as needed
**Peppermint enteric-coated oil**: 0.2-0.4mL three times daily (between meals)

**Chamomile tea**: 1-4 cups daily
**Chamomile extract**: 500mg twice daily (standardized extract)

**Fennel seeds**: 1-2 teaspoons crushed, steeped or chewed

**Cardamom**: 2-4 pods crushed in tea

- Preventive dose: 1g ginger 2 hours pre-op

- Initial symptom reduction: 1-2 weeks

## Mechanism-Based Treatment Selection

### Primary Mechanisms Targeted

**5-HT3 Receptor Antagonism** (most important for acute nausea):
- **Ginger**: Non-competitive (allosteric), won't saturate like ondansetron
  - IC50 potency: [6]-shogaol ≥ [8]-gingerol > [10]-gingerol ≥ [6]-gingerol
  - Clinical advantage: Modulates rather than blocks, fewer side effects

- **Peppermint**: Blocks serotonin receptors in GI tract
  - Clinical advantage: Works locally and systemically via aromatherapy

- **Cannabinoids**: (Note: Not covered in detail here, but share mechanism)

**Calcium Channel Blockade** (smooth muscle relaxation):
- **Peppermint**: Direct L-type Ca2+ channel blockade
  - IC50: 15.2-25.9 μg/mL
  - Effect: Inhibits GI smooth muscle contractility

- **Chamomile**: Voltage-dependent calcium channel inhibition
  - Active: Apigenin, bisabolol
  - Effect: Reduces intracellular Ca2+ in smooth muscle

**Gastric Motility Enhancement**:
- **Ginger**: Prokinetic action
  - Mechanism: Cholinergic M3 + serotonergic 5-HT4 stimulation
  - Effect: Reduced gastric emptying time (16.1 min → 12.3 min)
  - Best for: Nausea from delayed gastric emptying, functional dyspepsia

**Anti-inflammatory/Antioxidant** (for inflammation-driven nausea):
- **Ginger**: COX-2 selective inhibition (~3-fold selectivity)
- **Chamomile**: NF-κB pathway inhibition, cytokine suppression (IL-1β, TNF-α)
- **Cardamom**: COX inhibition, gastroprotective (89.3% increase in gastric mucus)

**Neurotransmitter Modulation**:
- **Ginger**: Regulates 5-HT (serotonin), SP (substance P), DA (dopamine)
- **Chamomile**: GABAergic modulation via apigenin

### Clinical Application by Mechanism

**For serotonin-mediated nausea** (CINV, PONV, IBS):
→ Ginger (non-competitive 5-HT3 antagonism) + Peppermint (serotonin receptor blockade)
→ Can combine with pharmaceutical 5-HT3 antagonists (ondansetron) - different binding sites

**For smooth muscle spasm** (cramping, bloating with nausea):
→ Peppermint (calcium channel blockade) + Fennel (antispasmodic) + Chamomile (calcium modulation)

**For delayed gastric emptying** (fullness, early satiety, nausea):
→ Ginger (prokinetic via 5-HT4 stimulation) + Cardamom (gastroprotective)

**For inflammation-related nausea** (CINV, IBD, gastritis):
→ Ginger (COX-2 selective) + Chamomile (NF-κB inhibition) + Cardamom (mucosal protection)

## Safety and Side Effects

### Ginger
**Safety profile**: Generally safe and well-tolerated
**Common side effects**: Minor (2.8% in one study)
- Heartburn (especially high doses)
- Diarrhea (rare)
- Mouth irritation (fresh root)

**Contraindications/Cautions**:
- Anticoagulant medications: Theoretical interaction (monitor INR, though clinical significance unclear)
- Diabetes medications: May lower blood sugar (monitor glucose)
- Pre-surgery: Some practitioners recommend stopping 1-2 weeks before (bleeding concern, though evidence weak)

**Pregnancy**: Safe according to multiple systematic reviews
**Lactation**: Generally regarded as safe (GRAS)

### Peppermint
**Safety profile**: Generally safe
**Common side effects**:
- Heartburn (may worsen GERD by relaxing lower esophageal sphincter)

**Contraindications**:
- **GERD or hiatal hernia**: May worsen symptoms
- **Achlorhydria**: Avoid in severe cases
- Gallstones: Use caution (theoretical cholagogue effect)

**Pregnancy**: Generally safe in moderate amounts (tea, culinary use)
**Lactation**: Safe in food amounts; concentrated oil not well-studied

### Chamomile
**Safety profile**: Generally safe
**Common side effects**: Rare

**Contraindications**:
- **Allergy to Asteraceae/Compositae family**: (ragweed, chrysanthemums, marigolds, daisies)
  - Can cause allergic reactions including anaphylaxis in sensitive individuals

**Pregnancy**: Generally safe in moderate amounts (1-2 cups tea); avoid excessive amounts
**Lactation**: Safe in food/tea amounts

### Fennel
**Safety profile**: Generally safe as food/spice
**Common side effects**: Rare allergic reactions

**Contraindications**:
- **Estrogen-sensitive conditions**: Theoretical concern with high medicinal doses due to anethole
  - Breast cancer, endometriosis, uterine fibroids (use food amounts only)

**Pregnancy**: Safe as culinary spice; medicinal doses not well-studied
**Lactation**: Traditionally used as galactagogue (increases milk production)

### Cardamom
**Safety profile**: Generally safe as culinary spice
**Side effects**: Rare
**Contraindications**: None well-established
**Pregnancy/Lactation**: Safe as culinary spice

### Vitamin B6
**Safety profile**: Safe for pregnancy
**Side effects**: Generally well-tolerated

**Contraindications**:
- Very high doses (>100mg/day long-term): May cause peripheral neuropathy
- Levodopa: B6 can reduce effectiveness (unless combined with carbidopa)

**Pregnancy**: Safe and recommended for NVP
**Lactation**: Safe

## Combination Strategies

### Synergistic Combinations (Evidence-Based)

**Ginger + Pharmaceutical Antiemetics**:
- **Ginger + Ondansetron** (5-HT3 antagonist): Superior to either alone
  - Mechanism synergy: Ginger (allosteric modulation) + Ondansetron (competitive antagonism) = more complete 5-HT3 blockade
  - Clinical context: CINV, PONV

- **Ginger + Metoclopramide**: Ginger equivalent to metoclopramide but fewer side effects
  - Consider: Ginger as alternative or adjunct

**Aromatherapy Blends**:
- **Ginger + Peppermint + Spearmint + Cardamom**: Proven in 1,151-patient RCT
  - Effect: Reduced nausea AND antiemetic medication requests
  - Mechanism: Multiple 5-HT3 pathways + olfactory nerve stimulation

- **Peppermint + Ginger + Lavender**: Studied for PONV after oral surgery
  - Effect: Synergistic calming + antiemetic

**Traditional Multi-Herb Formulas**:
- **Ban Xia Hou Po Tang**: Pinellia + Poria + Magnolia + Perilla + Ginger
  - Synergy: Antiemetic + qi regulation + phlegm transformation + emotional calming

- **CCF Tea**: Cumin + Coriander + Fennel
  - Synergy: Balanced dosha effects, agni stimulation without Pitta aggravation

### Sequential Strategies (Stepped Approach)

**Step 1 - Mild Nausea**:
- Single herb: Ginger tea OR Peppermint tea
- Dose: As needed
- Expected response: 1-2 days

**Step 2 - Moderate Nausea** (if Step 1 insufficient):
- Add: Ginger capsules (standardized dose 1-2g/day) + Peppermint aromatherapy
- Consider: Traditional formula (CCF tea, Banxia Houpo Tang)
- Expected response: 3-7 days

**Step 3 - Severe Nausea** (if Step 2 insufficient):
- Combine: Ginger (oral) + Peppermint (aromatherapy) + Pharmaceutical antiemetic
- Consider: TCM/Ayurvedic practitioner consult for pattern-specific formula
- Expected response: 1-2 weeks, reassess

## Context-Specific Protocols

### Pregnancy NVP Protocol
**First-line**:
1. Ginger 250mg capsules 4x/day (total 1g) OR Ginger tea 1-2g daily
2. Vitamin B6 10-25mg three times daily
3. Peppermint or lemon aromatherapy as needed

**Second-line** (if insufficient):
- Add chamomile tea 1-2 cups daily
- Add acupressure at P6 point

**Expected timeline**: Improvement in 2-4 days, maximum benefit 2-4 weeks

### PONV Protocol
**Preoperative** (2 hours before):
- Ginger 1g oral

**Postoperative**:
- Peppermint essential oil inhalation as needed (most effective aromatherapy)
- Alternative: Ginger aromatherapy
- Combination: Peppermint + Ginger + Lavender blend

**Expected timeline**: Reduced PONV incidence in first 24 hours

### CINV Protocol
**5 days before chemotherapy → through 5 days after**:
- Ginger 500-1000mg twice daily (in addition to standard antiemetics)
- Peppermint aromatherapy as needed (especially effective at 6 hours post-chemo)
- Alternative: Chamomile 500mg twice daily

**Traditional option**:
- Xiao-Ban-Xia Decoction (consult TCM practitioner)

**Expected timeline**: Acute phase (days 1-5), delayed phase (days 6-10)

### Motion Sickness Protocol
**Evidence note**: Mixed/weak evidence for ginger in motion sickness

**Trial approach**:
- Ginger 1g 30-60 minutes before travel
- Peppermint aromatherapy during travel
- Expected response: Variable (works for some, not others)

### IBS/Functional Dyspepsia Protocol
**Daily maintenance**:
- Peppermint oil (enteric-coated) 0.2-0.4mL three times daily between meals
- Ginger 1-2g daily (tea or capsules)
- CCF tea before meals (Ayurvedic approach)

**Expected timeline**: 4-6 weeks for significant improvement, 8-12 weeks maximum benefit

## Special Populations

### Pregnancy
**Safe herbs**: Ginger, vitamin B6, peppermint (moderate amounts), lemon, chamomile (moderate), cardamom
**Safety note**: All listed herbs safe per systematic reviews; avoid excessive amounts

**Avoid**: High-dose fennel (estrogenic concern), concentrated essential oils (except aromatherapy)

### Lactation
**Safe herbs**: Ginger, peppermint (food amounts), chamomile, fennel (traditionally used as galactagogue), cardamom
**Caution**: Peppermint in very large amounts may reduce milk supply (theoretical)

### Children
**Generally safe** (in age-appropriate doses):
- Ginger tea (diluted)
- Chamomile tea
- Peppermint aromatherapy

**Evidence**: Peppermint oil studied for childhood functional abdominal pain

**Caution**: Use lower doses, avoid concentrated essential oils on skin in infants

### Elderly
**Generally safe**: Standard doses usually appropriate
**Considerations**:
- Drug interactions (especially ginger + anticoagulants, monitor)
- Lower starting doses for concentrated extracts
- Monitor for side effects (heartburn from ginger)

### Cancer Patients (on chemotherapy)
**Highly recommended**: Ginger as adjunct to standard antiemetics
**Evidence**: Multiple RCTs show benefit, reduced antiemetic medication needs
**Safety**: Safe when combined with chemotherapy drugs
**Note**: Inform oncologist (theoretical herb-drug interactions with some chemo agents)

## Practical Tips

### Maximizing Effectiveness

**Timing**:
- **Preventive approach**: Start before nausea begins (e.g., pregnancy NVP, pre-chemo)
- **Acute relief**: Aromatherapy fastest (5-10 min), then tea (15-30 min), then capsules (30-60 min)

**Formulation selection**:
- **Fast relief needed**: Aromatherapy or tea
- **Sustained effect**: Capsules or traditional formulas
- **Oral intake difficult**: Aromatherapy only

**Consistency**:
- Chronic nausea requires regular dosing (not "as needed")
- Expect 1-2 weeks for full benefits in chronic conditions

### Cost-Effectiveness

**Most affordable**:
1. Dried ginger root (bulk) for tea - pennies per dose
2. CCF tea (bulk spices) - very inexpensive
3. Vitamin B6 tablets - generic versions cheap

**Moderate cost**:
- Ginger capsules (standardized extract)
- Peppermint tea bags
- Chamomile tea bags

**Higher cost**:
- Essential oils (peppermint, ginger) - but last long time with aromatherapy use
- Enteric-coated peppermint oil capsules
- TCM prepared formulas

### Quality Considerations

**Ginger**:
- Fresh root: Look for firm, smooth skin; store in refrigerator
- Dried powder: Store in airtight container, use within 6 months
- Capsules: Choose standardized extract (5% gingerols) from reputable brand

**Peppermint**:
- Tea: Organic preferred (menthol content varies)
- Essential oil: 100% pure, no additives; check for Mentha × piperita (not spearmint)
- Enteric-coated oil: Pharmaceutical grade (dissolves in intestine, not stomach)

**Chamomile**:
- Tea: German chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) preferred over Roman for GI use
- Avoid old tea (loses potency after 1 year)

**Traditional formulas**:
- TCM: Source from reputable suppliers; quality varies widely
- Ayurvedic: Organic sources, proper storage

## Red Flags: When to Seek Medical Care

**Seek immediate medical attention if nausea accompanied by**:
- Severe abdominal pain
- Bloody vomit or black/tarry stools
- High fever (>101°F/38.3°C)
- Severe headache or stiff neck
- Chest pain
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Signs of dehydration (no urination >8 hours, extreme thirst, dizziness)

**Seek medical evaluation if**:
- Nausea/vomiting persists >24-48 hours despite treatment
- Unable to keep down liquids
- Unintentional weight loss
- Pregnancy nausea severe enough to prevent eating/drinking (hyperemesis gravidarum)
- Nausea is new and unexplained (especially if age >50)

**Herbs are complementary**: For serious conditions (CINV, PONV, hyperemesis), use herbs as adjunct to standard medical care, not replacement.
