# Evidence Ranking: Strongest Herbs for Migraine

## 1. Butterbur (Petasites hybridus)
**Evidence Level:** Strong (Level A) - *with safety caveat*

### Clinical Efficacy
- **RCT Results (2004):** 48% reduction in migraine frequency at 75mg bid vs 26% placebo (p=0.0012)
- **American Headache Society:** Level A recommendation (effective for migraine prevention)
- **Dosing:** 75mg bid most effective; 50mg bid showed non-significant trend (36% reduction, p=0.127)
- **Duration:** Minimum 4 months treatment for full effect

### Mechanisms
- Inhibits leukotriene biosynthesis (LTB4, cysteinyl-leukotrienes)
- Blocks COX-2 activity → reduces PGE2 and prostaglandins
- Inhibits CGRP release from meningeal afferents
- Blocks L-type voltage-dependent Ca2+ channels → vasorelaxation
- Blocks intracellular calcium influx into neutrophils/eosinophils

### Safety Concerns ⚠️
- **Hepatotoxicity risk:** Pyrrolizidine alkaloids (PAs) in non-purified extracts
- **AAN withdrew recommendation in 2015** due to liver toxicity concerns
- **Use only PA-free standardized extracts with medical supervision**
- Requires liver function monitoring

## 2. Ginger (Zingiber officinale)
**Evidence Level:** Moderate-Strong (acute treatment)

### Clinical Efficacy
- **Meta-analysis (2021), 3 RCTs:**
  - Pain-free at 2h: RR=1.79 [CI: 1.04-3.09], p=0.04
  - Pain score reduction at 2h: MD=-1.27 [CI: -1.46, -1.07], p<0.00001
- **Best for:** Acute migraine attacks (not prevention)
- **Timeline:** Significant relief within 2 hours

### Mechanisms
- **Dual COX-2 + LOX inhibition** → reduces prostaglandins AND leukotrienes
  - 6-, 8-, 10-gingerols: Potent COX-2 mRNA suppressors
  - 6-shogaol: Suppresses PGE2, IL-1β, TNF, NF-κB activation
- Decreases NO levels in microglia
- Antioxidant activity
- Agonist of vanilloid nociceptors

### Advantages
- Concomitant COX + LOX inhibition may reduce side effects vs single-pathway inhibitors
- GRAS status (Generally Recognized As Safe)
- Rapid onset for acute treatment

## 3. Peppermint/Menthol (Mentha × piperita)
**Evidence Level:** Strong (topical application)

### Clinical Efficacy
- **RCT (2010):** 10% menthol solution topically applied
  - Pain-free at 2h: Statistically superior to placebo
  - 52% improvement rate (at least one significant improvement at 2h)
  - Sustained pain relief endpoints met
- **Associated symptoms:** Effective for nausea/vomiting, phonophobia, photophobia
- **Intranasal study:** Peppermint 1.5% similar efficacy to lidocaine 4%

### Mechanisms
- **TRPM8 activation** (transient receptor potential melastatin 8)
  - Generates long-lasting cooling effect
  - Alters calcium channels in cold receptors
  - Inhibits pain signals
- **GWAS finding:** TRPM8 gene associated with migraine susceptibility (especially migraine without aura)
- Effects on voltage-gated sodium channels
- CNS modulation of pain perception

### Application
- Topical 10% solution to forehead/temples at onset
- Intranasal 1.5% peppermint essential oil

## 4. Curcumin (Turmeric/Curcuma longa)
**Evidence Level:** Moderate (prevention)

### Clinical Efficacy
- **Double-blind RCT (2022), n=44:**
  - Dose: 500mg curcumin twice daily × 8 weeks
  - CGRP reduction: p<0.001 (highly significant)
  - IL-6 reduction: p=0.041 (significant)
  - Headache severity: p=0.001 (significant)
  - Headache duration: p=0.007 (significant)
- **Network meta-analysis (2024):** Ranked top for attack duration reduction

### Mechanisms
- Direct CGRP pathway inhibition (key migraine mediator)
- Anti-inflammatory: Suppresses COX-2 + iNOS (especially when combined with omega-3)
- Antioxidative
- Anti-protein aggregate
- Analgesic effects

### Dosing
- 500mg twice daily for prevention
- Minimum 8 weeks for preventive effect

## 5. Chuan Xiong (Ligusticum chuanxiong)
**Evidence Level:** Strong in TCM practice (most-used herb)

### Clinical Use
- **#1 most frequently used herb** in traditional migraine formulas (59.8% of prescriptions)
- Appears in 357 of 597 classical formulas analyzed
- Core component of top TCM formulas (CXCTS, Da Chuanxiong)

### Mechanisms (Ligustilide)
- **CGRP pathway:** High affinity for 5-HT1D and 5-HT7 receptors → reduces CGRP vasodilatation
- **Neuroprotection:**
  - Prevents neuronal apoptosis in parietal cortex/hippocampus
  - Regulates mitochondrial metabolism
  - Improves cognitive function in cerebral hypoperfusion
- **Anti-inflammatory:** Down-regulates NF-κB, IL-1β, COX-2
- **Vascular:** Increases cerebral blood flow in migraine models
- **BBB protection:** May regulate MMP-9 to reduce permeability
- **Pain:** Activates TRPA1, modulates monoamine neurotransmitters, decreases NO

## 6. Magnesium + Riboflavin + CoQ10 Combination
**Evidence Level:** Moderate (combination therapy)

### Clinical Efficacy
- **Multicenter RCT (2015), n=130:**
  - Composition: 400mg riboflavin + 600mg magnesium + 150mg CoQ10 daily
  - Duration: 3 months (4-week baseline)
  - Migraine frequency: Trend toward significance
  - Symptom burden: Statistically significantly reduced vs placebo

### Mechanisms (Mitochondrial Dysfunction Theory)
- **Magnesium:** ATP-synthase cofactor; NMDA-channel antagonist regulating excitability
- **Riboflavin:** Precursor for FMN/FAD; essential for complex I/II in electron transport
- **CoQ10:** Electron carrier from complex I/II to cytochrome C; anti-inflammatory; reduces oxidative stress

### Rationale
- Addresses mitochondrial energy deficit in migraine pathophysiology
- Complementary mechanisms across three pathways

## 7. Feverfew (Tanacetum parthenium)
**Evidence Level:** Weak-to-Moderate (monotherapy)

### Clinical Efficacy
- **Meta-analysis (2020), 6 RCTs, n=561:**
  - Reduction: -0.6 attacks/month [CI: -0.52, 0.14], p=0.26 (non-significant)
  - Cohen's d = -0.19 (small effect size)
  - Low quality evidence per Cochrane review
- **Conclusion:** Limited by study quality differences and dosing variations
- Works better in combinations than as monotherapy

### Mechanisms (Parthenolide)
- **TRPA1 modulation:**
  - Partial TRPA1 agonist → selective channel desensitization
  - Non-selective defunctionalization of CGRP-containing sensory neurons
- **CGRP inhibition:** Blocks CGRP release and CGRP-mediated meningeal vasodilatation
- **Serotonin pathway:**
  - Reduces platelet aggregation response to serotonin
  - Potent inhibitor of serotonin release from platelets
  - Noncompetitive inhibition of serotonin smooth muscle response
- **Anti-inflammatory:** Inhibits NF-κB and IκB kinase complex (IKKβ)

### Safety
- Very safe profile
- Insufficient efficacy evidence as monotherapy
- May enhance other treatments when combined

## 8. Lavender Essential Oil (Lavandula angustifolia)
**Evidence Level:** Moderate (acute aromatherapy)

### Clinical Efficacy
- **Placebo-controlled RCT (2012), n=47:**
  - Intervention: 15-minute inhalation at onset
  - Mean headache severity reduction: 3.6 ± 2.8 (VAS)
  - Effective and safe for acute management

### Mechanisms
- **Anti-inflammatory:** Inhibits peptide leukotriene biosynthesis (migraine inflammation cascade)
- **Neurotransmitter modulation:**
  - GABA receptor inhibition
  - Linalool reacts with glutamatergic NMDA receptors
  - Inhibits serotonin transporter (SERT)
  - Antagonizes NMDA receptor
  - Inhibits tension-dependent calcium channels
  - Affects 5HT-1A receptor in specific brain areas
- **Aromatherapy pathway:** Modulates limbic system via olfactory → regulates 5-HT and GABA
- **Anxiolytic:** Addresses stress/anxiety triggers

## 9. Tian Ma (Gastrodia elata) + Chuan Xiong Combination
**Evidence Level:** Moderate-Strong (TCM combination)

### Classical Formula: Da Chuanxiong (4:1 ratio)
- 4 parts Chuan Xiong : 1 part Tian Ma
- Traditional indication: Headache from blood stasis + wind-heat syndrome
- Used clinically for many years

### Tian Ma Mechanisms
- **Neuroprotection:**
  - Inhibits stress-related proteins
  - Mobilizes genes: Nxn, Dbnl, Mobkl3, Clic4, Mki67, Bax
  - Promotes neuro-regenerative processes
- **Targets:** AKT1, TNF, PTGS2, IL-6
- **Polysaccharides:** Anti-oxidant, immunomodulatory, neuroprotective
- **Traditional uses:** Headache, convulsions, hypertension, neurodegeneration

## Summary Ranking by Use Case

### Acute Attack (Fastest Relief)
1. **Ginger** - Evidence: Strong | Timeline: 2h | Safety: Excellent
2. **Peppermint/Menthol** - Evidence: Strong | Timeline: Rapid | Safety: Excellent
3. **Lavender** - Evidence: Moderate | Timeline: 15min | Safety: Excellent

### Prevention/Prophylaxis (Reduce Frequency)
1. **Butterbur** - Evidence: Strongest | Timeline: 4mo | Safety: ⚠️ PA-free only
2. **Curcumin** - Evidence: Moderate | Timeline: 8wk | Safety: Good
3. **Mag+B2+CoQ10** - Evidence: Moderate | Timeline: 3mo | Safety: Excellent
4. **TCM Formulas (CXCTS)** - Evidence: Strong in practice | Timeline: Variable | Safety: Good

### Traditional/Pattern-Based (TCM)
1. **Chuan Xiong** - Most frequently prescribed single herb
2. **Tian Ma + Chuan Xiong** - Classic herb pair
3. **CXCTS formula** - Most prescribed complete formula

### Safety-First Approach
1. Ginger (GRAS)
2. Peppermint/Menthol (topical)
3. Magnesium + Riboflavin + CoQ10
4. Lavender (aromatherapy)
5. Feverfew (safe but weak evidence)
6. Curcumin (well-tolerated)
7. TCM formulas (practitioner-guided)
8. Butterbur (only PA-free with monitoring) - last choice despite efficacy
