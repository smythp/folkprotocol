# Comparative Trials of Digestive Herbs: Head-to-Head Research

**Research Date:** January 15, 2026
**Focus:** Randomized controlled trials directly comparing herbs for digestive conditions

## Executive Summary

This document compiles head-to-head comparative trials and dose-response studies of herbal medicines for digestive disorders. The research reveals that while individual herbs are extensively studied against placebo, true head-to-head comparative trials are relatively rare. Most comparative research involves:

1. **Dose comparisons** of the same herb/combination
2. **Herbal formulas vs. conventional drugs** (equivalence trials)
3. **Multi-herb formulas vs. single herbs** (rare)
4. **Component analysis** of established herbal formulas

## Key Findings

### Limited Direct Herb vs. Herb Comparisons
- Very few RCTs directly compare different single herbs
- Most comparative trials test different doses of the same formulation
- Combination formulas (e.g., peppermint + caraway) are more commonly studied than single herbs head-to-head
- Traditional Chinese Medicine formulas are rarely compared against each other in RCTs

## 1. PEPPERMINT + CARAWAY OIL COMBINATIONS

### Study 1: Dose Comparison Trial

**Citation:** Freise J, Köhler S. Peppermint oil-caraway oil fixed combination in non-ulcer dyspepsia—comparison of the effects of enteric preparations. *Pharmazie*. 1999;54(3):210-215.

**Study Design:** Prospective, randomized, double-blind, reference-controlled multicenter trial

**Sample Size:** 223 enrolled; 213 analyzed (108 test, 105 reference)

**Condition:** Non-ulcer dyspepsia (dysmotility type or idiopathic)

**Duration:** 4 weeks

**Interventions:**
- **Test group (high-dose):** 90 mg peppermint oil + 50 mg caraway oil (enteric-coated capsule)
- **Reference group (low-dose):** 36 mg peppermint oil + 20 mg caraway oil (enteric-soluble)

**Primary Outcome:** Pain intensity reduction on visual analog scale (0-10)

**Results:**
| Outcome | Test Group (90/50 mg) | Reference Group (36/20 mg) | Statistical Significance |
|---------|----------------------|---------------------------|-------------------------|
| Pain intensity reduction | -3.6 points | -3.3 points | p < 0.001 (equivalent) |
| Pain frequency | Superior | Inferior | p = 0.04 |
| Adverse events | Fewer despite higher dose | More frequent | Better tolerability |

**Conclusion:** Both doses showed equivalent efficacy for pain intensity, but the **higher dose (90/50 mg) was superior for pain frequency** and had better tolerability due to enteric coating.

### Study 2: Peppermint-Caraway vs. Cisapride (Conventional Drug)

**Citation:** Madisch A, Heydenreich CJ, Wieland V, Hufnagel R, Hotz J. Treatment of functional dyspepsia with a fixed peppermint oil and caraway oil combination preparation as compared to cisapride. A multicenter, reference-controlled double-blind equivalence study. *Arzneimittelforschung*. 1999;49(11):925-932.

**Study Design:** Multicenter, randomized, double-blind equivalence trial

**Sample Size:** 120 enrolled; 118 analyzed for efficacy

**Condition:** Functional dyspepsia

**Duration:** 4 weeks

**Interventions:**
- **Peppermint-caraway oil (PCC):** 90 mg peppermint + 50 mg caraway, 2 capsules daily (n=60)
- **Cisapride (CIS):** 10 mg three times daily (n=58)

**Primary Outcome:** Pain score reduction on visual analog scale

**Results:**
| Outcome | PCC (Herbal) | Cisapride (Drug) | Statistical Significance |
|---------|--------------|------------------|-------------------------|
| Mean pain reduction | 4.62 points | 4.60 points | p = 0.021 (equivalent) |
| Pain frequency | Comparable | Comparable | NS |
| Adverse events | 12 events | 14 events | Similar tolerability |

**Conclusion:** **Therapeutic equivalence demonstrated** between the herbal combination and conventional prokinetic drug. The herbal preparation was equally effective across subgroups including H. pylori-positive patients.

**Clinical Significance:** This establishes peppermint-caraway oil as a viable alternative to pharmaceutical prokinetics for functional dyspepsia.

---

### Meta-Analysis: Multiple Dose Studies

**Citation:** Li Y, et al. A combination of peppermint oil and caraway oil for the treatment of functional dyspepsia: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Evid Based Complement Alternat Med*. 2019;2019:7654947.

**Key Finding - Dose Comparison:**

**Sun et al., 2016 (China):**
- Sample: 228 participants
- Design: Double-blind, multicenter RCT
- Duration: 4 weeks
- **High-dose group:** One capsule (90 mg peppermint + 50 mg caraway) twice daily
- **Low-dose group:** One capsule once daily
- **Results:** Total symptom scores at 4 weeks:
  - Combined POCO groups: 7.29 ± 5.56
  - Placebo: 15.42 ± 10.35
  - **p < 0.01**

**Note:** This meta-analysis found only ONE study comparing different doses of peppermint-caraway combination. Most studies test a single dose against placebo.

## 2. GINGER, CARAWAY, AND PEPPERMINT COMPARISON

### Study 3: Three-Way Herbal Comparison

**Citation:** Latif A, Ahmad H, Bashir I. Comparative study on the prokinetic effects of ginger, caraway, and peppermint in relieving gastrointestinal disturbances in pulmonary TB patients: A clinical trial. *Food Sci Nutr*. 2025;13(6). [NCT06157034]

**Study Design:** Randomized controlled trial with three intervention groups and placebo

**Sample Size:** 200 enrolled; 189 completed (11 dropouts)

**Condition:** Gastrointestinal disturbances in pulmonary TB patients on anti-tuberculosis treatment

**Duration:** 3 months intervention + 1 month washout

**Interventions:**
- **Ginger (*Zingiber officinale*):** 500 mg twice daily = 1 g/day (n=47)
- **Caraway (*Carum carvi*):** 1,000 mg twice daily = 2 g/day (n=48)
- **Peppermint (*Mentha spicata* L.):** 1.5 mL twice daily = 3 mL/day (n=46)
- **Placebo:** 500 mg roasted starch twice daily (n=48)

**Primary Outcomes:** GI disturbance symptoms via Rome IV criteria:
- Postprandial distress
- Early satiation
- Epigastric pain/burning
- Nausea, vomiting, belching

**Results:**
| Herb | Key Findings | Standout Effects |
|------|--------------|------------------|
| **Ginger** | **Best overall performer** | • No vomiting recurrence<br>• Highest phenolic content (1035.51 mgGAE/g)<br>• Superior antioxidant capacity<br>• Significant reduction in postprandial distress during washout |
| Peppermint | Promising reduction in GI disturbances | Particularly effective during washout phase |
| Caraway | Moderate effects | Less pronounced than ginger or peppermint |

**Conclusion:** **Ginger emerged as the most effective intervention** among the three herbs tested, with sustained benefits into the washout period. Both ginger and peppermint showed promise as adjunctive therapy for medication-induced GI disturbances.

**Limitations:**
- Specific population (TB patients on medication)
- Different dose amounts make direct comparison difficult
- Doses not standardized by weight/bioactivity

## 3. ARTICHOKE + GINGER COMBINATION

### Study 4: Combination vs. Placebo

**Citation:** Giacosa A, et al. The effect of ginger (Zingiber officinalis) and artichoke (Cynara cardunculus) extract supplementation on functional dyspepsia: A randomised, double-blind, and placebo-controlled clinical trial. *Evid Based Complement Alternat Med*. 2015;2015:915087.

**Study Design:** Prospective, multicenter, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled, parallel-group trial

**Sample Size:** 126 patients (65 supplementation, 61 placebo)

**Condition:** Functional dyspepsia

**Duration:** 4 weeks

**Intervention:** Ginger + artichoke leaf extract combination (2 capsules daily before lunch and dinner)

**Note:** This study tested the **combination** vs. placebo, not ginger vs. artichoke head-to-head

**Results:**
| Timepoint | Supplementation Group | Placebo | Significance |
|-----------|----------------------|---------|--------------|
| 14 days | Significant improvement | No improvement | p = 0.017 vs p = 0.513 |
| Secondary outcomes | Superior for nausea, epigastric fullness, pain, bloating | Inferior | Multiple domains improved |

**Conclusion:** The ginger-artichoke combination appears safe and efficacious. However, **this does not establish comparative effectiveness between the two herbs** individually.

## 4. CURCUMIN VS. OMEPRAZOLE (Drug Comparison)

### Study 5: Three-Way Comparison - Curcumin vs. PPI

**Citation:** Kongkam P, et al. Curcumin and proton pump inhibitors for functional dyspepsia: a randomised, double blind controlled trial. *BMJ Evid Based Med*. 2023.

**Study Design:** Randomized, double-blind controlled trial

**Sample Size:** 206 enrolled; 151 completed

**Condition:** Functional dyspepsia

**Duration:** 28 days treatment + 28 days follow-up

**Interventions:**
- **Curcumin alone (C):** 250 mg four times daily (total 1,000 mg/day)
- **Omeprazole alone (O):** 20 mg once daily
- **Combination (C+O):** Both agents at above doses

**Primary Outcome:** Severity of Dyspepsia Assessment (SODA) score on days 28 and 56

**Results:**
| Treatment Arm | Pain Score Improvement | Non-Pain Score | Satisfaction Score |
|---------------|------------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Curcumin (C) | -8.07 | Comparable | Comparable |
| Omeprazole (O) | -8.85 | Comparable | Comparable |
| Combination (C+O) | -7.19 | Comparable | Comparable |
| **Statistical significance** | **No significant differences among groups** | **No differences** | **No differences** |

**Adverse Events:** No serious adverse events in any group

**Conclusion:** **Curcumin demonstrated equivalent efficacy to omeprazole** with no synergistic benefit when combined. This is a landmark finding showing a spice/herb matching pharmaceutical efficacy.

**Clinical Significance:** Curcumin at 1,000 mg/day appears to be a viable alternative to PPIs for functional dyspepsia.

## 5. ARTICHOKE LEAF EXTRACT

### Study 6: Artichoke vs. Placebo (Dose-Finding)

**Citation:** Holtmann G, Adam B, Haag S, Collet W, Grünewald E, Windeck T. Efficacy of artichoke leaf extract in the treatment of patients with functional dyspepsia: a six-week placebo-controlled, double-blind, multicentre trial. *Aliment Pharmacol Ther*. 2003;18(11-12):1099-1105.

**Study Design:** Double-blind, RCT, placebo-controlled, multicenter

**Sample Size:** 247 enrolled; 244 analyzed (129 active, 115 placebo)

**Condition:** Functional dyspepsia

**Duration:** 6 weeks

**Intervention:** Artichoke leaf extract **640 mg twice daily** (2 × 320 mg t.i.d.)

**Results:**
| Outcome | Artichoke Extract | Placebo | p-value |
|---------|------------------|---------|---------|
| Overall symptom improvement | 8.3 ± 4.6 | 6.7 ± 4.8 | p < 0.01 |
| Quality of life (NDI score) | -41.1 ± 47.6 | -24.8 ± 35.6 | p < 0.01 |

**Conclusion:** Artichoke preparation was significantly superior to placebo in both symptom relief and quality of life improvement.

**Note:** While not a comparative trial with another herb, this establishes efficacy at a specific dose that can be compared to other studies.

## 6. STW-5 (IBEROGAST) - MULTI-HERB FORMULA RESEARCH

### Formula Composition

STW-5 contains **nine herbal extracts:**
1. Bitter candytuft (*Iberis amara*)
2. Chamomile flower (*Matricaria chamomilla*)
3. Peppermint leaves (*Mentha piperita*)
4. Caraway fruit (*Carum carvi*)
5. Licorice root (*Glycyrrhiza glabra*)
6. Lemon balm leaves (*Melissa officinalis*)
7. Angelica root (*Angelica archangelica*)
8. Celandine herb (*Chelidonium majus*)
9. Milk thistle fruit (*Silybum marianum*)

### Meta-Analysis Findings

**Citation:** Multiple studies reviewed in meta-analyses (Karger Publishers, PMC articles)

**Key Comparative Findings:**

**1. STW-5 vs. Cisapride:**
- Sample: 186 patients
- Design: Randomized, double-blind, 4-week trial
- Groups: STW-5, STW-5-II (refined formula), Cisapride
- **Results:**
  - Efficacy: Comparable to cisapride (prokinetic drug)
  - Tolerability: STW-5 rated very good/good by 93% (vs. 81-91% for cisapride)
  - Adverse events: **21% (STW-5) vs. 33% (cisapride)**
- **Conclusion:** **STW-5 showed equivalent efficacy with superior tolerability**

**2. Meta-Analysis Efficacy:**
- Largest meta-analysis: 637 patients
- Standardized mean difference: **-1.1** (large, clinically relevant effect)
- **p = 0.0005**
- Adverse drug reaction incidence: **0.04%**

**Component Research:**
- Studies have compared the 9-component formula (STW-5) vs. 6-component version (STW-5-II)
- Both showed efficacy superior to placebo
- Research examining individual component contributions is limited

**Clinical Significance:** STW-5 represents one of the best-studied multi-herb formulas with evidence of equivalence to conventional drugs and excellent safety profile.

## 7. TRADITIONAL CHINESE MEDICINE FORMULAS

### Meta-Analysis of CHM for IBS

**Citation:** Chinese herbal medicine for irritable bowel syndrome: A meta-analysis and trial sequential analysis of randomized controlled trials. *PMC*. 2021;PMC8353248.

**Key Finding:** **No head-to-head comparisons between different TCM formulas were found.**

**What Was Studied:**
- Chinese herbal medicine (CHM) vs. placebo
- CHM vs. active pharmaceutical controls (e.g., pinaverium)
- Standardized CHM formulas vs. individualized prescriptions

**Formula Consistency:**
- Nine trials assessed standardized CHM
- Most trials for diarrhea-predominant IBS used formulas **based on Tong-Xie-Yao-Fang** or its variations
- **No trials compared alternative TCM formulas against each other**

**Results:**
| Comparison | Outcome | Significance |
|------------|---------|--------------|
| CHM vs. Placebo | Effective for global IBS symptoms and abdominal pain | Significant |
| CHM vs. Conventional drugs | Comparable efficacy | Variable |
| Adverse events | Higher rate than placebo | Notable |

**Conclusion:** While TCM formulas show efficacy, **comparative effectiveness research between different herbal formulations is lacking**. Research focuses on establishing efficacy vs. placebo/conventional treatment rather than optimizing between herbal approaches.

## 8. GERD-SPECIFIC COMPARISONS

### DGL (Deglycyrrhizinated Licorice) Studies

**Citation:** Efficacy and safety of GutGard® in managing gastroesophageal reflux-related symptoms: A phase III, single-centre, double-blind, randomized placebo-controlled trial. *PMC*. 2024;PMC11892464.

**Study Design:** Phase III RCT, double-blind, placebo-controlled

**Intervention:** GutGard® (DGL extract)

**Duration:** 28 days

**Results:**
- GERD-HRQL total scores significantly reduced on day 28 (p = 0.014)
- Clinically significant improvement in heartburn and regurgitation
- Benefits started from week 2

**Note:** No direct comparison with chamomile or other anti-reflux herbs found.

### Multi-Herb GERD Formulas

**Finding:** STW-5 (Iberogast) contains both chamomile and licorice root among its components, suggesting synergistic use rather than comparative testing.

**Conclusion:** **No head-to-head trials of DGL vs. chamomile vs. other single herbs for GERD were found.** Most GERD herbal research tests single herbs vs. placebo or multi-herb formulas vs. placebo/conventional drugs.

## DISCUSSION

### Gaps in Comparative Research

**1. Lack of Direct Herb vs. Herb Comparisons:**
- Most RCTs compare herb vs. placebo or herb vs. conventional drug
- True head-to-head comparisons between different single herbs are rare
- This makes it difficult to determine which herb is "best" for a given condition

**2. Dose Standardization Issues:**
- When herbs are compared (e.g., ginger vs. peppermint vs. caraway), doses often differ
- Different preparations (oil, extract, whole herb) complicate comparisons
- Lack of bioequivalent dosing makes interpretation challenging

**3. Formula Research Dominance:**
- Multi-herb formulas (STW-5, TCM formulas) are better studied than individual herbs
- Component analysis of formulas is uncommon
- Difficult to attribute effects to specific herbs within formulas

**4. Publication Bias Concerns:**
- Equivalence/non-inferiority trials are more likely to be published than superiority trials showing no difference
- Pharmaceutical-funded research may favor drug comparisons over herb-vs-herb studies

### Methodological Challenges

**Study Design Barriers:**
1. **Ethical concerns:** If both herbs have established efficacy vs. placebo, comparing them head-to-head may require large samples to detect small differences
2. **Commercial incentives:** Limited funding for research that doesn't favor a proprietary product
3. **Standardization difficulties:** Herbal preparations vary in potency and composition
4. **Outcome measurement:** Functional digestive disorders have subjective outcomes that require large samples

### Clinical Implications

**What Clinicians Can Conclude:**

**For Functional Dyspepsia:**
- Peppermint + caraway oil (90/50 mg): Equivalent to cisapride
- Curcumin (1,000 mg/day): Equivalent to omeprazole 20 mg
- Artichoke extract (640 mg/day): Superior to placebo
- STW-5 (Iberogast): Equivalent to prokinetics, superior tolerability

**For Nausea:**
- Ginger: Best evidence for pregnancy-related nausea, motion sickness
- Peppermint: More evidence for functional dyspepsia than nausea specifically

**For IBS:**
- Peppermint oil: Established efficacy, particularly for pain and bloating
- TCM formulas based on Tong-Xie-Yao-Fang: Evidence for diarrhea-predominant IBS
- Chamomile: Preliminary evidence for symptom reduction

**For GERD:**
- DGL (licorice): Evidence for symptom reduction
- Multi-herb formulas: More studied than single herbs
- Direct comparisons lacking

### Dose-Response Insights

**Established Effective Doses:**
- **Peppermint oil:** 90 mg (with 50 mg caraway) appears superior to 36 mg (with 20 mg caraway) for pain frequency
- **Curcumin:** 250 mg × 4/day (1,000 mg total) effective for functional dyspepsia
- **Ginger:** 1,000-1,500 mg/day for nausea; 1,000 mg/day for GI disturbances
- **Artichoke extract:** 640 mg/day (320 mg × 2) for functional dyspepsia

## RECOMMENDATIONS FOR FUTURE RESEARCH

### Priority Head-to-Head Trials Needed:

1. **Ginger vs. Peppermint** for functional dyspepsia at equivalent bioactive doses
2. **Curcumin vs. Ginger vs. Artichoke** for functional dyspepsia (three-arm trial)
3. **DGL vs. Chamomile vs. Combination** for GERD
4. **Peppermint oil vs. STW-5** for IBS (single herb vs. formula)
5. **Different TCM formulas** head-to-head for same IBS subtype
6. **Dose-response trials** for individual herbs across multiple doses

### Methodological Improvements Needed:

1. **Bioequivalent dosing:** Standardize doses based on active constituent content
2. **Longer follow-up:** Most trials are 4-8 weeks; need 6-12 month studies
3. **Subgroup analysis:** Determine which herbs work best for which symptom patterns
4. **Component analysis:** Test multi-herb formulas vs. individual components
5. **Combination optimization:** Test different herb combinations systematically
6. **Cost-effectiveness:** Include economic analyses in comparative trials

## APPENDIX: Search Strategy

**Databases Searched:**
- PubMed/MEDLINE
- PMC (PubMed Central)
- Google Scholar (via web search)
- Medical journal websites

**Search Terms Used:**
- "head-to-head" OR "comparative trial" OR "comparative study"
- [Herb A] vs [Herb B]
- "dose comparison" OR "dose-response"
- Specific herb names + "functional dyspepsia" OR "IBS" OR "GERD"
- "traditional Chinese medicine" + "comparative"
- "herbal formula" + "comparison"

**Inclusion Criteria:**
- Randomized controlled trials
- Head-to-head comparisons of herbs or doses
- Comparative effectiveness trials
- Published in peer-reviewed journals
- Human studies only

**Exclusion Criteria:**
- Animal studies
- In vitro research
- Case reports
- Non-comparative observational studies
- Abstracts without full text available
