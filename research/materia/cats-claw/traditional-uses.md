# Cat's Claw - Traditional Uses

## Indigenous Traditional Medicine

### Ashaninka Indians (Peru, Amazon Basin)
- **Traditional Name**: "Savéntaro" (meaning "powerful plant")
- **Geographic Origin**: Peru, Amazon basin
- **Primary Species**: Uncaria tomentosa
- **Traditional Applications**: Documented in WHO and ethnobotanical sources

## WHO-Documented Traditional Uses

**Source**: WHO Monographs on Selected Medicinal Plants, Volume 3 (2007)

Traditional uses documented by indigenous Amazonian groups:

1. **Musculoskeletal Conditions**:
   - Arthritis
   - Rheumatism

2. **Gastrointestinal Issues**:
   - Gastric ulcers

3. **Infections and Inflammation**:
   - Abscesses
   - Viral infections
   - Urinary tract infections
   - Wounds

4. **Respiratory Conditions**:
   - Asthma
   - Fevers

5. **Women's Health**:
   - Emmenagogue (promotes menstrual flow)

## Traditional Preparation Methods

### Traditional Dosing
From clinical and ethnobotanical sources:
- **1 gram root bark** 2-3 times daily
- **20-30 mg root bark extract** daily

### Extract Types Used Traditionally
Based on comparative studies (Aguilar et al. 2002, PMID: 12065162):

1. **Hydroalcoholic Extract** (spray-dried)
   - Showed significantly superior anti-inflammatory activity vs aqueous extract (p < 0.05)

2. **Aqueous Extract** (freeze-dried)
   - Less potent than hydroalcoholic preparations
   - Still demonstrated anti-inflammatory effects

## Species Comparison

### Uncaria tomentosa
- **Alkaloid Content**: High (35-fold greater than U. guianensis)
- **Geographic Origin**: Peru, Amazon basin
- **Traditional Use**: Ashaninka Indians - 'savéntaro'

### Uncaria guianensis
- **Alkaloid Content**: Low
- **Antioxidant Activity**: Superior to U. tomentosa (p < 0.01)
- **Anti-inflammatory Potency**: More potent than U. tomentosa
- **TNF-alpha IC50**: 9.5 ng/mL (vs 14.1 ng/mL for U. tomentosa)
- **Equivalence**: Some studies show equiactive with U. tomentosa for DPPH radical quenching and TNF-alpha inhibition

**Source**: Sandoval et al. 2002 (PMID: 12120814)

## Traditional vs. Clinical Applications

### Overlap Between Traditional and Clinical Evidence

**Strong Clinical Support for Traditional Uses**:
1. **Arthritis/Rheumatism**:
   - Traditional: Primary use
   - Clinical: RCT support for RA (40 patients, 53.2% reduction in painful joints) and OA (45 patients, benefits within first week)

2. **Inflammatory Conditions**:
   - Traditional: Abscesses, general inflammation
   - Clinical: Systematic review confirms anti-inflammatory effects (IL-6 reduction SMD -0.72, p = 0.001)

**Traditional Uses with Limited Clinical Evidence**:
1. Gastric ulcers (traditional use, limited human trials)
2. Asthma (traditional use, no modern clinical trials)
3. Fevers (traditional use, no modern clinical trials)
4. Emmenagogue effects (traditional use, no modern clinical trials)
5. Urinary tract infections (traditional use, no modern clinical trials)

**Modern Clinical Applications Not in Traditional Use**:
1. Cancer supportive care (quality of life improvement)
2. Chemotherapy adjuvant (neutropenia reduction)

## Geographic and Cultural Context

### Primary Traditional Medicine Systems
- **Indigenous Amazonian Medicine**: Primary traditional use
- **NOT part of classical systems**: Not documented in Ayurveda, Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), or classical European herbalism
- **Modern Western Adoption**: Entered Western herbal practice in late 20th century based on Amazonian ethnobotany

### Plant Parts Used Traditionally
- **Cortex** (bark): Primary traditional and modern use
- **Roots**: Also used traditionally
- **Leaves**: Used in some preparations (stem bark, roots, and leaves all show activity per systematic review)

## Traditional Preparation Context

### Anti-inflammatory Activity by Extract Type
**Study**: Aguilar et al. 2002 (PMID: 12065162)
- **Model**: Carrageenan-induced paw edema in mice
- **Finding**: Hydroalcoholic extract showed significantly superior anti-inflammatory activity vs aqueous extract (p < 0.05)
- **Mechanism**: Little inhibitory activity on cyclooxygenase-1 and -2, suggesting anti-inflammatory effects operate through alternative pathways (not COX inhibition)

### Activity Independent of Alkaloid Content
**Study**: Sandoval et al. 2002 (PMID: 12120814)
- **Key Finding**: Alkaloid-rich and alkaloid-depleted fractions showed comparable anti-inflammatory effects
- **Implication**: Therapeutic activity independent of alkaloid content
- **Components**: Suggests polyphenols, carboxy alkyl esters, or other constituents contribute to effects

## Summary

- **Primary Traditional System**: Indigenous Amazonian medicine (Ashaninka people)
- **Not part of**: Ayurveda, TCM, or classical European herbalism
- **Traditional Timeline**: Centuries of use documented by WHO, specific dates not provided in modern literature
- **Modern Recognition**: WHO monograph (2007), EMA assessment (2015)
- **Best-Supported Traditional Uses**: Arthritis, rheumatism, inflammatory conditions
- **Traditional Preparations**: Root bark decoctions, aqueous and hydroalcoholic extracts
- **Species Used**: Both U. tomentosa and U. guianensis, though U. guianensis may be more potent for anti-inflammatory effects
