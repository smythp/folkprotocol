# Gotu Kola (Centella asiatica) - Active Compounds & Bioavailability

## Primary Active Constituents

### Triterpene Glycosides (Parent Compounds)

**Main glycosides:**
1. **Asiaticoside**
2. **Madecassoside** (also called asiaticoside A)

**Characteristics:**
- These are the parent compounds found in the whole plant
- Very low oral bioavailability (<1%)
- Serve as precursors to active metabolites
- Used as marker compounds for standardization in European Pharmacopoeia

### Triterpene Aglycones (Active Metabolites)

**Main aglycones:**
1. **Asiatic acid** - metabolite of asiaticoside
2. **Madecassic acid** - metabolite of madecassoside

**Characteristics:**
- Higher plasma levels than parent compounds after oral administration
- Active forms responsible for therapeutic effects
- Produced through metabolism of parent glycosides
- Used as marker compounds alongside glycosides

## Standardization Markers

### European Pharmacopoeia (Edition VI and later)

**Marker compounds for quality control:**
- Asiaticoside
- Madecassoside (asiaticoside A)
- Asiatic acid (aglycone)
- Madecassic acid (aglycone)

### World Health Organization (WHO) Standard

**Minimum quality requirement:**
- **2% total triterpene ester glycosides (saponins)**

### United States Pharmacopeia (USP)

**Powdered Centella asiatica Extract specifications:**
- **Triterpene content:** 90.0-110.0% of labeled amount of triterpene derivatives
- **Extraction ratio:** 65:1 to 30:1 plant material to extract
- **Solvents:** Alcohol, methanol, acetone, or mixtures
- **Reference standard:** USP-1297770

### Typical Standardization Ranges

**Common commercial standardization:**
- **Minimum:** 2% total triterpenes (WHO standard)
- **Typical:** 10-20% asiaticoside equivalents
- **High standardization:** Up to 90-110% labeled amount (USP)

## Bioavailability & Pharmacokinetics

### Oral Bioavailability

**Parent glycosides (asiaticoside, madecassoside):**
- **Oral bioavailability:** Less than 1%
- **Plasma presence:** Absent or undetectable (Wright et al., 2022)
- **Renal excretion:** Limited
- **Conclusion:** Parent glycosides are poorly absorbed intact

**Active metabolites (asiatic acid, madecassic acid):**
- **Plasma levels:** Higher than parent compounds
- **Detection:** Readily identified in plasma and urine
- **Active forms:** These aglycones are responsible for therapeutic activity

### Absorption & Distribution

**Time to peak concentration (Tmax):**
- **Rapid absorption:** 5-15 minutes for triterpenes
- **Not affected by:** Dose or treatment scheme (single vs. chronic dosing)

**Metabolism:**
- **Conversion:** Asiaticoside and madecassoside are rapidly converted to asiatic acid and madecassic acid
- **Location:** Likely intestinal/hepatic first-pass metabolism (though specific site not detailed in bibliography)

### Accumulation with Repeated Dosing

**Grimaldi et al., 1990 - TTFCA Pharmacokinetics Study (PMID: 2329813):**

**Study design:**
- Sample: 12 participants (crossover RCT)
- Dosing: 30 mg or 60 mg TTFCA (single dose vs. twice daily for 7 days)

**Key pharmacokinetic findings:**
- **Peak plasma concentrations:** Significantly higher after chronic dosing than single dose
- **AUC0-24 (area under curve):** Significantly higher with chronic dosing
- **Half-life:** Significantly longer with chronic dosing compared to single dose
- **Tmax:** Not affected by dose or treatment scheme

**Songvut et al., 2021 - Modified ECa 233 Study (PMID: 33767223):**
- Sample: 12 healthy volunteers
- Dosing: 250 mg or 500 mg modified ECa 233 (single and 7-day repeated)
- **Asiatic acid accumulated after 7 days repeated administration**
- Higher doses led to significantly greater plasma levels of active metabolites

**Clinical implication:** Steady-state plasma levels are achieved with repeated dosing, supporting clinical protocols using 2-6 month treatment durations.

## Standardized Clinical Preparations

### ECa 233 (Standardized Extract)

**Used in clinical trials for:**
- Cognitive function studies (Wright et al., 2022)
- Safety and pharmacokinetics (Songvut et al., 2019)
- Temporomandibular pain (Potewiratnanond et al., 2025)
- Wound healing (topical formulations)

**Characteristics:**
- Consistent composition across trials
- Available in both oral capsules and topical formulations (0.05% w/w gel)

**Standard dosing in trials:**
- **250 mg:** Single and multiple doses (well-tolerated)
- **500 mg:** Single and multiple doses (well-tolerated)
- **500 mg twice daily:** Used in pain studies

### TTFCA (Total Triterpenic Fraction of Centella asiatica)

**Used extensively in clinical trials for:**
- Venous insufficiency
- Diabetic microangiopathy
- Microcirculatory disorders

**Characteristics:**
- Concentrated preparation focusing on triterpene constituents
- Removes non-triterpene components
- Higher potency than crude extracts

**Standard dosing in trials:**
- **30 mg twice daily:** Lower therapeutic dose
- **60 mg twice daily (120 mg/day):** Standard therapeutic dose
- **60 mg three times daily (180 mg/day):** Higher therapeutic dose

**Dose-response relationship:** 120-180 mg/day more effective than 30-90 mg/day for vascular conditions

### CAP (Centella asiatica Aqueous Extract Product)

**Used in studies:**
- Cognitively impaired older adults (Wright et al., 2022)

**Characteristics:**
- Aqueous (water-based) extraction
- Contains triterpenes plus caffeoylquinic acids

**Dosing in trials:**
- **2 g CAP**
- **4 g CAP**

**Pharmacokinetic profile:**
- Triterpene aglycones (asiatic acid, madecassic acid) identified in plasma and urine
- Caffeoylquinic acids showed delayed absorption, limited plasma presence
- Well-tolerated in cognitively impaired older adults on cholinesterase inhibitors

### Asiaticoside Preparations

**Used in wound healing studies:**
- Isolated triterpene glycoside
- **50 mg asiaticoside per capsule, three times daily** (150 mg/day) for diabetic wound healing (Paocharoen study)

## Other Constituents

### Phytonutrients (Chandrika & Kumara, 2015)

**Secondary constituents:**
- **Vitamins:** B vitamins, Vitamin C
- **Proteins**
- **Important minerals** (specific minerals not detailed in bibliography)
- **Flavonoids**

**Clinical relevance:** While present, clinical trials focus primarily on triterpene standardization as the active therapeutic components.

### Caffeoylquinic Acids

**Identified in:** CAP (aqueous extract product)

**Pharmacokinetics:**
- Delayed absorption compared to triterpenes
- Limited plasma presence
- Clinical significance unclear from available data

## Mechanisms of Action

### Anti-inflammatory Effects
- Anti-inflammatory
- Anti-oxidative stress
- Anti-apoptotic effects
- Supported by strong preclinical data (Sun et al., 2020)

### Vascular Effects
- Protects venous endothelium
- Stimulates collagen synthesis through fibroblast modulation
- **Dose consideration:** Higher dosages may produce biphasic response (inhibitory effects on collagen and mucopolysaccharide production at very high doses)

### Mitochondrial Function
- Improvement in mitochondrial function (Sun et al., 2020)

### Neurological Effects
- Increased choline levels suggesting potential cognitive benefits (Songvut et al., 2021)
- Mechanism for anxiolytic effects unclear from bibliography data

## Quality Control & Standardization Challenges

### Poor Bioavailability Challenge

**Known limitation:**
- Oral bioavailability of parent glycosides (<1%) is a significant challenge
- Requires conversion to active metabolites for therapeutic effect

**Clinical implication:**
- Standardization should focus on total triterpene content rather than specific glycoside forms
- Formulation strategies may improve dissolution and absorption (modified ECa 233 showed improved dissolution properties)

### Marker Compound Selection

**Multiple approaches in pharmacopoeias:**
- **Parent glycosides only:** Asiaticoside, madecassoside (original European Pharmacopoeia approach)
- **Parent glycosides + aglycones:** All four compounds (current European Pharmacopoeia VI)
- **Total triterpene content:** WHO approach (minimum 2% total)
- **Labeled amount verification:** USP approach (90-110% of labeled triterpenes)

**Clinical trial consistency:**
- ECa 233 and TTFCA preparations used consistently across multiple trials
- Standardization to specific triterpene content allows dose-response studies
- Crude herb doses (1-4 g daily) less precise than standardized extracts

## Summary

**Primary active constituents:** Triterpene glycosides (asiaticoside, madecassoside) metabolized to active aglycones (asiatic acid, madecassic acid)

**Bioavailability challenge:** Parent glycosides have <1% oral bioavailability; rapid conversion to active metabolites

**Absorption:** Rapid (Tmax 5-15 minutes); accumulation occurs with repeated dosing over 7 days

**Standardization:** 2% minimum (WHO) to 90-110% labeled amount (USP); clinical trials typically use 10-20% asiaticoside equivalent preparations

**Clinical preparations:** ECa 233 (cognitive, pain, wound healing), TTFCA (vascular conditions), CAP (aqueous extract), isolated asiaticoside (wound healing)

**Quality control markers:** Asiaticoside, madecassoside, asiatic acid, madecassic acid (European Pharmacopoeia); total triterpene content (WHO, USP)
