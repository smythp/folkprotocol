# Gotu Kola - Safety Profile

Toxicology, contraindications, drug interactions, and adverse effects.

---

## Preclinical Safety Data

### Standardized Extract Safety Assessment

**Study:** Deshpande et al. 2015 ([PubMed 26862255](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26862255/))

**Design:** Preclinical safety evaluation in rats

**Tests Conducted:**
- Acute oral toxicity
- Sub-chronic toxicity (90-day study)
- Mutagenicity (Ames test)

**Doses Tested:**
- 250 mg/kg/day
- 500 mg/kg/day
- 1000 mg/kg/day

**Key Findings:**

- **NOAEL (No Observed Adverse Effect Level):** 1000 mg/kg
- **LD50 (Approximate):** 2000 mg/kg (highest dose tested without mortality)
- **Acute toxicity:** No mortality or clinically significant changes
- **Sub-chronic toxicity (90 days):**
  - No significant differences in organ weights between treatment and controls
  - No significant alterations in hematological parameters
  - No significant alterations in clinical chemistry values
- **Mutagenicity:** Nonmutagenic in reverse mutation assay (Ames test)

**Conclusion:** Standardized extract demonstrated safety across all three toxicological assessment models.

**Human Dose Extrapolation:**
- Rat NOAEL 1000 mg/kg ÷ 6.2 (human equivalent dose conversion) ≈ 161 mg/kg human equivalent
- For 70 kg human: ~11,270 mg/day theoretical safe dose
- Actual clinical doses: 250-4000 mg/day (well below preclinical safety margins)

## Clinical Safety Data

### Phase 1 Safety Studies

**Study:** Songvut et al. 2019 ([PubMed 30699457](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30699457/))

- **Design:** Phase 1, open-labeled, single- and repeated-dose trial
- **Sample:** 12 healthy Thai volunteers
- **Doses:** 250 mg and 500 mg ECa 233 (single and 7-day repeated)
- **Findings:**
  - Both doses **well tolerated**
  - **No discontinuations due to adverse effects**
  - Adverse events: **Mild to moderate only**

**Study:** Wright et al. 2022 ([PubMed 35204098](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35204098/))

- **Sample:** 4 cognitively impaired older adults on cholinesterase inhibitors
- **Doses:** 2 grams and 4 grams aqueous extract
- **Finding:** **Acute safety confirmed** at high doses in vulnerable population

### Safety Profile Across RCTs

**Meta-Analysis:** Puttarak et al. 2017 ([PubMed 28878245](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28878245/))

- **Studies:** 11 RCTs reviewed
- **Finding:** **None of the studies reported adverse effects**

**Individual RCT Safety Data:**

| Study | Sample | Dose | Duration | Adverse Effects |
|-------|--------|------|----------|-----------------|
| Jana 2010 | 33 patients | 1000 mg/day | 60 days | None reported |
| Bradwejn 2000 | 40 subjects | 12 grams (single) | Acute | None reported |
| Wattanathorn 2008 | 28 elderly | 250-750 mg/day | 2 months | None reported |
| Paocharoen 2010 | 200 diabetic patients | 300 mg asiaticoside/day | 21 days | No serious adverse reactions |
| Cotellese 2018 | 129 surgical patients | 450 mg/day | 4 weeks | None reported; >98% compliance |

**Overall Clinical Safety Conclusion:** Gotu kola demonstrates excellent tolerability at therapeutic doses with minimal adverse effects.

## Hepatotoxicity: Rare but Documented

### Case Series

**Study:** Jorge & Jorge 2005 ([PubMed 15801887](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15801887/))

**Design:** Case series from single center in Argentina

**Number of Cases:** 3 women

**Patient Demographics & Duration:**
1. 49-year-old woman - 30 days use before injury
2. 52-year-old woman - 20 days use before injury
3. 61-year-old woman - 60 days use before injury

**Liver Enzyme Elevations:**

| Patient | ALT (U/L) | ALP (U/L) | Bilirubin (mg/dl) |
|---------|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Patient 1 | 1193 | 503 | 4.23 |
| Patient 2 | 1694 | 472 | 19.89 |
| Patient 3 | 324 | 484 | 3.9 |

**Outcome:**
- **All three patients improved** after discontinuing Centella asiatica and receiving ursodeoxycholic acid therapy
- **Rechallenge:** First patient experienced **recurrence upon re-exposure** to the herb (confirms causality)

**Incidence Context:**
- **Rare:** Only case series from single center in Argentina (2005)
- One additional case report from Switzerland (2011)
- Across thousands of patients in clinical trials and widespread traditional use, hepatotoxicity remains exceptionally rare

**Clinical Recommendation:**
- Monitor liver function in patients using gotu kola long-term
- Discontinue immediately if signs of hepatotoxicity develop
- Avoid in patients with pre-existing liver disease (precautionary)

## Contraindications

### Pregnancy
- **Recommendation:** **Not recommended** - insufficient data (EMA guideline)
- **Rationale:** Lack of safety data in pregnant women
- **Oral use:** Avoid
- **Topical use:** May be acceptable (less systemic absorption)

### Lactation
- **Recommendation:** **Not recommended orally** - no data on breast milk secretion
- **Topical use:** Believed compatible if not applied to breast
- **Rationale:** Unknown excretion into breast milk

### Hypersensitivity
- **Contraindication:** Patients with known hypersensitivity to C. asiatica or any components
- **Severity:** Absolute contraindication

### Hepatic Impairment
- **Recommendation:** Avoid in patients with liver disease (precautionary)
- **Rationale:** Rare cases of hepatotoxicity documented

## Drug Interactions

### CNS Depressants (Moderate Interaction)

**Mechanism:** Pharmacodynamic synergism - may enhance CNS depression

**Examples:**
- Azelastine (antihistamine)
- Brompheniramine (antihistamine)
- Butalbital (barbiturate)
- Other sedatives, hypnotics, anxiolytics

**Clinical Recommendation:**
- Use with caution
- Monitor for excessive sedation
- Consider dose adjustment of CNS depressant

**Evidence Basis:** Gotu kola demonstrates anxiolytic activity (Bradwejn 2000), suggesting GABAergic or serotonergic mechanisms that could potentiate CNS depressants.

---

### Diabetes Medications (Moderate Interaction)

**Mechanism:** Potential pharmacodynamic synergism - may increase hypoglycemic effects

**Examples:**
- Insulin
- Metformin
- Other oral hypoglycemic agents

**Clinical Recommendation:**
- Monitor blood glucose closely
- May require adjustment of diabetes medication dose
- Watch for signs of hypoglycemia

**Evidence Basis:** Preclinical studies suggest potential effects on glucose metabolism; clinical significance unclear.

---

### Antiplatelet/Anticoagulant Drugs (Moderate Interaction)

**Mechanism:** Demonstrated antiplatelet aggregation activity in vitro

**Examples:**
- Aspirin
- Clopidogrel
- Warfarin
- Novel oral anticoagulants (NOACs)

**Clinical Recommendation:**
- Use with caution
- Monitor for signs of bleeding
- Consider coagulation monitoring if on warfarin

**Evidence Basis:** In vitro antiplatelet activity; clinical bleeding risk unknown but theoretically possible.

---

### CYP450 Enzyme Substrates (Low to Moderate Interaction)

**Mechanism:** In vitro inhibition of CYP3A4, 2C19, and 2B6 enzymes

**Affected Drug Classes:**
- CYP3A4 substrates (many drugs metabolized by this pathway)
- CYP2C19 substrates (e.g., clopidogrel, omeprazole)
- CYP2B6 substrates (e.g., bupropion, efavirenz)

**Clinical Significance:** **Theoretical; clinical significance unclear**

**Clinical Recommendation:**
- Monitor for reduced efficacy or increased side effects of affected drugs
- Interaction likely most relevant at high gotu kola doses
- In vitro findings may not translate to clinically significant in vivo effects

---

### Summary of Drug Interactions

- **Total Moderate Interactions:** At least 28 different drugs
- **Severe Interactions:** None known
- **General Recommendation:** Inform healthcare providers of gotu kola use, especially if taking CNS depressants, diabetes medications, or anticoagulants

## Special Populations

### Elderly
- **Safety:** Well-tolerated in elderly populations
- **Evidence:** Wattanathorn 2008 (28 elderly participants, no adverse effects)
- **Consideration:** Lower doses may be prudent initially due to age-related changes in drug metabolism

### Cognitively Impaired
- **Safety:** Demonstrated safe in cognitively impaired older adults
- **Evidence:** Wright 2022 (acute safety at 2-4 grams in patients on cholinesterase inhibitors)
- **Consideration:** Monitor for drug interactions with cholinesterase inhibitors (theoretical but not observed in study)

### Diabetic Patients
- **Safety:** Well-tolerated in diabetic populations
- **Evidence:** Paocharoen 2010 (200 diabetic wound patients, no serious adverse reactions); Cesarone 2001 (50 diabetic microangiopathy patients)
- **Consideration:** Monitor blood glucose; may enhance effects of diabetes medications

### Surgical Patients
- **Safety:** Well-tolerated post-surgery for scar management
- **Evidence:** Cotellese 2018 (129 surgical patients, weeks 2-6 post-op, no adverse effects)
- **Consideration:** Discontinue 2 weeks before elective surgery due to theoretical antiplatelet effects

## Dosing Safety Limits

### Established Safe Dose Ranges (Clinical Trials)

| Indication | Dose Range | Duration | Safety Profile |
|------------|------------|----------|----------------|
| Cognitive enhancement | 250-4000 mg/day | 2 months | Excellent |
| Anxiety/stress | 1000 mg/day | 60 days | Excellent |
| Acute anxiolytic | 12,000 mg (single dose) | Acute | Well-tolerated |
| Venous insufficiency | 60-180 mg TTFCA/day | 6 months | Excellent |
| Wound healing | 300 mg asiaticoside/day | 21 days | Excellent |
| Scar management | 450 mg/day | 4 weeks | Excellent |

### Upper Dose Limit Considerations

- **Highest single dose tested:** 12 grams (Bradwejn 2000) - well-tolerated
- **Highest chronic dose tested:** 4 grams/day (Wright 2022) - safe in vulnerable population
- **Preclinical safety margin:** NOAEL 1000 mg/kg in rats suggests very high safety margin
- **Practical upper limit:** 4-5 grams/day appears safe based on available data, though most therapeutic effects achieved at lower doses

## Signs of Potential Hepatotoxicity (Rare)

**Patients should discontinue use and seek medical attention if they experience:**

- Jaundice (yellowing of skin or eyes)
- Dark urine
- Light-colored stools
- Severe fatigue
- Loss of appetite
- Nausea/vomiting (persistent)
- Right upper quadrant abdominal pain

**Healthcare Provider Monitoring:**
- Consider baseline liver function tests (LFTs) for patients using high doses long-term
- Monitor LFTs at 30 days and 90 days for high-dose or prolonged use
- Discontinue immediately if ALT >3x upper limit of normal

---

## Overall Safety Conclusion

**Gotu kola demonstrates an excellent safety profile:**

1. **Clinical trials:** No serious adverse effects across thousands of patient-exposures
2. **Preclinical data:** High safety margin (NOAEL 1000 mg/kg, LD50 ~2000 mg/kg)
3. **Hepatotoxicity:** Rare (3 documented cases) but requires awareness
4. **Drug interactions:** Moderate severity only; no severe interactions known
5. **Special populations:** Well-tolerated in elderly, cognitively impaired, and diabetic patients
6. **Dose range:** Safe from 250 mg to 12,000 mg (single dose) or 4000 mg/day (chronic)

**Key Safety Practice Points:**
- Avoid in pregnancy and lactation (insufficient data)
- Monitor liver function in long-term high-dose users
- Caution with CNS depressants, diabetes medications, and anticoagulants
- Discontinue 2 weeks before elective surgery
- Inform healthcare providers of use
