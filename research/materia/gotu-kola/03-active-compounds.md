# Gotu Kola - Active Compounds & Bioavailability

Phytochemistry, standardization, and pharmacokinetics of Centella asiatica.

---

## Primary Active Compounds

### Triterpene Glycosides (Parent Compounds)
- **Asiaticoside**
- **Madecassoside**
- **Asiaticoside B**

### Triterpene Aglycones (Active Metabolites)
- **Asiatic acid** (from asiaticoside biotransformation)
- **Madecassic acid** (from madecassoside biotransformation)

### Other Compounds
- **Caffeoylquinic acids** (limited bioavailability)

## Bioavailability & Metabolism

### Key Finding: Glycosides Convert to Aglycones

**Wright et al. 2022** ([PubMed 35204098](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35204098/))
- **Sample:** 4 cognitively impaired older adults
- **Doses:** 2 grams and 4 grams CAP (aqueous extract)
- **Key Discovery:**
  - **Triterpene aglycones (asiatic acid and madecassic acid) detected** in plasma and urine
  - **Parent glycosides (asiaticoside and madecassoside) ABSENT** in plasma despite abundance in extract
  - **Bioavailable compounds were primarily metabolites** rather than parent compounds
  - **Caffeoylquinic acids:** Delayed absorption and limited presence in plasma or urine

**Conclusion:** Oral administration delivers active compounds via biotransformation of glycosides to aglycones.

### Biotransformation Pathway

**Songvut et al. 2021** ([PubMed 33767223](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33767223/))
- **Sample:** 12 healthy Thai volunteers
- **Doses:** 250 mg and 500 mg ECa 233 (single and multiple doses over 7 days)
- **Findings:**
  - **Madecassoside:** Rarely absorbed; undergoes biotransformation into madecassic acid
  - **Asiaticoside:** Rarely absorbed; undergoes biotransformation into asiatic acid
  - **Dose response:** Increasing dose resulted in significantly greater plasma levels of active metabolites
  - **Accumulation:** Asiatic acid accumulated after multiple oral administration for seven days
  - **Elimination:** Possibility of fecal route elimination
  - **Cognitive marker:** Associated with increased choline levels, an endogenous metabolite linked to cognitive benefits

### Pharmacokinetic Parameters

**Grimaldi et al. 1990** ([PubMed 2329813](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2329813/))
- **Sample:** 12 healthy volunteers
- **Doses (single):** 30 mg TTFCA, 60 mg TTFCA
- **Doses (multiple):** 30 mg or 60 mg TTFCA twice daily for 7 days
- **Parameters measured:** Cmax, Tmax, AUC₀₋₂₄, half-life
- **Findings:**
  - **Single dose:** Peak concentrations and AUC values increased proportionally with dose
  - **Chronic treatment (7 days):** Peak plasma concentrations, AUC0-24, and half-life were **significantly higher** than after single dose
  - **Mechanism:** Accumulation attributed to metabolic interaction between asiatic acid and asiaticoside

**Implication:** Repeated dosing leads to accumulation of active metabolites, potentially enhancing therapeutic effects over time.

## Standardized Extract Formulations

### ECa 233 (Most Studied Modern Extract)

**Composition:**
- **Total triterpenes:** ≥80-85% triterpenoid glycosides
- **Madecassoside:** 53.1%
- **Asiaticoside:** 32.3%
- **Ratio maintained:** Madecassoside:asiaticoside = 1.5 ± 0.5:1
- **Minor compounds:** <1% triterpenic acid metabolites (madecassic acid and asiatic acid)

**Clinical Doses:**
- 250 mg
- 500 mg

**Studies Using ECa 233:**
- Songvut 2019 - Safety and pharmacokinetics ([PubMed 30699457](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30699457/))
- Songvut 2021 - Metabolomics investigation ([PubMed 33767223](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33767223/))

**Safety Profile:** Both 250 mg and 500 mg doses well tolerated; single and multiple doses safe with mild to moderate adverse events only.

---

### TTFCA/TECA (Total Triterpenic Fraction)

**Composition:**
- **Asiaticoside:** 40%
- **Asiatic acid:** 30%
- **Madecassic acid:** 30%

**Alternative Names:**
- TECA (Total Triterpenic Extract of Centella Asiatica)
- Total triterpenic fraction

**Clinical Doses:**
- 30 mg twice daily (60 mg/day)
- 60 mg twice daily (120 mg/day)
- 60 mg three times daily (180 mg/day)

**Primary Indications:**
- Chronic venous insufficiency
- Venous hypertension
- Diabetic microangiopathy

**Studies Using TTFCA:**
- Grimaldi 1990 - Pharmacokinetics ([PubMed 2329813](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2329813/))
- Incandela 2001 - Venous hypertension ([PubMed 11666126](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11666126/))
- Cesarone 2001 - Diabetic microangiopathy ([PubMed 11666124](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11666124/))
- De Sanctis 2001 - Edema and capillary filtration ([PubMed 11666125](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11666125/))

## Standardization Markers & Quality Standards

### International Standards

| Organization | Minimum Triterpene Content | Notes |
|--------------|---------------------------|-------|
| **WHO (2003)** | ≥2% triterpene ester glycosides | General herbal material standard |
| **European Pharmacopoeia** | ≥6.0% total triterpenoid derivatives (as asiaticoside) | Polish and European standards |
| **Commission E** | Not specified | Recognizes wound healing and memory enhancement |
| **ECa 233 (commercial)** | ≥80-85% triterpenoid glycosides | Most concentrated modern extract |
| **TTFCA (commercial)** | 100% (defined mixture) | 40% asiaticoside + 30% asiatic acid + 30% madecassic acid |

### Key Standardization Markers
1. **Asiaticoside content** (primary marker)
2. **Madecassoside content** (primary marker)
3. **Asiatic acid content** (active metabolite)
4. **Madecassic acid content** (active metabolite)
5. **Ratio of madecassoside:asiaticoside** (quality control for ECa 233)

## Dose Equivalence Estimates

### Crude Herb vs. Standardized Extracts

**Traditional crude herb (Ayurvedic decoction):**
- Dose: 15-30 grams dry herb
- Triterpene content (at 6% standard): ~900-1800 mg triterpenes

**ECa 233 standardized extract:**
- Dose: 500 mg
- Triterpene content (at 80%): ~400 mg triterpenes

**TTFCA standardized extract:**
- Dose: 120 mg
- Triterpene content (100% active): 120 mg triterpenes

**Implication:** Traditional doses provided 2-4x the triterpene content of modern cognitive doses, though bioavailability differences may compensate.

## Mechanisms of Action (Supported by Active Compounds)

### Anti-Inflammatory
- Mediated by triterpene acids (asiatic acid, madecassic acid)
- Inhibition of pro-inflammatory cytokines

### Anti-Oxidative Stress
- Free radical scavenging
- Enhancement of endogenous antioxidant systems

### NRF2 Pathway Activation
- Demonstrated in Wright 2022 study
- Upregulation of cellular protective mechanisms

### Mitochondrial Function Improvement
- Enhancement of cellular energy metabolism
- Protection against mitochondrial dysfunction

### Anti-Apoptotic
- Protection against programmed cell death
- Relevant for neuroprotection

### Collagen Synthesis Enhancement
- Mechanism for wound healing effects
- Mediated by triterpene compounds

### Neuroprotection
- Combined effects of anti-oxidative, anti-inflammatory, and mitochondrial support
- Relevant for cognitive and mood applications

## Clinical Implications of Bioavailability Research

1. **Glycosides are pro-drugs:** Asiaticoside and madecassoside are converted to active aglycones (asiatic acid and madecassic acid)

2. **Accumulation with repeated dosing:** Multiple-day administration leads to higher steady-state levels than single doses

3. **Dose-dependent response:** Higher doses produce proportionally higher plasma levels of active metabolites

4. **Extract standardization matters:** ECa 233 (80% glycosides) and TTFCA (mixed glycosides and acids) have different pharmacokinetic profiles

5. **Cognitive biomarker:** Association with increased choline levels suggests mechanism for cognitive effects
