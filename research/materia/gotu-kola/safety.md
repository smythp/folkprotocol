# Gotu Kola (Centella asiatica) - Safety Profile

## Overall Safety Assessment

**General safety:** Generally well-tolerated with mild, transient side effects

**Clinical trial safety findings:**
- Adverse effects similar in frequency to placebo in small trials
- Mild to moderate adverse events only
- No participants discontinued due to adverse effects in Phase 1 trials at 250-500 mg doses (Songvut et al., 2019)

## Acute Toxicity (Animal Studies)

### Lethal Dose
**LD50:** 2000 mg/kg (Preclinical Safety Study, PMID: 26862255)

**Context:** This is an extremely high dose relative to human therapeutic dosing, suggesting low acute toxicity risk.

### No Observable Adverse Effect Level (NOAEL)
**NOAEL:** 1000 mg/kg (Preclinical Safety Study, PMID: 26862255)

**Preclinical safety findings:**
- Safe in acute oral toxicity studies in rats
- Safe in sub-chronic toxicity studies
- No significant differences in organ weights, hematological parameters, or clinical chemistry
- Nonmutagenic in reverse mutation assay (Ames test)

## Common Adverse Effects

### Mild, Transient Effects

**Most commonly reported (similar frequency to placebo):**
- **Headache** - Mild, transient
- **Dizziness** - Occasional
- **Nausea** - Occasional
- **Gastrointestinal upset** - Bloating, diarrhea

**Clinical significance:** These effects are generally mild and do not lead to treatment discontinuation.

### Topical Use
**Contact dermatitis** - Rare with topical application

**Cosmetic use assessment (Johnson et al., 2023):**
- Centella Asiatica Extract (30%) classified as weak sensitizer in guinea pig maximization test
- Generally regarded as safe for cosmetic use

## Rare Serious Adverse Effects

### Hepatotoxicity

**Frequency:** Very rare

**Documentation:**
- **LiverTox Clinical Reference (NCBI Bookshelf):**
  - "Rare instances of clinically apparent acute liver injury with jaundice"
  - Hepatotoxicity very rare but documented

- **Johnson et al., 2023 Safety Assessment:**
  - Rare instances of clinically apparent acute liver injury with jaundice reported

- **EudraVigilance data:**
  - 23 cases reported, with 10 taking only C. asiatica
  - 3 serious cases

**Clinical implication:** Hepatotoxicity is extremely rare but warrants caution in patients with pre-existing liver disease.

### Hypersensitivity Reactions

**Documented reactions:**
- **Urticaria** (hives)
- **Hypersensitivity reactions**

**EudraVigilance data (Johnson et al., 2023):**
- 3 serious cases of urticaria/hypersensitivity among 23 total adverse event reports

**Frequency:** Very rare

## Contraindications

### Known Liver Disease
**Rationale:** Due to rare hepatotoxicity reports

**Recommendation:** Avoid in patients with known liver disease or hepatic impairment

### Pregnancy
**Rationale:** Reproductive toxicity at high doses in animal studies

**Animal study findings (Johnson et al., 2023):**
- Reproductive toxicant in rats at 100-300 mg/kg/day
- These doses exceed typical human therapeutic doses but raise caution

**Human data:** Insufficient human safety data in pregnancy

**Recommendation:** Avoid during pregnancy due to precautionary principle

### Hypersensitivity to Centella asiatica
**Rationale:** Known hypersensitivity reactions documented (urticaria, contact dermatitis)

**Recommendation:** Avoid in individuals with known hypersensitivity

## Drug Interactions

### Sedative Medications
**Potential interaction:** May interact with sedative medications

**Rationale:**
- Anxiolytic properties demonstrated in clinical trials
- Attenuated acoustic startle response (Bradwejn et al., 2000)
- Theoretical additive sedative effects

**Recommendation:** Use caution when combining with sedatives, anxiolytics, or CNS depressants

### Hepatotoxic Drugs
**Potential interaction:** May interact with hepatotoxic drugs

**Rationale:** Rare hepatotoxicity reported; theoretical additive risk

**Recommendation:** Avoid combination with known hepatotoxic medications or monitor liver function if combination necessary

### Cholinesterase Inhibitors
**Safety data:** Well-tolerated in cognitively impaired older adults on cholinesterase inhibitors (Wright et al., 2022)

**Recommendation:** No known contraindication; appears safe to combine based on Phase 1 trial data

## Special Populations

### Elderly
**Safety profile:** Well-tolerated in older adults including cognitively impaired

**Evidence:**
- **Wright et al., 2022 (PMID: 35204098):**
  - Phase 1 RCT in cognitively impaired older adults
  - Doses: 2 g CAP, 4 g CAP
  - Well-tolerated
  - Safe in combination with cholinesterase inhibitors

**Recommendation:** Appropriate for use in elderly populations with standard dosing

### Children
**Safety data:** Limited safety data available in pediatric populations

**Recommendation:** Insufficient evidence to recommend use in children without further safety data

### Pregnancy & Lactation

**Pregnancy:**
- **Animal studies:** Reproductive toxicity at 100-300 mg/kg/day in rats
- **Human data:** Insufficient
- **Recommendation:** AVOID during pregnancy

**Fertility (male):**
- **Animal evidence:** Impaired fertility in male rats at traditional human doses (Johnson et al., 2023)
- **Human data:** No human fertility studies identified in bibliography
- **Clinical concern:** Evidence of impaired fertility in male rats at doses comparable to traditional human use raises caution

**Lactation:**
- **Data:** Not addressed in bibliography sources
- **Recommendation:** Insufficient data; avoid due to lack of safety information

## Safe Dosing Ranges from Clinical Trials

### Well-Tolerated Doses (No Discontinuations)

**ECa 233 standardized extract:**
- **250 mg:** Single and multiple doses (7 days) - Songvut et al., 2019
- **500 mg:** Single and multiple doses (7 days) - Songvut et al., 2019
- **500 mg twice daily (1000 mg/day):** 60-day trial - Jana et al., 2010

**TTFCA (Total Triterpenic Fraction):**
- **30 mg twice daily (60 mg/day):** Multiple trials
- **60 mg twice daily (120 mg/day):** 12-month diabetic microangiopathy trial
- **60 mg three times daily (180 mg/day):** Dose-ranging trials

**CAP (aqueous extract):**
- **2 g:** Single dose in cognitively impaired elderly
- **4 g:** Single dose in cognitively impaired elderly

**Asiaticoside:**
- **50 mg three times daily (150 mg/day):** 21-day wound healing trial

### Dose-Dependent Safety Considerations

**From Incandela et al., 2001 (PMID: 11666128):**
- Higher dosages may produce biphasic response
- At very high doses: inhibitory effects on collagen and mucopolysaccharide production
- **Clinical implication:** More is not always better; therapeutic window exists

**Therapeutic range consensus:**
- **Standard extracts:** 250-500 mg twice daily
- **TTFCA preparations:** 30-60 mg twice daily (60-180 mg total daily)
- **Treatment duration:** 2-6 months typical for chronic conditions

## Monitoring Recommendations

### Baseline Assessment
- **Liver function tests:** Consider baseline liver enzymes in patients with risk factors or planning long-term use
- **Allergy history:** Screen for known hypersensitivity to Apiaceae family or previous reactions to topical/oral Centella products

### During Treatment
- **Symptom monitoring:** Watch for signs of liver injury (jaundice, dark urine, abdominal pain, unexplained fatigue)
- **Hypersensitivity:** Monitor for rash, urticaria, or other allergic reactions
- **Gastrointestinal tolerance:** Mild GI upset is common but should be transient

### Long-Term Use (>6 months)
- **Liver function monitoring:** Consider periodic liver enzyme checks if using for extended periods beyond typical clinical trial durations
- **Rationale:** Most clinical trials span 2-6 months; long-term safety beyond this not well-documented

## Cosmetic Use Safety

**Johnson et al., 2023 - International Journal of Toxicology:**
- Generally regarded as safe for cosmetic use
- Centella Asiatica Extract (30%) classified as weak sensitizer in guinea pig maximization test
- Contact dermatitis possible with topical application
- Patch testing may be appropriate for individuals with sensitive skin

## Summary of Safety Profile

### Low Risk
- Acute toxicity (LD50 2000 mg/kg is extremely high)
- Common mild adverse effects (headache, GI upset - similar to placebo)
- Use in elderly populations
- Short to medium-term use (2-6 months)

### Moderate Risk (Caution Warranted)
- Combination with sedative medications (theoretical interaction)
- Combination with hepatotoxic drugs (theoretical interaction)
- Long-term use beyond 6 months (limited data)
- Male fertility (animal data suggests concern at traditional doses)

### High Risk (Contraindicated)
- Known liver disease
- Pregnancy (reproductive toxicity in animal studies)
- Known hypersensitivity to Centella asiatica

### Insufficient Data
- Pediatric use
- Lactation
- Long-term use (>6 months to years)
- Human fertility effects

## Regulatory Perspectives

**European Medicines Agency (2010):**
- Approved for symptomatic treatment of chronic venous insufficiency and topical wound healing
- Approval implies acceptable safety profile for these indications

**World Health Organization (1999-2003):**
- Included in WHO monographs
- Quality standards established (minimum 2% triterpenes)
- Recognition implies acceptable traditional safety profile

**United States Pharmacopeia:**
- Monograph established with quality standards
- Indicates acceptable pharmaceutical-grade material available

**Overall regulatory view:** Recognized as having an acceptable safety profile when used appropriately for approved indications at recommended doses.
