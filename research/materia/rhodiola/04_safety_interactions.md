# Rhodiola rosea - Safety, Contraindications, and Drug Interactions

*Extracted from: bibliographies/rhodiola.yaml*
*Date: 2025-12-31*

## General Safety Profile

### Overall Safety Assessment

**Summary:** R. rosea extract appears well-tolerated in short-term studies with favorable safety profile, particularly in contrast to most conventional antidepressants.

### Clinical Trial Safety Data

1. **Lu 2022 (Systematic Review)** - Athletic/Sports Use
   - Finding: "No obvious adverse reactions reported"

2. **Hung 2011 (Systematic Review)** - 11 RCTs
   - Finding: "Only few mild adverse events reported"

3. **Edwards 2012 (WS® 1375)** - Life-Stress Symptoms
   - Finding: "Most adverse events of mild intensity, not related to study drug"
   - Finding: "No serious adverse events"
   - Conclusion: "Safe, well-tolerated"

4. **Mao 2015** - Comparison vs Sertraline
   - Finding: "Significantly fewer adverse events vs sertraline, better tolerated"
   - Clinical implication: Favorable safety profile compared to conventional antidepressant

5. **Darbinyan 2007 (SHR-5)** - Depression Study
   - Finding: "No serious side effects in any group (340 mg, 680 mg, or placebo)"

## Drug Interactions

### CYP2C9 Inhibition - CLINICALLY RELEVANT

**Study:** Thu 2016 (European Journal of Clinical Pharmacology)

**Finding:** R. rosea inhibits metabolic capacity of CYP2C9 by approximately **21%**

**Clinical Relevance:** Potentially clinically relevant with narrow therapeutic index drugs

**Drugs Affected (CYP2C9 substrates):**
- **Phenytoin** (antiepileptic) - narrow therapeutic index
- **Warfarin** (anticoagulant) - narrow therapeutic index
- Other CYP2C9 substrates

**Recommendation:** Use caution when combining Rhodiola with CYP2C9 substrates, especially those with narrow therapeutic indices. Monitor drug levels or clinical effects closely.

---

### Warfarin and Theophylline Interaction Studies

**Study:** Panossian 2009 (Phytotherapy Research) - Animal study

**Drugs Tested:**
- Warfarin
- Theophylline

**Extract:** SHR-5

**Model:** Rats

**Note:** Animal study provides preclinical evidence of potential interactions. The Thu 2016 human study (above) confirms CYP2C9 inhibition, supporting warfarin interaction concern in humans.

---

### Antidepressant Interactions - POTENTIAL CONCERN

**Case Report:** Potential pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic interactions with antidepressants

**Specific Case:** Combination of Rhodiola with **paroxetine** (SSRI)

**Adverse Effects Reported:**
- Vegetative syndrome
- Restlessness
- Trembling

**Clinical Implication:** Exercise caution when combining Rhodiola with antidepressants, particularly SSRIs. Consider:
- Spacing doses
- Monitoring for activation symptoms
- Starting with lower doses if combined
- Consulting with prescribing physician

**Mechanism:** Unclear whether pharmacokinetic (affecting drug metabolism) or pharmacodynamic (additive effects on neurotransmitter systems)

## Contraindications

**Note:** The bibliography sources focus on clinical trial data and do not provide comprehensive contraindication lists. The following are based on the available safety data:

### Evidence-Based Cautions

1. **Narrow therapeutic index drugs (especially CYP2C9 substrates)**
   - Warfarin
   - Phenytoin
   - Monitor closely if combination necessary

2. **Concurrent antidepressant use**
   - Case report of adverse effects with paroxetine
   - Theoretical concern for serotonergic effects
   - Consult prescribing physician before combining

The following contraindications are commonly cited in herbalism but are **not specifically documented** in these clinical research sources:

- Pregnancy (lack of safety data)
- Lactation (lack of safety data)
- Bipolar disorder (theoretical activation concern)
- Autoimmune conditions (theoretical immune modulation)

## Adverse Events Profile

### Types of Adverse Events Reported

Based on clinical trials:

**Mild Adverse Events:**
- Generally mild in intensity
- Not typically related to study drug
- Comparable to or less than placebo in many studies

**Serious Adverse Events:**
- No serious adverse events reported in reviewed clinical trials

### Dose-Related Adverse Effects

**Finding:** No clear dose-response relationship for adverse events

**Example:** Darbinyan 2007 found no serious side effects at either 340 mg or 680 mg doses

**Implication:** Lower doses (340-400 mg/day) appear as safe as higher doses (600-680 mg/day)

## Duration of Use Safety

### Short-Term Use (4-12 weeks)

**Status:** Well-documented safety in clinical trials

**Evidence:**
- Multiple RCTs ranging from 4 weeks to 12 weeks
- Consistently favorable safety profile
- Few mild adverse events

### Long-Term Use (>12 weeks)

**Status:** Limited data

**Note:** Most clinical trials are 4-12 weeks duration. Long-term safety (6+ months) not extensively studied in the reviewed literature.

**Clinical Implication:** For chronic use beyond 12 weeks, consider periodic monitoring and breaks in treatment.

## Comparative Safety: Rhodiola vs Conventional Treatments

### vs. Antidepressants

**Mao 2015 Study:**
- R. rosea: Significantly **fewer adverse events** than sertraline
- R. rosea: **Better tolerated** than sertraline
- Trade-off: Slightly less antidepressant efficacy, but superior tolerability

**Clinical Context:** Favorable safety profile makes Rhodiola an attractive option for:
- Patients who cannot tolerate conventional antidepressants
- Mild-to-moderate depression (not severe)
- Preference for natural medicine with lower side effect burden

## Quality and Safety Concerns

### Product Authenticity Issues

**Study:** Booker 2016 (Phytomedicine)

**Issue:** "The authenticity and quality of Rhodiola rosea products"

**Safety Implication:**
- Quality and authenticity concerns exist in commercial market
- Adulteration or substitution possible
- Standardization not always accurate

**Recommendation:**
- Use products from reputable manufacturers
- Look for third-party testing (e.g., USP verification, ConsumerLab testing)
- Prefer products that match clinical trial standardization (3% rosavins, 1% salidroside)
- Consider products manufactured in licensed GMP facilities

## Safety Monitoring Recommendations

### When Starting Rhodiola

1. **Medication Review:**
   - Identify CYP2C9 substrates (warfarin, phenytoin, etc.)
   - Assess for antidepressant use
   - Consider potential interactions

2. **Baseline Assessment:**
   - Current symptoms (fatigue, depression, anxiety)
   - Blood pressure (if on cardiovascular medications)
   - For warfarin users: Baseline INR

3. **Monitoring During Use:**
   - Symptom improvement tracking
   - Adverse effects monitoring
   - For CYP2C9 substrate users: Monitor drug levels or clinical effects
   - For warfarin users: More frequent INR monitoring initially

4. **Red Flags - Discontinue and Consult Provider:**
   - Restlessness, agitation, trembling (especially with antidepressants)
   - Signs of bleeding (if on warfarin)
   - Seizures (if on phenytoin)
   - Any serious adverse effects

## Summary: Safety Profile

### Strengths

✓ Generally well-tolerated across multiple clinical trials
✓ Few mild adverse events
✓ No serious adverse events in reviewed studies
✓ Fewer adverse effects than conventional antidepressants
✓ Safe in short-term use (4-12 weeks)

### Cautions

⚠ CYP2C9 inhibition (~21%) - caution with narrow therapeutic index drugs
⚠ Potential interaction with antidepressants (case report with paroxetine)
⚠ Limited long-term safety data (>12 weeks)
⚠ No pregnancy/lactation safety data
⚠ Product quality and authenticity concerns in commercial market

### Contraindications

🛑 Use with caution or avoid:
- Concomitant use with warfarin or phenytoin (requires close monitoring)
- Combination with antidepressants (consult physician)
- Pregnancy and lactation (insufficient safety data)

---

## Clinical Bottom Line

Rhodiola rosea demonstrates a **favorable safety profile** in clinical trials for short-term use (4-12 weeks). The primary safety concerns are **drug interactions via CYP2C9 inhibition** (especially warfarin and phenytoin) and **potential interactions with antidepressants**. Product quality and authentication are important considerations due to commercial market variability. For most healthy adults not taking interacting medications, Rhodiola appears safe at typical doses (340-600 mg/day) for stress, fatigue, and performance enhancement.
