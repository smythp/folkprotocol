# Chamomile - Active Compounds and Standardization

## Chemical Profile Overview

**Total constituents**: Over 120 constituents identified

**Major chemical classes**:
- Essential oils (volatile oils)
- Flavonoids
- Coumarins
- Terpenes
- Polysaccharides
- Phenolic acids
- Organic acids

## Essential Oil Components

### Quantitative Profile

**Total essential oil content**: 0.24-2.0% of dried flowers
- **Note**: Wide variation based on growing conditions and processing
- **Color**: Distinctive blue (from chamazulene formation)
- **European Pharmacopoeia requirement**: ≥4 mL/kg blue essential oil

### Key Essential Oil Constituents

**Primary components (50-65% of total volatile oil combined)**:
- **(-)-α-bisabolol**: Sesquiterpene alcohol
- **Chamazulene**: Azulene (forms during steam distillation from matricin)

**Additional essential oil components**:
- **Bisabolone oxide A**: Sesquiterpene oxide
- **β-farnesene**: Sesquiterpene hydrocarbon

**German Commission E specification**:
- Must contain (-)-α-bisabolol OR (-)-α-bisabololoxides A and B in essential oil

## Flavonoids

### Quantitative Profile

**Total flavonoid content**: Up to 8% flavone glycosides in dried flowers

### Key Flavonoid Compounds

**Primary marker compound**:
- **Apigenin 7-glucoside (apigenin-7-O-glucoside)**
  - Role: Primary standardization marker
  - Typical standardization level: 1.2% apigenin content
  - Used in pharmaceutical-grade extracts for clinical trials

**Additional flavonoids**:
- **Apigenin 7-glucoside 6'-acetyl derivative**
- **Luteolin glucosides**
- **Quercetin glycosides**

### Apigenin Properties and Pharmacology

**Therapeutic properties** (PMID: 27766890):
- Primary flavonoid marker compound
- Low intrinsic toxicity
- Health-promoting agent
- Anxiolytic mechanisms: May modulate hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenocortical axis

**Clinical significance**:
- Used as primary standardization marker in clinical trials
- 220 mg capsules standardized to 1.2% apigenin used in depression/anxiety trials (Amsterdam et al., 2012)
- 100 mg capsules containing 1.2% apigenin used in menopausal symptoms trial (Mohsenzadeh-Ledari et al., 2025)

## Polysaccharides

**Mucilage polysaccharides**: Up to 10% of dried flowers
- Contribute to demulcent properties
- Traditional use for soothing irritated mucous membranes

## Coumarins

**Total coumarin content**: ~0.1%
- **7-methoxycoumarin**: Identified as quality marker in pharmaceutical-grade extracts (HPLC fingerprinting)

## Other Constituents

**Choline**: Up to 0.3%
**Phenolic acids**: Present (specific quantities not detailed)
**Organic acids**: Present (specific quantities not detailed)
**Terpenes**: Multiple terpene compounds beyond essential oil

## Standardization Methods

### Clinical Trial Standardization

**Pharmaceutical-grade extract specifications**:
- **Apigenin content**: 1.2% (typical standardization marker)
- **HPLC fingerprinting**: Confirms presence of apigenin-7-glucoside and 7-methoxycoumarin
- **Quality control**: Pharmaceutical-grade extracts provide highest quality control

**Examples from clinical trials**:

**GAD trials (Mao et al., 2016)**:
- 500 mg capsules standardized with HPLC fingerprinting
- Confirmed apigenin-7-glucoside and 7-methoxycoumarin
- 1500 mg daily dose (3 x 500 mg)

**Depression/anxiety trial (Amsterdam et al., 2012)**:
- 220 mg capsules
- Standardized to 1.2% apigenin

**Menopausal symptoms (Mohsenzadeh-Ledari et al., 2025)**:
- 100 mg capsules containing 1.2% apigenin
- 400 mg daily dose (4 x 100 mg)

### Traditional vs. Standardized Preparations

**Traditional tea preparation** (German Commission E):
- ~3 g dried flower heads
- No standardization to specific markers
- Relies on whole plant synergy

**Standardized extracts**:
- Concentrated to specific marker compounds (typically apigenin)
- 270-1500 mg doses of standardized extract
- Ratio equivalence: Not directly comparable to traditional preparations due to concentration

## Pharmacopeia Standards

### European Pharmacopoeia
- **Essential oil requirement**: ≥4 mL/kg blue essential oil
- Blue color indicates presence of chamazulene

### Quality Control Parameters (WHO Monograph)

**Specifications for**:
- Essential oil content and composition
- Purity tests:
  - Microbiological contamination
  - Pesticide residues
  - Heavy metals
- Botanical/microscopic identification characteristics

## Pharmacokinetics and Bioavailability

### Current Knowledge

**Limited comprehensive pharmacokinetic data available** in published literature

**Apigenin pharmacokinetics**:
- Low intrinsic toxicity
- Absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) properties require further research
- Reference: PMID 27766890

**Glycemic dose-response data (animal studies)**:
- Each 100 mg/day increase in extract lowered blood glucose ~54.35 mg/dL (p<0.001)
- Suggests dose-dependent effects in animal models
- Source: PMID 38932814

**Recommendation**: Further research needed on human ADME properties

## Mechanisms of Action by Compound Class

### Essential Oils

**Anti-inflammatory**:
- α-bisabolol: Anti-inflammatory properties
- Chamazulene: Anti-inflammatory and antioxidant

**Antimicrobial**:
- Essential oil components demonstrate antibacterial and antifungal activity

**Spasmolytic**:
- Essential oil components contribute to smooth muscle relaxation

### Flavonoids

**Anxiolytic**:
- Apigenin: Modulates hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenocortical axis
- May interact with GABA receptors

**Antioxidant**:
- Flavonoids contribute to free radical scavenging
- Quercetin and luteolin compounds have documented antioxidant properties

**Anti-inflammatory**:
- Flavonoids inhibit prostaglandin and leukotriene synthesis
- Reduces inflammatory mediators

### Polysaccharides

**Demulcent/protective**:
- Mucilage provides protective coating for irritated tissues
- Traditional use for gastrointestinal and oral/throat applications

### Synergistic Effects

**Whole plant matrix**:
- Over 120 constituents likely work synergistically
- Traditional use values whole plant synergy
- Clinical extracts maintain complex constituent profile beyond single markers

**Complex mechanisms**:
- Anti-inflammatory (multiple pathways)
- Anti-spasmodic (smooth muscle effects)
- Anxiolytic (neuromodulation)
- Hormonal modulation (women's health applications)

## Quality Control Concerns

### Commercial Supplements

**LactMed Database warning (2021)**:
- Differences often found between labeled and actual ingredients/amounts in dietary supplements
- Quality control remains concern in commercial products

**Contamination risks**:
- Clostridium botulinum spores found in some loose-leaf chamomile teas from health food stores
- Importance of sourcing from reputable suppliers

### Recommendations for Quality

**Standardization markers**:
- Apigenin content (1.2% typical for clinical use)
- HPLC fingerprinting for pharmaceutical-grade extracts
- Essential oil content (European Pharmacopoeia: ≥4 mL/kg blue essential oil)

**Purity testing**:
- Microbiological testing
- Pesticide residue testing
- Heavy metal testing
- Botanical identity confirmation

## Clinical Implications

**Dosing considerations**:
- Standardized extracts: 270-1500 mg/day (depending on indication)
- Traditional tea: ~3 g dried flowers per cup
- Topical preparations: 1-2.5% concentration for oral mucositis

**Standardization importance**:
- Ensures consistent therapeutic effect
- Allows reproducibility in clinical research
- Pharmaceutical-grade extracts with HPLC fingerprinting provide highest quality control

**Variable essential oil content**:
- 0.24-2.0% range means significant variability in raw material
- Standardization to apigenin or essential oil content provides consistency
- Growing conditions, harvest timing, and processing affect constituent levels

## Research Gaps

**Pharmacokinetics**:
- Limited human ADME data
- Bioavailability studies needed
- Interaction potential with medications

**Synergistic effects**:
- Mechanisms of whole plant synergy not fully elucidated
- Individual constituent vs. whole extract comparisons limited

**Toxicity**:
- More comprehensive toxicity testing recommended (Dai et al., 2022)
- Long-term safety studies needed
- Dose-response relationships for adverse effects

**Standardization optimization**:
- Best marker compounds for different therapeutic indications
- Optimal extract concentration methods
- Constituent stability during processing and storage
