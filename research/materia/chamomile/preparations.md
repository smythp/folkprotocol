# Chamomile - Preparations and Dosing

## Standardized Extract Preparations

### Pharmaceutical-Grade Capsules

**Most common in clinical trials**:
- Standardized to 1.2% apigenin (typical marker)
- HPLC fingerprinting confirms apigenin-7-glucoside and 7-methoxycoumarin
- Quality control ensures consistency

**Capsule strengths used in research**:
- 100 mg capsules (1.2% apigenin): Menopausal symptoms, PMS
- 220 mg capsules (1.2% apigenin): Depression/anxiety studies
- 270 mg: Insomnia studies (taken twice daily)
- 500 mg capsules: GAD studies (taken three times daily)

### Extract Concentration

**Standardization markers**:
- **Primary**: Apigenin content (typically 1.2%)
- **Secondary**: HPLC fingerprinting for apigenin-7-glucoside and 7-methoxycoumarin
- **European Pharmacopoeia**: ≥4 mL/kg blue essential oil

**Quality specifications** (WHO Monograph):
- Essential oil content and composition verified
- Purity testing:
  - Microbiological contamination limits
  - Pesticide residue testing
  - Heavy metal testing
- Botanical identity confirmation

## Traditional Tea Preparation

### German Commission E Standard Method

**Ingredients**:
- Heaped tablespoon (~3 g) dried chamomile flower heads
- 150 mL boiling water

**Preparation steps**:
1. Pour boiling water over flower heads
2. Cover vessel to prevent volatile oil loss
3. Steep for 5-10 minutes
4. Strain before drinking

**Important notes**:
- Covering during steeping is critical to retain essential oils
- Do not steep longer than 10 minutes (may increase bitterness)
- Fresh preparation preferred; do not prepare in advance

**Dosing frequency**: Not specified in Commission E monograph; traditionally used as needed, often 2-3 times daily

### Traditional Tea vs. Standardized Extracts

**Key differences**:
- Tea: ~3 g dried flowers = one cup
- Standardized extract: 270-1500 mg concentrated extract per day
- Tea relies on whole plant synergy; extract standardized to specific markers
- Tea dosing less precise; extract dosing reproducible
- Clinical trials predominantly use standardized extracts

## Liquid Extracts and Tinctures

### Liquid Extract (Used in Clinical Trials)

**Cyclic mastalgia study** (Saghafi et al., 2018):
- **Dosing**: 5 drops liquid extract
- **Frequency**: Three times daily
- **Duration**: 2 consecutive months
- **Results**: Significant pain reduction (p=0.007 vs. placebo)

**Hyperprolactinemia study** (Kabiri et al., 2019):
- **Dosing**: 5 mL chamomile syrup
- **Frequency**: Twice daily
- **Duration**: 4 weeks
- **Results**: Significant prolactin reduction (p <0.0001)

### Tincture Preparation (Traditional)

**Standard tincture ratios** (not specified in bibliography, but traditional herbalism):
- Typical ratio: 1:5 (1 part herb to 5 parts menstruum)
- Alcohol percentage: 40-60% for optimal extraction of both polar and non-polar constituents
- Maceration time: 2-4 weeks

**Dosing** (traditional, not from clinical trials):
- 2-4 mL (40-80 drops) 2-3 times daily
- Dilute in small amount of water

**Note**: Clinical trials used proprietary liquid extracts; standardization details often not fully disclosed

## Topical Preparations

### Oral/Throat Applications

**Oral mucositis** (Dantas et al., 2022 systematic review):
- **Concentration**: 1-2.5%
- **Frequency**: Single to 4 times daily
- **Application**: Topical to oral mucosa
- **Evidence**: 4 of 6 RCTs (492 patients total) showed effectiveness

**Form**: Typically liquid or gel preparations for direct mucosal application

### Topical Oil

**Pediatric enuresis** (Sharifi et al., 2017):
- **Form**: Chamomile oil (pure or diluted - specific concentration not stated)
- **Application**: Topical (location not specified in abstract)
- **Frequency**: Daily
- **Duration**: 6 weeks
- **Results**: Significant reduction in enuresis frequency (P < .001 at weeks 2, 4, 6)
- **Safety**: No adverse events in 80 children

### Topical Ointment/Cream

**Traditional use** (EMA Monograph indications):
- Skin irritation
- Sunburn
- Superficial wounds
- Minor boils
- Irritation of genital and anal areas

**Concentration**: Not specified in bibliography; traditionally varies from 3-10% chamomile extract in ointment base

**Caution**: Contact dermatitis reported with topical preparations, especially Kamillosan ointment on nipples/areolas (LactMed Database)

### Bath Preparations

**EMA Monograph contraindications for baths**:
- Severe cardiac/circulatory conditions
- Open wounds, extensive skin damage
- Acute skin disease
- High fever, severe infections

**Traditional preparation**: 50-100 g dried flowers per full bath, or essential oil added to bathwater

## Dosing by Indication (Clinical Trial Data)

### Generalized Anxiety Disorder

**Evidence-based dosing**:
- **1500 mg/day** standardized extract (strongest evidence)
  - 500 mg capsules, three times daily
  - Pharmaceutical-grade with HPLC fingerprinting
  - Duration: 8-38 weeks in clinical trials
  - Studies: Mao et al., 2016; Keefe et al., 2016

**Alternative protocol**:
- 220 mg capsules standardized to 1.2% apigenin (Amsterdam et al., 2009)
- Specific mg/day not stated in abstract, but used for 8 weeks

**Response rates**:
- 58.1% clinical response at 1500 mg/day for 8 weeks (Keefe et al., 2016, n=179)

### Sleep Disorders

**Chronic primary insomnia**:
- **540 mg/day**: 270 mg twice daily (Zick et al., 2011, n=34)
- Duration: 28 days
- Modest effects on sleep latency and fatigue

**Elderly sleep quality**:
- **400 mg/day**: 200 mg twice daily (Adib-Hajbaghery & Mousavi, 2017, n=60)
- Duration: 28 consecutive days
- Significant improvement in sleep quality (P<0.05)

**Dosing range**: 270-400 mg/day standardized extract

### Depression (Comorbid with Anxiety)

**Dosing**:
- **220 mg capsules** standardized to 1.2% apigenin (Amsterdam et al., 2012, n=57)
- Specific total daily dose not stated
- Duration: 8 weeks
- Results: Significant HAM-D reduction (P < .05)

### Menopausal Symptoms

**Evidence-based dosing**:
- **400 mg/day**: 100 mg capsules (1.2% apigenin), 4 capsules daily (Mohsenzadeh-Ledari et al., 2025, n=80)
- Duration: 12 weeks
- Results: Overall symptom reduction -10.36 points (P < 0.001)

**Symptom-specific reductions**:
- Vasomotor: -2.25 points
- Psychological: -3.74 points
- Locomotor: -2.70 points
- Urological: -1.89 points

### Premenstrual Syndrome

**Evidence-based dosing**:
- **300 mg/day**: 100 mg capsules, 3 times daily (Sharifi et al., 2014, n=90)
- Duration: 2 menstrual cycles
- Results: 30-33% emotional symptom reduction (P < 0.001 vs. mefenamic acid)

### Cyclic Mastalgia (Breast Pain)

**Evidence-based dosing**:
- **5 drops liquid extract**, 3 times daily (Saghafi et al., 2018, n=60)
- Duration: 2 consecutive months
- Results: Significant pain reduction (p<0.0001 within group, p=0.007 vs. placebo)

**Note**: Specific mg/day not calculable from "drops" without knowing extract concentration

### Hyperprolactinemia

**Evidence-based dosing**:
- **5 mL chamomile syrup**, twice daily (10 mL/day total) (Kabiri et al., 2019, n=56)
- Duration: 4 weeks
- Results: Significant prolactin reduction (p <0.0001)

**Note**: Syrup concentration not specified; mg equivalent unknown

### Pediatric Enuresis

**Evidence-based dosing**:
- **Topical chamomile oil**, daily application (Sharifi et al., 2017, n=80)
- Duration: 6 weeks
- Application site and amount not specified in abstract
- Results: Significant reduction in enuresis frequency (P < .001)

## Preparation Storage and Stability

### Dried Flowers

**Storage**:
- Store in airtight container
- Protect from light and moisture
- Cool, dry location
- Shelf life: Typically 1 year for optimal potency

**Quality indicators**:
- Color: Flowers should retain yellow/white color (not brown)
- Aroma: Characteristic apple-like scent should be present
- Essential oil content degrades with poor storage

### Standardized Extracts

**Storage**:
- Follow manufacturer recommendations
- Typically room temperature, protected from moisture
- Capsules: Moisture-resistant containers
- Liquid extracts: Amber glass bottles to protect from light

**Stability**:
- Pharmaceutical-grade extracts: Expiration dates provided
- Standardization ensures consistent potency through expiration
- HPLC testing can verify potency

### Prepared Tea

**Stability**:
- Consume immediately after preparation
- Do not prepare in advance and store
- Essential oils and active compounds degrade quickly in aqueous solution
- Maximum: Refrigerate and use within 24 hours (quality degradation likely)

## Administration Guidelines

### With or Without Food

**Data in bibliography**: Limited specific information

**General guidance**:
- Capsules: Can typically be taken with or without food
- Traditional tea: Often taken between meals or before bedtime for sleep
- Gastrointestinal complaints: May be taken with meals

### Timing Considerations

**Sleep/insomnia**:
- Evening dosing appropriate
- Some protocols use twice daily (morning and evening)
- Allow 1-2 hours before bedtime for absorption

**Anxiety (GAD)**:
- Three times daily dosing (morning, afternoon, evening) used in major trials
- Maintains more consistent blood levels throughout day

**Women's health (PMS, menopausal symptoms)**:
- Divided doses throughout day (3-4 times daily)
- Consistency important for hormonal conditions

## Bioequivalence Considerations

### Tea vs. Extract

**Not directly equivalent**:
- Traditional tea (~3 g flowers) ≠ standardized extract doses (270-1500 mg)
- Extraction efficiency differs
- Tea contains whole plant matrix; extracts are concentrated
- Standardization ensures specific compound levels in extracts
- Clinical trials predominantly used extracts, not traditional tea

**Estimating equivalence**:
- Difficult without knowing exact extraction ratios and yields
- Conservative approach: Do not assume equivalence
- Use clinical trial dosing for therapeutic applications
- Use traditional tea preparation for traditional indications/general wellness

### Liquid Extract vs. Capsule

**Bioavailability**:
- Limited comparative data
- Liquid extracts may have faster absorption
- Capsules provide more precise, consistent dosing
- Clinical trial evidence predominantly with capsules

## Quality Control in Preparations

### Pharmaceutical-Grade Requirements

**HPLC fingerprinting**:
- Confirms apigenin-7-glucoside
- Confirms 7-methoxycoumarin
- Ensures batch-to-batch consistency

**Standardization**:
- 1.2% apigenin (typical marker)
- Essential oil content (European Pharmacopoeia: ≥4 mL/kg)

**Purity testing**:
- Microbial contamination
- Pesticide residues
- Heavy metals
- Botanical identity

### Commercial Supplement Concerns

**LactMed Database (2021) warning**:
- Differences often found between labeled and actual ingredients/amounts
- Quality varies widely in commercial supplements
- Choose reputable manufacturers with third-party testing

**Contamination risks**:
- Clostridium botulinum spores in some loose-leaf teas
- Pesticide residues if not organically grown or tested
- Heavy metal contamination possible with poor sourcing

**Recommendations**:
- Select pharmaceutical-grade or USP-verified products when possible
- Look for third-party testing certifications (e.g., NSF, USP, ConsumerLab)
- Verify standardization on label
- Check for Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) certification

## Special Preparation Considerations

### For Allergic Individuals

**Contraindicated**:
- Chamomile absolutely contraindicated in Asteraceae family allergy
- Cross-reactivity with ragweed, chrysanthemum, marigold, echinacea, feverfew, milk thistle

**Testing**:
- Consider small test dose for new users
- Watch for allergic symptoms (rash, itching, oral swelling)
- Topical patch test before extensive topical use

### For Pediatric Use

**Safety**:
- Used safely in infants for colic and diarrhea (LactMed)
- 80 children used topical oil safely for 6 weeks (Sharifi et al., 2017)
- EMA: Some preparations approved for children under 12

**Preparations**:
- Diluted tea for infants (consult pediatrician)
- Ensure high-quality, tested products (no Clostridium botulinum contamination)
- Topical oil for external use

**Dosing**:
- Reduce adult doses proportionally by body weight
- Medical supervision recommended
- Start with minimal doses and increase as tolerated

### For Topical Use

**Concentration guidelines** (from clinical studies):
- Oral mucositis: 1-2.5%
- Ointments/creams: Typically 3-10% (traditional use; not specified in bibliography)

**Preparation**:
- Ensure sterile technique for oral/wound applications
- Use appropriate base (aqueous for mucosa, oil/cream for skin)
- May use strong tea as compress (soak cloth in concentrated tea)

**Application**:
- Clean area before application
- Apply directly to affected area
- Frequency: 1-4 times daily depending on indication
- Monitor for contact dermatitis
