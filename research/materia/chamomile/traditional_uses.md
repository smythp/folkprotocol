# Chamomile - Traditional Uses

## Historical Recognition

### European Traditional Medicine

**European Medicines Agency (EMA) Recognition**:
- **Status**: European Union Herbal Monograph (2015)
- **Evidence basis**: Traditional use (30+ years documented, ≥15 years in EU)
- **Traditional indications**:
  - Minor gastro-intestinal complaints (bloating, minor spasms)
  - Relief of common cold symptoms
  - Minor oral and throat ulcers/inflammations
  - Skin irritation, sunburn, superficial wounds, minor boils
  - Irritation of genital and anal areas (after medical evaluation)

**German Commission E**:
- **Status**: Monograph approved
- **Drug definition**: Dry flowering heads of Chamomilla recutita (L.) Rauschert
- **Recognition**: Listed in 26 country pharmacopeias (Germany, Belgium, France, UK)
- **Mentioned in**: ESCOP (European Scientific Cooperative on Phytotherapy) monographs

### Pharmacopeia Listings

**Countries with official pharmacopeia entries (26 total)**:
- Germany
- Belgium

- United Kingdom
- Additional 22 countries (specific names not enumerated in source)

**World Health Organization (WHO)**:
- **Publication**: WHO Monographs on Selected Medicinal Plants (1999)
- **Drug definition**: Dry flowering heads of Chamomilla recutita (L.) Rauschert
- **Synonyms recognized**: Matricaria chamomilla, Matricaria recutita L., Matricaria suaveolens
- **Content**: Common names, botanical/microscopic descriptions, specifications for essential oil, purity tests (microbes, pesticides, heavy metals), uses supported by clinical data

### North American Recognition

**US FDA**:
- **Status**: Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS)
- **Category**: Spice, seasoning, or flavoring agent in food
- **Use in infants**: Safely and effectively used alone and with other herbs in infants for colic, diarrhea, other conditions (per LactMed Database, 2021)

## Traditional Preparation Methods

### Traditional Tea (German Commission E Standard)

**Preparation**:
1. Use heaped tablespoon (~3 g) of dried chamomile flower heads
2. Pour 150 mL boiling water over flowers
3. Steep covered for 5-10 minutes
4. Strain before drinking

**Notes**:
- Covering during steeping is important to prevent loss of volatile oils
- Traditional tea preparation differs from standardized extract dosing used in clinical trials

### Essential Oil Extraction

**Traditional recognition**:
- Essential oil content: 0.24-2.0% of dried flowers
- Color: Distinctive blue color (from chamazulene)
- European Pharmacopoeia requirement: ≥4 mL/kg blue essential oil

**Key components traditionally valued**:
- (-)-α-bisabolol: Part of 50-65% of total volatile oil
- Chamazulene: Part of 50-65% of total volatile oil
- Bisabolone oxide A
- β-farnesene

## Traditional Medical Systems

### European Folk Medicine

**Documented uses across European traditions**:
- Gastrointestinal complaints (bloating, spasms, digestive upset)
- Inflammatory conditions (internal and external)
- Wound healing and skin conditions
- Nervous system support (calming, sleep aid)
- Women's health (menstrual complaints, reproductive support)
- Respiratory complaints (common cold symptoms)

**Application methods**:
- Oral: Teas, infusions, tinctures
- Topical: Poultices, compresses, baths, ointments
- Inhalation: Steam inhalations for respiratory complaints

### Duration of Traditional Use

**EMA Documentation**:
- 30+ years of documented use globally
- Minimum 15 years of documented use within the European Union
- This meets the threshold for "traditional use" recognition under EU herbal medicine regulations

## Traditional Contraindications

**From EMA Monograph**:
- Allergy to Matricaria or Asteraceae family plants
- Severe cardiac/circulatory conditions (for full baths)
- Open wounds, extensive skin damage, acute skin disease (for baths)
- High fever, severe infections (for baths)

## Traditional Safety Considerations

**Historical safety profile**:
- Generally regarded as safe with long history of use
- Allergic reactions noted, particularly in individuals sensitive to Asteraceae/Compositae family
- Cross-reactivity possible with related plants:
  - Echinacea
  - Feverfew
  - Milk thistle
  - Ragweed
  - Chrysanthemums
  - Marigolds

**Traditional wisdom on quality**:
- Flowers should be harvested at peak bloom
- Proper drying preserves active constituents
- Blue color of essential oil indicates quality
- Fresh preparations valued, but dried material also effective

## Modern Continuation of Traditional Knowledge

**Traditional use basis for regulatory approval**:
- EMA approval based on 30+ years documented traditional use
- WHO monograph acknowledges traditional applications alongside clinical data
- German Commission E monograph provides standardized traditional preparation method
- ESCOP monographs document traditional European uses

**Traditional knowledge informing modern research**:
- Clinical trials investigating traditional uses (anxiety, sleep, digestive complaints, women's health)
- Pharmacological research confirming traditional mechanisms (anti-inflammatory, anti-spasmodic, anxiolytic)
- Quality control standards derived from traditional knowledge of active constituents

## Notes on Traditional vs. Modern Use

**Key differences**:
- Traditional preparations (teas, infusions): ~3g dried flowers
- Modern clinical extracts: 270-1500 mg standardized extracts (concentrated)
- Traditional use: Whole plant synergy valued
- Modern use: Standardization to specific markers (1.2% apigenin typical)
- Traditional assessment: Empirical observation over centuries
- Modern assessment: RCTs and systematic reviews

**Continuity**:
- Many traditional indications validated by modern research (anxiety, sleep, digestive complaints, women's health)
- Safety profile in clinical trials consistent with traditional safety knowledge
- Traditional preparation methods still recognized as valid (Commission E tea preparation)
- Core therapeutic uses remain consistent between traditional and modern applications
