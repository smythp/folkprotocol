# Chamomile - Safety Profile

## General Safety Assessment

**FDA Status**: Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) as spice, seasoning, or flavoring agent in food

**EMA Assessment**: Traditional use status (30+ years documented, ≥15 years in EU) with well-documented safety profile

**WHO Recognition**: Included in WHO Monographs on Selected Medicinal Plants (1999) with safety specifications

**Overall assessment**: Low intrinsic toxicity with favorable safety profile when used appropriately

## Adverse Effects

### Documented Adverse Reactions

**Allergic reactions** (frequency undetermined per EMA):
- Can be severe in sensitized individuals
- More common with topical preparations, especially on moist internal surfaces
- Manifestations:
  - Contact dermatitis
  - Anaphylaxis (rare, in sensitized individuals)
  - Mouth sores
  - Skin spots
  - Itching

**Clinical trial adverse events**:

**GAD studies (1500 mg/day)**:
- Treatment-emergent adverse events: 11.7% of subjects (Keefe et al., 2016, n=179)
- Serious adverse events: None reported
- Discontinuation due to AEs: 15.6%
- Similar or lower AE rates compared to placebo in most trials
- 1 patient per group discontinued in first GAD trial (Amsterdam et al., 2009, n=57)

**Menopausal symptoms study (400 mg/day)**:
- 2 of 80 participants experienced adverse effects leading to discontinuation:
  - Mouth sores
  - Skin spots
  - Itching
- Authors note use warrants caution due to associated side effects (Mohsenzadeh-Ledari et al., 2025)

**Other trials**:
- Most RCTs report no significant difference in adverse events between chamomile and placebo
- Sleep studies: No differences in adverse events (Zick et al., 2011, n=34; Adib-Hajbaghery & Mousavi, 2017, n=60)
- PMS study: No adverse effects reported (Sharifi et al., 2014, n=90)
- Depression study: No adverse effects explicitly reported (Amsterdam et al., 2012, n=57)
- Pediatric enuresis: No adverse events reported (Sharifi et al., 2017, n=80)

### Systematic Review Safety Data

**Anxiety & sleep meta-analysis (2019)**:
- 12 RCTs reviewed
- Mild adverse events reported by only 3 RCTs
- Favorable safety profile overall

**Sleep meta-analysis (2024)**:
- 10 clinical trials, 772 participants
- No adverse events reported in any studies

**Glycemic meta-analysis (2024)**:
- 4 human RCTs, 8 animal studies
- No adverse events reported

## Allergic Reactions and Cross-Reactivity

### Asteraceae/Compositae Family Allergy

**Primary contraindication**: Allergy to Matricaria or Asteraceae family plants

**Cross-reactive plants**:
- Echinacea
- Feverfew
- Milk thistle
- Ragweed
- Chrysanthemums
- Marigolds

**Clinical significance**:
- Individuals with known Asteraceae allergy should avoid chamomile
- Ragweed-allergic individuals at particular risk
- Allergic reactions can be severe in sensitized individuals

### Topical Sensitization

**Contact dermatitis risk**:
- Two women developed contact dermatitis of nipples/areolas after applying Kamillosan ointment for cracked nipples (LactMed Database, 2021)
- Higher sensitization risk with topical preparations than oral preparations
- Particular risk with application to moist internal surfaces

## Contraindications

### Absolute Contraindications
- **Allergy to Matricaria or Asteraceae family plants**

### Contraindications for Bath Preparations
- Severe cardiac/circulatory conditions (full baths)
- Open wounds, extensive skin damage (baths)
- Acute skin disease (baths)
- High fever (baths)
- Severe infections (baths)

**Note**: These contraindications specific to full bath preparations, not oral or topical spot applications

## Special Populations

### Pregnancy

**Evidence**: Limited specific data in bibliography

**Regulatory stance**: EMA monograph does not list pregnancy as explicit contraindication

**Traditional use**: Used in various cultures during pregnancy, though specific safety data limited

**Recommendation**: More research needed; consult healthcare provider

### Lactation

**LactMed Database (2021) findings**:
- Safely and effectively used alone and with other herbs in infants for colic, diarrhea, and other conditions
- Risk of contact dermatitis when applied topically to nipples/areolas in nursing mothers (2 case reports with Kamillosan ointment)

**Overall assessment**: Generally considered compatible with breastfeeding when used orally; caution with topical nipple applications

### Pediatric Use

**Infants**:
- Used safely in infants for colic and diarrhea (per LactMed)
- Contamination risk with loose-leaf teas (Clostridium botulinum spores)

**Children**:
- Pediatric enuresis study: 80 children, topical chamomile oil for 6 weeks, no adverse events (Sharifi et al., 2017)
- EMA: Some preparations approved for children under 12 years

**Recommendations**:
- Source from reputable suppliers to avoid contamination
- Appropriate dosing adjustments for children
- Medical supervision recommended

### Elderly

**Evidence**:
- Elderly sleep quality study: 60 participants aged 60+, 400 mg/day for 28 days, well-tolerated (Adib-Hajbaghery & Mousavi, 2017)
- Can be used as safe modality for promoting elderly sleep
- No specific elderly-related adverse effects identified in studies

## Drug Interactions

### Theoretical Interactions

**Data in bibliography**: Limited specific drug interaction data

**Considerations based on pharmacology**:
- Potential for additive sedative effects with CNS depressants (theoretical)
- Anticoagulant/antiplatelet drugs: Coumarin content (~0.1%) theoretically relevant, though clinical significance unclear

**Clinical trial evidence**:
- No significant drug interactions reported in major RCTs
- Participants in GAD trials taking 1500 mg/day without reported drug interactions (Mao et al., 2016; Keefe et al., 2016)

**Recommendation**: Further research needed on specific drug-herb interactions

## Quality and Contamination Concerns

### Microbial Contamination

**Clostridium botulinum**:
- Spores found in some loose-leaf chamomile teas from health food stores (LactMed Database, 2021)
- Particular concern for infant use
- Importance of sourcing from reputable suppliers with proper testing

### Ingredient Quality Control

**Supplement quality concerns** (LactMed Database, 2021):
- Differences often found between labeled and actual ingredients/amounts in dietary supplements
- Variable quality in commercial products
- Importance of pharmaceutical-grade or quality-controlled products

**Purity testing needed**:
- Microbiological contamination
- Pesticide residues
- Heavy metals
- Botanical identity confirmation

## Long-Term Safety

### Duration of Use in Clinical Trials

**Longest trials**:
- 38 weeks total (12 weeks open-label + 26 weeks double-blind) in GAD prevention trial (Mao et al., 2016, n=93)
- No serious adverse events reported
- Low adverse event rates similar to placebo

**Shorter trials**:
- 12 weeks: Menopausal symptoms (n=80)
- 8 weeks: GAD trials, PMS (2 cycles), mastalgia (2 months)
- 28 days: Sleep studies

**Gaps**:
- Limited data on multi-year continuous use
- More long-term safety studies recommended (Dai et al., 2022)

## Toxicity

### Acute Toxicity

**Apigenin** (primary marker compound):
- Low intrinsic toxicity (PMID: 27766890)

**Clinical evidence**:
- No serious adverse events in clinical trials
- Doses up to 1500 mg/day well-tolerated in GAD studies

### Chronic Toxicity

**Data available**: Limited

**Recommendations** (Dai et al., 2022):
- More toxicity tests needed to confirm long-term safety
- Further research needed for concrete scientific evidence

## Overdose

**Data in bibliography**: No specific overdose cases documented

**Clinical trial high-dose data**:
- 1500 mg/day used safely in GAD trials for up to 38 weeks
- 11.7% treatment-emergent adverse events, none serious

## Safety by Route of Administration

### Oral

**Safety profile**: Favorable
- Most common route in clinical trials
- Lower allergic sensitization risk than topical
- Well-tolerated across multiple indications and populations

### Topical

**Safety considerations**:
- Higher allergic sensitization risk, especially on moist internal surfaces
- Contact dermatitis reported (particularly nipple/areola applications)
- Effective for oral mucositis (1-2.5% concentration, 4 of 6 studies) without major safety concerns in controlled trials

### Topical Oil

**Pediatric use**:
- Used safely in 80 children for enuresis for 6 weeks
- No adverse events reported (Sharifi et al., 2017)

## Comparative Safety

### vs. Pharmaceutical Alternatives

**Anxiety medications**:
- "Effective and less dangerous compared to pharmaceutical anxiety treatments" (Saadatmand et al., 2024)
- Comparable symptom reduction to conventional anxiolytic medications with favorable safety profile (Keefe et al., 2016)

**PMS treatment**:
- Compared to mefenamic acid 250 mg 3x daily
- Chamomile 100 mg 3x daily: No adverse effects reported
- Superior emotional symptom reduction, similar physical symptom effects (Sharifi et al., 2014)

### vs. Placebo

**Clinical trial data**:
- Most trials show similar or lower adverse event rates compared to placebo
- No consistent pattern of chamomile-specific adverse effects
- Discontinuation rates similar between chamomile and placebo groups

## Clinical Recommendations

### Pre-Treatment Screening
- Screen for Asteraceae family allergies
- Ask about ragweed, chrysanthemum, marigold allergies
- Caution with topical use if history of contact dermatitis

### Monitoring
- Watch for allergic reactions, especially with first exposures
- Monitor for contact dermatitis with topical applications
- Discontinue if allergic symptoms develop

### Product Selection
- Choose pharmaceutical-grade or quality-controlled products
- Verify standardization (1.2% apigenin typical)
- Ensure microbial, pesticide, and heavy metal testing
- Avoid loose-leaf teas of unknown quality, especially for infants

### Dosing Safety
- Clinical trial doses: 270-1500 mg/day standardized extract
- Traditional tea: ~3 g dried flowers per cup
- Start with lower doses and increase as tolerated
- Medical supervision for therapeutic use in vulnerable populations

## Adverse Event Reporting

**From clinical trials**:
- Overall low rate of treatment-emergent adverse events
- Most common: Mild allergic reactions (skin, oral)
- Rare: Severe allergic reactions (anaphylaxis in sensitized individuals)
- Very rare: Serious adverse events (none reported in major RCTs)

**Discontinuation rates**:
- GAD study (Keefe et al., 2016): 15.6% premature discontinuation (reasons not all AE-related)
- Most studies: 0-2 discontinuations due to adverse events per group

## Safety Conclusions

**Strong evidence for safety**:
- GRAS status (FDA)
- 30+ years traditional use documentation
- Favorable clinical trial safety profile
- Low intrinsic toxicity
- Comparable or better safety than conventional alternatives

**Cautions**:
- Asteraceae family allergy (absolute contraindication)
- Cross-reactivity with ragweed and related plants
- Topical sensitization risk
- Quality control concerns in commercial supplements
- Contamination risk with untested products

**Research needs**:
- More comprehensive long-term toxicity testing
- Drug-herb interaction studies
- Pharmacokinetic/bioavailability data
- Pregnancy safety data
- Standardized adverse event reporting across studies
