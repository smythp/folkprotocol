# Oat Straw - Safety

## Overall Safety Assessment

Oat straw (*Avena sativa* L., herba) and standardized green oat herb extracts (Neuravena®, cognitaven®) have an exceptionally clean safety record. No hepatotoxicity, serious adverse events, or clinically significant drug interactions have been identified in any published clinical trial or case report literature. The FDA considers oats (grain) to be GRAS. No case reports of toxicity from green oat herb extract have been identified in PubMed as of 2026-02-27. Distinguished from many herbal nervines by the complete absence of adverse event signals across 7 published RCTs totaling several hundred participants.

## Contraindications

### Celiac Disease

**Status**: Contraindicated — formal warning from EMA HMPC

**Evidence basis**:
- Green oat herb extracts are manufactured from *Avena sativa* aerial parts. Even when the herba itself does not contain significant gluten (which is a grain protein), shared manufacturing facilities with oat grain products create risk of gluten cross-contamination
- EMA HMPC Avenae herba monograph (EMEA/HMPC/202966/2007) explicitly lists celiac disease as a contraindication
- Individuals with celiac disease or non-celiac gluten sensitivity should avoid oat straw herbal preparations unless the product is certified gluten-free and manufactured in a dedicated gluten-free facility

### Oat Allergy

**Status**: Avoid; rare but documented cross-reactive sensitization

**Evidence basis**:
- Oat-specific proteins (avenins) can trigger IgE-mediated reactions in oat-sensitized individuals
- Oat allergy is less common than wheat allergy but is documented, particularly in individuals with atopic dermatitis
- Cross-reactivity with other grasses is possible
- No case reports specifically linking oat straw herbal preparations to anaphylaxis have been identified, but individuals with documented oat allergy should avoid all *Avena sativa* preparations

## Drug Interactions

**No human drug interaction studies have been identified for green oat herb extract.**

**No clinically significant drug interactions have been reported** in any published RCT, case series, or spontaneous adverse event report.

### Theoretical Interactions (Mechanism-Based, No Clinical Evidence)

| Proposed Mechanism | Potential Drug Class | Evidence Level | Clinical Significance |
|-------------------|---------------------|---------------|----------------------|
| MAO-B inhibition | Selegiline, rasagiline, other MAO inhibitors | In vitro; review literature only | Not established; mild in vitro activity only; no case reports |
| PDE-4 inhibition | Roflumilast (prescription PDE-4 inhibitor) | In vitro; review literature only | Not established |
| eNOS enhancement / vasodilation (avenanthramides) | Antihypertensives, PDE-5 inhibitors, nitrates | Preclinical; consistent with vascular data | Not established; theoretical additive hypotension possible |
| Angiotensin II inhibition (avenacosides) | ACE inhibitors, ARBs | Preclinical only | Not established |

**Assessment**: The proposed MAO-B inhibitory mechanism is mild in vitro and has not produced any clinically significant drug interactions in published literature. The typical warnings for strong MAO inhibitors (avoid tyramine-containing foods, serotonergic drugs) do not apply to oat straw extract at standard doses based on available evidence.

## Pregnancy and Lactation

### Pregnancy

**Status**: Insufficient data for formal recommendation; traditional food use considered safe at culinary levels

**Evidence basis**:
- EMA HMPC states: "No adverse reactions reported; insufficient data to assess risk"
- Traditional use of oat grain as a dietary staple throughout pregnancy is widespread and not associated with adverse outcomes; this safety record is for food quantities, not concentrated extracts
- No human clinical trials of oat straw herbal extract have been conducted in pregnant populations
- No animal reproductive toxicology studies specific to green oat herb extract have been identified in the published literature
- No traditional use of oat straw herb as an abortifacient or uterotonic is documented (contrasts with some nervine herbs)

**Practical guidance**: Insufficient evidence to formally recommend use during pregnancy; absence of adverse reports is reassuring but does not constitute safety clearance for standardized extracts at clinical doses. Standard precautionary advice applies: consult healthcare provider before using concentrated herbal extracts during pregnancy.

### Lactation

**Status**: No adverse reactions reported; insufficient data for formal recommendation

**Evidence basis**:
- EMA HMPC: no adverse reactions reported; data insufficient for formal risk assessment
- No case reports of adverse effects in nursing infants from maternal oat straw use identified
- Oat grain consumed as food during lactation is standard; transfer of herba extract phytochemicals to breast milk has not been characterized in published human studies

**Practical guidance**: Same precautionary approach as pregnancy; consult healthcare provider.

---

## Hepatotoxicity

**Status**: No hepatotoxicity identified

- No case reports of hepatotoxicity from *Avena sativa* herba extract identified in PubMed or published literature as of 2026-02-27
- No hepatotoxic constituent classes (e.g., pyrrolizidine alkaloids, unsaturated pyrrolizidine alkaloids) are present in the described phytochemical profile
- FDA GRAS status for oats (grain) provides additional indirect safety support
- Contrasts favorably with herbs with known hepatotoxic risk (kava, valerian at very high doses, some pyrrolizidine-containing plants)

---

## Topical Safety (for completeness)

- Colloidal oatmeal and oat straw preparations for topical/bath use have a well-established safety record for skin conditions
- The Commission E (1987) approval for topical oat straw bathing preparations identifies no side effects
- Topical oat preparations are used in eczema, psoriasis, sunburn, and pruritus with good tolerability
- Skin sensitization to oat proteins can occur in oat-allergic individuals — relevant to both topical and oral preparations

## Summary of Safety Profile

**Known safety signals**: None serious in published clinical literature

**Established contraindications** (evidence-based):
1. Celiac disease — risk of gluten cross-contamination in manufacturing (EMA HMPC)
2. Oat allergy — IgE-mediated reactions possible in sensitized individuals

**Precautionary considerations** (insufficient evidence, not established risks):
1. Pregnancy and lactation — insufficient data; standard precautionary guidance applies
2. Antihypertensive drug combinations — theoretical eNOS/vasodilatory additive effect; not clinically documented
3. MAO inhibitor combinations — theoretical; very weak in vitro signal; no clinical cases

**Monitoring**: No specific monitoring parameters established

**Duration limits**: No established maximum treatment duration from clinical evidence; trials have run to 12 weeks without safety signals; chronic use beyond this timeframe has not been evaluated in published RCTs
