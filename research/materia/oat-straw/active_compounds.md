# Oat Straw - Active Compounds

## Overview

The medicinal activity of green oat herb extract (Avenae herba, *Avena sativa* L.) is attributed to three main compound classes: flavone C-glycosides (vitexin, isovitexin), triterpenoid saponins (avenacosides), and phenolic amides (avenanthramides). Vitexin and isovitexin are the primary standardization markers for commercial extracts (Neuravena®). Avenanthramides and avenacosides are also relevant bioactives. Additionally, an indole alkaloid (gramine) and proposed MAO-B/PDE-4 inhibitory activity have been reported but await in vivo mechanistic confirmation at clinical doses.

The oat straw (herba) has a distinct phytochemical profile from oat grain (fructus): beta-glucan (fiber) is a grain-specific component; avenanthramides and avenacosides are present across plant parts but the herba extract is specifically standardized for flavonoids and saponins.

---

## Primary Standardization Markers (Clinical Extracts)

### Neuravena® (ELFA®955)

**Manufacturer**: Frutarom Switzerland Ltd. (now IFF)

**Standardization parameters**:
- **Flavonoids**: Vitexin and isovitexin (C-glycosylflavones) — primary identity/potency markers
- **Triterpene saponins**: Avenacosides A and B — secondary markers

**Proposed mechanisms of standardized extract**:
1. MAO-B (monoamine oxidase B) inhibition — slows degradation of noradrenaline, dopamine, serotonin
2. PDE-4 (phosphodiesterase-4) inhibition — increases neuronal cAMP, supporting alertness and mood
3. Endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) enhancement — vasodilation and cerebrovascular effects

**Note**: Mechanism data comes primarily from in vitro studies and the review literature (Singh et al. 2013); in vivo confirmation of MAO-B and PDE-4 inhibition at human clinical doses has not been published.

### cognitaven®

**Manufacturer**: Gencor / licensed ingredient

**Standardization**: Green oat extract; exact marker compound percentages not published in available clinical literature. Used in Kennedy et al. 2020 (PMID 32485993).

## Constituent Classes

### 1. Flavone C-Glycosides (Vitexin and Isovitexin)

- **Class**: Flavonoids (apigenin C-glycosides)
- **Part of plant**: Aerial parts (herba)
- **Role**: Primary standardization markers for Neuravena®; identity and potency indicators
- **Mechanisms proposed**: Antioxidant, anti-inflammatory; contribution to cognitive effects via MAO/PDE pathways proposed but not directly demonstrated for these specific compounds in human studies
- **Human PK data**: No published human pharmacokinetic studies specifically for vitexin/isovitexin from green oat herb extract identified in the literature as of 2026-02-27
- **Standardization status**: Specified as markers in Neuravena® but exact percentage content not published in accessible literature

---

### 2. Avenacosides A and B (Triterpenoid Saponins)

- **Class**: Steroidal saponins (triterpenoid glycosides)
- **Part of plant**: Green oat aerial parts (herba); also present in grain
- **Role**: Secondary standardization markers for Neuravena®; pharmacologically active constituents
- **Mechanisms proposed**: Oat saponins inhibit angiotensin II-mediated vasoconstriction (Liska et al. 2022, PMID 36432463); contribution to vascular effects of oat herb extract
- **Human PK — avenacosides**:
  - **Source**: Armeni et al. 2025 (PMID 40926245) — note: data from grain/food products, not isolated herba extract
  - Tmax (solid food): 1.1–2.3 hours
  - Half-life (solid food): 3.3–3.8 hours
  - **Limitation**: PK data are from grain (flakes/drink) preparations; PK from encapsulated herba standardized extract not yet confirmed in published literature
- **Standardization status**: Secondary marker in Neuravena®; no published threshold percentages in accessible clinical literature

---

### 3. Avenanthramides (Phenolic Amides)

- **Class**: Polyphenolic amides (N-cinnamoylanthranilic acid derivatives); unique to oats
- **Key compounds**: Avenanthramide A (AV-A), Avenanthramide B (AV-B), and others
- **Part of plant**: Present across oat plant; also present in grain
- **Mechanisms**:
  - Antioxidant: potent free-radical scavenging
  - Anti-inflammatory: inhibit NF-κB signaling
  - Vasodilation: enhance eNOS activity → increased nitric oxide production (Liska et al. 2022, PMID 36432463)
  - Anti-itch / anti-inflammatory topically
- **Relevance to vascular effects**: Avenanthramides are a proposed mechanism for cerebrovascular improvements seen in Wong et al. 2013 (42% CVR, 41% FMD improvement)
- **Human PK** (Armeni et al. 2025, PMID 40926245):
  - Tmax (liquid oat drink): 0.7–1.6 hours
  - Tmax (solid oat flakes): 1.1–2.3 hours
  - Half-life (liquid): 1.3–3.2 hours (compound-dependent)
  - Half-life (solid): 1.4–2.6 hours
  - **AV-A vs AV-B bioavailability**: AV-A is 4-fold more bioavailable than AV-B at avenanthramide-enriched dose
  - **Liquid > solid**: Liquid preparations show higher AUC (area under curve) than solid for most avenanthramide compounds
  - **Repeated dosing**: No accumulation; short elimination half-lives mean trough concentrations are low with standard dosing intervals
- **Standardization**: Not a primary standardization marker for herba extracts (Neuravena® standardizes to flavonoids/saponins), but present as pharmacologically active component

---

### 4. Gramine (Indole Alkaloid)

- **Class**: Indole alkaloid (3-(dimethylaminomethyl)indole)
- **Part of plant**: Aerial parts (herba)
- **Proposed mechanism**: Neurological activity; proposed contribution to nervine/sedative properties
- **Evidence**: Reviewed in Singh et al. 2013 (PMID 23072529) — review article; no direct human clinical data confirming neurological activity of gramine from oat straw at clinical doses
- **Safety note**: Gramine is toxic at high doses in some animal models; at typical herbal preparations doses the quantities present are not considered to pose significant risk, but human PK data are absent
- **Standardization**: Not a standardization marker; content not routinely reported

---

### 5. Beta-Glucan (for completeness — grain-specific)

- **Class**: Polysaccharide fiber
- **Part of plant**: Grain (fructus) — NOT a significant component of oat straw herba extract
- **Relevance**: FDA-approved health claim for cardiovascular/cholesterol outcomes applies to beta-glucan from grain, not to herba extract
- **Note**: Beta-glucan is cited here solely to clarify that it is a grain constituent; it is not the active component in clinical cognitive/vascular RCTs using green oat herb extract

## Pharmacokinetics Summary

**Primary sources**: Chen et al. 2007 (PMID 17513394) — first human PK characterization; Armeni et al. 2025 (PMID 40926245) — comprehensive single/repeated dose study

*Note: All published human PK data are from oat grain/food products (flakes, liquid oat drinks, or isolated avenanthramide-enriched mixtures), not from standardized green oat herb extract capsules. PK of vitexin, isovitexin, and avenacosides from encapsulated herba extract has not been confirmed in published human studies.*

### Chen et al. 2007 — Foundational Avenanthramide Bioavailability Study (PMID 17513394)

**Chen CY, Milbury PE, Collins FW, Blumberg JB. "Avenanthramides are bioavailable and have antioxidant activity in humans after acute consumption of an enriched mixture from oats." J Nutr. 2007;137(6):1375–1382.**

- **Design**: Randomized 3-way crossover; n=6 healthy older adults (3M/3F; mean age 60.8±3.6 yr)
- **Doses**: 0.5 g or 1.0 g avenanthramide-enriched mixture (AEM) with skim milk vs placebo

**Peak plasma concentrations at 1.0 g AEM:**
- AV-A: 374.6 nmol/L
- AV-B: 96.0 nmol/L
- AV-C: 89.0 nmol/L

**Elimination kinetics**: First-order; half-lives 1.75–3.75 hours

**Antioxidant effect (plasma reduced glutathione)**:
- +21% at 15 min post-dose (p≤0.005)
- Still +14% elevated at 10 hours (p≤0.05)

*First human pharmacokinetic characterization of individual avenanthramide compounds; uses isolated AEM rather than whole oat, but directly relevant to oat straw pharmacokinetics.*

---

### Armeni et al. 2025 — Comprehensive PK Study (PMID 40926245)

**Armeni M, et al. "Avenanthramides and avenacosides as biomarkers of oat intake: a pharmacokinetic study." Nutr J. 2025;24(1):136.**

- **Design**: Non-blinded randomized crossover; n=21 healthy adults; single and repeated doses

| Compound | Product Form | Tmax | Half-Life | Relative Bioavailability |
|----------|-------------|------|-----------|-------------------------|
| Avenanthramide A (AV-A) | Liquid oat drink | 0.7–1.6 hr | 1.3–3.2 hr | 4× higher than AV-B |
| Avenanthramide A (AV-A) | Solid oat flakes | 1.1–2.3 hr | 1.4–2.6 hr | Lower than liquid form |
| Avenanthramide B (AV-B) | Liquid | Similar to AV-A | Similar | 4× lower than AV-A |
| Avenacosides | Solid | 1.1–2.3 hr | 3.3–3.8 hr | Not compared to liquid |

**Key PK implications**:
- Short half-lives (consistent across both studies: ~1.75–3.75 hr) indicate transient exposure; largely cleared within 6–8 hours of dosing
- Liquid formulations show meaningfully higher bioavailability than solid/capsule forms for avenanthramides
- AV-A is the dominant bioavailable avenanthramide compound — 4-fold higher than AV-B — relevant for supplement standardization choices
- Antioxidant effects (glutathione elevation) persist beyond plasma avenanthramide clearance (still significant at 10 hr post-dose)

## Standardization Status Summary

| Compound | Neuravena® | cognitaven® | Published Threshold |
|----------|-----------|------------|---------------------|
| Vitexin | Marker | Not specified | Not published |
| Isovitexin | Marker | Not specified | Not published |
| Avenacosides A and B | Secondary marker | Not specified | Not published |
| Avenanthramides | Not primary marker | Not specified | Not published |
| Gramine | Not standardized | Not standardized | Not applicable |

**Critical note**: Neither Neuravena® nor cognitaven® publishes specific percentage content thresholds for marker compounds in the accessible academic literature. Identity/standardization is claimed by manufacturers but exact content per capsule for each RCT is not disclosed in study methods. This limits dose-response analysis at the phytochemical level.

## Proposed Mechanism of Action Summary

| Mechanism | Compounds Implicated | Evidence Level |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------|
| MAO-B inhibition (slows monoamine degradation) | Vitexin/isovitexin, potentially gramine | In vitro / review; not confirmed in vivo in humans |
| PDE-4 inhibition (increases neuronal cAMP) | Unspecified flavonoids | In vitro; not confirmed in vivo in humans |
| eNOS enhancement → vasodilation | Avenanthramides | In vitro; consistent with CVR/FMD clinical outcomes |
| Angiotensin II vasoconstriction inhibition | Avenacosides | Preclinical; consistent with vascular clinical data |

*Source for mechanism overview: Singh et al. 2013 (PMID 23072529) review; Liska et al. 2022 (PMID 36432463)*
