# Elderberry (Sambucus nigra) - Safety Profile

## General Safety Assessment

**Overall safety rating:** Generally safe for short-term use in non-pregnant adults

**Clinical trial safety data:**
- No serious adverse events reported across reviewed trials (Wieland 2021, Hawkins 2019)
- Adverse events rare and typically mild
- Events occur less frequently than placebo groups in some studies
- Multiple RCTs with 60-936 participants show consistent safety profile

## Documented Adverse Events

### Common Adverse Events (Macknin 2020, 87 participants)

**Elderberry group vs placebo:**
- Dry mouth: 5.7% (no significant difference between groups)
- Constipation: 4.6%
- Rash: 4.6%
- Bad taste: 3.4%

**Statistical significance:** No significant differences between elderberry and placebo groups

**Severity:** All adverse events mild, none serious

### Systematic Review Safety Findings (Wieland 2021)

**Sample:** 5 studies, 1,187 records screened

**Key findings:**
- No evidence that elderberry overstimulates the immune system
- No studies directly linked elderberry to clinical inflammatory outcomes
- Some effect on inflammatory markers observed
- This inflammatory marker effect may decline with ongoing supplementation

**Conclusion:** Elderberry appears safe, with no evidence of harmful immune overstimulation

## Toxicology Concerns

### Cyanogenic Glycosides

**Source of toxicity:**
- Compound: Sambunigrin (cyanogenic glycoside)
- Location: Bark, root, and leaves
- Mechanism: Potential cyanide release upon ingestion

**Plant parts to AVOID:**
1. **Raw bark** - Contains sambunigrin
2. **Raw roots** - Contains sambunigrin
3. **Raw leaves** - Contains sambunigrin
4. **Unripe berries** - Contain toxic constituents including cyanogenic glycosides

**Risk:** Cyanide poisoning
- CNS depression possible
- Use cautiously in patients with pre-existing CNS disorders

**Safe preparations:**
- **Cooked berries** - DO NOT carry cyanide poisoning risk
- Heat processing destroys cyanogenic glycosides
- All clinical trials used processed/cooked berry preparations

**EMA 2014 safety note:**
"Unripe berries contain toxic constituents such as cyanogenic glycosides and should be avoided"

### Allergic Reactions

**Frequency:** Rare

**Documentation:**
"Elderberry preparations have rarely caused allergic reactions" (NIH LactMed Database)

**Type of reactions:** Not specified in bibliography

**Clinical significance:** Low risk, but possible hypersensitivity in susceptible individuals

## Contraindications

### Pregnancy

**Safety data:** NONE AVAILABLE

**Official recommendations:**

**EMA position:**
"Does not recommend use during pregnancy due to lack of information"

**Literature review findings:**
- No animal studies of safety in pregnancy identified
- No human studies of safety in pregnancy identified
- Health care personnel should NOT advise pregnant women to use elderberry against upper respiratory tract infections

**Recommendation:** **AVOID in pregnancy** (insufficient safety data)

### Lactation

**Safety data:** NONE AVAILABLE

**Specific concerns:**
1. No data exist on excretion of elderberry components into breastmilk
2. No data on safety and efficacy in nursing mothers
3. No data on safety in nursing infants

**EMA position:**
"Does not recommend use during lactation due to lack of information"

**NIH LactMed Database conclusion:**
"No recommendations can be made on use of medicinal doses during breastfeeding"

**Recommendation:** **AVOID during lactation** (insufficient safety data)

**Note:** This is a **precautionary** contraindication due to lack of data, NOT due to evidence of harm

### Central Nervous System Disorders

**Rationale:** Potential CNS depression from cyanide poisoning (if raw plant parts consumed)

**Recommendation:** Use cautiously in patients with CNS disorders

**Note:** This applies primarily to improper preparations (raw plant parts), not properly processed berry extracts

## Drug Interactions

### Diabetes Medications - Minor/Unknown Significance

**Risk:** May increase risk of hypoglycemia

**Mechanism:** Based on in vitro research (not confirmed clinically in humans)

**Affected medications:**
1. Insulin (all formulations)
2. Metformin
3. Sulfonylureas (glyburide, glipizide, glimepiride, etc.)
4. Other glucose-lowering agents

**Clinical significance:** Minor/significance unknown

**Recommendation:**
- Monitor blood glucose more frequently if using elderberry with diabetes medications
- Watch for signs of hypoglycemia (shakiness, sweating, confusion, rapid heartbeat)
- Consider dose adjustment of diabetes medications if needed

**Evidence level:** Based on in vitro data, not confirmed in human studies

### Other Drug Interactions

**Severity classification:**
- No known SEVERE interactions
- No known SERIOUS interactions
- No known MODERATE interactions
- Mild interactions with at least 28 different drugs

**Source:** Medscape Drug Interaction Database

**Clinical significance:** Low risk for most drug interactions

**Note:** The 28 mild interactions not specifically detailed in bibliography

## Special Populations

### Children

**Clinical trial data:**
- Macknin 2020 included children ages 5-12
- Dose: 15 ml twice daily for 5 days
- Safety: No significant adverse events in pediatric subgroup

**Age considerations:**
- Clinical data available for ages 5+
- Safety in children <5 years: NOT established
- No serious adverse events in 5-12 age group (Macknin 2020)

**Toxicity risk:**
Raw/unripe berry ingestion more dangerous in children due to lower body weight (cyanide poisoning risk)

### Elderly

**Clinical trial data:**
- Wu 2002: 10 elderly women (bioavailability study)
- No age-specific safety concerns identified

**Consideration:** Elderly may be on multiple medications, increasing potential for drug interactions (especially diabetes medications)

### Patients with Diabetes

**Risk:** Possible hypoglycemia when combined with diabetes medications

**Mechanism:** In vitro research suggests glucose-lowering effects

**Monitoring:**
- Check blood glucose more frequently
- Watch for hypoglycemic symptoms
- Adjust diabetes medication doses if needed

**Evidence level:** Theoretical risk based on in vitro data

## Long-Term Safety

**Data available:** **INSUFFICIENT**

**Clinical trial durations:**
- Most trials: 5 days treatment
- Longest trial: 16 days (Tiralongo 2016 - prevention protocol)

**Research gap:**
No data on safety beyond ~2-3 weeks of continuous use

**Implication:**
- Short-term use (5-16 days) appears safe
- Long-term safety (months to years) unknown
- Chronic daily use not supported by safety data

## Dosage-Related Safety

**Clinical trial dose range:**
- Syrup: 15 ml 2x/day (children) to 15 ml 4x/day (adults)
- Capsules: 600-900 mg/day standardized extract
- Anthocyanins: 90-135 mg/day in successful trials

**High-dose considerations:**
- Vlachojannis 2015 mentions 3.5 g anthocyanins/day for influenza
- 26-39x higher than doses used in most trials (90-135 mg)
- Safety at high doses (>1 g anthocyanins/day) not well established

**Bioavailability paradox:**
- Very low absorption (<0.5%)
- May limit potential for systemic toxicity
- Local GI effects possible at high doses

## Quality and Contamination Concerns

**Product variability (Vlachojannis 2015):**
- Three products deemed inappropriate for clinical use
- Insufficient anthocyanin content
- Significant variability among commercial products

**Implications for safety:**
1. **Adulteration risk:** Variable quality suggests poor manufacturing controls
2. **Contamination potential:** Products not meeting specifications may have other quality issues
3. **Dosing errors:** Variable content makes consistent dosing difficult

**Recommendation:**
Use products with:
- HPLC-verified anthocyanin content
- Good manufacturing practices (GMP) certification
- Third-party testing verification

## Immune System Considerations

**Concern (theoretical):** Overstimulation of immune system

**Evidence:**
- Wieland 2021: No evidence of harmful immune overstimulation
- No studies directly linked elderberry to clinical inflammatory outcomes
- Some effect on inflammatory markers, but declines with ongoing supplementation

**Autoimmune conditions:**
- No specific contraindication identified
- Theoretical concern about immune stimulation in autoimmune diseases
- No clinical evidence of harm

**Immunosuppressed patients:**
- No specific safety data available
- No documented concerns in literature

## Comparison to Oseltamivir (Tamiflu)

**Adverse event profile (Wieland 2021):**
- Elderberry-containing product associated with LOWER risk of adverse events compared to oseltamivir
- Elderberry associated with lower risk of influenza complications vs oseltamivir

**Implication:** Elderberry may have better tolerability than standard pharmaceutical influenza treatment

**Note:** Direct head-to-head comparison limited; this finding from systematic review synthesis

## Reporting and Post-Market Surveillance

**Rare adverse events:** Elderberry preparations have rarely caused allergic reactions

**Post-market data:** Not systematically reviewed in bibliography

**Recommendation:**
Report adverse events to:
- FDA MedWatch (United States)
- National reporting systems (country-specific)
- Product manufacturer

## Safety Summary by Risk Category

### HIGH RISK - AVOID:
1. **Pregnancy** (no safety data)
2. **Lactation** (no safety data)
3. **Raw plant parts** (bark, roots, leaves, unripe berries - cyanide poisoning risk)
4. **Known allergy** to Sambucus species

### MODERATE RISK - USE WITH CAUTION:
1. **Diabetes medications** (possible hypoglycemia - monitor glucose)
2. **CNS disorders** (if using improperly processed products)
3. **Children <5 years** (no safety data)
4. **Long-term use >2-3 weeks** (no safety data)

### LOW RISK - GENERALLY SAFE:
1. **Short-term use** (5-16 days) in healthy adults
2. **Children 5-12 years** (15 ml twice daily studied in Macknin 2020)
3. **Elderly** (no specific concerns identified)
4. **Properly processed berry preparations** (cooked/extracted products)

## Clinical Monitoring Recommendations

**For patients using elderberry:**

1. **Baseline assessment:**
   - Current medications (especially diabetes drugs)
   - Pregnancy/lactation status
   - History of allergies to plants

2. **During use:**
   - Monitor for allergic reactions (rash, itching, difficulty breathing)
   - If diabetic: Check blood glucose more frequently
   - Monitor for GI side effects (constipation, dry mouth)

3. **Duration:**
   - Limit use to 5-16 days (studied duration)
   - Discontinue if no improvement after 5 days
   - Do not use as long-term preventive without additional safety data

## Bottom Line Safety Assessment

**Evidence-based safety rating:**
- Short-term use in non-pregnant, non-lactating adults: **SAFE**
- Pregnancy/lactation: **INSUFFICIENT DATA - AVOID**
- Children 5+: **APPEARS SAFE** (limited data)
- Children <5: **INSUFFICIENT DATA**
- Long-term use: **INSUFFICIENT DATA**

**Key safety concerns:**
1. Lack of pregnancy/lactation data (contraindication)
2. Cyanogenic glycosides in raw plant parts (easily avoided)
3. Theoretical hypoglycemia risk with diabetes medications (monitor)
4. Product quality variability (choose verified products)

**Comparison to alternatives:**
- Safer adverse event profile than oseltamivir (Tamiflu)
- No serious adverse events in clinical trials
- Generally well-tolerated
