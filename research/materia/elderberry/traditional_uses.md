# Elderberry (Sambucus nigra) - Traditional Uses

## Official Traditional Use Recognition

### World Health Organization (WHO) - 2002

**Plant part:** Elderflower (Flos)
**Document:** WHO Monographs on Selected Medicinal Plants, Volume 2, pp. 269-275

**Approved traditional uses:**
1. Diaphoretic (sweat-inducing) for treatment of fever and chills
2. Expectorant for treatment of mild inflammation of upper respiratory tract
3. Symptomatic treatment of common cold

**WHO statement:**
"Although no controlled clinical data available, descriptions in pharmacopoeias and traditional systems of medicine justify use"

**Source:** WHO Monographs on Selected Medicinal Plants Vol. 2

### German Commission E

**Plant part:** Elderflower (Sambuci flos)
**Document:** Complete German Commission E Monographs

**Approved use:**
- Colds

**Status:** Official approval for medicinal use in Germany

### European Medicines Agency (EMA) - 2008

**Plant part:** Elderflower (Flos)
**Document:** Community Herbal Monograph on Sambucus nigra flos

**Traditional use classification:**
"Herbal medicinal product traditionally used for relief of early symptoms of common cold"

**Quality standard:**
Defined in European Pharmacopoeia (Ph. Eur.)

**Regulatory status:** Approved for traditional use in EU member states

### European Medicines Agency (EMA) - 2014

**Plant part:** Elderberry fruit (Fructus)
**Document:** Assessment Report on Sambucus nigra L., fructus

**Critical finding:**
"Community herbal monograph on Sambucus nigra L., fructus could NOT be established due to insufficient data on traditional use with specified posology and strength"

**Implication:**
Unlike elderflower, elderberry FRUIT lacks sufficient documented traditional use data to establish official monograph with specific dosing recommendations.

**Safety note:**
Unripe berries contain toxic constituents such as cyanogenic glycosides and should be avoided.

## Traditional Use Patterns

### Plant Parts with Traditional Recognition

**Elderflower (Flos) - WELL DOCUMENTED:**
- Officially recognized by WHO, German Commission E, and EMA
- Long history of use in European traditional medicine
- Documented in multiple pharmacopoeias
- Traditional preparations: Teas, infusions
- Traditional indications: Fever, sweating therapy, upper respiratory inflammation, early cold symptoms

**Elderberry Fruit (Fructus) - INSUFFICIENT DOCUMENTATION:**
- Could NOT establish EMA traditional use monograph
- Insufficient historical data on:
  - Specific posology (dosing)
  - Standardized strength
  - Consistent traditional preparation methods
- Modern clinical use differs from historical patterns

### Historical Context

**European Traditional Medicine:**
The elderflower has documented use in European folk medicine traditions, evidenced by:
- Inclusion in multiple European pharmacopoeias
- Recognition across multiple European regulatory bodies
- Consistent preparation methods (primarily as diaphoretic/expectorant)

**Century/Era:**
Specific centuries not documented in bibliography, but inclusion in pharmacopoeias suggests multi-century use (pharmacopoeial inclusion typically requires generations of documented traditional use).

### Traditional Preparations

**Elderflower (historically documented):**
- Tea/infusion preparations
- Used as diaphoretic (sweat therapy)
- Expectorant preparations
- Symptomatic treatment formulations

**Elderberry fruit (modern, NOT traditionally documented):**
- Modern standardized extracts (15-22% anthocyanins)
- Syrups
- Capsules
- Juice concentrates

Note: The modern use of elderberry fruit in standardized extracts represents a DEPARTURE from historical traditional use patterns, which focused primarily on elderflower preparations.

## Traditional vs. Modern Use: Critical Distinction

### Traditional Focus: ELDERFLOWER
- Well-documented traditional use
- Official monographs established
- Clear traditional indications (diaphoretic, expectorant)
- Recognized by multiple regulatory authorities
- Consistent preparation methods

### Modern Focus: ELDERBERRY FRUIT
- Clinical research focuses on fruit extracts
- Standardized anthocyanin content (modern innovation)
- Lacks traditional use monograph (per EMA 2014)
- Modern preparations differ significantly from historical use
- Insufficient traditional documentation for specific dosing

This represents a **fundamental disconnect** between:
1. What is traditionally recognized (elderflower)
2. What modern clinical research studies (elderberry fruit)

## Traditional Medical Systems

**Note:** The bibliography does NOT document use in specific traditional medical systems such as:
- Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)
- Ayurveda
- Native American medicine
- Other indigenous traditions

The documented traditional use is primarily **European folk medicine** as evidenced by:
- WHO recognition (based on pharmacopoeial descriptions)
- German Commission E approval
- European Pharmacopoeia inclusion
- EMA regulatory assessment

## Traditional Preparation Methods

### Elderflower (from traditional use)

**Diaphoretic preparation (fever/sweating):**
- Form: Hot infusion/tea
- Traditional use: Consumed hot to induce sweating
- Indication: Fever, chills

**Expectorant preparation:**
- Form: Infusion/decoction
- Traditional use: Upper respiratory inflammation
- Indication: Productive cough, respiratory mucus

### Modern Elderberry (NOT traditional)

Modern preparations represent pharmaceutical innovation rather than traditional use:
- Standardized extracts (15-22% anthocyanins)
- Specific anthocyanin content (90-135 mg/dose)
- Syrup formulations with defined volumes
- Encapsulated powders

These modern preparations have **no documented traditional precedent** sufficient for EMA monograph establishment.

## Toxicity Concerns in Traditional Use

**Traditional warnings (still relevant):**
1. **Raw plant parts:** Bark, root, and leaves contain cyanogenic glycoside sambunigrin - potentially toxic
2. **Unripe berries:** Contain toxic constituents including cyanogenic glycosides
3. **Traditional preparation requirement:** Cooking/processing removes toxins

**Traditional wisdom validated:**
The traditional practice of cooking elderberries before use aligns with modern understanding that:
- Cooked berries do NOT carry cyanide poisoning risk
- Raw/unripe berries should be avoided
- Processing is necessary for safety

## Traditional Use Summary

**What is well-documented:**
- Elderflower use for fever, sweating therapy, respiratory symptoms
- European traditional medicine recognition
- Multiple official pharmacopoeial monographs
- Consistent preparation as hot infusions/teas

**What is NOT well-documented:**
- Elderberry FRUIT traditional use (insufficient per EMA 2014)
- Specific traditional dosing for fruit preparations
- Use in non-European traditional medical systems (TCM, Ayurveda, etc.)
- Traditional preparation methods for fruit (modern methods are pharmaceutical innovations)

**Critical insight:**
Modern clinical research on elderberry fruit represents a **departure from traditional use**, not a continuation of it. The traditional evidence base supports elderflower, not elderberry fruit in standardized extracts.

## Regulatory Implications

**Elderflower can be marketed with traditional use claims:**
- "Traditionally used for relief of early cold symptoms" (EMA approved)
- "For symptomatic treatment of common cold" (WHO recognized)
- "For colds" (German Commission E approved)

**Elderberry fruit CANNOT establish traditional use claims:**
- No EMA traditional use monograph possible
- Insufficient traditional documentation
- Modern preparations lack traditional precedent
- Must rely on clinical research data, not traditional use status

This regulatory distinction is **critical** for understanding the evidence base and appropriate marketing claims.
