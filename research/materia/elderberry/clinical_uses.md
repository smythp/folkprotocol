# Elderberry (Sambucus nigra) - Clinical Uses

## Evidence Overview

Evidence quality is **mixed** with conflicting RCT results. Systematic reviews note uncertain evidence quality, with most recent 2020 RCT contradicting earlier positive studies from 1995, 2004, and 2016.

## Primary Indication: Upper Respiratory Infections

### Common Cold

**Meta-analysis findings (Hawkins 2019):**
- Sample: 180 participants (89 elderberry, 91 control)
- Effect size: Large (1.717)
- Outcome: Substantial reduction in upper respiratory symptoms

**Air traveler study (Tiralongo 2016):**
- Sample: 312 air travelers (158 elderberry, 154 placebo)
- Dosing: 600-900 mg daily standardized extract (15% anthocyanins = 90-135 mg/day)
- Duration: 15-16 days total (10 days pre-travel + travel period)
- Results:
  - Cold episode days: 57 vs 117 (p=0.02)
  - Mean duration: 4.75 days vs 6.88 days (~2 days reduction)
  - Average symptom score: 247 vs 583 (p=0.05)
  - Symptom severity: 21 vs 34 mean score

**Rapid review findings (Harnett 2020):**
- Sample: 5 studies, 936 adults
- Timing: Most effective when taken within 48 hours of symptom onset
- Outcome: 50% symptom reduction within 2-4 days
- Effective for: Fever, headache, nasal congestion, nasal discharge
- Unclear for: Cough relief, antibiotic use reduction

### Influenza

**Positive RCT (Zakay-Rones 2004):**
- Sample: 60 patients, ages 18-54
- Location: Norway, 1999-2000 flu season
- Intervention: 15 ml elderberry syrup 4x/day for 5 days
- Outcome: Symptoms relieved 4 days earlier than placebo
- Secondary: Significantly less rescue medication use

**Early positive RCT (Zakay-Rones 1995):**
- Setting: Influenza B/Panama outbreak in kibbutz
- Results:
  - 93.3% showed significant improvement within 2 days (including fever resolution)
  - 91.7% of placebo group required 6 days for improvement (p<0.001)
  - ~90% complete recovery within 2-3 days vs ≥6 days for placebo

**Negative RCT (Macknin 2020) - CONTRADICTS earlier studies:**
- Sample: 87 patients (43 elderberry, 44 placebo), ages 5+
- Intervention:
  - Children 5-12: 15 ml 2x/day for 5 days
  - Adults 13+: 15 ml 4x/day for 5 days
- Primary outcome:
  - Time to symptom alleviation: 5.3±3.6 days (elderberry) vs 4.9±2.8 days (placebo), p=0.57
  - NO significant difference
- Secondary outcome:
  - Time to complete resolution: 8.6±3.9 days vs 8.7±3.9 days, p=0.87
- Post-hoc analysis:
  - Elderberry alone: 7.3±3.4 days
  - Placebo alone: 5.1±3.4 days (p=0.02)
  - Elderberry significantly WORSE when used alone
- Conclusion: No evidence of benefit for influenza duration or severity

**Comparative efficacy (Wieland 2021 systematic review):**
- Elderberry vs oseltamivir (Tamiflu): Elderberry-containing product showed lower risk of influenza complications and adverse events
- However, overall evidence quality remains uncertain

## Clinical Dosing Regimens

### For Active Infection (Treatment)

**Elderberry syrup:**
- Adults: 15 ml 4x/day for 5 days (Zakay-Rones 2004, Macknin 2020)
- Children 5-12: 15 ml 2x/day for 5 days (Macknin 2020)
- Note: Recent Macknin 2020 study found NO benefit with this dosing

**Standardized capsules (prevention/treatment):**
- Treatment dose: 900 mg/day (Tiralongo 2016)
- Standardization: 15-22% anthocyanins = 90-135 mg anthocyanins/day
- Timing: Most effective within 48 hours of symptom onset

### For Prevention

**Pre-exposure prophylaxis (air travelers):**
- Priming phase: 600 mg/day for 10 days before travel
- Travel phase: 900 mg/day during travel and 4-5 days post-arrival
- Total duration: 15-16 days
- Standardization: 15% anthocyanins (90-135 mg/day)
- Results: 2-day reduction in cold duration, 50% reduction in symptom severity

### Anthocyanin Content Requirements

**Minimum effective doses (Vlachojannis 2015):**
- Metabolic syndrome applications: 110 mg anthocyanins/day
- Influenza treatment: 3.5 g anthocyanins/day
- Note: Most positive clinical trials used products standardized to 15-22% anthocyanins

**Product equivalencies:**
- Elderberry concentrate: 11 ml/day sufficient (high concentration)
- Chokeberry juice: 100 ml/day required (lower concentration)

## Conflicting Evidence: Critical Assessment

### Studies showing benefit (1995, 2004, 2016):
- Consistent ~2-4 day reduction in symptom duration
- 50% symptom severity reduction
- Most effective within 48 hours of onset
- Sample sizes: 60-312 participants

### Study showing NO benefit (2020):
- 87 participants
- Same dosing as earlier positive studies
- Post-hoc analysis suggested elderberry alone performed WORSE than placebo
- Authors note conflict with earlier studies

### Systematic review conclusions (Wieland 2021):
- May reduce cold duration/severity but evidence uncertain
- May reduce influenza duration but evidence uncertain
- May not reduce risk of developing colds
- Stronger evidence from recent studies needed

## Safety in Clinical Use

**Adverse events (Macknin 2020):**
- Dry mouth: 5.7%
- Constipation: 4.6%
- Rash: 4.6%
- Bad taste: 3.4%
- No significant differences between elderberry and placebo groups
- No serious adverse events

**General safety profile:**
- Generally safe for short-term use in adults
- Adverse events rare and typically mild
- No serious adverse events reported across reviewed trials
- Events occur less frequently than placebo in some studies

## Clinical Bottom Line

**When evidence suggests benefit:**
- Initiate within 48 hours of symptom onset
- Use standardized products (15-22% anthocyanins, 90-135 mg/day)
- Expected outcome: 2-4 day reduction in duration, 50% symptom reduction
- Duration: 5 days treatment course

**Evidence limitations:**
- Conflicting RCT results (2020 study contradicts earlier findings)
- Small sample sizes (60-312 participants)
- Uncertain evidence quality per systematic reviews
- Product standardization highly variable
- Optimal dosing not definitively established

**Recommendation strength:**
- Weak/uncertain - may be tried as safe alternative for routine upper respiratory infections
- NOT recommended as replacement for standard influenza treatment in high-risk patients
- Further high-quality RCTs needed to resolve conflicting evidence
