# Beetroot - Traditional Uses

## Egyptian Herbal Medicine

### Egyptian Herbal Monograph Volume 3

**Source**: Egyptian Drug Authority (EDA)
**Reference**: https://edaegypt.gov.eg/media/nvkjhbnp/beta-vulgaris-l-%D8%A8%D9%86%D8%AC%D8%B1.pdf

**Botanical Classification**:
- **Scientific name**: Beta vulgaris L.
- **Family**: Amaranthaceae
- **Common names**: Beet, Beetroot, Common beet, Garden beet

**Traditional Uses Documented**:
- **Supportive therapy in diseases of the liver**
- **Supportive therapy in fatty liver**

**Note**: PDF extraction was limited, but this represents formal Egyptian pharmacopoeia recognition for hepatic conditions.

- **Status**: NOT included
- **Note**: Not included in Commission E monographs, which reviewed over 300 herbal drugs

### Overall Regulatory Status
**Primary classification**: Food and nutritional supplement rather than traditional medicinal herb in formal herbal medicine systems

**Interpretation**: While beetroot has documented traditional use in Egyptian medicine for liver support, it has not achieved widespread recognition in Western or international herbal pharmacopoeias. This reflects its primary status as a culinary vegetable with nutritional/supplemental properties rather than a dedicated medicinal herb.

## Historical Context

### Traditional Medicine Systems

**Note on absence from major systems**:
- **Ayurveda**: No documented traditional use
- **Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)**: No documented traditional use
- **European traditional medicine**: Limited documentation beyond Egyptian sources

**Likely reasons for limited traditional medicinal use**:
1. **Primary culinary status**: Beetroot has been predominantly cultivated and consumed as a food vegetable throughout history
2. **Recent research focus**: Modern scientific interest in beetroot's cardiovascular and athletic performance benefits is relatively new (primarily 21st century)
3. **Geographical distribution**: Beta vulgaris originated in the Mediterranean region and was primarily used as food rather than medicine

---

## Modern vs. Traditional Use Distinction

### Traditional Applications
- Egyptian medicine: Liver and fatty liver support
- Folk use: Primarily nutritional/culinary

### Modern Clinical Applications (Not Traditional)
- Blood pressure reduction (based on nitrate research since ~2010s)
- Athletic performance enhancement (based on nitric oxide pathway research)
- Cardiovascular health (recent evidence-based application)

**Important distinction**: The cardiovascular and athletic performance applications of beetroot are **modern, evidence-based uses** derived from scientific research on nitrate metabolism, NOT traditional applications passed down through historical medical systems.

## Traditional Preparations

### Egyptian Traditional Use
- **Form**: Not specified in available monograph excerpts
- **Preparations**: Likely whole beetroot or juice based on traditional Egyptian food practices
- **Indications**: Liver support, fatty liver

### Folk/Culinary Preparations (Historical)
- **Whole cooked beetroot**: Traditional food preparation
- **Beetroot juice**: Folk remedy and nutritional beverage
- **Fermented beetroot**: Eastern European traditional preparation (kvass)
- **Dried beetroot**: Storage and preservation method

**Note**: Specific traditional dosing, ratios, or preparation methods are not documented in the reviewed literature or monographs. Modern clinical use relies on standardized extracts and juices rather than traditional preparation methods.

---

## Summary

**Traditional use documentation**: MINIMAL
- Only formal documentation: Egyptian Herbal Monograph for liver/fatty liver support
- No inclusion in WHO, Commission E, or other major international herbal pharmacopoeias
- No documented use in Ayurveda, TCM, or European traditional herbal medicine systems

**Modern use basis**: EVIDENCE-BASED RESEARCH
- Current clinical applications derive from 21st-century research on nitrate metabolism and nitric oxide pathways
- Beetroot's primary historical role has been as a nutritional food vegetable, not a medicinal herb

**Gap between traditional and modern use**:
The modern clinical applications (cardiovascular health, athletic performance) are NOT based on traditional knowledge but on recent scientific discoveries about dietary nitrate and its conversion to nitric oxide. This distinguishes beetroot from herbs with long-standing traditional medicinal applications later validated by research.
