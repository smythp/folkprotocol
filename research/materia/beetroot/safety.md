# Beetroot - Safety Profile

## Adverse Events from Clinical Trials

### Serious Adverse Events

**Pinheiro et al., 2024** - 12-week RCT, older adults, 20g/day standardized extract
- **Serious adverse events**: **NONE reported**
- **Duration**: 12 weeks
- **Population**: Older participants
- **Conclusion**: Safe and well tolerated in vulnerable population

**General finding across all reviewed studies**:
- No serious adverse events reported in any clinical trial
- Studies ranged from acute (single dose) to chronic (13 weeks)
- Populations included: healthy adults, older adults, athletes, hypertensive patients, postmenopausal women

---

### Minor Adverse Events

**Beeturia (Benign)**:
- **Description**: Reddish urine and/or stool coloring
- **Cause**: Betalain pigments (betanin) passing through digestive system
- **Incidence**: Common with beetroot consumption
- **Clinical significance**: **None - completely benign**
- **Reference**: Pinheiro et al., 2024

**Other minor events**:
- Flu-like symptoms reported in some studies, but determined to be **unrelated** to beetroot consumption
- No consistent pattern of minor adverse events across trials

---

## Physiological Changes (Non-Adverse)

### Metabolic Effects

**Pinheiro et al., 2024** - 12 weeks, 20g/day extract
- **Plasma nitrate**: Notable elevation (p = 0.076, f = 0.50) - expected and therapeutic
- **Insulin levels**: Statistically significant decrease (p = 0.026, f = 0.59) - potentially beneficial
- **HOMA-IR and HOMA-β**: No significant alterations
- **Anthropometric measures**: No statistically significant changes
- **Biochemical parameters**: No significant changes
- **Hemodynamic parameters**: No significant changes (aside from intended BP reduction)

**Interpretation**: Changes observed are either therapeutic (nitrate elevation, insulin reduction) or absent, with no concerning alterations.

---

## Safety Concerns from Systematic Reviews

### Nitrate Intake and N-Nitroso Compounds (NOCs)

**Zamani et al., 2021** - Systematic review of benefits and risks
- **Concern**: May easily increase nitrate intake above acceptable daily intake (ADI)
- **Mechanism**: Stimulates endogenous formation of N-nitroso compounds (NOCs)
- **Risk**: NOCs are carcinogenic and may cause adverse effects
- **Evidence limitation**: Limited literature on negative effects compared to benefits
- **Context**: Risk appears minimal with vegetable-source nitrate vs. processed meat nitrate

**Acceptable Daily Intake (ADI)**:
- WHO ADI for nitrate: 3.7 mg/kg body weight/day (~222 mg for 60 kg person)
- Clinical dosing: 200-800 mg/day (may exceed ADI)
- **Important distinction**: Vegetable-source nitrate (with vitamin C, polyphenols) may not carry same cancer risk as processed meat nitrate

**Overall assessment**: Theoretical concern, but no adverse events observed in clinical trials. Vegetable nitrate sources contain antioxidants that may inhibit NOC formation.

---

### Breath-Hold Diving/Swimming Safety

**Specific population concern** (not general safety issue):
- **Issue**: Beetroot-induced vasodilation may counteract normal cardiovascular responses during breath-hold diving
- **Population affected**: Breath-hold athletes, military training involving underwater breath-holding
- **Reference**: PMID 38997903
- **Recommendation**: Caution in breath-hold athletes and military personnel engaged in underwater training

**Not applicable to**: General population, athletes in other sports, recreational swimmers

---

## Contraindications

### Documented Contraindications

**Based on reviewed literature**: No absolute contraindications identified in clinical trials

**Theoretical contraindications** (require clinical judgment):
1. **Kidney stones (calcium oxalate)**: Beetroot contains oxalates, may contribute to stone formation in susceptible individuals (not studied in reviewed literature)
2. **Low blood pressure (hypotension)**: Given documented BP-lowering effects, may exacerbate hypotension
3. **Breath-hold diving/swimming**: As noted above

---

## Drug Interactions

### Blood Pressure Medications

**Theoretical interaction** (not documented in trials):
- Beetroot lowers blood pressure by 3.55-5.31 mmHg systolic
- **Potential additive effect** with antihypertensive medications
- **Clinical consideration**: Monitor blood pressure when combining; may allow medication reduction under medical supervision

**Not contraindicated**, but requires monitoring.

### Nitrate Medications (e.g., Nitroglycerin)

**Theoretical interaction** (not studied):
- Beetroot provides dietary nitrate → converted to nitrite and nitric oxide
- Nitrate medications (nitroglycerin, isosorbide) → also metabolized to nitric oxide
- **Potential additive vasodilation**
- **Clinical consideration**: Theoretical risk of excessive vasodilation, orthostatic hypotension

**Recommendation**: Consult healthcare provider before combining.

### Caffeine (Antagonistic Interaction)

**Domínguez et al., 2017**:
- Combining beetroot juice with caffeine may **undermine ergogenic benefits**
- **Not a safety concern**, but reduces athletic performance benefits
- **Mechanism**: Opposing vascular effects

### Antibacterial Mouthwash

**General principle** (not specific to safety):
- Antibacterial mouthwash kills oral bacteria needed for NO3- → NO2- conversion
- **May impair therapeutic effects** of beetroot
- **Not a safety issue**, but reduces efficacy

---

## Pregnancy and Lactation

### Safety Data

**Reviewed literature**: No studies specifically addressing pregnancy or lactation safety

**General considerations**:
1. **Food vs. supplement**: Beetroot as a food vegetable has been consumed safely throughout history, including by pregnant women
2. **High-dose supplementation**: Safety of concentrated extracts or high-dose beetroot juice (200-800 mg nitrate/day) in pregnancy not established
3. **Nitrate concerns**: High nitrate intake during pregnancy theoretically could affect fetal development, but vegetable-source nitrate not associated with adverse outcomes in epidemiological studies

**Recommendation**:
- **Food amounts**: Likely safe (normal dietary consumption)
- **Supplement doses**: Insufficient data - consult healthcare provider
- **Lactation**: No data available

**Status**: Insufficient evidence for high-dose supplementation in pregnancy/lactation

---

## Special Populations

### Older Adults

**Pinheiro et al., 2024** - 12 weeks, 20g/day
- **Safety**: Well tolerated, no serious adverse events
- **Efficacy**: Insulin reduction observed
- **Conclusion**: Safe for long-term supplementation in older adults

**Babateen et al., 2021 & 2022** - 13 weeks, overweight/obese older adults (60-75 years)
- **Compliance**: >90% self-reported
- **Completion rate**: 80.6% (50/62)
- **Safety**: No safety concerns reported
- **Feasibility**: Confirmed for prolonged supplementation in this population

### Hypertensive Patients

**Multiple meta-analyses** (Benjamim 2022, Grönroos 2024, Siervo 2013, Bahadoran 2017)
- **Total participants**: >1,800 across studies
- **Duration**: 3-60 days
- **Safety**: No serious adverse events reported
- **Efficacy**: Consistent blood pressure reduction
- **Conclusion**: Safe in hypertensive populations

### Athletes

**Domínguez et al., 2017; Evangelista et al., 2024**
- **Studies**: 23-27 studies reviewed
- **Safety**: No adverse events reported
- **Performance**: Enhanced endurance and muscular performance
- **Caution**: Breath-hold athletes (see above)

### Postmenopausal Women with Hypertension

**Benjamim et al., 2024** - 14 participants, mean age 59±4 years
- **Dosing**: Acute 800 mg; chronic 400 mg/day for 6 days
- **Safety**: No adverse events reported
- **Efficacy**: Improved vascular function and BP reduction

---

## Long-Term Safety

### Study Durations

**Clinical trials**:
- Acute: Single dose (2 hours to 1 day)
- Short-term: 3-7 days
- Medium-term: 13 weeks (longest studied)

**Longest duration**: 13 weeks (Babateen et al., 2021 & 2022)
- No adverse events
- High compliance (>90%)
- Well tolerated

**Long-term safety beyond 13 weeks**: Not studied in reviewed literature

---

## Tolerance Development

**Grönroos et al., 2024**:
- Daily beetroot juice ingestion may reduce systolic BP with **no sign of tolerance development**
- Sustained effects observed over study durations

**Implication**: Long-term daily use does not appear to diminish efficacy (within studied timeframes up to 13 weeks)

---

## Compliance and Acceptability

**Babateen et al., 2021** - 13-week feasibility study
- **Self-reported compliance**: >90%
- **Completion rate**: 80.6%
- **Dosing frequency**: Daily to alternate-day
- **Conclusion**: Prolonged beetroot juice supplementation is feasible and acceptable

---

## Summary - Safety Profile

### Established Safety

✅ **Safe populations**:
- Healthy adults
- Older adults (60-75 years)
- Overweight/obese individuals
- Hypertensive patients
- Postmenopausal women
- Athletes (excluding breath-hold athletes)

✅ **Safe duration**:
- Up to 13 weeks studied
- No tolerance development

✅ **Safe dosing**:
- 200-800 mg nitrate/day
- 70-250 mL beetroot juice/day
- 20g/day standardized extract

✅ **No serious adverse events** across all clinical trials

### Cautions

⚠️ **Theoretical concerns**:
- Exceeding acceptable daily nitrate intake → NOC formation (minimal risk with vegetable source)
- Additive effects with blood pressure medications
- Potential interaction with nitrate medications
- Breath-hold diving/swimming athletes

⚠️ **Insufficient data**:
- Pregnancy and lactation safety (high-dose supplementation)
- Long-term use >13 weeks
- Pediatric populations

### Documented Benign Effects

- **Beeturia**: Reddish urine/stool (harmless pigmentation)
- **Blood pressure reduction**: Therapeutic but may require monitoring with antihypertensive medications
- **Insulin reduction**: Potentially beneficial metabolic effect

### Contraindications

**None absolute**, but exercise caution with:
- Hypotension (low blood pressure)
- Concurrent use of blood pressure or nitrate medications
- Breath-hold diving/swimming
- Pregnancy/lactation (insufficient data for high-dose supplementation)

### Overall Assessment

**Beetroot supplementation at clinically studied doses (200-800 mg nitrate/day) is safe and well-tolerated in adults, including older and hypertensive populations, for durations up to 13 weeks. No serious adverse events have been reported. Primary concern is theoretical risk from exceeding acceptable daily nitrate intake, but this appears minimal with vegetable-source nitrate. Monitoring recommended when combined with blood pressure medications.**
