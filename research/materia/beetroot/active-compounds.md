# Beetroot - Active Compounds and Mechanisms

## Primary Active Compounds

### 1. Inorganic Nitrate (NO3-)

**Content in beetroot**:
- Beetroot juice: 200-800 mg nitrate per serving (70-250 mL)
- Beetroot powder: ~200 mg nitrate per 15g
- High variability depending on growing conditions and processing

**Bioavailability**:
- **Cooked beetroot**: 106±15% bioavailability (near-complete absorption)
- **Beetroot juice/whole beetroot**: ~100% bioavailability
- **Conclusion**: Nitrate from vegetables absorbed very effectively by the body

**Mechanism of Action**:
1. **NO3-/NO2-/NO pathway**:
   - Dietary nitrate (NO3-) → absorbed in small intestine
   - Circulated to salivary glands → concentrated in saliva
   - Oral bacteria convert NO3- → nitrite (NO2-)
   - Nitrite swallowed → stomach/tissues convert NO2- → nitric oxide (NO)

2. **Effects of increased nitric oxide**:
   - Vasodilation (widening of blood vessels)
   - Blood pressure reduction
   - Improved blood flow
   - Enhanced mitochondrial efficiency
   - Reduced oxygen cost of exercise

**Clinical significance**:
- **Primary mechanism** for cardiovascular and athletic performance benefits
- Dose-response relationship observed (p < 0.05) for blood pressure effects
- Optimal dosing: 200-800 mg/day for cardiovascular health; 316-985 mg/day for athletic performance

---

### 2. Betalains (Betacyanins and Betaxanthins)

**Chemical class**: Water-soluble nitrogen-containing pigments unique to certain plant families

**Major betalains in beetroot**:
- **Betanin** (betanidin 5-O-glucoside): Primary red pigment
- **Isobetanin**: Isomer of betanin
- **Vulgaxanthin**: Yellow pigment (betaxanthin)

**Bioavailability**:
- **Betanin urinary excretion**: 0.5-0.9% of ingested betacyanins in 12 hours (from 300 mL juice)
- **Interpretation**: Limited absorption (<1%) but detectable in plasma and urine
- Responsible for beeturia (reddish urine/stool coloring)

**Biological activities**:
- Antioxidant properties
- Anti-inflammatory effects
- May contribute to blood pressure reduction independent of nitrate
- Potential hepatoprotective effects (traditional Egyptian use for liver support)

**Clinical significance**:
- Secondary to nitrate in terms of cardiovascular effects
- May account for some nitrate-independent blood pressure lowering observed in studies

---

### 3. Polyphenols

**Types present in beetroot**:
- Flavonoids
- Phenolic acids
- Other polyphenolic compounds

**Evidence for biological activity**:
- **McIlvenna et al., 2017**: Greater increase in RXNO (nitrosylated species) with beetroot juice vs. chard gel, likely due to higher polyphenol content in beetroot
- **Bahadoran et al., 2017**: Nitrate-depleted beetroot juice showed weaker but present blood pressure effects, suggesting polyphenols contribute to cardiovascular benefits

**Mechanisms**:
- Antioxidant activity
- Endothelial function improvement
- May enhance nitric oxide bioavailability through complementary pathways
- Synergistic effects with nitrate

**Clinical significance**:
- Contribute to **nitrate-independent blood pressure lowering**
- May explain paradoxical finding that higher nitrate concentrations sometimes show smaller BP reductions (non-nitrate compounds matter)

---

## Nitrate-Independent Mechanisms

### Evidence from Clinical Studies

**Bahadoran et al., 2017** - 22 studies, 1,248 participants
- **Key finding**: Paradoxically, higher nitrate concentrations showed smaller BP reductions
- **Nitrate-depleted placebo**: Showed weaker but present effects
- **Conclusion**: Blood pressure lowering attributed to bioactive compounds beyond nitrate, including polyphenols and other phytochemicals

**Compounds implicated in nitrate-independent effects**:
1. **Polyphenols**: Antioxidant and vascular effects
2. **Betalains**: Anti-inflammatory and antioxidant
3. **Other phytochemicals**: Not fully characterized

## Standardization Markers

### For Beetroot Extracts and Supplements

**Primary marker**: **Nitrate content**
- Most clinical studies dose by nitrate content (mg NO3-)
- Range: 200-800 mg/day for cardiovascular; 316-985 mg/day for athletic performance

**Secondary markers** (less commonly used):
- Betanin content (for betalain-standardized products)
- Total polyphenol content

### Standardized Preparations

**RedNite®**:
- Form: Chewable tablets
- Dose: 4 tablets containing 3g Beta vulgaris extract
- Reference: PMC10799154

**Standardized Beetroot Extract** (safety study):
- Dose: 20g daily
- Duration: 12 weeks safe in older adults
- Reference: PMID 38931296

**Beetroot Powder**:
- Dose: 15g providing 200 mg nitrate and 1.2 mg nitrite
- Reference: PMID 33207606

## Pharmacokinetics

### Nitrate and Nitrite

**Absorption and Distribution**:
- **Nitrate bioavailability**: ~100-106% (complete absorption)
- **Peak plasma levels**: 2 hours post-ingestion
- **Duration**: Elevated plasma NO3- and NO2- remain at 6 hours
- **Reference**: Jakubcik et al., 2021

**Metabolism**:
1. Absorbed in small intestine
2. ~25% secreted into saliva (concentrated 10-fold)
3. Oral bacteria reduce NO3- → NO2-
4. Swallowed NO2- → further reduced to NO in acidic stomach
5. NO absorbed → systemic effects

**Elimination**:
- Primarily renal excretion
- Measurable in urine for extended period after ingestion

### Betanin

**Absorption**:
- Very limited: 0.5-0.9% urinary excretion of ingested amount
- Detectable in plasma and urine
- Peak levels not well-characterized

**Metabolism**:
- Extensive degradation in GI tract
- Some absorption of intact betanin
- Metabolites not fully characterized

**Clinical implication**: Low bioavailability suggests betanin's health effects may be primarily local (GI tract) or mediated by metabolites

## Dose-Response Relationships

### Blood Pressure Effects

**Siervo et al., 2013**:
- Significant dose-response relationship observed (p < 0.05)
- Higher doses and longer durations → greater BP reduction

**Bahadoran et al., 2017**:
- Optimal duration: ≥14 days
- Optimal dose: 500 mL/day
- Greater effects with longer duration and higher doses
- **Paradox**: Higher nitrate concentrations showed smaller BP reductions (suggests saturation or nitrate-independent mechanisms dominate)

### Athletic Performance

**Evangelista et al., 2024**:
- Optimal nitrate range: 316-985 mg/day (8.3-16.4 mmol)
- Timing: 2-3 hours before exercise
- Chronic loading: ≥3 days for full effects

---

## Individual Variability

### Factors Affecting Response

**McIlvenna et al., 2017**:
- **Considerable inter-individual variation** in nitrate/nitrite response
- Blood pressure response varies significantly between individuals

**Potential factors** (not fully elucidated in beetroot literature):
- Oral microbiome composition (affects NO3- → NO2- conversion)
- Baseline nitrate dietary intake
- Genetic factors in nitric oxide metabolism
- Existing cardiovascular health status
- Use of antibacterial mouthwash (reduces oral bacteria → impairs conversion)

---

## Synergistic and Antagonistic Interactions

### Caffeine (Antagonistic)

**Domínguez et al., 2017**:
- Combining beetroot juice with caffeine may **undermine ergogenic benefits**
- Mechanism: Possibly opposing vascular effects (caffeine = vasoconstriction, nitrate = vasodilation)

### Mouthwash (Antagonistic)

**General principle** (not specific to beetroot studies):
- Antibacterial mouthwash kills oral bacteria
- Reduces NO3- → NO2- conversion in saliva
- May impair beetroot's cardiovascular benefits

---

## Comparison to Other Nitrate Sources

### Beetroot vs. Swiss Chard

**McIlvenna et al., 2017** - 9 healthy volunteers
- **Plasma NO3- and NO2-**: Increased similarly with beetroot juice and chard gel
- **RXNO (nitrosylated species)**: Greater increase with beetroot juice (likely due to higher polyphenol content)
- **Systolic BP**: Reduced similarly in both groups
- **Conclusion**: Beetroot may offer additional benefits beyond nitrate due to polyphenol content

---

## Summary of Active Compounds

### Primary Active: Inorganic Nitrate
- **Content**: 200-800 mg per serving
- **Bioavailability**: ~100%
- **Mechanism**: NO3- → NO2- → NO pathway
- **Effects**: Vasodilation, BP reduction, improved exercise efficiency
- **Clinical dosing**: 200-800 mg/day (cardiovascular); 316-985 mg/day (athletic)

### Secondary Actives: Polyphenols and Betalains
- **Bioavailability**: Low for betalains (<1%); moderate for polyphenols
- **Mechanism**: Antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, nitrate-independent vascular effects
- **Effects**: Contribute to blood pressure lowering, may enhance NO bioavailability
- **Clinical significance**: Important for nitrate-independent effects

### Standardization
- **Preferred marker**: Nitrate content (mg NO3-)
- **Dosing**: By nitrate content rather than total extract weight
- **Quality consideration**: Polyphenol content may contribute to efficacy beyond nitrate alone
