## Available Traditional Use Information from Bibliography

### Traditional Dosing (From Regulatory Documents)

**German Commission E Monograph**:
- **Dried root/rhizome**: 2-3 g multiple times daily or single dose before bedtime
- **Tincture (1:5, 45% ethanol)**: 1-3 mL single dose
- **Fluid extract**: 2-3 mL single dose

**Note**: These represent traditional dosing incorporated into modern regulatory standards, but specific historical sources and dates of origin are not provided in the bibliography.

### Traditional Indications (Inferred from Regulatory Status)

#### Sleep Disorders
- **Evidence**: All major regulatory bodies (WHO, EMA, Commission E) recognize valerian for sleep
- **Implication**: Long-standing traditional use as sedative/sleep aid
- **Modern Evidence**: Confirmed by meta-analyses (Bent 2006, Fernández-San-Martín 2010, Shinjyo 2020)

#### Nervous Tension and Anxiety
- **Evidence**: Included in regulatory monographs for nervousness/anxiety
- **Modern Evidence**: Supported by clinical trials (Roh 2019, meta-analysis by Shinjyo 2020)

### Traditional Preparations (From Pharmacopoeial Standards)

#### Well-Established Forms
The following preparations have been codified in multiple national pharmacopoeias, suggesting long traditional use:

1. **Valerian Root** (dried herb)
   - USP, EP, BP, and multiple European national pharmacopoeias
   - Suggests centuries of use in herbal medicine

2. **Valerian Tincture**
   - EP, EMA monograph
   - Traditional alcoholic extraction (1:5, 45% ethanol)
   - Reflects historical preservation and administration methods

3. **Valerian Dry Hydroalcoholic Extract**
   - EP, EMA monograph

   - Evolution of traditional tincture technology

## Traditional vs. Modern Use: Continuity and Evolution

### Continuity
**Indications**: Sleep disorders and nervous tension remain primary uses from traditional to modern contexts

**Preparation Types**: Tinctures, dried root, and aqueous extracts continue from traditional methods

**Dosing Ranges**: Traditional doses (2-3 g dried root, 1-3 mL tincture) align with modern clinical trial dosing

### Evolution
**Standardization**: Modern focus on valerenic acid content for quality control (not traditionally measured)

**Dosage Forms**: Capsules and tablets replace some traditional teas and tinctures for convenience

**Evidence Base**: Clinical trials and pharmacokinetic studies provide mechanistic understanding and efficacy data

## Inference: Traditional Use Profile

Based on regulatory recognition, pharmacopoeial inclusion, and modern clinical evidence, we can reasonably infer the following traditional use profile:

### Primary Traditional Indications
1. **Sleep disturbances/insomnia** - Confirmed by modern evidence
2. **Nervous tension/anxiety** - Confirmed by modern evidence
3. **Restlessness** - Commonly mentioned in regulatory monographs

### Traditional Preparation Methods
1. **Infusion/tea** - Dried root steeped in hot water
2. **Tincture** - Alcoholic extraction (codified as 1:5, 45% ethanol)
3. **Decoction** - Possible for roots/rhizomes
4. **Powdered root** - In capsules or mixed with food/liquid

### Traditional Dosing Practices
- **Timing**: Before bedtime for sleep (aligns with modern PK data showing 1-2 hour onset)
- **Frequency**: Single evening dose or multiple times daily for anxiety
- **Duration**: Likely short-term or intermittent use (long-term traditional use patterns not documented in current bibliography)

## Modern Validation of Traditional Use

### Confirmed by Clinical Evidence
- **Sleep improvement**: Moderate evidence (Bent 2006 RR 1.8; Shinjyo 2020 meta-analysis)
- **Anxiety reduction**: Preliminary evidence (Roh 2019, Shinjyo 2020)

### Confirmed by Safety Data
- **Generally well-tolerated**: No severe adverse events in subjects aged 7-80 years (Shinjyo 2020)
- **Long-term safety**: Data lacking >6 months, but traditional use suggests acceptable safety profile

### Refinements from Modern Evidence
- **Onset timing**: PK data supports 30 min-2 hour pre-bedtime dosing
- **Extract quality matters**: Whole root preparations may be more reliable than isolated extracts (Shinjyo 2020)
- **Individual variability**: PK variability may explain why traditional use recognized variable response
