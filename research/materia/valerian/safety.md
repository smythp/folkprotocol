# Valerian - Safety Profile

## Overall Safety Assessment

**General Conclusion**: Generally well-tolerated with minimal adverse effects in short-term use; rare hepatotoxicity documented; long-term safety data lacking.

**Age Range Safety**: No severe adverse events in subjects aged 7-80 years (Shinjyo et al. 2020, n=6,894 across 60 studies)

## Toxicology Studies

### Comprehensive Safety Evaluation (Bao et al. 2024)

**Test Article**: Aqueous extract from Valeriana officinalis L. roots

#### Genotoxicity Testing
**Tests Performed**:
1. Bacterial reverse mutation (Ames test)
2. Mouse erythrocyte micronucleus test
3. In vitro mammalian cell chromosome aberration test

**Results**: No genotoxicity detected in any assay

#### Acute Toxicity
- **Study Design**: 14-day oral toxicity in mice
- **Dose**: 96 g/kg body weight (extremely high dose)
- **Results**: No deaths or behavioral abnormalities at highest dose

#### Subchronic Toxicity
- **Study Design**: 90-day oral toxicity in rats
- **Dose Levels**: 0, 3.5, 7, 14 g/kg body weight
- **Parameters Assessed**:
  - Clinical observation
  - Body weight
  - Organ weight
  - Hematology
  - Serum biochemistry
  - Urinalysis
- **Results**: No dose-related effects on any measured parameter
- **NOAEL**: ≥14 g/kg body weight

#### Teratogenicity/Developmental Toxicity
- **Study Design**: Testing in pregnant rats
- **Dose Levels**: 0, 3.5, 7, 14 g/kg body weight
- **Results**: No significant toxicological changes in embryonic formation or fetal development

**Overall Conclusion**: Aqueous extract demonstrated excellent safety profile across all toxicity endpoints

### Developmental Toxicity Screening (Yao et al. 2007)

**Study Design**: Rat developmental toxicity study
- **Dosing Period**: Gestation days 1-8 or 8-15
- **Vehicle**: Valerian extract in 45% ethanol
- **Assessment**: Fetuses, placentae, ovaries examined on gestation day 20
- **Dose Range**: Up to 65x human equivalent dose

**Results**:
- **In Vivo**: No adverse effects on fertility or fetal development
- **Maternal Toxicity**: None observed
- **Embryo Culture**: Toxicity observed in cultured embryos (day 10.5, 26h exposure)

**Conclusion**: No reproductive toxicity at high doses in vivo, though extract composition (valepotriate levels) may affect outcomes

## Hepatotoxicity

### LiverTox Database Assessment

**Likelihood Score**: C (probable rare cause of clinically apparent liver injury)

**Incidence**: Rare

**Latency**: 3-12 weeks post-initiation

**Pattern**: Hepatocellular or mixed hepatocellular-cholestatic

**Severity**: Mild-to-moderate in most cases

### Case Reports

#### Severe Case
- **Patient**: 57-year-old woman
- **Timing**: Within 3 weeks of herbal blend containing valerian
- **Presentation**:
  - Jaundice
  - Marked elevations: bilirubin 18.4 mg/dL, ALT 1165 U/L
- **Progression**: Ascites and hepatic encephalopathy despite discontinuation
- **Recovery**: 10 months with incomplete normalization
- **Biopsy**: Confirmed fibrosis
- **Note**: **Herbal blend** (concurrent hepatotoxic herbs likely contributed)

#### Typical Cases
- **Recovery Time**: 2-4 months, self-limiting
- **Chronic Outcomes**: No cases of chronic hepatitis, cirrhosis, or acute liver failure attributed to valerian **alone**

### Hepatotoxicity Context

**Important Considerations**:
- Severe cases typically involved concurrent hepatotoxic herbs (skullcap, black cohosh)
- No specific compound identified as hepatotoxic
- Mechanism unknown
- Causality difficult to establish in herbal blend cases

**Clinical Recommendation**: Monitor liver function if using long-term or in combination with other potentially hepatotoxic herbs

## Contraindications

**Note**: Regulatory monographs provide specific contraindications, but detailed contraindication data not explicitly extracted in available bibliography. Consult EMA and Commission E monographs for complete information.

**General Considerations Based on Safety Data**:
- Pre-existing liver disease (due to rare hepatotoxicity reports)
- Pregnancy (limited human data; animal data reassuring at normal doses)
- Lactation (insufficient data)

## Drug Interactions

**Note**: Drug interaction data not extensively detailed in the clinical/safety studies included in bibliography. PK data suggests potential for interactions based on:

### Pharmacokinetic Considerations

**Anderson et al. 2005, 2010 - Valerenic Acid PK**:
- **T½**: 1.1 ± 0.6 hours (short elimination half-life)
- **No accumulation** with repeated dosing
- **High PK variability**: Large inter- and intra-subject differences

**Theoretical Interaction Potential**:
- Short half-life suggests low risk of accumulation-based interactions
- Variability suggests individual responses may differ

**Research Gap**: Specific drug interaction studies not identified in bibliography; consult regulatory monographs (EMA, Commission E, Health Canada) for documented interactions

## Pregnancy and Lactation

### Pregnancy

**Animal Data** (Yao et al. 2007):
- No reproductive toxicity at doses up to 65x human equivalent
- No adverse effects on fertility or fetal development in vivo
- Teratogenicity study (Bao et al. 2024): No significant toxicological changes in embryonic formation or fetal development

**Human Data**: Limited clinical data in pregnant women

**Safety Classification**: Insufficient human data; animal data reassuring at therapeutic doses

### Lactation

**Data Availability**: Insufficient data on excretion in breast milk or effects on nursing infants

**Safety Classification**: Unknown; use with caution

## Adverse Effects in Clinical Trials

**Shekhar et al. 2024** (8-week RCT, n=80):
- **Adverse Events**: None reported

**Zare Elmi et al. 2021** (30-day trial, n=72 post-CABG patients):
- **Conclusion**: "Safely improved sleep quality"

**Shinjyo et al. 2020** (Systematic review, n=6,894):
- **Severe Adverse Events**: None in subjects aged 7-80 years
- **Minor Adverse Effects**: Not systematically detailed in meta-analysis summary

**General Tolerability**: Well-tolerated with minimal adverse effects across clinical studies

## Long-Term Safety

**Research Gap**: Long-term safety studies (>6 months) lacking

**Available Data**:
- Up to 8 weeks in controlled trials (Shekhar 2024)
- 90-day subchronic toxicity study in rats (Bao 2024) showed no adverse effects

**Recommendation**: Prudent to periodically reassess need for continued use in chronic therapy; monitor liver function if using >3 months

## Overdose and Toxicity

**Acute Toxicity** (Bao et al. 2024):
- No deaths or behavioral abnormalities in mice at 96 g/kg body weight
- Extremely high dose suggests wide therapeutic window

**Human Overdose Data**: Not systematically reported in available bibliography

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Use
- **Age Range in Studies**: Children as young as 7 years included in systematic review (Shinjyo et al. 2020)
- **Safety**: No severe adverse events reported
- **Data Quality**: Limited specific pediatric dosing and safety data

### Geriatric Use
- **PK Study** (Anderson et al. 2010): Conducted in older women
- **Finding**: Body weight inversely correlated with concentration and half-life
- **Implication**: May require dose adjustment based on body weight, not age per se

### Post-Surgical Patients
- **Zare Elmi et al. 2021**: Safe and effective in post-CABG surgery patients (n=72)
- **Duration**: 30 days post-operatively
- **No Safety Concerns**: Documented in this vulnerable population

## Quality and Contamination Concerns

**Extract Variability** (Shinjyo et al. 2020):
- Variable quality of herbal extracts affects clinical outcomes
- Valepotriate content varies by preparation method and stability

**Recommendation**:
- Use standardized preparations from reputable manufacturers
- Follow pharmacopoeial quality standards (USP, EP, BP)
- Contamination risk reduced with pharmacopoeia-compliant products

## Summary: Safety Risk Stratification

### Low Risk (Well-Established)
- Short-term use (≤8 weeks) in healthy adults
- Standardized preparations from reputable sources
- No concurrent hepatotoxic medications/herbs

### Moderate Risk (Caution Advised)
- Long-term use (>6 months) - data lacking
- Pre-existing liver disease - rare hepatotoxicity documented
- Pregnancy/lactation - insufficient human data
- Concurrent use of hepatotoxic herbs or drugs

### Research Priorities
- Long-term safety studies (>6 months)
- Specific drug interaction studies
- Hepatotoxicity mechanism and risk factors
- Pregnancy/lactation safety data
- Pediatric dosing and safety
