# Passionflower (Passiflora incarnata) - Safety Profile

## Overall Safety Assessment

### Systematic Review Evidence

**Janda et al. 2020 (9 trials reviewed):**
- No adverse effects observed across studies
- No memory loss documented
- No collapse of psychometric functions
- Study durations: 1-30 days
- Participants: Adults 18+

**Miroddi et al. 2013:**
- "Plant showed a good safety profile"
- Preclinical and clinical evidence supports safety

**Ulbricht et al. 2008 (Natural Standard Review):**
- Evidence-based systematic review
- Safety profile generally favorable

---

## Hepatotoxicity Assessment

### LiverTox Database (2020)

**Likelihood Score:** E (unlikely cause of clinically apparent liver injury)

**Key Findings:**
- **No documented cases** of serum enzyme elevations from passionflower
- **No clinically apparent liver injury** documented
- **Major US prospective study** (2004-2013): Reviewed 85 cases of herbal/dietary supplement liver injury; passionflower was not implicated in any cases
- **Despite widespread use**, no convincing link to liver injury

**Common Side Effects:** Dizziness, sedation, confusion, ataxia (none hepatic)

**Conclusion:** Passionflower has not been convincingly linked to clinically apparent liver injury despite widespread use.

**Clinical Significance:** Hepatotoxicity is not a safety concern with passionflower.

---

## Cardiac Safety

### Case Report: Fisher et al. 2000

**Patient:** 34-year-old woman

**Adverse Effects:**
- Severe nausea and vomiting
- Drowsiness
- Prolonged QTc interval
- Episodes of nonsustained ventricular tachycardia

**Circumstances:**
- Occurred at therapeutic doses
- Required hospitalization for cardiac monitoring
- Required intravenous fluid support
- Connection to herbal remedy not recognized for several days

**Clinical Course:**
- Patient recovered after discontinuation
- Symptoms resolved with supportive care

**Significance:**
- **Only documented case** of serious cardiac effects
- Rare adverse event
- Highlights importance of medication history including herbals

### Cardiac Safety Implications

**QT Prolongation:**
- One case report of prolonged QTc
- May prolong QT interval with large doses
- **Contraindication:** Use with other QT-prolonging medications

**Clinical Recommendations:**
- Caution in patients with known QT prolongation
- Avoid concurrent use with Class IA or Class III antiarrhythmics
- Consider ECG monitoring if using with QT-prolonging drugs
- Risk appears very low (single case report)

## Common Adverse Effects

### Frequently Reported (Non-Serious)

**Central Nervous System:**
- Drowsiness (most common)
- Dizziness
- Sedation
- Confusion
- Ataxia (incoordination)

**Gastrointestinal:**
- Nausea (rare; primarily in Fisher case report)
- Vomiting (rare; primarily in Fisher case report)

**Incidence:** Exact rates not well-quantified; appears low based on clinical trial safety data

### Clinical Trial Safety Data

**Akhondzadeh et al. 2001 (GAD, n=36):**
- "Significantly more problems relating to impairment of job performance were encountered with subjects on oxazepam" [vs. passionflower]
- Passionflower group had **fewer** performance-impairing effects than oxazepam

**Movafegh et al. 2008 (preoperative, n=60):**
- "Extract reduces anxiety without inducing sedation"
- Psychomotor recovery comparable to placebo
- Discharge times similar to placebo
- No adverse effects mentioned

**Dantas et al. 2017 (dental anxiety, n=40):**
- Passionflower showed "minimal memory effects"
- Midazolam caused amnesia in 20% of participants
- Passionflower: no amnesia cases

**Harit et al. 2024 (stress/sleep, n=65):**
- "No adverse effects during study period"
- 30-day duration
- 600 mg daily dose

**Lee et al. 2020 (insomnia, n=110):**
- 2-week duration
- Adverse effects not prominently reported (suggests low incidence)

## Contraindications

### Pregnancy

**Contraindication:** Passionflower use during pregnancy

**Rationale:**
- May induce uterine contractions
- Traditional use as emmenagogue (promotes menstruation)
- Insufficient safety data for pregnancy

**Evidence Level:** Theoretical risk based on traditional use; human pregnancy data lacking

**Clinical Recommendation:** Avoid use during pregnancy.

### Breastfeeding/Lactation

**Status:** Insufficient safety data

**Recommendation:** Avoid use during lactation due to lack of safety data.

### Children

**Pediatric Use:**
- Not studied in children
- Traditional use primarily in adults
- No established pediatric dosing

**Recommendation:** Not recommended for pediatric use without medical supervision.

## Drug Interactions

### CNS Depressants

**Interaction Risk:** Moderate

**Mechanism:** Additive CNS depression

**Affected Medications:**
- Benzodiazepines (diazepam, alprazolam, lorazepam, etc.)
- Barbiturates
- Sleep medications (zolpidem, eszopiclone)
- Opioids
- Sedating antihistamines (diphenhydramine)
- Alcohol

**Clinical Significance:**
- May enhance sedative effects
- Increased risk of drowsiness, dizziness
- May impair psychomotor performance

**Recommendation:**
- Use caution when combining with CNS depressants
- Consider dose reduction of one or both agents
- Monitor for excessive sedation

**Note:** Akhondzadeh 2001 study deliberately combined passionflower with clonidine (sedating) without major safety concerns in monitored setting; suggests additive CNS depression is manageable but requires caution.

### Anesthesia

**Interaction Risk:** Moderate to High

**Concern:**
- Additive sedation during perioperative period
- Potential interaction with general anesthetics

**Clinical Recommendations:**
- **Discontinue 24-48 hours before surgery** (conservative approach)
- Inform anesthesiologist of passionflower use
- Monitor for prolonged sedation post-anesthesia

**Counterpoint:**
- Movafegh et al. 2008 safely used passionflower 90 minutes before ambulatory surgery
- No prolonged sedation or delayed discharge
- May be safe in controlled perioperative setting with anesthesiologist awareness

**Resolution:** Risk is theoretical; one controlled study showed safety. Inform anesthesia team regardless.

### QT-Prolonging Medications

**Interaction Risk:** Low to Moderate (based on single case report)

**Mechanism:** Potential additive QT prolongation

**Affected Medications:**
- **Class IA antiarrhythmics:** Quinidine, procainamide, disopyramide
- **Class III antiarrhythmics:** Amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide
- **Antipsychotics:** Haloperidol, ziprasidone, quetiapine
- **Antibiotics:** Macrolides (erythromycin, azithromycin), fluoroquinolones
- **Antifungals:** Fluconazole, ketoconazole
- **Others:** Methadone, ondansetron

**Clinical Significance:**
- Based on one case report (Fisher 2000)
- Risk appears rare but potentially serious
- Torsades de pointes theoretical risk

**Recommendation:**
- Avoid concurrent use with known QT-prolonging drugs
- If use necessary, consider ECG monitoring
- Inform patients of cardiac symptoms to report (palpitations, syncope)

### Antiplatelet/Anticoagulant Drugs

**Interaction Risk:** Theoretical (no documented cases)

**Rationale:**
- Some flavonoids have antiplatelet activity
- Theoretical bleeding risk

**Affected Medications:**
- Warfarin
- Aspirin
- Clopidogrel
- Novel anticoagulants (DOACs)

**Recommendation:** Use caution; monitor for signs of bleeding; theoretical interaction not clinically documented for passionflower.

### CYP450 Interactions

**Data:** Limited

**Known:** Flavonoids can affect CYP enzymes

**Passionflower-Specific Data:** Not well-characterized

**Clinical Implication:** Potential for drug interactions via CYP inhibition/induction, but specific interactions not documented. Exercise general caution with narrow therapeutic index drugs.

## Special Populations

### Elderly

**Safety Considerations:**
- Increased sensitivity to CNS effects possible
- Higher risk of falls due to dizziness/ataxia
- Polypharmacy increases interaction risk

**Recommendations:**
- Start with lower doses
- Monitor for excessive sedation
- Assess fall risk
- Review medication list for interactions

**Evidence:** No specific studies in elderly; extrapolated from general CNS depressant considerations.

### Patients with Cardiovascular Disease

**Considerations:**
- QT prolongation case report
- Use caution in patients with:
  - Known long QT syndrome
  - History of arrhythmias
  - Structural heart disease
  - Concurrent QT-prolonging medications

**Recommendations:**
- Obtain baseline ECG if high-risk patient
- Monitor for palpitations, syncope
- Low threshold to discontinue if cardiac symptoms develop

### Hepatic Impairment

**Safety:** No documented hepatotoxicity

**Pharmacokinetics:** Unknown (no PK data available)

**Recommendation:** Likely safe, but consider starting with lower doses due to unknown metabolism/clearance.

### Renal Impairment

**Safety:** No documented nephrotoxicity

**Pharmacokinetics:** Unknown excretion pathways

**Recommendation:** Likely safe; dose adjustment not established (probably not necessary).

## Dosing and Safety

### Therapeutic Index

**Assessment:** Appears wide

**Evidence:**
- Multiple clinical trials at varying doses without serious adverse effects
- Doses ranging from 45 drops to 600 mg/day studied safely
- Regulatory bodies (EMA, WHO, Commission E) consider it safe for use without medical supervision (for appropriate indications)

**Margin of Safety:**
- No documented overdose cases in literature
- Traditional use range (4-8 g crude herb) well-tolerated
- Upper limit not established

### Duration of Use

**Short-term (studied):**
- 1-30 days extensively studied
- Appears safe

**Long-term:**
- Safety beyond 30 days not systematically studied
- Traditional use suggests long-term use acceptable
- No documented cumulative toxicity

**Regulatory Guidance:**
- EMA/WHO recommend medical consultation if symptoms persist beyond 2 weeks
- Not due to safety concern, but to evaluate underlying condition

---

## Overdose

**Documented Cases:** None in medical literature

**Expected Effects (Extrapolated):**
- Excessive CNS depression
- Profound sedation
- Ataxia
- Possible cardiac effects (based on Fisher case)

**Management (Theoretical):**
- Supportive care
- Gastric decontamination if recent ingestion
- Monitor cardiac rhythm if large overdose
- IV fluids
- Observation until symptoms resolve

## Comparison to Benzodiazepines

### Safety Advantages Over Benzodiazepines

**Memory:**
- Passionflower: No amnesia (Dantas 2017)
- Benzodiazepines: Amnesia in 20% (Dantas 2017)

**Psychomotor Performance:**
- Passionflower: No job performance impairment (Akhondzadeh 2001)
- Oxazepam: Significantly more job performance problems (Akhondzadeh 2001)

**Sedation:**
- Passionflower: Anxiolytic without sedation (Movafegh 2008)
- Benzodiazepines: Sedation common

**Dependence:**
- Passionflower: No documented dependence or withdrawal
- Benzodiazepines: Well-known dependence liability

**Discharge Time:**
- Passionflower: No delay in surgical discharge (Movafegh 2008)
- Benzodiazepines: Often delay discharge

### Safety Disadvantages

**Standardization:**
- Passionflower: Variable product quality
- Benzodiazepines: Precise dosing

**Drug Interactions:**
- Passionflower: Less well-characterized
- Benzodiazepines: Extensively documented

**Onset:**
- Passionflower: Slower onset (Akhondzadeh 2001)
- Benzodiazepines: Faster therapeutic onset

## Regulatory Safety Assessment

### EMA Assessment Report 2014

**Safety Conclusion:** Adequate for traditional use registration

**Basis:**
- Long-standing use (>30 years, including >15 in EU)
- Good safety profile in clinical experience
- Rare adverse effects
- Most effects are minor (drowsiness, dizziness)

### WHO Monograph

**Safety Status:** Positive

**Contraindications Listed:**
- Pregnancy
- Use with CNS depressants (caution)

### Commission E

**Safety:** No contraindications listed in original monograph (German regulatory acceptance)

## Quality and Adulteration Safety

### Adulteration Risk

**Concern:** Passionflower products may be adulterated with:
- Other Passiflora species (not P. incarnata)
- Synthetic anxiolytics (contamination)
- Other herbs

**Mitigation:**
- Purchase from reputable manufacturers
- Request Certificate of Analysis
- HPLC fingerprint verification
- Third-party testing (USP, NSF, ConsumerLab)

### Contamination

**Potential Contaminants:**
- Heavy metals (lead, mercury, cadmium)
- Pesticides
- Microbial contamination

**Mitigation:**
- Good Agricultural and Collection Practices (GACP)
- Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP)
- Third-party testing

## Clinical Monitoring Recommendations

### Before Starting Passionflower

**History:**
- Current medications (especially CNS depressants, QT-prolonging drugs)
- Cardiac history (arrhythmias, long QT)
- Pregnancy/breastfeeding status
- Upcoming surgeries

**Baseline (If High-Risk Patient):**
- ECG if cardiac risk factors or QT-prolonging drug use
- Generally not necessary for healthy adults

### During Use

**Monitor For:**
- Excessive sedation
- Dizziness (fall risk)
- Cardiac symptoms (palpitations, syncope)
- Nausea/vomiting (rare)

**Duration of Monitoring:**
- First 1-2 weeks (adjustment period)
- Ongoing if combined with other CNS depressants

### When to Discontinue

**Indications:**
- Cardiac symptoms develop
- Excessive sedation interfering with daily activities
- Allergic reaction (rash, swelling)
- No benefit after 2-4 weeks of adequate dose

## Safety Bottom Line

**Overall Safety Profile:** Good. Passionflower is generally well-tolerated with rare adverse effects.

**Most Common Adverse Effects:** Drowsiness, dizziness (generally mild)

**Serious Adverse Effects:** Rare. One case report of cardiac effects (prolonged QTc, nonsustained VT).

**Hepatotoxicity:** No documented cases; rated "E" (unlikely) by LiverTox.

**Contraindications:**
1. Pregnancy (may induce uterine contractions)
2. Concurrent use with anesthesia (inform anesthesiologist)
3. Use with caution with QT-prolonging medications

**Drug Interactions:**
- CNS depressants (additive sedation - use caution)
- QT-prolonging drugs (avoid or monitor)
- Theoretical antiplatelet interaction (not documented)

**Advantages Over Benzodiazepines:**
- No memory impairment
- No job performance impairment
- No documented dependence
- Anxiolytic effect without excessive sedation

**Clinical Recommendations:**
- Generally safe for short-term use (up to 30 days studied)
- Obtain medication history before use
- Monitor for excessive CNS effects
- Discontinue 24-48 hours before surgery (conservative)
- Avoid in pregnancy
- Use caution in elderly (fall risk)
- Purchase standardized products from reputable sources

**Risk Level:** Low. Most patients tolerate passionflower well without significant adverse effects.
