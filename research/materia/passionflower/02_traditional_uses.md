# Passionflower (Passiflora incarnata) - Traditional Uses

## European Traditional Medicine

### German Commission E (Regulatory Recognition)
- **Status:** Positive monograph
- **Indication:** Nervous restlessness (possibly associated with pectanginous complaints)
- **Dosing:** Daily dose 4-8 g
- **Assessment:** Positively assessed for alleviating nervousness
- **Significance:** Official recognition by German regulatory body for herbal medicines

### European Medicines Agency (EMA) - Traditional Use
- **Year:** 2014 (Community Herbal Monograph)
- **Reference:** EMA/HMPC/669740/2013
- **Traditional Use Criteria:** Based on long-standing use
- **Indications:**
  - Relief of mild symptoms of mental stress
  - Sleep aid
  - Nervous restlessness
  - Nervous stomach complaints

**EMA Traditional Use Definition:** Requires at least 30 years of documented medicinal use, including 15 years within the European Union. Passionflower meets these criteria.

## Historical European Dosing Traditions

### British Herbal Pharmacopoeia 1976
**Frequency:** 3 times daily

**Preparations:**
- **Infusion:** 0.25-1 g
- **Liquid extract:** 0.5-1 ml (1:1 in 25% alcohol)
- **Tincture:** 0.5-2 ml (1:8 in 45% alcohol)

### Hagers Handbuch (German Reference)
- **Daily dose:** 4-8 g crude drug or corresponding extract
- **Tea:** 15 g crude drug in 150 ml water

## World Health Organization Recognition

### WHO Monograph on Passiflora incarnata
- **Status:** Positive monograph
- **Indications:**
  - Mild symptoms of mental stress
  - Sleep disorders
  - Nervous stomach complaints

**WHO Traditional Dosing:**
- **Sedative daily dose:** 0.5-2.0 g of aerial parts, three to four times daily
- **Infusion:** 2.5 g of aerial parts as an infusion, three to four times daily
- **Tincture:** 1.0-4.0 ml tincture (1:8), three to four times daily

**Significance:** WHO recognition indicates global traditional use evidence and safety profile acceptable for international health guidance.

## Traditional Preparation Methods

### Infusion (Tea)
**Traditional European Method:**
- 2.5-15 g dried aerial parts
- Steep in 150-250 ml boiling water
- Consume 3-4 times daily

**British Tradition:**
- Lower dose: 0.25-1 g per infusion
- 3 times daily
- For nervous restlessness and sleep

### Tinctures
**British Tradition (BHP 1976):**
- **Ratio:** 1:8 in 45% alcohol
- **Dose:** 0.5-2 ml, 3 times daily

**WHO Recommendation:**
- **Ratio:** 1:8
- **Dose:** 1.0-4.0 ml, 3-4 times daily

**German Practice:**
- Variable ratios, typically standardized to flavonoid content
- Daily equivalent to 4-8 g crude drug

### Liquid Extracts
**British Tradition:**
- **Ratio:** 1:1 in 25% alcohol
- **Dose:** 0.5-1 ml, 3 times daily

## Traditional Indications by System

### Nervous System
- **Primary:** Nervous restlessness, anxiety
- **Sleep:** Insomnia, restless sleep
- **Mood:** Nervous irritability
- **Cognitive:** Mental stress relief
- **Historical note:** Used for "hysteria" and "neurasthenia" in older European texts

### Gastrointestinal
- **Nervous stomach complaints** (WHO, EMA)
- **Digestive upset** associated with anxiety
- **Pectanginous complaints** (Commission E - likely referring to nervous digestive discomfort)

### Cardiovascular
- **Historical:** "Pectanginous complaints" in Commission E monograph
- **Traditional association:** Nervous heart complaints
- **Note:** Limited modern evidence; may refer to anxiety-related palpitations rather than true cardiac pathology

## North American Traditional Use

### Native American Use
- **Limited documentation** for Passiflora incarnata specifically
- **Species:** North American native species (P. incarnata indigenous to southeastern United States)
- **Historical gap:** Less documented than European use, though plant is native to Americas

**Note:** Most documented traditional use comes from European adoption after plant introduction to European medicine, not from indigenous American traditions. This represents a reverse pattern from many medicinal plants.

### Eclectic Medicine (19th-20th Century American)
- **Era:** Late 1800s to early 1900s
- **Practitioners:** Eclectic physicians in United States
- **Uses:** Nervous conditions, insomnia, hysteria
- **Preparations:** Fluid extracts, tinctures
- **Significance:** Bridge between folk use and modern phytotherapy

## Traditional Dosing Summary

### Daily Dose Ranges (Crude Herb Equivalent)

**Conservative (British):**
- 0.75-3 g daily (0.25-1 g × 3 doses)

**Moderate (WHO):**
- 1.5-8 g daily (0.5-2 g × 3-4 doses)

**Standard (German/Commission E):**
- 4-8 g daily

**Upper range (Hagers):**
- Up to 15 g as tea

### Tincture Dose Ranges

**1:8 Ratio (Traditional):**
- 1.5-6 ml daily (0.5-2 ml × 3 doses, British)
- 3-16 ml daily (1-4 ml × 4 doses, WHO)

**1:1 Ratio (Stronger):**
- 1.5-3 ml daily (0.5-1 ml × 3 doses)

## Traditional Preparation Wisdom

### Timing
- **Evening dose emphasized** for sleep support
- **Regular dosing** (3-4 times daily) for anxiety
- **Not typically acute/as-needed** in traditional use (differs from modern perioperative studies)

### Duration
- **Traditional use:** Ongoing, as needed for symptoms
- **Not specified:** Maximum duration in traditional sources
- **Modern caution:** EMA/WHO recommend medical consultation if symptoms persist

### Combinations
- **Traditionally combined with:** Valerian, lemon balm, hops for sleep
- **For anxiety:** Combined with hawthorn, lemon balm
- **Digestive:** Combined with chamomile, lemon balm

## Traditional vs. Modern Use Patterns

### Similarities
- **Anxiety/nervous restlessness** - consistent across traditional and modern
- **Sleep disorders** - traditional indication confirmed by RCTs
- **Dose range** - traditional 4-8 g aligns with modern recommendations
- **Safety profile** - traditionally considered safe, confirmed by modern studies

### Differences
- **Acute dosing** - modern studies use single pre-procedure doses (not traditional pattern)
- **Standardization** - modern extracts standardized to flavonoids (not traditional concern)
- **Dental anxiety** - specific modern application, not traditional indication
- **Opioid withdrawal** - modern clinical finding, no traditional precedent

### Traditional Claims Not Yet Validated
- **Antitussive** (cough suppressant) - preclinical evidence, limited human studies
- **Antiasthmatic** - preclinical evidence only
- **Cardiovascular** ("pectanginous complaints") - unclear if effective or symbolic
- **Antidiabetic** - preliminary preclinical evidence only

## Regulatory Context

### Well-Established Use vs. Traditional Use (EMA Framework)

**Passiflora incarnata status:** Traditional Use (2014 Community Monograph)

**Requirements for Traditional Use classification:**
- Medicinal use ≥30 years (including ≥15 years in EU): ✓
- Safety established: ✓
- Pharmacological effects plausible: ✓
- Intended for use without medical supervision: ✓

**Implications:**
- Sufficient traditional use evidence for regulatory approval
- Safety profile adequate based on long-standing use
- Does not require same level of clinical evidence as "well-established use"

## Traditional Use Bottom Line

**Primary Traditional Indications:**
1. Nervous restlessness/anxiety
2. Sleep disorders
3. Nervous stomach complaints

**Traditional Dose:** 4-8 g daily crude herb or equivalent preparations, taken 3-4 times daily.

**Geographic Tradition:** Primarily European (German, British, French, Swiss), with adoption in North America (Eclectic medicine, USP) and recognition in WHO guidance.

**Regulatory Status:** Officially recognized in multiple pharmacopoeias and positive monographs (Commission E, WHO, EMA, ESCOP) indicating well-established traditional medicinal use.

**Historical Depth:** At least 150-200 years documented use in European phytotherapy; meets EMA 30-year traditional use criteria.

**Traditional Preparations:** Infusions (tea), tinctures (1:8 in 45% alcohol), and liquid extracts (1:1 in 25% alcohol).
