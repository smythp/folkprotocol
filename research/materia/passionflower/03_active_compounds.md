# Passionflower (Passiflora incarnata) - Active Compounds

## Major Flavonoid Compounds

### Primary Markers (Standardization)

**Vitexin** (Apigenin-8-C-glucoside)
- **Content:** 0.867 ± 0.011% in aerial parts
- **Role:** Primary standardization marker
- **Pharmacopoeia standard:** European Pharmacopoeia requires minimum 1.5% total flavonoids expressed as vitexin
- **Commercial extracts:** Often standardized to 3.5% flavonoids as vitexin

**Isovitexin** (Apigenin-6-C-glucoside)
- **Content:** 1.198 g% (major flavonoid in fluidextract)
- **Standardization:** 2.5-4% flavonoids as isovitexin in some preparations
- **Role:** Secondary marker, structural isomer of vitexin

**Orientin** (Luteolin-8-C-glucoside)
- **Role:** Major flavonoid, characterized by HPLC in anxiolytic studies
- **Pharmacological relevance:** Identified in extracts showing GABA-mediated effects

**Homoorientin** (Luteolin-6-C-glucoside)
- **Role:** Major flavonoid identified in HPLC characterization
- **Pharmacological relevance:** Present in anxiolytic extracts

## Total Flavonoid Content

### Quantitative Ranges

**Dried Aerial Parts:**
- **Total flavonoids (as vitexin):** 3.762 ± 0.049%
- **European Pharmacopoeia minimum:** 1.5% (dried drug)

**Dry Extracts:**
- **Preparation:** Ethanol (40-90% V/V), methanol (60% V/V), or acetone (40% V/V)
- **Minimum content:** 2.0% flavonoids expressed as vitexin
- **Commercial standardization:** Often 2.5-3.5%

**Fluid Extracts:**
- **Major component:** Isovitexin (1.198 g%)
- **Variable:** Depends on extraction method and solvent

## Standardization Methods

### European Pharmacopoeia Standards

**Raw Material (Dried Herb):**
- **Monograph:** 01/2005:1459
- **Minimum:** 1.5% total flavonoids (expressed as vitexin, dried drug basis)

**Extracts:**
- **Solvents:** Ethanol 40-90%, methanol 60%, or acetone 40%
- **Minimum:** 2.0% flavonoids (expressed as vitexin)

### Commercial Standardization Practices

**Common Standards:**
- 3.5% flavonoids as vitexin
- 2.5-4% flavonoids as isovitexin

**Rationale:**
- Higher standardization than pharmacopoeial minimum
- Ensures batch-to-batch consistency
- Aligns with clinical trial doses (though many trials did not report standardization)

## Analytical Methods

### HPLC Characterization (Grundmann et al. 2008)

**Technique:** Reverse phase HPLC-UV
- **Column:** Atlantis C18 (250 mm × 4.6 mm × 5 μm)
- **Detection wavelength:** 350 nm
- **Quantification wavelength:** 330 nm
- **Mobile phase:** 0.01% formic acid solution and methanol:acetonitrile (25:75)

**Purpose:**
- Identify major flavonoid constituents
- Quantify marker compounds
- Characterize extracts used in pharmacological studies
- Quality control for commercial products

**Major Compounds Identified:**
1. Homoorientin
2. Orientin
3. Vitexin
4. Isovitexin

**Significance:** First study to demonstrate in vivo GABA-mediated anxiolytic activity used HPLC-characterized extract, establishing which compounds are present in active preparations.

## Mechanism of Action

### GABAergic Activity (Grundmann et al. 2008)

**Study Design:**
- In vivo animal study (elevated plus maze in mice)
- HPLC-characterized Passiflora incarnata extract
- Anxiolytic effect at 375 mg/kg (comparable to diazepam 1.5 mg/kg)

**Key Mechanistic Findings:**
- **GABA receptor mediation confirmed:** Effect antagonized by flumazenil (GABA receptor antagonist)
- **GABAergic pathway:** Activity mediated via benzodiazepine site on GABA-A receptor
- **Not serotonergic:** Mechanism does not appear to involve serotonin system

**Active Compounds Implicated:**
- Extract characterized to contain: homoorientin, orientin, vitexin, isovitexin
- Specific compound(s) responsible not isolated
- Likely synergistic effect of multiple flavonoids

**Clinical Relevance:**
- Explains comparable efficacy to benzodiazepines (oxazepam, midazolam) in clinical trials
- Mechanism similar to benzodiazepines but without memory impairment
- Suggests potential for interaction with other GABAergic medications

## Bioavailability and Pharmacokinetics

### Current State of Knowledge

**Critical Gap:** No pharmacokinetic data available for Passiflora incarnata in humans.

### Missing Data

**Absorption:**
- No data on gastrointestinal absorption of flavonoid constituents
- Unknown bioavailability percentages
- Absorption rate not characterized

**Distribution:**
- No data on tissue distribution
- Protein binding unknown
- Volume of distribution not measured

**Metabolism:**
- Metabolic pathways not characterized
- First-pass metabolism extent unknown
- Active metabolites (if any) not identified

**Elimination:**
- Plasma clearance not measured
- Half-life unknown
- Excretion routes not characterized

### Bioavailability Challenges

**Flavonoid-Specific Issues:**
- **C-glycosides** (vitexin, isovitexin, orientin, homoorientin) have different absorption characteristics than O-glycosides
- **Solubility variability:** Different compounds have varying water/lipid solubility
- **Gut microbiome role:** May metabolize flavonoids before absorption
- **Individual variation:** Gut flora differences may affect bioavailability

**Implications:**
- In vitro and in vivo (animal) effects may not correlate to clinical effects
- Optimal dosing difficult to determine without PK data
- Species differences (mouse vs. human) may affect translation of preclinical findings

### EMA Assessment (2014)

**Quote from EMA report:** "No pharmacokinetic data available regarding passionflower herbal substances/preparations"

**Noted Issues:**
- Bioavailability unknowns affect clinical correlation
- Metabolism questions unresolved
- Species differences complicate extrapolation from animal studies

**Regulatory Implication:** Despite PK gap, traditional use evidence sufficient for approval under Traditional Use framework (does not require full PK characterization).

## Extract Ratios and Equivalence

### Clinical Trial Extract Specifications

**Akhondzadeh et al. 2001 (GAD study):**
- **Dose:** 45 drops daily
- **Extract ratio:** Not specified
- **Standardization:** Not reported

**Movafegh et al. 2008 (preoperative anxiety):**
- **Product:** Passipy IranDarouk
- **Dose:** 500 mg
- **Extract ratio:** Not specified
- **Standardization:** Not reported

**Dantas et al. 2017 (dental anxiety):**
- **Dose:** 260 mg
- **Extract ratio:** Not specified
- **Standardization:** Not reported

**Harit et al. 2024 (stress/sleep):**
- **Product:** SIVI (aerial parts extract)
- **Dose:** 600 mg daily
- **Extract ratio:** Not specified
- **Standardization:** Not reported

### Problem: Poor Extract Characterization

**Miroddi et al. 2013 Critique:**
- "Poor details regarding drug extract ratio" in clinical studies
- Limits reproducibility
- Makes dose equivalence calculations difficult
- Hampers comparison between studies

### Regulatory Extract Specifications

**WHO Monograph Equivalences:**
- **Tincture 1:8:** 1-4 ml = approximately 0.125-0.5 g crude herb
- **Liquid extract 1:1:** 0.5-1 ml = approximately 0.5-1 g crude herb

**Dose Equivalence Challenge:**
- 45 drops ≈ 2.25 ml (assuming 20 drops/ml)
- If 1:8 tincture: equivalent to ~0.28 g crude herb (very low)
- If 1:1 extract: equivalent to ~2.25 g crude herb (moderate)
- **Unknown actual ratio makes equivalence calculation impossible**

## Quality Control Issues

### Standardization Variability

**Problems Identified:**
- Not all commercial products standardized to flavonoids
- Standardization marker varies (vitexin vs. isovitexin)
- Some products may contain minimal active compounds
- Batch-to-batch variation in unstandardized products

**Best Practice:**
- **Standardization to:** 3.5% flavonoids as vitexin (or 2.5-4% as isovitexin)
- **Minimum acceptable:** European Pharmacopoeia standard (1.5% flavonoids for crude herb, 2.0% for extracts)

### Analytical Verification

**HPLC Required:**
- Confirms presence of marker compounds
- Quantifies flavonoid content
- Detects adulteration
- Ensures batch consistency

**Certificates of Analysis:**
- Should include HPLC fingerprint
- Quantify vitexin/isovitexin content
- Verify European Pharmacopoeia compliance
- Test for contaminants (pesticides, heavy metals)

## Other Potentially Active Compounds

### Alkaloids (Controversial)

**Historical Claims:**
- Small amounts of β-carboline alkaloids (harman, harmalin, harmine) reported in older literature
- **Current consensus:** These are either absent or present in trace amounts insufficient for pharmacological activity
- **Not relevant** to clinical anxiolytic effects

### Maltol
- Identified in some analyses
- Pharmacological significance unclear
- Not used for standardization

### Unidentified Compounds
- Full phytochemical profile not completely characterized
- Possible synergistic compounds beyond major flavonoids
- Whole-plant extract may have different activity profile than isolated flavonoids

## Compound-Activity Relationships

### Known Relationships

**GABAergic anxiolytic activity:**
- Associated with flavonoid-rich extracts
- Confirmed in HPLC-characterized preparation containing vitexin, isovitexin, orientin, homoorientin
- Individual compound contributions not isolated

**Sleep effects:**
- Associated with whole extracts
- Specific compounds responsible unknown

**Dental/surgical anxiety:**
- Studies used whole extracts
- Flavonoid content assumed but often not verified

### Unknown Relationships

**Questions:**
1. Do vitexin and isovitexin have equivalent activity?
2. Are orientin and homoorientin necessary for full effect?
3. Is activity synergistic (requiring multiple compounds)?
4. Are there minor compounds essential for activity?

**Implication:** Whole-plant extracts standardized to total flavonoids are safest approach until individual compound studies are conducted.

## Formulation Considerations

### Extract Types Used Clinically

**Liquid Extracts (Tinctures):**
- Traditional preparations
- Alcohol-based (25-45%)
- Used in Akhondzadeh trials (drops)

**Dry Extracts:**
- Capsules/tablets
- Standardized to flavonoid content
- Used in Movafegh, Dantas, Harit trials

**Herbal Tea:**
- Low-dose preparation
- Used in Ngan & Conduit 2011 (sleep quality)
- Less standardized

### Optimal Preparation

**For Anxiolytic Effect:**
- Standardized dry extract: 3.5% flavonoids as vitexin
- Dose: 260-600 mg
- Alternative: Liquid extract 1:1 or 1:8 equivalent

**For Sleep:**
- Standardized extract: 600 mg at bedtime
- Or tea preparation for mild sleep issues

## Active Compounds Bottom Line

**Primary Active Constituents:** C-glycosyl flavonoids (vitexin, isovitexin, orientin, homoorientin)

**Mechanism:** GABAergic, mediated via benzodiazepine binding site on GABA-A receptor (confirmed in vivo)

**Standardization:** Minimum 1.5% total flavonoids as vitexin (EP); commercial products often 3.5%

**Critical Gap:** No human pharmacokinetic data (absorption, distribution, metabolism, elimination all unknown)

**Quality Control:** HPLC verification essential; standardization to flavonoid content recommended

**Extract Ratio Problem:** Most clinical trials did not report extract ratios or standardization, limiting reproducibility

**Whole-Plant Superiority:** Until individual compound studies conducted, whole-plant extracts standardized to total flavonoids are the evidence-based choice.
