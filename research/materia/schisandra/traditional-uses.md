# Schisandra - Traditional Uses

## Traditional Medicine Systems

### Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)

**Chinese Name:** Wu Wei Zi (五味子) - "Five Flavor Fruit"
- References all five basic tastes: sour, sweet, salty, bitter, pungent

**Pharmacopoeial Recognition:**
- **Chinese Pharmacopoeia (2005):** Recognizes both *Schisandra chinensis* and *S. sphenanthera* as two different raw materials since 2005
- Official inclusion demonstrates long-standing traditional use

**Traditional Preparation:**
- **Crude drug decoction:** 1.5-6 g/day (traditional dose range)
- **Powdered crude drug (boiled tea):** 500-1500 mg/day
- Dried ripe fruit used as primary material

**Traditional Indications (TCM):**
Based on pharmacopoeial monographs and traditional references, schisandra has been used for:
- Tonifying and strengthening (adaptogenic effects)
- Supporting liver function
- Promoting vitality and endurance
- Mental clarity and focus
- General weakness and fatigue

**Source:** Chinese Pharmacopoeia Commission, 2005

---

### Korean Traditional Medicine

**Korean Name:** Omija (오미자)
- Also means "five flavors"

**Traditional Use:**
- Long history of use in Korean traditional medicine
- **Korean Pharmacopoeia (2002):** Official monograph for Schisandra fruit
- Traditional decoctions and preparations

**Modern Korean Applications:**
- Continues to be used in combination formulas (e.g., Yoon et al., 2022 study with Omija + soybean mixture)
- Popular as herbal tea and health beverage

**Source:** Korea Food and Drug Administration, Korean Pharmacopoeia, 2002

---

### Japanese Traditional Medicine (Kampo)

**Recognition:**
- **Japanese Pharmacopoeia (2006):** Official monograph for Schisandra fruit
- Indicates traditional use in Kampo medicine system

**Source:** Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare (Japan), 2006

---

### Russian Traditional Medicine

**Recognition:**
- **Russian Pharmacopoeia (1990):** Official monograph for Schisandra fruit
- Long history of use in Russian Far East where plant is native

**Traditional Applications:**
Based on Russian ethnopharmacological use:
- Adaptogenic support
- Physical and mental performance enhancement
- General strengthening tonic

**Source:** Ministry of Health (Russia), Russian Pharmacopoeia, 1990

## International Regulatory Recognition

### World Health Organization (WHO)

**WHO Monograph (2007):** "Schisandrae chinensis fructus"
- **Organization:** World Health Organization
- **Publication:** International Pharmacopoeia, Volume 3
- **Definition:** "Dried ripe fruit of *Schisandra chinensis* (Turcz.) Baill."
- **Significance:** First official internationally recognized monograph on Schisandra fruit
- **Location:** Geneva, Switzerland

**Source:** https://apps.who.int/iris/bitstream/handle/10665/42052/9789241547024_eng.pdf

---

### European Recognition

**European Pharmacopoeia (2008):** "Schisandrae chinensis fructus"
- **Organization:** European Directorate for the Quality of Medicines & HealthCare
- **First Appearance:** 6th edition, Supplement 6.3 (2008)
- **Current Edition:** 8th edition (2013, valid since 2014) - monograph remains in unchanged form
- **Significance:** Adoption into European Pharmacopoeia indicates recognition of traditional use and medicinal value

**Commission E Status:**
- **Note:** German Commission E did not evaluate Schisandra chinensis
- Commission E primarily assessed herbs used in traditional German phytotherapy
- Absence of monograph reflects geographic focus, not safety concerns

## Traditional Preparations and Forms

### Historical Forms

**Based on pharmacopoeial definitions and traditional references:**

1. **Crude Dried Fruit:**
   - Primary traditional form
   - Whole berries dried for preservation
   - Used in decoctions and teas

2. **Aqueous Decoction:**
   - Traditional water extraction
   - Boiled tea preparation
   - Dose: 1.5-6 g crude drug/day

3. **Powdered Crude Drug:**
   - Ground dried berries
   - Used for boiled tea
   - Dose: 500-1500 mg/day

## Traditional vs. Modern Understanding

### Traditional Concepts

**"Five Flavor Fruit" (Wu Wei Zi/Omija):**
- Traditional understanding based on taste profile
- Considered to balance multiple organ systems
- Used as adaptogen and tonic

### Modern Pharmacological Validation

**Contemporary research has identified mechanisms underlying traditional uses:**

1. **Adaptogenic Effects:**
   - Confirmed in systematic reviews (Rybnikář et al., 2019)
   - Network pharmacology analysis supports traditional use as exercise supplement (Hong et al., 2021)

2. **Hepatoprotective Effects:**
   - Traditional liver support use validated by:
     - 54 animal studies (Huang et al., 2025 meta-analysis)
     - Human trials showing improved liver enzymes (Chiu et al., 2013)
   - Mechanisms: Regulating cytochrome P450, antioxidant protection, reducing inflammation, promoting liver tissue regeneration (Zhu et al., 2019)

3. **Central Nervous System Stimulation:**
   - Traditional use for mental clarity supported by clinical trials on mental fatigue (7 trials, N=1,712)
   - Cognitive function improvement (Aslanyan et al., 2010)

4. **General Strengthening and Vitality:**
   - Traditional fatigue/weakness use supported by clinical evidence (5 trials, N=406)
   - Muscle strength improvement (Park et al., 2020: 7.7% increase, n=45)

**Source:** Rybnikář et al., 2019 (Česká a slovenská farmacie)

## Summary

Schisandra has a well-documented traditional use history across multiple Asian medical systems (Chinese, Korean, Japanese, Russian) spanning centuries. This traditional knowledge has been formally recognized through:

1. **Eight major pharmacopoeial monographs** (WHO, European, Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Russian, American)
2. **Traditional dose ranges preserved**: 1.5-6 g/day crude drug; 500-1500 mg/day powdered
3. **Traditional preparation methods**: Decoctions, boiled teas, dried fruit
4. **Modern validation**: Scientific research increasingly supports traditional uses for liver protection, mental/physical fatigue, adaptogenic effects, and vitality

The progression from regional traditional use to international pharmacopoeial recognition (WHO 2007, European 2008) demonstrates the bridge between traditional knowledge and contemporary evidence-based medicine.
