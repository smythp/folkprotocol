# Cordyceps - Safety Profile

## Regulatory Safety Status

### FDA GRAS Designation
**Status**: Generally Regarded As Safe (GRAS)
**Source**: [NCBI LiverTox Database](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK615916/)

**Significance**: This designation indicates that cordyceps is recognized by the FDA as safe for its intended uses based on a history of common use in food or on the results of research.

### Chinese FDA (CFDA) Approval
- **50+ medicines** with cultured cordyceps approved since 2002
- **2 dietary supplements** approved
- Regulatory oversight and post-market surveillance in China

**Source**: [NCBI Bookshelf - Cordyceps Monograph](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK92758/)

---

## Preclinical Toxicology Studies

### Comprehensive Toxicity Evaluation (Long 2021)

**Source**: [Journal of Ethnopharmacology](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33220357/)

#### Study Design
- **Test organism**: ICR mice, Chinese hamster lung cells
- **Methods**:
  - Chromosomal aberration testing
  - Ames bacterial mutation assay (5 bacterial strains)
  - Acute oral toxicity study
  - Micronucleus testing (bone marrow cells)

#### Doses Tested
- **Ames test**: 128-5,000 μg per plate
- **Acute toxicity**: Up to 20 g/kg oral administration

#### Key Findings

**LD50 (Lethal Dose 50%)**: >5 g/kg
- **Classification**: Non-toxic

**Maximum Tolerable Dose**: >20 g/kg
- No mortality within 14 days
- No tissue abnormalities observed

**Chromosomal Aberration**: No dose-dependent increase detected

**Micronucleus Testing**: No significant differences versus negative controls

**Conclusion**: "Cultivated cordyceps demonstrated non-toxic safety profile at high single doses via oral administration"

---

### Extreme Dose Testing

**Mice Study** (cited in NCBI Bookshelf):
- **Dose**: 80 g/kg/day intraperitoneally for 7 days
- **Result**: No fatality
- **Interpretation**: Extraordinarily high tolerance; well beyond any conceivable human dose

**Animal Safety Duration**: Safe for up to 3 weeks in extended studies

**Source**: [NCBI Bookshelf](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK92758/)

---

## Hepatotoxicity (Liver Safety)

### NCBI LiverTox Database Assessment (2023)

**Source**: [LiverTox - Cordyceps](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK615916/)

**Serum Aminotransferase Elevations**: NOT associated with elevations during therapy

**Clinically Apparent Liver Injury**: No documented cases

**Clinical Trials Review**: Multiple short- and long-term trials show:
- Adverse effects uncommon and minimal
- No mention of hepatotoxicity
- No ALT elevations reported

**Liver Histology (Animal Studies)**:
- No hepatic steatosis (fatty liver)
- No necrosis
- No inflammation
- No fibrosis
- Duration: Up to 3 weeks extended use

**Conclusion**: Cordyceps has an excellent liver safety profile with no evidence of hepatotoxicity.

---

## Clinical Trial Safety Data

### Long COVID Study (2025) - Most Detailed Safety Reporting

**Source**: [Acta Materia Medica](https://www.scienceopen.com/hosted-document?doi=10.15212/AMM-2024-0089)

**Sample Size**: 110 participants (55 cordyceps, 55 control), 12 weeks

**Treatment-Emergent Adverse Events (TEAEs)**:
- **Total patients with AEs**: 10 (17.2% overall)
- **Cordyceps group**: 6 patients (20.0%) with 12 TEAEs
- **Placebo group**: 4 patients (14.3%) with 4 TEAEs

**Severity**: No severe adverse events related to interventions

**Interpretation**: Slightly higher TEAE count in cordyceps group, but no severe events and rates comparable to placebo, suggesting good tolerability.

---

### Immune Function Study (Ontawong 2024)

**Source**: [Scientific Reports](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38580687/)

**Sample Size**: 20 participants, 8 weeks
**Dose**: 2.85 mg cordycepin per serving (C. militaris beverage)

**Comprehensive Safety Monitoring**:
- ✓ No liver toxicity (hepatic enzymes normal)
- ✓ No kidney toxicity (renal function normal)
- ✓ No blood component abnormalities
- ✓ Blood sugar indices: No differences between groups
- ✓ Lipid indices: No differences between groups

**Conclusion**: "FCM can potentially be developed as immune-boosting supplement without toxicity"

---

### Exercise Performance Study (Chen 2010)

**Sample Size**: 20 elderly subjects (ages 50-75), 12 weeks

## Contraindications

### Evidence-Based Contraindications
**None identified in medical literature.**

**Rationale**:
- No documented serious adverse events across thousands of participants in clinical trials
- FDA GRAS status supports general safety
- No organ toxicity demonstrated in comprehensive toxicology studies
- No drug interaction studies showing harmful effects

---

### Theoretical Considerations (Precautionary)

#### 1. Autoimmune Conditions (Theoretical)
**Concern**: Cordyceps demonstrates immune-stimulating properties (enhanced NK cells, cytokine production, CD4+ T cells)

**Theoretical Risk**: Could potentially exacerbate autoimmune conditions

**Evidence Status**:
- No clinical reports of autoimmune exacerbation
- Immune effects appear regulatory rather than purely stimulatory (both pro- and anti-inflammatory cytokines affected)
- IL-10 (anti-inflammatory, regulatory) production noted in mechanistic studies

**Clinical Guidance**:
- No documented contraindication in literature
- Practitioners may choose to exercise caution in severe autoimmune disease
- Monitoring recommended if used in this population

---

#### 2. Bleeding Disorders (Theoretical, Traditional Basis)
**Traditional Note**: Classical TCM texts record cordyceps action to "stop bleeding" (止血), suggesting hemostatic rather than anticoagulant properties

**Theoretical Concern**: Some sources suggest potential anticoagulant effects

**Evidence Status**:
- **No clinical trials report increased bleeding**
- Traditional use suggests opposite effect (hemostatic)
- No documented bleeding complications in clinical literature

**Clinical Guidance**:
- No documented contraindication
- Theoretical caution in severe bleeding disorders or with anticoagulant medications (see drug interactions)
- No evidence requiring discontinuation before surgery

---

#### 3. Pregnancy and Lactation
**Evidence Status**: **No safety data available**

**Clinical Studies**: Pregnant and lactating women excluded from clinical trials (standard practice)

**Traditional Use**: Not specifically documented for pregnancy or postpartum in traditional sources

**Clinical Guidance**:
- **Avoid during pregnancy** (precautionary principle, not evidence-based contraindication)
- **Avoid during lactation** (no safety data)
- Standard approach for substances without pregnancy safety data

---

## Drug Interactions

### Documented Interactions
**None reported in clinical literature.**

**Note**: Absence of documented interactions across multiple trials involving various patient populations and medications.

---

### Theoretical Drug Interactions (Monitoring Recommended)

#### 1. Immunosuppressant Medications (Theoretical)
**Examples**: Cyclosporine, tacrolimus, corticosteroids, chemotherapy

**Mechanism**: Cordyceps demonstrates immune-stimulating effects; could theoretically reduce immunosuppressant efficacy

**Clinical Evidence**:
- **CONTRARY evidence exists**: Bailing Capsule (cordyceps preparation) shows benefits in post-renal transplant patients (improved function, reduced infection) who are necessarily on immunosuppressants
- Lung cancer trials involved patients on chemotherapy; cordyceps enhanced rather than interfered with treatment

**Interpretation**:
- Immune "stimulation" may be more accurately "modulation" or "regulation"
- Clinical evidence does not support harmful interaction
- May actually complement immunosuppressive regimens by reducing infection risk

**Clinical Guidance**: No documented contraindication; monitoring immune markers and transplant function reasonable if used

---

#### 2. Anticoagulant/Antiplatelet Medications (Theoretical)
**Examples**: Warfarin, aspirin, clopidogrel, heparin

**Mechanism**: Theoretical concern about additive bleeding risk

**Clinical Evidence**:
- **No bleeding complications reported** in any clinical trials
- Traditional use suggests hemostatic (anti-bleeding) rather than pro-bleeding effects
- No case reports of interaction

**Clinical Guidance**: Theoretical caution; no documented interaction requiring avoidance

---

#### 3. Antidiabetic Medications (Theoretical)
**Mechanism**: Cordyceps may have blood glucose-lowering effects

**Clinical Evidence**:
- Safety study (Ontawong 2024) showed **no differences in blood sugar indices** versus placebo
- Diabetic nephropathy trials (0.5-2 g TID with ACE inhibitors/ARBs) report no glycemic complications

**Clinical Guidance**: No documented interaction; blood glucose monitoring standard practice in diabetic patients regardless

---

#### 4. Antihypertensive Medications (Theoretical)
**Mechanism**: Potential blood pressure effects via various mechanisms

**Clinical Evidence**: No documented interactions or blood pressure complications in clinical trials

**Clinical Guidance**: Routine blood pressure monitoring; no documented interaction

---

## Special Populations

### Elderly (Ages 50-75+)
**Safety Evidence**: **Excellent**

**Studies**:
- Chen 2010: 20 elderly subjects, 1 g/day × 12 weeks, no adverse events
- Chinese 2003 study: 37 elderly subjects, 3 g/day × 6 weeks, effective and safe

**Conclusion**: Well-tolerated in elderly population; age-related vulnerability not a concern

---

### Chronic Kidney Disease
**Safety Evidence**: **Excellent, extensive**

**Studies**:
- Meta-analysis: 1,310 patients with renal dysfunction, no adverse events
- Hemodialysis meta-analysis: 655 patients, no complications
- Diabetic nephropathy use: 0.5-2 g TID, safe

**Conclusion**: Particularly strong safety profile in renal disease; one of the primary evidence-based indications

---

### Cancer Patients (Adjuvant Therapy)
**Safety Evidence**: **Good**

**Studies**:
- Lung cancer meta-analysis: 928 patients receiving cordyceps with chemotherapy/radiation
- **Benefit**: Reduced chemotherapy side effects (myelosuppression, leukopenia, radiation pneumonitis)
- No safety concerns during concurrent chemotherapy

**Conclusion**: Safe as adjuvant; may improve tolerability of conventional cancer treatment

---

### COPD and Respiratory Disease
**Safety Evidence**: **Good**

**Studies**:
- Meta-analysis: 1,238 COPD patients (GOLD stages 2-3)
- No serious adverse events

**Conclusion**: Safe in chronic respiratory disease

---

### Long COVID and Post-Viral Fatigue
**Safety Evidence**: **Good**

**Studies**:
- 110 Long COVID patients, 12 weeks
- 20.0% TEAEs in cordyceps group vs. 14.3% in placebo
- No severe events

**Conclusion**: Well-tolerated in post-viral fatigue syndromes

## Mutagenicity and Carcinogenicity

### Ames Bacterial Mutation Assay (Long 2021)
**Test Strains**: 5 bacterial strains (standard mutagenicity screening)
**Dose Range**: 128-5,000 μg per plate

**Result**: **No mutagenic activity detected**

### Chromosomal Aberration Testing (Long 2021)
**Test System**: Chinese hamster lung cells

**Result**: **No dose-dependent increase in chromosomal aberrations**

### Long-Term Carcinogenicity Studies
**Status**: No long-term animal carcinogenicity studies identified in literature

**Interpretation**:
- No mutagenic activity suggests low carcinogenic risk
- Absence of long-term studies represents data gap
- Historical use and clinical trial safety support low risk
- Anticancer properties demonstrated in mechanistic studies

---

## Dose-Dependent Safety

### Clinical Trial Dose Range: 0.5-6 g/day
**Lowest effective dose**: 1 g/day Cs-4 (exercise performance)
**Typical dose range**: 3-4.5 g/day
**Higher therapeutic doses**: 6 g/day (lung cancer, severe renal disease)
**Traditional upper limit**: 9 g/day (Chinese Pharmacopoeia)

**Safety Margin**:
- Preclinical LD50 >5,000 mg/kg (>5 g/kg)
- Human dose ~6 g/day for 70 kg person = ~0.086 g/kg
- Safety margin: >58-fold between high therapeutic dose and LD50
- Animal maximum tolerable dose >20 g/kg = >232-fold safety margin

**Conclusion**: Extremely wide safety margin between therapeutic and toxic doses

---

## Duration of Safe Use

### Clinical Trial Durations
- **6 weeks**: Chinese 2003 study (elderly, 3 g/day)
- **8 weeks**: Ontawong 2024 (immune, 2.85 mg cordycepin), Chen 2010 (exercise, 1 g/day)
- **12 weeks**: Long COVID study (110 participants)
- **21 days/cycle, 3-4 cycles**: Lung cancer adjuvant (6 g/day)

### Animal Studies
- **Up to 3 weeks**: Safe in extended animal toxicology studies
- **7 days at extreme dose**: 80 g/kg/day IP, no fatality

### Long-Term Use Data Gap
**Evidence**: Clinical trials typically 6-12 weeks; limited data beyond 3 months
**Traditional Use**: Historical use suggests long-term safety, but systematic long-term human studies lacking
**Clinical Guidance**: Long-term use (>3 months) based on traditional precedent and short-term safety rather than controlled long-term studies

---

## Quality and Contamination Concerns

### Heavy Metals and Contaminants
**Study Data**: Not extensively reported in clinical trial literature

**Risk Factors**:
- Wild-harvested cordyceps from polluted areas may accumulate heavy metals
- Cultivated cordyceps under controlled conditions lower contamination risk

**Quality Assurance**:
- Cs-4, JinShuiBao, and other Chinese Pharmacopoeia-recognized products subject to regulatory testing
- Choose products from reputable manufacturers with third-party testing

### Adulteration Concerns
**Issue**: Products labeled "Cordyceps sinensis" may contain:
- C. militaris (higher cordycepin, different profile)
- Other Paecilomyces species
- Non-cordyceps mycelium

**Quality Control**: Advanced markers (CSWP-40, CCP) provide better authentication than traditional adenosine/ergosterol alone

**Clinical Significance**: Adulteration affects consistency but not necessarily safety

---

## Summary: Safety Profile Assessment

### Overall Safety Rating: **Excellent**

**Strengths**:
✓ FDA GRAS status
✓ No serious adverse events across 4,000+ participants in multiple trials
✓ LD50 >5 g/kg (non-toxic classification)
✓ No hepatotoxicity, nephrotoxicity, or hematologic toxicity
✓ No mutagenic or chromosomal aberration activity
✓ Safe in vulnerable populations (elderly, renal disease, cancer patients)
✓ Minimal and non-severe adverse events
✓ Wide safety margin (>58-fold) between therapeutic and toxic doses
✓ 1,000+ years of traditional use without documented toxicity

**Data Gaps**:
- No pregnancy/lactation safety data
- Limited long-term studies (>3 months)
- No systematic heavy metal testing data in literature
- No long-term carcinogenicity studies

**Contraindications**:
- **Absolute**: None documented
- **Precautionary**: Pregnancy and lactation (lack of data, not evidence of harm)
- **Theoretical monitoring**: Autoimmune disease, bleeding disorders (no documented problems)

**Drug Interactions**:
- **Documented**: None
- **Theoretical monitoring**: Immunosuppressants, anticoagulants (no documented problems; some evidence of benefit in combination)

---

## Clinical Recommendations Based on Safety Data

1. **General Use**: Excellent safety profile supports use at doses of 3-6 g/day for most adults

2. **Elderly**: Particularly well-studied and safe population

3. **Renal Disease**: Strong safety evidence; one of the primary indications

4. **Cancer Adjuvant**: Safe with chemotherapy; may reduce treatment side effects

5. **Pregnancy/Lactation**: Avoid due to lack of safety data

6. **Long-Term Use**: Based on traditional precedent and short-term trial safety; systematic long-term data needed

7. **Quality**: Choose standardized products (Cs-4, JinShuiBao) with clinical trial evidence and regulatory oversight

8. **Monitoring**: Routine monitoring appropriate in any chronic condition; no cordyceps-specific monitoring required based on safety data

**Adverse Event Reporting**: Any unexpected adverse events should be reported to healthcare providers and regulatory authorities to contribute to pharmacovigilance databases.
