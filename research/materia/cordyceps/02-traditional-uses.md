# Cordyceps - Traditional Uses

Historical documentation and traditional medicine applications across cultures.

---

## Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)

### Official Recognition
**Chinese Pharmacopoeia:** Official herbal drug since **1964**

**Species:** Ophiocordyceps sinensis (Berk.) Sacc. / Cordyceps sinensis

**Definition:** Dried fungus-larva complex of Ophiocordyceps sinensis infecting Hepialus larvae

**Source:** Chinese Pharmacopoeia Commission ([NCBI Books NBK92758](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK92758/))

---

### Common Names

**Chinese:**
- **Dong Chong Xia Cao** (冬虫夏草) - "Winter Worm Summer Grass"

**Tibetan:**
- **Yarsagumba**

**English:**
- Caterpillar Fungus
- Winter Worm Summer Grass

**Note:** Only Cordyceps sinensis officially recorded in Chinese Pharmacopoeia; C. militaris not traditionally recognized in official TCM monographs.

---

### Earliest Recorded Mention

**Source:** Ben Cao Bei Yao (本草备要)
**Date:** **1694 CE** (Qing Dynasty)
**Significance:** Earliest documented mention of Cordyceps in traditional Chinese medical texts

**Source:** Lin & Li 2011, Herbal Medicine monograph

---

### Traditional Properties (TCM Classification)

**Taste (Wei):** Sweet (甘)

**Nature (Xing):** Neutral (平)

**Meridians/Channels (Gui Jing):**
- **Lung meridian** (肺经)
- **Kidney meridian** (肾经)

**Source:** Chinese Pharmacopoeia 1964

---

### Traditional Actions (TCM Functions)

**Primary Functions:**

1. **Replenish Kidney (补肾 - Bu Shen)**
   - Tonifies kidney yang and kidney yin
   - Traditional use for sexual dysfunction, infertility, impotence
   - "Hyposexuality" (low libido)

2. **Soothe Lung (润肺 - Run Fei)**
   - Moistens and nourishes lung tissue
   - Traditional use for chronic cough
   - Respiratory weakness

3. **Stop Bleeding (止血 - Zhi Xue)**
   - Hemostatic properties
   - Traditional use for various bleeding disorders

4. **Eliminate Phlegm (化痰 - Hua Tan)**
   - Resolves phlegm accumulation
   - Traditional use for productive cough with thick sputum

**Source:** Chinese Pharmacopoeia traditional functions

---

### Traditional Clinical Applications

**Historical Indications:**

1. **Fatigue and Weakness**
   - General debility
   - Qi deficiency states
   - Post-illness recovery

2. **Chronic Cough**
   - Lung qi deficiency
   - Chronic bronchitis patterns

3. **Hyposexuality**
   - Low libido
   - Sexual dysfunction
   - Infertility (both sexes)

4. **Asthenia After Severe Illness**
   - Convalescence support
   - Recovery from chronic disease
   - Post-surgical weakness

5. **Renal Dysfunction and Renal Failure**
   - Kidney deficiency patterns
   - Support for chronic kidney disease

**Source:** Chinese Pharmacopoeia clinical applications

---

### Traditional Preparations (TCM)

**Decoction (Tang):**
- **Dosage:** 3-9 g crude drug
- **Method:** Boiled in water to make medicinal tea
- **Traditional standard dosing range**

**Source:** Chinese Pharmacopoeia traditional dosing

**Form:** Typically used as whole dried fungus-larva complex, not powdered

**Note:** Traditional preparation was the actual wild-harvested Cordyceps sinensis fungus attached to caterpillar larvae, not cultivated mycelium or extracts.

## Absence in Other Traditional Medicine Systems

### European Phytotherapy

**Commission E (German):**
- **Status:** No monograph published
- **Reason:** Commission E primarily assessed herbs used in German phytotherapy
- Cordyceps is primarily an Asian medicinal fungus
- No traditional use in European herbal medicine

**Source:** Commission E monograph status review

---

### World Health Organization

**WHO Monograph:**
- **Status:** No specific WHO monograph found as of 2025
- Primarily documented in Chinese traditional medicine sources
- Not part of WHO Traditional Medicine Program monograph series

**Source:** WHO monograph status review

---

### Absence in Ayurveda

**No Traditional Ayurvedic Use:**
- Cordyceps (Ophiocordyceps sinensis) is NOT found in classical Ayurvedic texts
- Native to Tibetan Plateau and high-altitude regions of China (3,000-5,000m elevation)
- Not part of Indian subcontinent traditional materia medica
- Confusion may arise from modern marketing, but Cordyceps has no documented Ayurvedic lineage

**Geographic Distribution:**
- Tibetan Plateau
- Western China (Qinghai, Tibet, Sichuan, Yunnan, Gansu)
- Nepal (high elevations)
- Bhutan (high elevations)
- Not native to India or traditional Ayurvedic regions

## Traditional Sourcing and Cultural Context

### Wild Harvesting

**Traditional Source:**
- Hand-collected from high-altitude grasslands (3,000-5,000m)
- Tibetan Plateau primary source region
- Harvested in late spring/early summer when fruiting body emerges from mummified caterpillar

**Economic Impact:**
- Wild yield decreased **>90% in past 25 years**
- Price reached approximately **US$25,000/kg in 2007**
- Extreme scarcity led to development of cultivated alternatives

**Source:** Chinese Pharmacopoeia notes; NCBI Herbal Drug monograph

---

### Modern Context: Cultivated Alternatives

**Reason for Cultivation:**
- Wild Cordyceps sinensis nearly extinct due to over-harvesting
- Extreme cost prohibitive for general use
- Quality control difficult with wild material

**Cultivated Forms:**
- Fermented mycelium of related species (Paecilomyces hepiali, now Hirsutella sinensis)
- Cordyceps militaris (higher cordycepin content)
- Standardized commercial preparations (Cs-4, JinShuiBao, Bailing)

**Regulatory Approval:**
- Over **50 medicines and 2 dietary supplements** with cultured Cordyceps approved by Chinese FDA since 2002
- Cs-4 recognized by Chinese Pharmacopoeia as acceptable alternative

**Source:** NCBI Herbal Drug monograph 2011

---

## Traditional vs. Modern Preparations

### Traditional (Pre-1960s)
- **Whole dried fungus-larva complex**
- **Wild-harvested only**
- **Dosing:** 3-9 g as decoction
- **Extremely expensive and rare**

### Modern TCM (1964-Present)
- **Cultivated mycelium extracts**
- **Standardized capsules and tablets**
- **Fermentation products (Cs-4, etc.)**
- **Accessible pricing**
- **Quality-controlled for active compounds**

**Key Shift:** From whole wild fungus to cultivated mycelium extracts with standardized bioactive content (polysaccharides, adenosine, cordycepin).

---

## Cultural Significance

### Tibetan Medicine
- Known as **Yarsagumba** in Tibet
- Major economic resource for Tibetan communities
- Traditional use similar to TCM applications
- Critical income source for high-altitude herders

### Status Symbol
- Historically reserved for emperors and elite classes in China
- Extreme rarity and cost made it inaccessible to common people
- Modern cultivation has democratized access
