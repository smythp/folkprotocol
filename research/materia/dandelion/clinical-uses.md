# Clinical Uses - Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale)

## Human Clinical Evidence

### Diuretic Effects
**Study:** Clare BA, Conroy RS, Spelman K. The Diuretic Effect in Human Subjects of an Extract of Taraxacum officinale Folium over a Single Day. J Altern Complement Med. 2009;15(8):929-934.

**Study Design:** Pilot study over 4 days with healthy female volunteers aged 18-65

**Sample Size:** 17 subjects completed (28 initially enrolled; 11 withdrew due to difficulty with measurement procedures)

**Preparation & Dosage:**
- Fresh leaf ethanolic extract (1 g to 1 mL of 95% EtOH menstruum)
- 8 mL three times daily at 8:00 AM, 1:00 PM, and 6:00 PM on treatment day

**Results:**
- Significant increase (p < 0.05) in urination frequency during 5-hour period following first dose
- Significant increase (p < 0.001) in excretion ratio (urine output/fluid intake) during 5-hour period after second dose
- Third dose produced no significant changes in measured parameters
- No adverse events reported

**Clinical Significance:** Ethanolic extract showed promise as diuretic in humans with good safety profile

### Dyshidrotic Hand Eczema
**Trial:** NCT00442091, Odense University Hospital

**Study Design:** Pilot clinical trial (results not published)

**Condition:** Dyshidrotic hand eczema (vesicular palmoplantar eczema)

**Eligibility:**
- Adult patients with dyshidrotic eczema for at least 1 year
- Negative patch test reactions to Compositae (tested within last 3 years)
- Normal renal and hepatic blood tests

**Treatment:** 20-30 days of dandelion juice

**Background:** Dandelion juice used in herbal medicine for at least 1000 years; one case report showed beneficial effect

**Status:** Results not published; only patients with negative patch tests to Compositae offered treatment

**Reference:** Paulsen E. Oral ingestion of dandelion juice for vesicular hand eczema: Treatment or challenge? Contact Dermatitis. 2020. doi:10.1111/cod.13473

## Regulatory Approvals & Traditional Medicine Use

### German Commission E (Root with Herb, 1984)
**Approved Indications:**
- Disturbances of bile flow
- Diuretic stimulation
- Loss of appetite
- Dyspepsia

**Dosage:** 3-4 g of the root or 10-15 drops of root tincture twice daily

**Preparations:** Infusion, decoction, or tincture

**Status:** Approved as nonprescription medicine

### German Commission E (Leaf, 1992)
**Approved Indications:**
- Loss of appetite
- Dyspepsia
- Bloating
- Flatulence

**Dosage:** 4-10 g of the leaves or 2-5 mL of leaf tincture 3 times daily

### European Medicines Agency (EMA) - Root (2016)
**Indications:**
- Relief of symptoms related to mild digestive disorders
- Diuretic

**Dosage:** 1.7-8.7 g in 250 mL water as infusion or decoction

**Traditional Use Status:** Used as traditional herbal medicinal product for at least 30 years including at least 15 years in EU

**Age Recommendation:** Adults and adolescents; children under 12 not established due to lack of data

**Duration Limit:** Maximum 2 weeks for leaf preparations due to lack of clinical safety studies

**Contraindications:**
- Obstruction of bile duct
- Cholangitis
- Liver disease
- Gallstones

**Adverse Effects:** Allergic reactions may occur; sesquiterpene lactones (taraxinic acid) can cause allergic contact dermatitis

**EMA Assessment:** "No data available on clinical studies; well-established use cannot be supported"

### European Medicines Agency (EMA) - Leaf
**Dosage:** 4-10 g dried leaves daily or by infusion

**Duration Limit:** Maximum 2 weeks

### Chinese Pharmacopoeia (2020 Edition)
**Status:** Currently recorded in official Chinese pharmacopoeia

**Notes:** Reflects traditional Chinese medicine use spanning centuries

## Critical Research Gap

**MAJOR LIMITATION:** Despite strong traditional use spanning at least 1000 years and regulatory approvals in multiple jurisdictions, there is essentially only ONE published human clinical trial (Clare et al. 2009 on diuretic effects with n=17).

All other evidence for dandelion's therapeutic effects comes from:
- In vitro cell studies
- Animal models
- Traditional use documentation
- Case reports

**Needed:** Well-controlled randomized clinical trials for hepatoprotective, antidiabetic, antimicrobial, and anticancer applications despite promising preclinical evidence.

## Recommended Daily Dose (General)
**Traditional Medicine Consensus:** 10-15 g daily (based on comprehensive review in Molecules 2023;28(13):5022)

## Preclinical Evidence (No Human Trials)

### Hepatoprotective Effects
**Animal Evidence:**
- Abd El-Ghffar et al. 2021: Acute on chronic liver failure in rats
- Sample: Male Wistar rats, 5 groups
- Dosages tested: 200 mg, 100 mg, 50 mg/kg body weight/day
- Best dose: 100 mg ethanolic root extract/kg b.w./day
- Results: All three doses significantly prevented liver damage
  - Reduced serum liver injury markers: AST, ALT, ALP, GGT, total bilirubin
  - Reduced renal tests: creatinine, urea
  - Reduced oxidative stress markers
- Mechanism: Antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities; taraxasterol modulates inflammatory and oxidative stress pathways

**In Vitro Evidence:**
- Jia et al. 2014: Hepatocytes and hepatitis B virus
- Concentration: 100 µg/mL
- Models: Chemically-injured rat hepatocytes, DHBV-infected duck hepatocytes, HBV-transfected HepG2.2.15 cells
- Results:
  - Markedly inhibited DHBV DNA replication
  - Reduced HBsAg and HBeAg levels with inhibition rates of 91.39% and 91.72% at 100 µg/mL
  - Protection rates of 42.2%, 34.6%, and 43.8% against D-galactosamine, thioacetamide, and tert-butyl hydroperoxide injury
- Mechanisms: Oxidative stress reduction; blocks protein synthesis and DNA replication (antiviral)

**Clinical Gap:** No clinical studies on hepatoprotective efficacy in humans despite strong preclinical evidence

### Antidiabetic Effects
**In Vitro Evidence - Study 1:**
- Murtaza et al. 2022: Comparative analysis of fresh vs shade-dried leaves
- Sample: In vitro enzyme inhibition assays
- Key findings:
  - Shade-dried leaves superior to fresh material
  - Aqueous extract demonstrated potent antidiabetic activity in dose-dependent manner
  - Inhibited α-amylase and α-glucosidase enzymes involved in carbohydrate metabolism
  - Potential to suppress post-prandial glucose rise

**In Vitro Evidence - Study 2:**
- Li et al. 2021: Water extract of dandelion root (DRE-w)
- Composition: Polysaccharides 63.92 ± 1.82 mg/g; Total flavonoid 2.57 ± 0.06 mg/g
- Key findings:
  - Inhibitory effects on α-glucosidase and α-amylase enzymes
  - Combined with Radix Astragali extract: significantly increased glucose consumption and intracellular glycogen content
  - Improved enzyme activity (hexokinase and pyruvate kinase) in insulin-resistant liver cells
  - Both individual and combined extracts relieved insulin resistance in experimental cell models

**Clinical Gap:** No completed human clinical trials examining effects on glucose in diabetic patients

### Anti-Inflammatory Effects
**In Vitro Evidence - Study 1:**
- Jeon et al. 2017: LPS-stimulated human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVECs)
- Preparation: Methanolic extract
- Results:
  - Pro-inflammatory cytokines TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6 induced by 9-, 157-, and 30-fold respectively
  - Cytokine mRNA expression levels markedly reduced by T. officinale pretreatment (p < 0.001)
  - Dramatically inhibited LPS-induced endothelial cell-monocyte interactions
  - Reduced vascular cell adhesion molecule-1 and monocyte chemoattractant protein-1
  - Suppressed LPS-induced nuclear translocation of NF-κB
- Mechanism: Attenuates LPS-induced endothelial cell activation by inhibiting the NF-κB pathway

**In Vitro Evidence - Study 2:**
- Koh et al. 2010: RAW 264.7 macrophage cells
- Preparation: Chloroform fraction of dandelion leaves
- IC50 values:
  - Nitric oxide (NO): 66.51 µg/mL
  - Prostaglandin E2 (PGE₂): 90.96 µg/mL
  - TNF-alpha: 114.76 µg/mL
  - IL-1beta: 171.06 µg/mL
- Results: Chloroform fraction significantly suppressed production of NO, PGE₂, TNF-α, and IL-1β in dose-dependent manner
- Mechanism: Anti-inflammatory properties via inactivation of the MAP kinase signal pathway

### Immunomodulatory Effects
**In Vitro Evidence:**
- Wang et al. 2021: Selenized polysaccharides from dandelion roots
- Polysaccharides tested:
  - DRP (native): 8700 Da molecular weight
  - sDRP-1: 7900 Da, selenium content 170 ± 1.13 µg/g
  - sDRP-2: 5600 Da, selenium content 710 ± 4.00 µg/g
- Results: Selenized polysaccharides possessed greater antioxidant and immunomodulatory activities than native polysaccharide
- Clinical potential: Could be used as new-type immunoenhancers

**Animal Evidence:**
- Li et al. 2024: LPS-induced acute enteritis in mice
- Sample: 48 mice randomly divided into 6 groups
- Results:
  - Down-regulated expression of TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β
  - Up-regulated IL-22 anti-inflammatory factor
  - Repaired intestinal barrier damaged by LPS exposure
  - Improved antioxidant capacity
  - Improved structure of intestinal flora
- Conclusion: Dandelion polysaccharides demonstrated effectiveness in both prevention and treatment of LPS-induced acute enteritis

### Antimicrobial Effects
**In Vitro Evidence:**
- Kenny et al. 2015: LC-SPE-NMR characterization
- Preparation: Methanol hydrophobic crude extract from dandelion root
- Bacterial strains tested: Staphylococcus aureus, MRSA, Bacillus cereus, additional Gram-positive and Gram-negative strains
- Results: Strongest inhibition of microbial growth against S. aureus, MRSA, and B. cereus strains
- Active compounds identified:
  - 9-hydroxyoctadecatrienoic acid
  - 9-hydroxyoctadecadienoic acid
  - Vanillin
  - Coniferaldehyde
  - p-methoxyphenylglyoxylic acid

**Review Evidence:**
- Sharifi-Rad et al. 2018: Ethnobotanical and antimicrobial review
- Findings: Aqueous and organic extracts exhibit promising in vitro antimicrobial activity
- Spectrum: Effective against fungi and Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria
- Limitation: Clinical evidence remains sparse and human safety data limited

**Clinical Gap:** Limited information on oral bioavailability, pharmacological activities, and safety in humans for antimicrobial applications

## Research Priorities

1. **Hepatoprotective Applications:** Designing large human clinical studies essential given emerging preclinical evidence
2. **Diabetes Management:** Extensive clinical research required to investigate pharmacological, physiological, and biochemical mechanisms
3. **Standardization:** Need for standardized extracts with defined bioactive compound concentrations
4. **Dosing Precision:** Precise prophylactic and treatment doses currently unknown for most conditions
5. **Safety Studies:** Reproductive toxicity, genotoxicity, and carcinogenicity testing needed
