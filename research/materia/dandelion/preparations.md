# Preparations - Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale)

## Traditional Preparations with Official Recognition

### Root Preparations

#### Root Tincture
**German Commission E (1984) Dosing:**
- 10-15 drops twice daily
- Crude root equivalent: 3-4 g

**Preparation Method:** Not specified in Commission E; traditionally uses dried root in alcohol menstruum

**Traditional Ratio:** Typically 1:5 (1 part root to 5 parts alcohol) in traditional Western herbalism

**Alcohol Percentage:** Usually 40-60% ethanol for root tinctures

**Indications:** Disturbances of bile flow, diuretic stimulation, loss of appetite, dyspepsia

**Administration:** Typically taken before meals for digestive complaints, diluted in water

#### Root Infusion or Decoction

**EMA Assessment Report (2016) Dosing:**
- 1.7-8.7 g root in 250 mL water

**Method - Infusion:**
- Pour boiling water over dried root
- Steep 10-15 minutes
- Strain

**Method - Decoction (Preferred for Root):**
- Place dried root in cold water
- Bring to boil
- Simmer 10-20 minutes
- Strain

**Rationale for Decoction:** Root material requires more vigorous extraction than leaves due to cellular structure

**Administration:** Drink as tea, multiple times daily

**Traditional Duration:** Maximum 2 weeks continuous use (EMA recommendation)

**Storage:** Prepare fresh daily; refrigerate if storing <24 hours

#### Root Infusion/Decoction (German Commission E)
**Dosing:** 3-4 g of the root

**Preparation:** As infusion or decoction

**Frequency:** Twice daily

**Indications:** Bile flow disturbances, diuretic, appetite loss, dyspepsia

### Leaf Preparations

#### Leaf Tincture

**German Commission E (1992) Dosing:**
- 2-5 mL three times daily
- Crude leaf equivalent: 4-10 g

**Traditional Ratio:** Typically 1:5 (1 part leaf to 5 parts alcohol)

**Alcohol Percentage:** Usually 25-40% ethanol for leaf tinctures (lower than root due to more water-soluble constituents)

**Indications:** Loss of appetite, dyspepsia, bloating, flatulence

**Administration:** Diluted in water, before meals for digestive effects

#### Fresh Leaf Ethanolic Extract (Clinical Trial)

**Clare et al. 2009 Preparation:**
- Ratio: 1:1 (1 g fresh leaf to 1 mL menstruum)
- Alcohol: 95% ethanol
- Dosing: 8 mL three times daily (at 8:00 AM, 1:00 PM, 6:00 PM)

**Results:** Significant diuretic effect demonstrated

**Advantages:**
- Preserves fresh plant constituents
- High alcohol extraction captures both polar and non-polar compounds
- Clinically validated dosing

**Preparation Method:**
- Use fresh dandelion leaves
- Chop or macerate
- Cover with 95% ethanol at 1:1 ratio by weight
- Macerate 2-4 weeks
- Press and filter
- Store in dark glass bottle

**Stability:** High alcohol content provides good preservation

#### Leaf Infusion (Tea)

**EMA Assessment Report (2016) Dosing:**
- 4-10 g dried leaves daily
- Prepared by infusion

**Method:**
- Place dried leaves in cup or teapot
- Pour boiling water over leaves
- Steep 10-15 minutes
- Strain

**Volume:** Typically 150-250 mL per serving

**Frequency:** 2-3 times daily

**Indications:** Diuretic, mild digestive disorders

**Duration Limit:** Maximum 2 weeks continuous use

**Taste:** Bitter; can add honey or lemon if desired

#### Shade-Dried Leaf Preparations (Enhanced Phytochemical Content)

**Murtaza et al. 2022 Findings:**
- Shade-dried leaves superior to fresh for phytochemical content
- Total phenols: 5833.12 ± 4.222 mg/100 g
- Total flavonoids: 188.84 ± 0.019 mg/100 g
- Ascorbic acid: 34.70 ± 0.026 mg/100 g

**Drying Method:**
- Harvest fresh leaves
- Spread in single layer in shaded, well-ventilated area
- Dry at room temperature away from direct sunlight
- Drying time: Several days to 1-2 weeks depending on humidity
- Store in airtight container away from light

**Advantages Over Fresh:**
- Higher antidiabetic activity in vitro
- Better phytochemical stability
- Easier storage and transport
- Year-round availability

**Applications:** Particularly suitable for antidiabetic preparations

### Fresh Juice

#### Dandelion Juice (Clinical Trial)

**NCT00442091 Protocol:**
- Indication: Dyshidrotic hand eczema
- Duration: 20-30 days
- Background: Used in herbal medicine for at least 1000 years

**Preparation Method:**
- Harvest fresh dandelion (leaves, roots, or whole plant)
- Wash thoroughly
- Juice using mechanical juicer or press
- Consume fresh or preserve

**Dosing:** Not specified in available trial documents

**Preservation Options:**
- Fresh: Use within hours
- Refrigerated: Use within 24-48 hours
- Frozen: Can freeze in ice cube trays for longer storage
- Lactofermented: Traditional preservation method

**Safety Note:** Only for individuals with negative patch test to Compositae family (Paulsen E, Contact Dermatitis 2020)

**Traditional Use Context:** At least 1000 years in herbal medicine

## Research-Based Preparations

### Root Water Extract (DRE-w)

**Li et al. 2021 Preparation:**
- Type: Water extract of dandelion root
- Composition:
  - Polysaccharides: 63.92 ± 1.82 mg/g
  - Total flavonoid: 2.57 ± 0.06 mg/g

**Applications:**
- Antidiabetic effects (α-glucosidase and α-amylase inhibition)
- Synergistic combination with Radix Astragali extract

**Preparation Method:**
- Dried dandelion root
- Water extraction (decoction or hot water infusion)
- Filter
- Can concentrate or use as prepared

**Advantages:**
- High polysaccharide content
- Water-soluble, safe
- No alcohol required
- Suitable for broader population (children, alcohol-sensitive)

**Clinical Potential:** Merits development as therapeutic agent or functional food for diabetes management

### Root Ethanolic Extract

**Abd El-Ghffar et al. 2021 (Hepatoprotective Study):**
- Type: Ethanolic dandelion root extract (TOERE)
- Doses tested: 200 mg, 100 mg, 50 mg/kg body weight/day in rats
- Optimal dose: 100 mg/kg body weight/day

**Human Equivalent Dose Calculation:**
- Rat to human conversion factor: ~1/6
- 100 mg/kg rat ≈ 16 mg/kg human
- For 70 kg adult: ~1120 mg (1.1 g) dandelion root extract daily

**Note:** This is theoretical conversion from animal study; human clinical trials needed to confirm

**Preparation Method:**
- Dried dandelion root powder
- Ethanol extraction (typically 70-95% ethanol)
- Maceration or percolation
- Filter and concentrate
- Standardize to marker compounds (e.g., taraxasterol)

**Applications:** Hepatoprotective effects, bile flow stimulation

**Advantages:** Concentrates lipophilic active compounds including taraxasterol

### Methanolic Extract

**Jeon et al. 2017 (Anti-inflammatory Study):**
- Type: Methanolic extract of Taraxacum officinale
- Cell model: LPS-stimulated human umbilical vein endothelial cells
- Activity: Inhibits NF-κB pathway, reduces cytokine expression (p < 0.001)

**Preparation Method:**
- Dried dandelion (whole plant, leaves, or root)
- Methanol extraction
- Filter
- Concentrate
- Can further fractionate

**Note:** Methanol not suitable for internal human consumption; research extract only

**Human Application:** Would require conversion to ethanol extract or aqueous extract while maintaining bioactive compound profile

**Advantages:** Broad spectrum extraction of polar and semi-polar compounds

### Chloroform Fraction (Leaves)

**Koh et al. 2010 (Anti-inflammatory Study):**
- Type: Chloroform fraction of dandelion leaves
- Cell model: RAW 264.7 macrophage cells
- IC50 values:
  - Nitric oxide (NO): 66.51 µg/mL
  - Prostaglandin E2 (PGE₂): 90.96 µg/mL
  - TNF-alpha: 114.76 µg/mL
  - IL-1beta: 171.06 µg/mL

**Preparation Method:**
- Dried dandelion leaves
- Initial extraction (usually with alcohol or water)
- Partition with chloroform
- Concentrate chloroform fraction

**Note:** Chloroform not suitable for internal human consumption; research extract only

**Human Application:** Would require identification of specific lipophilic anti-inflammatory compounds and alternative extraction method

**Mechanisms:** Inactivates MAP kinase signal pathway

**Advantages:** Concentrates potent lipophilic anti-inflammatory compounds

### Methanol Hydrophobic Crude Extract (Root)

**Kenny et al. 2015 (Antimicrobial Study):**
- Type: Methanol hydrophobic crude extract from dandelion root
- Activity: Strongest inhibition against S. aureus, MRSA, B. cereus
- Active compounds identified:
  - 9-hydroxyoctadecatrienoic acid
  - 9-hydroxyoctadecadienoic acid
  - Vanillin
  - Coniferaldehyde
  - p-methoxyphenylglyoxylic acid

**Preparation Method:**
- Dried dandelion root
- Methanol extraction
- Separation of hydrophobic fraction
- Further fractionation via LC-SPE-NMR

**Note:** Research preparation; not for direct human use

**Human Application:** Identification of specific antimicrobial compounds could lead to standardized extracts

**Advantages:** Selectively extracts antimicrobial fatty acid derivatives

### Aqueous Extract (Antidiabetic)

**Murtaza et al. 2022:**
- Type: Aqueous extract from shade-dried leaves
- Activity: Potent antidiabetic activity in vitro, dose-dependent manner
- Inhibits α-amylase and α-glucosidase enzymes

**Preparation Method:**
- Shade-dried dandelion leaves
- Hot water extraction (infusion or decoction)
- Filter
- Can concentrate or use as prepared

**Advantages:**
- Water-based, safe
- Suitable for all populations
- Easy preparation
- Preserves polysaccharides and water-soluble compounds

**Applications:** Diabetes management, functional foods

**Clinical Potential:** Further research needed before phytotherapy for diabetes can be advanced to clinic

### Polysaccharide Preparations

**Wang et al. 2021:**
- Type: Native and selenized polysaccharides from dandelion roots

**Native DRP:**
- Molecular weight: 8700 Da
- Preparation: Water extraction, precipitation, purification

**Selenized sDRP-1:**
- Molecular weight: 7900 Da
- Selenium content: 170 ± 1.13 µg/g
- Enhanced antioxidant and immunomodulatory activities

**Selenized sDRP-2:**
- Molecular weight: 5600 Da
- Selenium content: 710 ± 4.00 µg/g
- Greatest immunomodulatory activity

**Preparation Method:**
- Water extraction of dandelion root
- Polysaccharide precipitation (ethanol or ammonium sulfate)
- Purification (dialysis, column chromatography)
- Selenization (chemical modification with selenium compounds)
- Characterization (molecular weight, monosaccharide composition, selenium content)

**Applications:** Immunoenhancers, antioxidants

**Advantages:** Highly purified, well-characterized, enhanced bioactivity

**Clinical Potential:** Could be developed as new-type immunoenhancers

## Standardized Extract Recommendations

### For Hepatoprotective Applications

**Proposed Standardization:**
- Extract type: Ethanolic root extract (70-95% ethanol)
- Primary marker: Taraxasterol content
- Secondary markers: Total sesquiterpene lactones, total phenolics
- Functional assay: Antioxidant capacity (ORAC)

**Dosing (Theoretical from Animal Studies):**
- Target dose: ~1000-1500 mg extract per day (based on 100 mg/kg optimal in rats)
- Administration: Divided doses, 2-3 times daily
- Duration: 4-8 weeks with medical monitoring

**Quality Control:**
- Heavy metals, pesticides, microbial limits
- Taraxasterol quantification by HPLC
- Sesquiterpene lactone content (for allergen monitoring)

### For Diuretic Applications

**Proposed Standardization:**
- Extract type: Fresh or dried leaf ethanolic extract (25-95% ethanol)
- Primary marker: Total flavonoid content
- Secondary markers: Total phenolics, specific flavonoids (quercetin)
- Functional assay: Diuretic bioassay

**Dosing (Based on Clare et al. 2009):**
- 8 mL of 1:1 fresh leaf extract (95% ethanol) three times daily
- Or dried leaf equivalent: 4-10 g daily
- Duration: Short-term use (days to 2 weeks maximum)

**Quality Control:**
- Flavonoid quantification
- Microbial limits
- Allergen declaration

### For Antidiabetic Applications

**Proposed Standardization:**
- Extract type: Aqueous or hydroethanolic extract from shade-dried leaves
- Primary markers: Total flavonoid content, quercetin content
- Secondary marker: Total phenolic content
- Functional assay: α-glucosidase inhibition assay

**Dosing (Theoretical):**
- Based on in vitro potency and traditional dosing
- Target: 500-1000 mg shade-dried leaf extract 2-3 times daily before meals
- Duration: Ongoing with glucose monitoring

**Quality Control:**
- Quercetin and total flavonoid quantification
- α-glucosidase inhibition potency
- Heavy metals, microbial limits

**Clinical Need:** Extensive clinical research required before advancing to clinic

### For Immunomodulatory Applications

**Proposed Standardization:**
- Extract type: Polysaccharide-rich water extract from roots
- Primary marker: Total polysaccharide content
- Secondary markers: Molecular weight distribution, monosaccharide composition
- Functional assay: Cytokine modulation assay (in vitro)

**Dosing (Theoretical):**
- Based on polysaccharide content: 500-1000 mg polysaccharides daily
- Equivalent to ~8-15 g crude root water extract (based on 63.92 mg/g polysaccharides)
- Administration: Divided doses, 2-3 times daily

**Quality Control:**
- Polysaccharide quantification
- Molecular weight characterization
- Monosaccharide composition
- Endotoxin testing

**Advanced Form:** Selenized polysaccharides with defined selenium content for enhanced activity

## Combination Preparations

### Dandelion + Astragalus (Radix Astragali)

**Evidence:** Li et al. 2021 demonstrated synergistic antidiabetic effects

**Synergistic Benefits:**
- More significantly increased glucose consumption than either alone
- Enhanced intracellular glycogen content
- Improved enzyme activity in insulin-resistant liver cells

**Proposed Formulation:**
- Dandelion root water extract: 50% of formula
- Astragalus root water extract: 50% of formula
- Target polysaccharide content from both herbs
- Standardize flavonoids from dandelion

**Applications:** Diabetes management, metabolic support

**Dosing:** Based on individual herb dosing; clinical trials needed for optimal ratio

### Traditional European Bitter Formulas

**Common Combinations in Traditional Herbalism:**
- Dandelion root + Gentian root (bitter tonics)
- Dandelion leaf + Nettle leaf (diuretic combinations)
- Dandelion root + Burdock root (alterative/blood purifier combinations)
- Dandelion root + Milk thistle seed (hepatoprotective combinations)

**Rationale:** Synergistic or complementary actions on digestive and hepatobiliary systems

**Preparation:** Usually as tincture or decoction combinations

**Dosing:** Typically 1:1 or based on individual herb traditional doses

## Preparation Storage and Stability

### Dried Plant Material
**Storage Conditions:**
- Airtight container
- Cool, dark, dry location
- Away from direct sunlight
- Low humidity (<60% RH)

**Shelf Life:**
- Leaves: 1-2 years
- Root: 2-3 years
- Monitor for mold, discoloration, loss of aroma

**Stability Note:** Shade-dried leaves retain higher phytochemical content than sun-dried

### Tinctures
**Storage Conditions:**
- Dark glass bottles (amber or cobalt blue)
- Cool location (room temperature or cooler)
- Tightly sealed

**Shelf Life:**
- High alcohol tinctures (>40%): 3-5 years or longer
- Lower alcohol tinctures: 2-3 years
- Monitor for cloudiness, precipitation, off-odors

**Stability:** Alcohol provides good preservation; high alcohol content (95% in Clare et al. study) maximizes stability

### Aqueous Preparations (Infusions, Decoctions)
**Storage:**
- Refrigerate if not consumed immediately
- Use within 24-48 hours maximum

**Stability:** Limited; no preservatives, prone to microbial growth

**Recommendation:** Prepare fresh daily for optimal safety and potency

### Fresh Juice
**Storage:**
- Refrigerate immediately
- Use within hours for maximum nutrient content
- Can freeze in small portions (ice cube trays)

**Frozen Shelf Life:** 3-6 months

**Stability:** Vitamins and enzymes degrade rapidly; fresh is best

### Standardized Extracts (Commercial)
**Storage:**
- Follow manufacturer recommendations
- Typically cool, dry location
- Protect from light and moisture

**Shelf Life:** Usually 2-3 years from manufacture date

**Stability:** Depends on extract type and excipients; standardized products generally more stable than crude extracts

## Preparation Quality Issues

### Identification and Adulteration
**Common Issues:**
- Misidentification of Taraxacum species (>2,500 species exist)
- Substitution with other Asteraceae family plants
- Harvesting from contaminated areas (roadsides, industrial sites)

**Quality Control:**
- Botanical identification (microscopic, macroscopic)
- DNA barcoding for species verification
- Source documentation

### Contamination Concerns
**Heavy Metals:**
- Dandelion can accumulate heavy metals from soil
- Lead, cadmium, arsenic, mercury testing required
- Source from clean, tested growing areas

**Pesticides:**
- Testing for common herbicides (dandelion often targeted for lawn control)
- Organic certification preferred
- Wild-harvested from known clean areas

**Microbial Contamination:**
- Total aerobic count
- Yeast and mold count
- E. coli, Salmonella testing
- Particularly important for aqueous preparations

### Variability in Potency
**Sources of Variation:**
- Species differences (T. officinale vs. T. mongolicum vs. other species)
- Plant part used (leaf vs. root)
- Harvest timing (season, plant age)
- Growing conditions (soil, climate)
- Processing method (shade-dried vs. sun-dried, fresh vs. dried)
- Extraction method and solvent

**Solution:** Standardization to marker compounds and functional assays

## Preparation Selection Guide

### For Digestive Support (Appetite, Bloating, Dyspepsia)
**Recommended Preparation:** Leaf tincture or root decoction

**Dosing:**
- Leaf tincture: 2-5 mL three times daily before meals
- Root decoction: 3-4 g in 250 mL water, twice daily before meals

**Duration:** Up to 2 weeks

### For Diuretic Effect
**Recommended Preparation:** Fresh leaf ethanolic extract or dried leaf infusion

**Dosing:**
- Fresh leaf extract (1:1, 95% EtOH): 8 mL three times daily (clinically validated)
- Dried leaf infusion: 4-10 g in 250 mL water, 2-3 times daily

**Duration:** Days to maximum 2 weeks

### For Bile Flow Support (Cholagogue)
**Recommended Preparation:** Root tincture or decoction

**Dosing:**
- Root tincture: 10-15 drops twice daily
- Root decoction: 3-4 g in 250 mL water, twice daily

**Duration:** Up to 2 weeks

**Contraindications:** Bile duct obstruction, cholangitis, liver disease, gallstones

### For Hepatoprotective Support (Theoretical - No Human Trials)
**Recommended Preparation:** Ethanolic root extract standardized to taraxasterol

**Theoretical Dosing:** 1000-1500 mg extract daily in divided doses

**Duration:** 4-8 weeks with medical monitoring

**Clinical Gap:** Human clinical trials urgently needed

### For Blood Glucose Support (Theoretical - No Human Trials)
**Recommended Preparation:** Aqueous extract from shade-dried leaves standardized to flavonoids/quercetin

**Theoretical Dosing:** 500-1000 mg extract 2-3 times daily before meals

**Monitoring:** Blood glucose monitoring essential

**Clinical Gap:** Extensive clinical research required before clinical application

### For Immune Support (Theoretical - No Human Trials)
**Recommended Preparation:** Polysaccharide-rich water extract from roots

**Theoretical Dosing:** 500-1000 mg polysaccharides daily (≈8-15 g crude root water extract)

**Duration:** Ongoing as needed

**Clinical Gap:** Human immunomodulatory studies needed

## Practical Preparation Instructions for Home/Clinical Use

### Simple Leaf Tea (Infusion)
1. Measure 2-3 teaspoons (4-6 g) dried dandelion leaves
2. Place in teapot or cup
3. Pour 250 mL (1 cup) boiling water over leaves
4. Cover and steep 10-15 minutes
5. Strain
6. Drink 2-3 times daily, preferably before meals
7. Maximum 2 weeks continuous use

### Root Decoction
1. Measure 1-2 teaspoons (3-4 g) dried dandelion root (can chop or powder for better extraction)
2. Place in small pot with 250-300 mL cold water
3. Bring to boil
4. Reduce heat and simmer 10-20 minutes
5. Strain
6. Drink twice daily, preferably before meals
7. Can add honey or lemon to improve taste
8. Maximum 2 weeks continuous use

### Fresh Juice
1. Harvest fresh dandelion (whole plant or leaves) from clean, unsprayed area
2. Wash thoroughly to remove soil and debris
3. Chop roughly
4. Process through juicer or blend and press through cheesecloth
5. Consume fresh (within hours) or refrigerate (up to 48 hours)
6. Can freeze in ice cube trays for longer storage
7. Typical dose: 5-15 mL (1-3 teaspoons) 1-3 times daily

### Simple Tincture (Folk Method)
1. Fill clean jar 1/3 to 1/2 full with dried dandelion (leaf or root)
2. Cover completely with 40-60% alcohol (vodka works well for root; 25-40% for leaf)
3. For fresh leaf 1:1 extract (Clare et al. method): use equal weights fresh leaf and 95% alcohol
4. Cap tightly
5. Shake daily
6. Macerate 2-4 weeks
7. Strain through cheesecloth or muslin
8. Press plant material to extract remaining liquid
9. Bottle in dark glass bottles
10. Label with herb, date, alcohol percentage
11. Dosing: 2-5 mL for leaf tincture, 10-15 drops (≈0.5-0.75 mL) for root tincture, 2-3 times daily

## Safety Notes for All Preparations

1. **Allergy Screening:** Check for Compositae/Asteraceae family allergies before first use
2. **Source Quality:** Use clean, unsprayed, properly identified dandelion; avoid roadsides and industrial areas
3. **Duration Limits:** Maximum 2 weeks continuous use without medical supervision
4. **Drug Interactions:** Separate from ciprofloxacin; monitor if on antidiabetic drugs, diuretics, warfarin, or lithium
5. **Contraindications:** Avoid if bile duct obstruction, cholangitis, liver disease, or gallstones present
6. **Special Populations:** Not recommended in pregnancy, lactation, or children <12 years
7. **Storage:** Proper storage critical for safety and potency
8. **Medical Supervision:** Seek medical advice for chronic conditions, prolonged use, or if taking medications

## Future Development Priorities

1. **Clinical Validation:** Human clinical trials for hepatoprotective, antidiabetic, and immunomodulatory applications
2. **Standardization:** Develop standardized extracts with validated marker compounds
3. **Optimal Extraction:** Research to determine best extraction methods for each application
4. **Dosing Precision:** Establish evidence-based human dosing for each indication
5. **Combination Formulas:** Investigate synergistic combinations (e.g., with Astragalus)
6. **Bioavailability Enhancement:** Research methods to improve absorption of key compounds
7. **Novel Delivery Systems:** Develop sustained-release or targeted delivery preparations
8. **Safety Studies:** Long-term safety, reproductive toxicity, genotoxicity studies
9. **Commercial Viability:** Improve extraction yields to make clinical development economically attractive (as noted in Martinez et al. 2015)
