# Traditional Uses - Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale)

## Historical Duration of Use

**Documented History:** At least 1000 years in herbal medicine

**Source:** NCT00442091 clinical trial background, Odense University Hospital

**European Union Status:** Used as traditional herbal medicinal product for at least 30 years including at least 15 years in EU (EMA Assessment Report 2016)

## Traditional Medical Systems

### European Traditional Medicine

#### German Commission E Recognition (1984-1992)
The German Commission E, established in 1978, evaluated the safety and efficacy of herbs based on scientific evidence and traditional use. Dandelion received official recognition in two separate monographs:

**Root with Herb (1984):**
- Traditional uses: Disturbances of bile flow, diuretic stimulation, loss of appetite, dyspepsia
- Status: Approved as nonprescription medicine
- Traditional preparations: Infusion, decoction, tincture
- Traditional dosing: 3-4 g of the root or 10-15 drops of root tincture twice daily

**Leaf Preparation (1992):**
- Traditional uses: Loss of appetite, dyspepsia, bloating, flatulence
- Traditional dosing: 4-10 g of the leaves or 2-5 mL of leaf tincture 3 times daily

#### European Medicines Agency (EMA) Recognition (2016)
**Root Assessment:**
- Traditional indication: Relief of symptoms related to mild digestive disorders, diuretic
- Traditional use qualification: At least 30 years including at least 15 years in EU
- Traditional dosing: 1.7-8.7 g in 250 mL water as infusion or decoction
- Duration guidance: Maximum 2 weeks for leaf preparations

**Leaf Assessment:**
- Traditional dosing: 4-10 g dried leaves daily or by infusion
- Duration guidance: Maximum 2 weeks

**Traditional Contraindications:**
- Obstruction of bile duct
- Cholangitis
- Liver disease
- Gallstones (due to possible stimulation on bile secretion)

**Traditional Safety Notes:**
- Allergic reactions may occur
- Sesquiterpene lactones (taraxinic acid) can cause allergic contact dermatitis
- Not traditionally recommended for children under 12 years
- Traditional caution during pregnancy and lactation

#### European Pharmacopoeia
**Entry:** Taraxaci radix (dandelion root)

**Purpose:** Quality standards for root preparations

**Significance:** Inclusion in European Pharmacopoeia reflects long-standing traditional use across Europe and need for standardization

### Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)

#### Chinese Pharmacopoeia (2020 Edition)
**Status:** Currently recorded in official Chinese pharmacopoeia

**Significance:**
- Reflects centuries of traditional use in Chinese medicine
- Official recognition in modern Chinese medical practice
- Indicates ongoing integration into contemporary healthcare in China

**Note:** Specific traditional Chinese indications and dosing not detailed in bibliography; further research into TCM texts needed for comprehensive traditional use profile

### Asian Traditional Medicine (Beyond TCM)

**Taraxacum mongolicum:**
- Related species with documented traditional use in Mongolia and surrounding regions
- Traditional applications similar to T. officinale
- Used in traditional preparations alongside other herbs

**Evidence Source:** Pharmacokinetic study by Zhu et al. 1999 examining T. mongolicum extract at traditional dose of 2 g crude drug/kg in rats

## Traditional Applications by Body System

### Digestive System

#### Bile Flow Stimulation (Cholagogue)
**Traditional Use:** Disturbances of bile flow, cholelithiasis (gallstones)

**Historical Recognition:** German Commission E 1984

**Traditional Rationale:** Bitter compounds in dandelion traditionally believed to stimulate bile production and flow

**Traditional Preparation:** Root preparations (infusion, decoction, tincture)

**Traditional Dosing:** 3-4 g root or 10-15 drops root tincture twice daily

**Traditional Contraindications:** Not to be used if bile duct obstruction, cholangitis, liver disease, or gallstones present

#### Digestive Aid (Bitter Tonic)
**Traditional Use:** Loss of appetite, dyspepsia, bloating, flatulence

**Historical Recognition:** German Commission E 1984 (root), 1992 (leaf)

**Traditional Rationale:** Bitter principle traditionally used to stimulate digestive secretions and improve appetite

**Traditional Preparation:** Leaf preparations preferred for digestive complaints

**Traditional Dosing:** 4-10 g of leaves or 2-5 mL leaf tincture 3 times daily

**Traditional Administration:** Typically taken before meals to stimulate appetite and digestion

### Urinary System

#### Diuretic (Aquaretic)
**Traditional Use:** Diuretic stimulation, fluid retention, urinary tract support

**Historical Duration:** At least 1000 years documented use

**Historical Recognition:** German Commission E 1984, EMA 2016

**Traditional Rationale:** Traditionally believed to increase urine production without significant electrolyte loss (aquaretic rather than classical diuretic)

**Traditional Preparation:** Leaf preparations considered more diuretic than root

**Traditional Dosing:**
- Leaf: 4-10 g or 2-5 mL tincture 3 times daily
- Root: 3-4 g or 10-15 drops tincture twice daily
- Infusion/decoction: 1.7-8.7 g in 250 mL water

**Clinical Validation:** Single human trial (Clare et al. 2009, n=17) showed significant diuretic effect with 8 mL ethanolic leaf extract three times daily

### Hepatobiliary System

#### Liver Support (Hepatoprotective)
**Traditional Use:**
- Liver damage from toxic agents
- Alcohol-induced liver injury
- Carbon tetrachloride exposure
- Paracetamol toxicity

**Traditional Rationale:** Traditionally considered a "liver tonic" and "blood purifier"

**Traditional Preparation:** Root preparations primarily used

**Evidence Basis:** Preclinical studies only; no human clinical trials despite long traditional use

**Traditional Context:** Part of European and Asian folk medicine traditions for liver complaints

**Modern Validation Status:** Strong animal and in vitro evidence (Abd El-Ghffar et al. 2021, Jia et al. 2014) but human trials lacking

### Dermatological Applications

#### Eczema Treatment
**Traditional Use:** Dyshidrotic hand eczema (vesicular palmoplantar eczema)

**Historical Duration:** Documented use in herbal medicine with case reports showing beneficial effects

**Traditional Preparation:** Dandelion juice

**Traditional Duration:** 20-30 days of continuous use

**Clinical Investigation:** NCT00442091 pilot study at Odense University Hospital (results not published)

**Traditional Safety Note:** Only individuals with negative patch tests to Compositae family should use (Paulsen E, Contact Dermatitis 2020)

## Traditional Preparations

### Infusion (Tea)
**Traditional Method:** Steeping dried leaves or root in hot water

**Traditional Dosing Range:** 1.7-8.7 g plant material in 250 mL water

**Traditional Parts Used:**
- Leaves: For diuretic and digestive effects
- Root: For liver/bile and digestive effects

**Traditional Administration:** Multiple times daily, often before meals for digestive complaints

**Traditional Duration:** Maximum 2 weeks continuously (EMA guidance based on traditional use patterns)

### Decoction
**Traditional Method:** Simmering root material in water (stronger extraction than infusion)

**Traditional Dosing:** 1.7-8.7 g root in 250 mL water

**Traditional Rationale:** Decoction traditionally preferred for extracting compounds from harder root material

**Traditional Administration:** Similar to infusion; multiple daily doses

### Tincture (Alcoholic Extract)
**Root Tincture Traditional Dosing:**
- 10-15 drops twice daily (German Commission E 1984)
- Alternative: 3-4 g crude root equivalent

**Leaf Tincture Traditional Dosing:**
- 2-5 mL three times daily (German Commission E 1992)
- Alternative: 4-10 g crude leaf equivalent

**Traditional Preparation Note:** Clare et al. 2009 clinical trial used fresh leaf ethanolic extract at 1:1 ratio (1 g to 1 mL of 95% EtOH) with dosing of 8 mL three times daily

### Fresh Juice
**Traditional Preparation:** Expressed juice from fresh plant material

**Traditional Application:** Particularly for dermatological conditions (eczema)

**Traditional Duration:** 20-30 days (based on clinical trial protocol NCT00442091)

**Traditional Safety:** Only for individuals without Compositae family allergies

### Dried Plant Material
**Leaf Drying:** Shade-dried leaves traditionally preferred over fresh material

**Traditional Rationale:** Murtaza et al. 2022 found shade-dried leaves had higher phytochemical content:
- Total phenols: 5833.12 ± 4.222 mg/100 g
- Total flavonoids: 188.84 ± 0.019 mg/100 g
- Ascorbic acid: 34.70 ± 0.026 mg/100 g

**Traditional Dosing of Dried Material:**
- Leaves: 4-10 g daily
- Root: 3-4 g daily

**Traditional Duration:** Maximum 2 weeks for leaf preparations

## Traditional Formulations and Combinations

### Combination with Radix Astragali (Astragalus)
**Traditional Practice:** Combination used in Asian traditional medicine

**Traditional Rationale:** Synergistic effects for metabolic and immune support

**Modern Validation:** Li et al. 2021 found combination significantly increased glucose consumption and intracellular glycogen content beyond either herb alone

### Taraxacum mongolicum (Asian Species)
**Traditional Use:** Related species used interchangeably in Mongolian and Chinese traditional medicine

**Traditional Dose:** 2 g crude drug/kg documented in pharmacokinetic studies

**Traditional Context:** Part of traditional formulas for liver and digestive support

**Note:** Less than 1% of over 2,500 identified Taraxacum species have been studied (Martinez et al. 2015)

**Implication:** Vast traditional knowledge exists across cultures that has not been scientifically documented or validated

## Traditional Safety and Contraindications

### Traditional Contraindications
Based on EMA Assessment Report 2016 and German Commission E monographs:

**Absolute Contraindications:**
- Bile duct obstruction
- Cholangitis
- Liver disease
- Gallstones

**Traditional Cautions:**
- Allergy to Asteraceae/Compositae family plants
- Pregnancy and lactation (use not recommended due to lack of traditional safety data)
- Children under 12 years (traditional use not established)

### Traditional Adverse Effects
**Documented in Traditional Use:**
- Allergic reactions (particularly in those sensitive to Compositae family)
- Contact dermatitis from sesquiterpene lactones (taraxinic acid)
- Most undesirable effects related to pollen allergies or leaf contact

**Rare Traditional Reports:**
- Hives
- Asthma
- Toxic epidermal necrosis (very rare)
- Tachycardia
- Strong hypoglycemic effects
- Hypokalemia

### Traditional Duration Limits
**Maximum Continuous Use:** 2 weeks for leaf preparations (EMA guidance based on traditional use patterns and lack of long-term safety studies)

**Rationale:** Traditional practice typically involved shorter courses or intermittent use rather than continuous long-term administration

## Traditional vs. Scientific Evidence

### Strong Traditional-Scientific Alignment
**Diuretic Effects:**
- Traditional use: At least 1000 years
- Scientific validation: Clare et al. 2009 (n=17, significant results)
- Alignment: Strong

**Digestive/Bitter Tonic:**
- Traditional use: Centuries (Commission E 1984, 1992)
- Scientific validation: Mechanism plausible (bitter compounds stimulate digestion)
- Alignment: Moderate (mechanistic but limited human trials)

### Traditional Use Lacking Human Trials
**Hepatoprotective:**
- Traditional use: Well-documented in European and Asian traditions
- Scientific validation: Strong preclinical evidence, no human trials
- Gap: Critical need for human studies despite promising traditional use

**Antidiabetic:**
- Traditional use: Implied in traditional "blood purifier" and metabolic support applications
- Scientific validation: Strong in vitro evidence, no human trials
- Gap: Extensive research needed to validate traditional applications

**Antimicrobial:**
- Traditional use: Less explicitly documented
- Scientific validation: Promising in vitro evidence
- Gap: Clinical evidence sparse

## Ethnopharmacological Insights

### Traditional Medicine Patterns
**Martinez et al. 2015 Review Findings:**
- Contains sesquiterpenes, saponins, phenolic compounds, flavonoids, and sugars
- Less than 1% of over 2,500 Taraxacum species studied
- Pharmacology is main research area but less than enthusiastic interest in human clinical trials
- Traditionally considered natural remedy but low commercial applicability until extraction yields improve

**Research Gap:** Human clinical trials needed only after achieving commercially attractive compound recovery methods

**Implication:** Economic factors may be limiting translation of traditional knowledge into clinical evidence

### Traditional Use Suggesting Safety
**Sharifi-Rad et al. 2018 Assessment:**
- Clinical evidence remains sparse
- Human safety data limited
- However, traditional use suggesting safety is extensive

**Traditional Safety Profile:**
- At least 1000 years of documented human use
- Few serious adverse events in traditional contexts
- Regulatory recognition (Commission E, EMA, Chinese Pharmacopoeia) based partly on traditional safety record

**Modern Safety Assessment:** Low toxicity with multi-target biological activity; no serious side effects reported to date despite limited formal clinical safety data

## Recommended Daily Dose (Traditional Consensus)
**General Traditional Dose:** 10-15 g daily

**Source:** Comprehensive review published in Molecules 2023;28(13):5022 synthesizing traditional dosing across multiple sources

**Part-Specific Traditional Dosing:**
- Root: 3-4 g daily
- Leaf: 4-10 g daily
- Combined: Up to 15 g total daily
