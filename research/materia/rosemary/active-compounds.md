# Active Compounds and Phytochemistry of Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis L.)

## Primary Active Constituents

### Phenolic Diterpenes

#### Carnosic Acid

**Content in Plant Material**:
- Dried leaf: 1.5-2.5%
- Commercial extracts: Up to 20% (common standardization level)
- Combined with carnosol: Up to 33% in some standardized extracts

**Bioavailability**:
- **Oral bioavailability (rat study)**: 40.1% at 360 minutes post-administration
- **Alternate study**: 65.09% absolute bioavailability
- **Superior to rosmarinic acid**: Higher bioavailability due to increased glucuronidation

**Pharmacokinetics** (Doolaege et al. 2011, rat model):
- **Route**: Oral administration (64.3 ± 5.8 mg/kg)
- **Distribution**: Traces detected in intestinal content, liver, and skeletal muscle tissue
- **Form in vivo**: Exists in free (unmetabolized) form
- **Elimination**: Primary route is FECAL, not urinary
  - 15.6 ± 8.2% recovered in feces at 24 hours
- **Metabolism**: Minimal metabolic transformation; remains largely intact

**Clinical Trial Dosing** (Kuwata et al. 2025):
- Low dose (efficacy study): 4 mg carnosic acid + carnosol per capsule, 2 capsules daily = 8 mg/day total diterpenes
- High dose (safety study): 4 mg carnosic acid + carnosol per capsule, 10 capsules daily = 40 mg/day total diterpenes

**Mechanism of Action**:
- Primary antioxidant compound
- Acts through different mechanisms than carnosol
- Nrf2 pathway activation
- Anti-inflammatory properties

#### Carnosol

**Content**: Present in rosemary extracts; often combined with carnosic acid in standardization

**Pharmacokinetics** (Veenstra et al. 2021, mouse model):
- Analyzed via LC-MS/MS in standardized rosemary extract
- Dosing: 100 mg/kg and 10 mg/kg rosemary extract daily
- PK timepoints: 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 6, 12, and 24 hours
- **Efficacy findings**:
  - 100 mg/kg significantly improved Disease Activity Index in DSS-induced colitis
  - Reduced sestrin-2 protein expression
  - Enhanced intestinal barrier integrity (decreased FITC-dextran permeability)
  - Increased zonula occludens-1 expression at both 10 and 100 mg/kg doses

**Mechanism**: Acts through different pathways than carnosic acid, providing complementary antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects

### Phenolic Acids

#### Rosmarinic Acid

**Content in Plant Material**:
- Dried plant: 21.13 ± 0.56 mg/g (from Azizi 2022 clinical trial material)
- Common in Lamiaceae (Labiateae) family plants

**Clinical Trial Dosing**:
- **Kuwata et al. 2025**:
  - Low dose (efficacy): 2.5 mg per capsule, 2 capsules daily = 5 mg/day
  - High dose (safety): 2.5 mg per capsule, 10 capsules daily = 25 mg/day
- **Azizi et al. 2022**: Capsules with ~21 mg rosmarinic acid/g dried plant, twice daily

**Pharmacokinetics** (Hitl et al. 2021, human PK review):
- **Absorption**: Peroral application main route of entry
- **Metabolism**: Metabolized by gut microflora into simple, more easily absorbed phenolic units
  - Structural changes and conjugation reactions (glucuronidation, methylation)
- **Elimination**: Renal excretion represents main elimination pathway (differs from carnosic acid's fecal route)
- **Bioavailability**: Lower than carnosic acid

**Health Benefits Documented**:
- Dermatological conditions
- Allergic disorders
- Osteoarthritic conditions
- Cognitive performance improvement
- Metabolic syndrome management

**Safety**: No serious adverse effects reported in herbal remedies containing rosmarinic acid

### Triterpenes

#### Ursolic Acid
- Present in rosemary extracts
- High-concentration preparations (50% ursolic acid) used in safety studies
- NOAEL >4000 mg/kg/day in 13-week rat study with 50% ursolic acid rosemary concentrate

#### Betulinic Acid
- Present in rosemary extracts
- Contributes to overall phytochemical activity

### Other Active Compounds

#### Rosmanol
- Phenolic compound
- Contributes to antioxidant activity

## Standardization and Quality Control

### Commercial Standardization Markers

**Primary Markers**:
1. **Carnosic acid**: 20% (most common standardization level)
2. **Rosmarinic acid**: 2.5-5 mg per capsule (clinical trial formulations)
3. **Combined diterpenes** (carnosic acid + carnosol): 4-8 mg per dose (clinical trials)

### Standardized Extract Preparations

**From Clinical Trials**:
- Rosemary extract standardized to 20% carnosic acid (commercial products)
- Combined carnosic acid and carnosol content up to 33%
- Rosmarinic acid 2.5-5 mg per capsule
- Diterpenes (carnosic acid + carnosol) 4-8 mg per dose

### Pharmacopoeial Standards

**European Pharmacopoeia (Ph. Eur.)**:
- **Rosmarini folium** (Rosemary Leaf): Quality standards for dried leaf material
- **Rosmarini aetheroleum** (Rosemary Essential Oil): Quality standards for essential oil

**Alignment**: WHO monograph standards align with British Pharmacopoeia specifications

## Bioavailability Comparison

| Compound | Bioavailability | Primary Elimination Route | Metabolism |
|----------|-----------------|---------------------------|------------|
| Carnosic acid | 40.1-65.09% | Fecal (15.6% at 24h) | Minimal; exists in free form |
| Rosmarinic acid | Lower than carnosic acid | Renal | Extensive gut microflora metabolism into phenolic units |

**Clinical Significance**: Carnosic acid has higher oral bioavailability than rosmarinic acid, primarily due to increased glucuronidation and minimal metabolic breakdown. This makes carnosic acid content a critical marker for standardization.

## Active Constituent Ratios in Clinical Trials

### Kuwata et al. 2025 (Stress/Anxiety Study)

**Efficacy Dose (2 capsules/day)**:
- Rosmarinic acid: 5 mg/day (2.5 mg per capsule)
- Diterpenes (carnosic acid + carnosol): 8 mg/day (4 mg per capsule)
- **Ratio**: ~1.6:1 (diterpenes:rosmarinic acid)

**Safety Dose (10 capsules/day)**:
- Rosmarinic acid: 25 mg/day (2.5 mg per capsule)
- Diterpenes (carnosic acid + carnosol): 40 mg/day (4 mg per capsule)
- **Ratio**: ~1.6:1 (diterpenes:rosmarinic acid)
- **Safety outcome**: No clinically relevant adverse events or abnormal laboratory findings at 4 weeks

### Azizi et al. 2022 (Depression Study)

**Formulation**: Dried plant capsules, twice daily
- Rosmarinic acid content: 21.13 ± 0.56 mg/g dried plant
- **Outcome**: Significant reduction in depression and anxiety scores at 8 weeks

## Phytochemical Mechanisms of Action

### Antioxidant Activity
- **Primary compounds**: Carnosic acid, carnosol, rosmarinic acid
- **Mechanism**: Free radical scavenging, lipid peroxidation prevention
- **Clinical relevance**: Neuroprotection, skin health, anti-aging effects

### Anti-Inflammatory Activity
- **Primary compounds**: Rosmarinic acid, carnosic acid, carnosol
- **Mechanisms**:
  - COX-2 inhibition
  - NF-κB pathway modulation
  - Cytokine regulation
- **Clinical applications**: Joint health, skin conditions, inflammatory bowel disease

### Neuroprotective Effects
- **Primary compounds**: Carnosic acid, rosmarinic acid
- **Mechanisms**:
  - Acetylcholinesterase inhibition (mild)
  - Nrf2 pathway activation
  - Mitochondrial protection
  - BDNF modulation
- **Clinical evidence**: Cognitive enhancement, memory improvement, mood regulation

### Antimicrobial Activity
- **Primary compound**: Essential oil constituents, rosmarinic acid
- **Applications**: Wound healing, skin infections, seborrheic dermatitis

### Hair Growth Stimulation
- **Primary compounds**: Essential oil and phenolic compounds
- **Mechanism**: Not fully elucidated; likely multifactorial
  - Increased scalp circulation
  - 5α-reductase inhibition (theoretical)
  - Anti-inflammatory effects on follicles
- **Clinical evidence**: Equal efficacy to minoxidil 2% at 6 months

## Essential Oil Composition

### Components (from EMA monographs and aromatherapy review)
- **1,8-Cineole (Eucalyptol)**: Major component
- **Camphor**: Stimulant, rubefacient
- **α-Pinene**: Antimicrobial
- **Borneol**: Analgesic, anti-inflammatory
- **Camphene**: Antimicrobial

### Applications
- **Aromatherapy**: Cognitive function, sleep quality, mood, pain management (Aćimović 2025)
- **Topical**: Rheumatic diseases, circulatory complaints, wound healing
- **Internal**: Dyspeptic complaints (small doses only)

### Safety Concerns
- Contact dermatitis from prolonged topical application
- Hypersensitivity reactions
- Rare anaphylaxis cases
- NEVER use undiluted essential oil internally (toxic in large amounts)

## Dose-Dependent Effects and Active Constituent Implications

### Cognitive Function - Inverted U-Curve

**Pengelly et al. 2012**:
- **750 mg dried leaf** (beneficial): ~11-19 mg carnosic acid, beneficial for memory
- **6000 mg dried leaf** (impairing): ~90-150 mg carnosic acid, impaired memory and cognition

**Implication**: Optimal cognitive effects at moderate doses; high doses counterproductive

### Safety Threshold

**Animal NOAEL**: >4000 mg/kg/day (50% ursolic acid rosemary concentrate, 13-week rat study)

**Human Safety Dose**: 25 mg rosmarinic acid + 40 mg diterpenes daily for 4 weeks (Kuwata 2025) showed no adverse effects in 21 participants

## Quality Markers for Standardization

### Recommended Markers Based on Clinical Evidence

**Primary Markers**:
1. **Carnosic acid content**: Most reliable due to high bioavailability and stability
2. **Rosmarinic acid content**: Complementary marker for quality

**Optimal Ratios** (from clinical trials):
- Diterpenes (carnosic acid + carnosol): 8-40 mg/day
- Rosmarinic acid: 5-25 mg/day
- Approximate ratio: 1.6:1 (diterpenes:rosmarinic acid)

### European Pharmacopoeia Requirements

**Rosmarini folium (Rosemary Leaf)**:
- Minimum essential oil content
- Identification tests for key constituents
- Purity standards (heavy metals, pesticides, microbial limits)

**Rosmarini aetheroleum (Rosemary Essential Oil)**:
- Chemical composition standards (1,8-cineole, camphor, α-pinene ranges)
- Optical rotation
- Refractive index
- Relative density

## Phytochemical Stability and Storage

### Stability Concerns
- **Carnosic acid**: Can oxidize to carnosol; proper storage essential
- **Essential oil**: Volatile; requires sealed containers, cool storage
- **Rosmarinic acid**: Relatively stable in dried plant material

### Storage Recommendations
- Cool, dry conditions
- Protection from light (especially essential oil)
- Sealed containers to prevent oxidation
- Use standardized extracts within expiry dates

## Research Gaps and Future Directions

### Needed Phytochemical Research

1. **Human pharmacokinetics**: Most PK data from animal models; human data limited
2. **Metabolite profiling**: Full characterization of human metabolites
3. **Synergistic effects**: Interaction between carnosic acid, carnosol, and rosmarinic acid
4. **Essential oil standardization**: Better correlation between essential oil chemotypes and clinical effects
5. **Bioavailability enhancement**: Formulation strategies to improve absorption

### Clinical Implications

- **Standardization critical**: Wide variation in phytochemical content necessitates quality control
- **Formulation matters**: Extract type (dried leaf, essential oil, standardized extract) significantly impacts clinical outcomes
- **Dose optimization**: More research needed on optimal constituent ratios for specific indications

## Key Insights

1. **Carnosic acid** is the most bioavailable and stable marker for standardization; extracts should specify carnosic acid content (ideally 20%)

2. **Rosmarinic acid** complements carnosic acid but has lower bioavailability due to extensive gut metabolism

3. **Optimal clinical ratios**: ~1.6:1 diterpenes:rosmarinic acid based on successful clinical trials

4. **Dose-response is critical**: Moderate doses (750 mg leaf, 5-8 mg diterpenes) beneficial for cognition; very high doses (6000 mg leaf, >40 mg diterpenes) may impair function

5. **Pharmacopoeial standards** ensure quality but don't specify optimal therapeutic constituent levels; clinical trial data provides better dosing guidance

6. **Elimination routes differ**: Carnosic acid (fecal), rosmarinic acid (renal) — implications for drug interactions and individual metabolism
