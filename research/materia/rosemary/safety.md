# Safety Profile of Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis L.)

## General Safety Assessment

**Overall Conclusion**: Rosemary is generally recognized as safe at normal culinary and therapeutic doses. Caution is warranted for chronic use and high doses due to potential adverse effects on kidneys, liver, reproductive system, and teratology.

**Source**: Ghasemzadeh Rahbardar & Hosseinzadeh 2025 (Comprehensive safety review, searched Google Scholar, PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science from inception through February 2024)

## Clinical Trial Safety Data

### Large-Scale Safety Studies

#### Kuwata et al. 2025 - High-Dose Safety Trial

**Design**: Single-arm, open-label safety evaluation
- **Sample**: 22 adults (21 completed)
- **Duration**: 4 weeks
- **Dosing**: 10 capsules daily
  - Rosmarinic acid: 25 mg/day (2.5 mg per capsule)
  - Diterpenes: 40 mg/day (4 mg carnosic acid + carnosol per capsule)
- **Safety Findings**: **No clinically relevant adverse events or abnormal laboratory findings**

**Efficacy Study Safety**:
- **Sample**: 40 randomized (38 analyzed)
- **Duration**: 4 weeks + 4-week washout (crossover)
- **Dosing**: 2 capsules daily
  - Rosmarinic acid: 5 mg/day
  - Diterpenes: 8 mg/day
- **Safety Findings**: No adverse events reported

#### Draelos et al. 2025 - Skin Health Study

**Design**: Double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled
- **Sample**: 104 women aged 40-65 years (52 rosemary, 52 placebo)
- **Duration**: 12 weeks
- **Dosing**: Tapered schedule
  - Weeks 1-4: 6 capsules/day
  - Weeks 5-8: 4 capsules/day
  - Weeks 9-12: 2 capsules/day
  - All with food
- **Adverse Events**:
  - **One participant discontinued** due to moderate GI upset possibly related to treatment
  - **Six possibly related transient GI events**, all resolved
- **Overall**: Well tolerated

#### Panahi et al. 2015 - Topical Rosemary Oil Safety

**Design**: Randomized comparative trial (rosemary oil vs. minoxidil 2%)
- **Sample**: 100 participants (50 per group)
- **Duration**: 6 months
- **Formulation**: Rosemary essential oil (topical)
- **Adverse Effects**:
  - **Scalp itching**: Increased significantly in both groups by months 3 and 6 (p < 0.05)
    - **More frequent in minoxidil group than rosemary group**
  - **Other effects**: Dry hair, greasy hair, and dandruff showed no significant changes
- **Conclusion**: Rosemary oil caused LESS scalp itching than minoxidil 2%

### Other Clinical Trial Safety

#### Nematolahi et al. 2018 - Students Study
- **Sample**: 68 university students
- **Duration**: 1 month
- **Dosing**: 500 mg twice daily (1000 mg/day)
- **Safety**: No adverse events reported

#### Azizi et al. 2022 - Depression Study
- **Population**: Patients with major depressive disorder
- **Duration**: 8 weeks
- **Dosing**: 1 capsule twice daily (2 capsules/day, ~21 mg rosmarinic acid/g dried plant)
- **Safety**: No adverse events reported

#### Pengelly et al. 2012 - Elderly Cognitive Study
- **Sample**: 28 older adults, mean age 75
- **Duration**: Single doses with 7-day washout
- **Doses**: 750 mg to 6000 mg
- **Safety Finding**: High dose (6000 mg) showed **impairing effects** on speed of memory and other cognitive measures
- **Implication**: Very high doses contraindicated for cognitive purposes

## Animal Toxicology Data

### No Observed Adverse Effect Level (NOAEL)

**Study**: 13-week repeated oral dose toxicity study in rats
- **Test Material**: Rosemary concentrate with 50% ursolic acid
- **NOAEL**: **>4000 mg/kg/day in both sexes**
- **Duration**: 13 weeks
- **Source**: Cited in Ghasemzadeh 2025 toxicity review

**Implication**: Wide safety margin for normal therapeutic use

### High-Dose and Chronic-Use Toxicity

**Source**: Ghasemzadeh Rahbardar & Hosseinzadeh 2025 (Comprehensive safety review)

#### Hepatorenal Effects (High Doses)
- **Findings**:
  - Atrophic and degenerative changes in liver tissue
  - Atrophic and degenerative changes in kidney tissue
  - Elevated blood urea nitrogen (BUN)
  - Elevated liver enzymes
- **Dose/Duration**: Chronic and/or high doses (specific thresholds not detailed)
- **Clinical Relevance**: Monitor liver and kidney function with long-term high-dose use

#### Reproductive Toxicity (Chronic/High Doses)
- **Findings**:
  - Decreased spermatogenesis
  - Decreased testosterone levels
  - Decreased sperm density
  - Decreased sperm motility
- **Dose/Duration**: Chronic and/or high doses
- **Clinical Relevance**: Caution for men with fertility concerns using high-dose chronic supplementation

#### Genotoxicity and Teratology (High/Chronic Exposure)
- **Findings**:
  - DNA fragmentation
  - Apoptotic body formation
  - Fetal anomalies
- **Dose/Duration**: High and/or chronic exposure
- **Clinical Relevance**: **Contraindicated in pregnancy at high doses**

## Contraindications

### Pregnancy and Lactation

**Source**: EMA/HMPC Community Herbal Monograph (section 4.6: Pregnancy and lactation)
- **Recommendation**: Caution warranted
- **Rationale**: Potential genotoxicity and teratogenicity at high doses (animal data)
- **Traditional practice**: Avoid medicinal doses during pregnancy; culinary use generally considered safe

**Evidence**:
- Animal studies show DNA fragmentation, apoptotic bodies, and fetal anomalies with high/chronic doses
- No adequate human pregnancy safety data
- Breast milk transfer not characterized

**Clinical Guidance**: Avoid medicinal doses during pregnancy and lactation; small culinary amounts likely safe

### High-Dose Use

**Contraindication**: Doses >6000 mg dried leaf powder
- **Rationale**: Cognitive impairment observed in elderly at 6000 mg (Pengelly 2012)
- **Recommendation**: Stay within therapeutic ranges (750-1000 mg dried leaf; 5-25 mg rosmarinic acid + 8-40 mg diterpenes)

### Chronic High-Dose Use

**Contraindication**: Prolonged use of high doses without medical supervision
- **Rationale**: Potential hepatorenal toxicity, reproductive effects, genotoxicity
- **Recommendation**: Monitor liver enzymes, kidney function, and reproductive parameters if using high doses long-term

### Allergy and Hypersensitivity

**Source**: Aćimović 2025 (Aromatherapy systematic review)

**Contraindications**:
- Known allergy to Lamiaceae family plants (mint family)
- History of rosemary-induced allergic reactions
- Severe atopic dermatitis with history of plant allergies

**Adverse Reactions**:
- Contact dermatitis from prolonged topical application
- Hypersensitivity reactions to essential oil compounds
- **Rare cases of anaphylaxis reported**

**Clinical Guidance**:
- Patch test before widespread topical use
- Discontinue immediately if signs of allergic reaction develop
- Avoid essential oil in individuals with severe allergies

## Drug Interactions

**Source**: Limited documentation in provided studies

### Theoretical Interactions (Based on Mechanisms)

#### Anticoagulant/Antiplatelet Drugs
- **Mechanism**: Potential antiplatelet effects of phenolic compounds
- **Caution**: Monitor INR if using with warfarin; watch for bleeding with aspirin, clopidogrel
- **Evidence Level**: Theoretical; no documented clinical interactions

#### Antidiabetic Medications
- **Mechanism**: Potential blood glucose-lowering effects
- **Caution**: Monitor glucose; may require dose adjustment of diabetes medications
- **Evidence Level**: Theoretical; metabolic syndrome benefits documented (Hitl 2021)

#### Cytochrome P450 Substrates
- **Mechanism**: Potential enzyme induction/inhibition by diterpenes and essential oil
- **Caution**: Possible interaction with CYP-metabolized drugs
- **Evidence Level**: Theoretical; specific interactions not documented in clinical trials

#### Iron Absorption
- **Mechanism**: Phenolic compounds may chelate iron
- **Caution**: Take rosemary supplements separate from iron supplements
- **Evidence Level**: Theoretical; common to phenolic-rich herbs

### Documented Lack of Interactions

**Source**: Hitl 2021 (Rosmarinic acid review)
- **Finding**: "No serious adverse effects reported in herbal remedies containing rosmarinic acid"
- **Implication**: Rosmarinic acid component appears safe with minimal interaction potential

**Recommendation**: Comprehensive drug interaction studies needed; exercise caution with polypharmacy until more data available

## Adverse Effects by System

### Gastrointestinal
- **Incidence**: Low; 1 in 104 (0.96%) discontinued due to moderate GI upset (Draelos 2025)
- **Nature**: Transient GI events (nausea, dyspepsia); all resolved
- **Prevention**: Take with food (recommended in Draelos study)

### Dermatological
- **Topical use**:
  - Scalp itching (common with essential oil, but less than minoxidil 2%)
  - Contact dermatitis (prolonged application)
  - Hypersensitivity reactions
  - Rare anaphylaxis
- **Prevention**: Patch test, avoid prolonged undiluted application, discontinue if irritation develops

### Hepatic
- **High doses**: Elevated liver enzymes, atrophic/degenerative changes (animal data)
- **Therapeutic doses**: No hepatotoxicity in clinical trials
- **Monitoring**: Consider liver function tests if using high doses long-term

### Renal
- **High doses**: Elevated BUN, atrophic/degenerative changes (animal data)
- **Therapeutic doses**: No nephrotoxicity in clinical trials
- **Monitoring**: Consider kidney function tests if using high doses long-term

### Reproductive
- **High/chronic doses**: Decreased spermatogenesis, testosterone, sperm parameters (animal data)
- **Therapeutic doses**: No reproductive toxicity documented in human trials
- **Caution**: Men with fertility concerns should use moderate doses

### Neurological
- **High doses (>6000 mg)**: Cognitive impairment (Pengelly 2012)
- **Therapeutic doses (750-1000 mg)**: Cognitive enhancement
- **Implication**: Inverted U-curve; stay within therapeutic range

### Immunological
- **Essential oil**: Hypersensitivity reactions, rare anaphylaxis
- **Dried leaf/extracts**: Minimal allergic reactions

## Special Populations

### Elderly (Age ≥75)

**Source**: Pengelly et al. 2012
- **Sample**: Mean age 75 years
- **Safety**: Well tolerated at 750 mg dose
- **Caution**: Avoid high doses (≥6000 mg) due to cognitive impairment
- **Recommendation**: Start with lower doses; 750 mg dried leaf or equivalent standardized extract safe

### Children and Adolescents

**Evidence**: No pediatric clinical trials in bibliography
- **Recommendation**: Insufficient data for dosing recommendations
- **Traditional use**: Culinary amounts likely safe; avoid medicinal doses without professional guidance

### Pregnant and Lactating Women

**Recommendation**: **Avoid medicinal doses**
- **Rationale**: Genotoxicity and teratogenicity at high doses (animal data); insufficient human safety data
- **Culinary use**: Small amounts in food generally recognized as safe
- **Source**: EMA/HMPC monograph section 4.6

### Patients with Liver Disease

**Recommendation**: Use with caution
- **Rationale**: High doses show hepatotoxic potential in animal studies
- **Monitoring**: Liver function tests if using therapeutically
- **Dose**: Use lower end of therapeutic range

### Patients with Kidney Disease

**Recommendation**: Use with caution
- **Rationale**: High doses show nephrotoxic potential in animal studies; rosmarinic acid renally eliminated
- **Monitoring**: Kidney function tests if using therapeutically
- **Dose**: Use lower end of therapeutic range

### Patients with Fertility Concerns

**Recommendation**: Moderate doses only
- **Rationale**: High/chronic doses decrease spermatogenesis and sperm parameters in animal studies
- **Monitoring**: Semen analysis if using high doses long-term
- **Dose**: Stay within established therapeutic ranges (5-25 mg rosmarinic acid, 8-40 mg diterpenes)

### Patients with Seizure Disorders

**Source**: Traditional caution (not extensively documented in modern clinical trials)
- **Rationale**: Essential oil contains camphor, which may lower seizure threshold at high doses
- **Recommendation**: Avoid high-dose essential oil; dried leaf and standardized extracts likely safer
- **Evidence Level**: Traditional precaution; limited modern clinical data

## Dosing Safety Guidelines

### Safe Therapeutic Ranges (Based on Clinical Trials)

| Formulation | Safe Daily Dose | Duration Studied | Population | Adverse Events |
|-------------|-----------------|------------------|------------|----------------|
| Dried leaf powder | 750 mg single dose | Acute | Elderly (age 75) | None |
| Rosemary capsules | 500 mg twice daily (1000 mg/day) | 1 month | University students | None |
| Standardized extract | 5 mg rosmarinic acid + 8 mg diterpenes | 4 weeks | Adults with stress | None |
| Standardized extract (high dose) | 25 mg rosmarinic acid + 40 mg diterpenes | 4 weeks | Healthy adults | None |
| Rosemary extract (tapered) | 6→4→2 caps/day | 12 weeks | Women 40-65 | 1/104 GI upset |
| Rosemary oil (topical) | Topical application | 6 months | Alopecia patients | Scalp itching (less than minoxidil) |

### Unsafe Doses

- **Dried leaf powder**: ≥6000 mg causes cognitive impairment in elderly (Pengelly 2012)
- **Essential oil (internal)**: Undiluted or large amounts toxic; never use undiluted internally
- **Chronic high doses**: >40 mg diterpenes daily for extended periods (>3 months) lacks long-term safety data

## Risk-Benefit Assessment

### Low Risk (Recommended Uses)
- **Dried leaf**: 750-1000 mg/day for cognitive support, anxiety, depression (1-8 weeks)
- **Standardized extract**: 5-25 mg rosmarinic acid + 8-40 mg diterpenes daily (4-12 weeks)
- **Topical oil**: Hair growth, seborrheic dermatitis (2-6 months)
- **Culinary use**: Normal food quantities

### Moderate Risk (Use with Caution)
- **High-dose extracts**: >40 mg diterpenes daily or >1000 mg dried leaf daily for extended periods
- **Pregnancy/lactation**: Medicinal doses (culinary use likely safe)
- **Liver/kidney disease**: Therapeutic doses with monitoring
- **Fertility concerns**: High-dose chronic use in men
- **Polypharmacy**: Potential drug interactions (theoretical)

### High Risk (Contraindicated)
- **Very high doses**: ≥6000 mg dried leaf
- **Essential oil (undiluted internal use)**: Toxic
- **Pregnancy (medicinal doses)**: Genotoxicity and teratogenicity risk
- **Known allergy**: Anaphylaxis risk
- **Severe liver/kidney disease**: Organ toxicity potential

## Monitoring Recommendations

### No Monitoring Needed
- Short-term use (1-4 weeks) at therapeutic doses in healthy adults
- Topical use for hair/skin conditions
- Culinary use

### Consider Monitoring
- **Duration >3 months** at high doses (>25 mg rosmarinic acid, >40 mg diterpenes):
  - Liver function tests (ALT, AST, bilirubin)
  - Kidney function tests (BUN, creatinine)
  - Reproductive hormones in men with fertility concerns (testosterone, semen analysis)
- **Pre-existing liver/kidney disease**: Baseline and periodic function tests
- **Polypharmacy**: Monitor for drug interactions (INR if on warfarin, glucose if diabetic)

## Key Safety Insights

1. **Wide Safety Margin**: NOAEL >4000 mg/kg/day in rats; therapeutic human doses 750-1000 mg dried leaf or 5-40 mg diterpenes well below toxicity threshold

2. **Clinical Trial Safety**: 104-person 12-week study showed only 1 moderate GI upset; 22-person 4-week high-dose study (25 mg rosmarinic acid + 40 mg diterpenes) showed no adverse events

3. **Dose-Dependent Risks**: Low-moderate doses (750-1000 mg dried leaf) safe and beneficial; very high doses (≥6000 mg) impair cognition

4. **Organ Toxicity at High Doses**: Hepatorenal and reproductive toxicity documented in animal studies with chronic high doses; not observed in human therapeutic trials

5. **Pregnancy Contraindication**: Genotoxicity and teratogenicity at high doses (animal data); avoid medicinal doses during pregnancy

6. **Topical Safety**: Essential oil causes less scalp itching than minoxidil 2%; contact dermatitis possible with prolonged use

7. **Allergy Rare but Serious**: Anaphylaxis documented in rare cases; patch test recommended before widespread topical use

8. **Drug Interactions**: Theoretical but not extensively documented; exercise caution with anticoagulants, antidiabetics, and CYP substrates

9. **Long-Term Safety Data Limited**: Most clinical trials 1-12 weeks; safety of >1 year continuous use not well-characterized

10. **Quality Matters**: Use pharmacopoeial-quality (European Pharmacopoeia) or clinically-tested formulations to ensure safety and efficacy
