# Traditional Uses of Rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis L.)

**Source**: Community Herbal Monograph on Rosmarinus officinalis L., folium (EMA/HMPC/13633/2009)
**Date**: 15 July 2010
**Authority**: European Medicines Agency - Herbal Medicinal Products Committee

- **Dyspeptic complaints**: Digestive discomfort, indigestion

### Traditional Formulations

Based on regulatory monographs and historical use:

#### Internal Preparations
- **Dried leaf powder**: Culinary and medicinal use
- **Infusion/tea**: Hot water extraction of dried leaves
- **Tincture**: Alcohol-based extract
- **Essential oil**: Steam distillation for internal use (small doses)

#### External Preparations
- **Essential oil**: Topical application for rheumatic complaints
- **Poultice**: Fresh or dried leaves applied externally
- **Bath preparations**: Essential oil added to bath water for circulatory support
- **Liniment**: Oil-based preparations for joint and muscle application

## Traditional Indications by System

### Digestive System (Primary Traditional Use)
- **Dyspepsia**: Indigestion, bloating, flatulence
- **Liver support**: Hepatic function enhancement
- **Gallbladder support**: Bile production and flow
- **Gastrointestinal spasm**: Antispasmodic effects

**Traditional Rationale**: Bitter and aromatic principles stimulate digestive secretions; carminative action relieves gas and bloating

### Musculoskeletal System
- **Rheumatic diseases**: Joint inflammation, arthritis
- **Muscle pain**: Myalgia, overexertion
- **Stiffness**: Joint and muscle rigidity

**Traditional Application**: External use of essential oil or preparations applied topically to affected areas; warming and rubefacient action increases local circulation

### Circulatory System
- **Peripheral circulation**: Cold extremities, poor circulation
- **Circulatory complaints**: General circulatory insufficiency

**Traditional Application**: Both internal (tea, tincture) and external (bath, topical oil) for circulatory stimulation

### Wound Healing
- **Antiseptic**: Essential oil for wound cleansing
- **Antimicrobial**: Prevention of infection

**Traditional Application**: Diluted essential oil applied topically to minor wounds and skin injuries

## Traditional Dosing and Administration

### From EMA/HMPC Monograph (Section 4.2: Posology and Method of Administration)

**Note**: Specific traditional dosing information is contained in EMA/HMPC monograph sections:
- 4.2 Posology and method of administration
- 4.3 Contraindications
- 4.4 Special warnings and precautions for use
- 4.6 Pregnancy and lactation

**General Traditional Principles**:
- **Culinary use**: Normal food quantities considered safe
- **Medicinal use**: Higher concentrated forms (tinctures, extracts, essential oil)
- **Duration**: Traditionally used for short-term relief of acute symptoms
- **Frequency**: Multiple daily doses for internal use; as-needed for external application

## Traditional Contraindications and Precautions

### From Safety Reviews

**Source**: EMA/HMPC monograph section 4.6 (Pregnancy and lactation)
- Caution warranted for use during pregnancy and lactation
- Traditional herbalists recommended avoiding medicinal doses during pregnancy

**Source**: Ghasemzadeh Rahbardar & Hosseinzadeh 2025 (Safety review)
- Traditional use generally safe at normal doses
- Modern evidence supports caution with chronic and high doses

### Traditional Warnings
- **Essential oil**: Never use undiluted internally; can be toxic in large amounts
- **Epilepsy**: Traditional caution with essential oil in epileptic individuals
- **Allergic individuals**: Potential for contact dermatitis with prolonged topical use

- Integration of traditional wisdom with contemporary safety data

## Key Insights

1. **Well-Established Traditional Use**: Rosemary has centuries of documented use in European herbal medicine, formally recognized by major regulatory bodies (EMA, Commission E, WHO)

2. **Primary Traditional Domain**: Digestive support is the most consistently documented traditional internal use across all regulatory monographs

3. **Geographic Focus**: Bibliography sources focus on European and Mediterranean traditional use; other traditional medicine systems (Ayurveda, TCM) not documented

4. **Modern Validation**: Some traditional uses (memory, mood) have gained strong modern clinical evidence, while others (rheumatic diseases, circulatory support) remain primarily traditional

5. **Quality Standards**: Traditional use is now supported by pharmacopoeial quality standards (European Pharmacopoeia, British Pharmacopoeia)

6. **Regulatory Recognition**: Inclusion in EMA/HMPC monographs, Commission E, and WHO monographs indicates official recognition of traditional therapeutic value
