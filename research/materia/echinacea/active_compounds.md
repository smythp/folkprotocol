# Echinacea - Active Compounds

## Standardization Markers

### Alkamides (Alkylamides)

**Primary pharmacologically active compounds**

**Standardization Levels** (from clinical trials):
- **Goel et al. 2004**: 0.25 mg/mL in standardized Echinilin preparation (282 participants)
- **Guiotto et al. 2008**: Lozenge doses of 0.07 mg, 0.21 mg, and 0.9 mg tested
- **Summary range**: 0.07-0.9 mg per dose across various formulations

**Species Distribution**:
- Present in E. purpurea (aerial parts and root)
- Present in E. angustifolia (root) - Woelkart et al. 2005 studied specifically
- Primary markers for standardization in modern extracts

### Cichoric Acid (Caffeic Acid Derivatives)

**Standardization Levels**:
- **Goel et al. 2004**: 2.5 mg/mL in standardized preparation
- **Alternative specification**: 4% cichoric acid (summary notes)

**Role**: Secondary marker for quality control and standardization

### Polysaccharides

**Standardization Levels**:
- **Goel et al. 2004**: 25 mg/mL in standardized preparation
- **Di Pierro et al. 2012**: Standardized to polysaccharide IDN5405

**Species-Specific**:
- Present across echinacea species but composition varies

### Echinacoside

**Occurrence**:
- Present in E. angustifolia (noted in WHO monographs)
- Present in E. pallida
- Less prominent in E. purpurea
- Used as marker for E. angustifolia/pallida preparations

## Bioavailability and Pharmacokinetics

### Alkamide Absorption - E. purpurea

**Woelkart et al. 2006** - Tincture vs Tablet Comparison
- **Sample size**: 10 participants (8 test, 2 placebo)
- **Primary alkamide**: Dodeca-2E,4E,8Z,10E/Z-tetraenoic acid isobutylamides

**Tincture Absorption**:
- **T-max**: 30 minutes
- **C-max**: 0.40 ng/mL serum

**Tablet Absorption**:
- **T-max**: 45 minutes
- **C-max**: 0.12 ng/mL (approximately 25% of tincture levels)

**Immunological Effect**:
- Both formulations produced equivalent immune responses
- Cytokines reduced: TNF-alpha and IL-8
- Time point: 23 hours post-administration

**Conclusion**: Tincture shows superior absorption but tablets produce equivalent immune response despite lower alkamide delivery, suggesting immunological effects may occur at lower systemic alkamide concentrations

### Alkamide Absorption - E. angustifolia Root

**Woelkart et al. 2005** - E. angustifolia Pharmacokinetics
- **Sample size**: 11 healthy subjects
- **Extract**: 60% ethanolic extract from E. angustifolia roots
- **Dose**: 2.5 mL
- **Detection method**: Liquid chromatography electrospray ionization ion-trap mass spectrometry

**Absorption Parameters**:
- **T-max (major alkamide)**: 30 minutes
- **C-max (major alkamide)**: 10.88 ng/mL
- **Alkamides identified**: 6 major constituents

**Conclusion**: Main alkamide reaches peak concentration rapidly within 30 minutes; provides quantitative bioavailability data for E. angustifolia alkamides in humans

### Lozenge Formulation - Dose-Response

**Guiotto et al. 2008** - E. purpurea Lozenges
- **Doses tested**: 0.07 mg, 0.21 mg, 0.9 mg alkamides

**Pharmacokinetics**:
- **Absorption onset**: Rapidly absorbed, measurable within 10-20 minutes
- **Absorption half-life**: Approximately 6 minutes
- **Absorption route**: Fraction absorbed through oral mucosa
- **Pharmacokinetic pattern**: Linear
- **C-max by dose**:
  - 0.21 mg dose: 1.00 ng/mL
  - 0.9 mg dose: 8.88 ng/mL

**Immunomodulation** (dose-independent):
- Significant cytokine downregulation at 24 hours post-administration
- Cytokines affected: IL-12p70, IL-8, IL-6, IL-10, TNF
- Effect was dose-independent (occurred at all doses)

**Conclusion**: Alkamides rapidly absorbed with partial oral mucosal absorption; significant anti-inflammatory effects regardless of dose, suggesting threshold effect rather than dose-response for immunomodulation

## Bioavailability Summary

### Absorption Timeline
- **Onset**: 10-20 minutes (lozenges with oral mucosal absorption)
- **T-max**: 30-45 minutes (tinctures and tablets)
- **Absorption half-life**: Approximately 6 minutes

### Peak Concentrations (C-max)
- **Tinctures**: 0.40-10.88 ng/mL (depending on dose and species)
- **Tablets**: 0.12 ng/mL (approximately 25% of tincture)
- **Lozenges**: 1.00-8.88 ng/mL (dose-dependent, 0.21-0.9 mg alkamides)

### Formulation Comparison
1. **Tinctures (liquid alcoholic extracts)**:
   - Highest bioavailability
   - T-max 30 minutes
   - C-max 0.40-10.88 ng/mL

2. **Lozenges**:
   - Partial oral mucosal absorption
   - Faster onset (10-20 minutes)
   - Linear dose-response
   - C-max 1.00-8.88 ng/mL (0.21-0.9 mg doses)

3. **Tablets**:
   - Lower bioavailability (25% of tincture)
   - T-max 45 minutes
   - C-max 0.12 ng/mL
   - Equivalent immunological effect despite lower levels

## Immunological Activity

### Cytokine Modulation

**Woelkart et al. 2006** - 23 hours post-administration
- **TNF-alpha**: Reduced
- **IL-8**: Reduced
- **Effect**: Equivalent between tincture and tablet despite different alkamide levels

**Guiotto et al. 2008** - 24 hours post-administration (dose-independent)
- **IL-12p70**: Downregulated
- **IL-8**: Downregulated
- **IL-6**: Downregulated
- **IL-10**: Downregulated
- **TNF**: Downregulated

**Pattern**: Anti-inflammatory cytokine modulation occurs at low systemic alkamide concentrations, suggesting threshold effect rather than linear dose-response

### Clinical Immunological Effects

**Schapowal et al. 2015** - High-risk populations
- Individuals with elevated stress or immunological weakness show strongest preventative effects
- RR = 0.501 [95% CI: 0.380-0.661] for recurrent infections in this population

**Melchart et al. 1994** - Immunomodulation systematic review
- 26 trials examining immunomodulatory effects
- 30 of 34 treatment strategies showed superior efficacy to control
- Supports immunomodulatory mechanism of action

## Extract Type and Potency

### Alcoholic Extracts vs Pressed Juice

**Gancitano et al. 2024 findings**:
- Alcoholic extracts from fresh E. purpurea showed strongest antibiotic-sparing effects
- Antibiotic days reduction: IRR = 0.21 (80% reduction) - strongest effect
- Ethanolic extracts superior to pressed juices (Schapowal et al. 2015)

**Mechanism**: Higher alkamide extraction and bioavailability in alcoholic extracts

### Fresh vs Dried Plant Material

**Clinical trial emphasis**:
- Fresh-pressed E. purpurea: Commission E approved preparation (22% alcohol)
- Fresh-harvested alcoholic extracts: Strongest clinical effects (Ogal et al. 2021, Jawad et al. 2012)
- Dried extracts: Used in some standardized preparations (Goel et al. 2004)

**Pattern**: Fresh plant material in alcoholic extraction appears most effective

## Species-Specific Active Compounds

### E. purpurea
**Primary actives**:
- Alkamides (aerial parts and root)
- Cichoric acid (high levels)
- Polysaccharides
- Lower echinacoside content

**Clinical validation**: Most extensively studied; fresh aerial parts in alcoholic extract most effective

### E. angustifolia
**Primary actives**:
- Alkamides (root) - distinct profile from E. purpurea
- Echinacoside (higher levels)
- Cichoric acid (lower than E. purpurea)

**Clinical validation**: Bioavailability established (Woelkart et al. 2005); WHO recommends (1999) but fewer modern clinical trials

### E. pallida
**Primary actives**:
- Echinacoside (high levels)
- Lower alkamide content
- Distinct polysaccharide profile

**Clinical validation**: Commission E approved (root, water-alcohol extract) but less commonly studied in modern trials

## Standardization Practices

### Modern Standardization Approaches

1. **Alkamide standardization** (primary):
   - 0.07-0.9 mg per dose
   - Marker for E. purpurea and E. angustifolia

2. **Cichoric acid co-standardization**:
   - 2.5 mg/mL or 4%
   - Quality control marker
   - Higher in E. purpurea

3. **Polysaccharide content**:
   - 25 mg/mL in some preparations
   - May contribute to immunomodulation

4. **Multi-marker standardization** (Goel et al. 2004 example):
   - Alkamides: 0.25 mg/mL
   - Cichoric acid: 2.5 mg/mL
   - Polysaccharides: 25 mg/mL

### Implications for Efficacy

**Schapowal et al. 2015 findings**:
- Ethanolic extracts superior to pressed juices
- Increased dosing during acute episodes enhances effectiveness
- Suggests alkamide concentration and bioavailability are key factors

**Clinical pattern**: Standardization to alkamides with high-extraction alcoholic methods produces strongest clinical effects, especially for antibiotic-sparing outcomes

## Bioavailability Enhancement Strategies

1. **Alcoholic extraction**: Superior to aqueous or pressed juice
2. **Fresh plant material**: Enhanced over dried
3. **Tincture formulations**: Highest alkamide bioavailability
4. **Oral mucosal absorption** (lozenges): Faster onset, bypasses first-pass metabolism
5. **High-dose loading**: Modern protocols use 10,000-16,800 mg day 1 to achieve therapeutic levels

## Threshold vs Dose-Response Effects

**Key finding** (Guiotto et al. 2008):
- Immunomodulatory effects (cytokine downregulation) occurred at all doses tested (0.07-0.9 mg alkamides)
- Effect was dose-independent at 24 hours
- Suggests threshold effect for immunological activity

**Clinical implication**:
- Minimum effective alkamide dose may be lower than previously thought for prevention
- High loading doses for acute treatment may work through different mechanisms (viral clearance, symptom reduction) than immunomodulation
- Preventative dosing (400-900 mg daily) may achieve threshold immunomodulation
- Acute high-dose protocols (10,000+ mg) may provide additional benefits beyond immunomodulation
