# Areca Nut — Clinical Uses

*Areca catechu L. | Arecaceae*

---

## Overview

Clinical data for areca nut splits into two distinct bodies: (1) historical IV arecoline studies in Alzheimer's disease (1981–1996), and (2) a major cessation RCT (BENIT, 2023). No RCTs of standardized oral areca nut extract for any therapeutic indication have been identified. Traditional TCM anthelmintic use is pharmacopoeia-documented but without modern controlled trials.

---

## Alzheimer's Disease / Cognitive Enhancement (Arecoline IV)

All human therapeutic studies used **intravenous arecoline infusion** — not oral areca nut. Results cannot be extrapolated to oral supplementation.

### Sitaram et al. (1981) — *Science* 211:1085–1087
- **Design:** Crossover, placebo-controlled IV infusion
- **n = 10** patients with Alzheimer presenile dementia
- **Intervention:** IV arecoline vs physostigmine vs placebo
- **Outcome:** Both arecoline and physostigmine produced significant improvement in serial learning vs placebo
- **Significance:** First human clinical demonstration of cholinomimetic benefit in Alzheimer's

### Davidson et al. (1988) — *Psychopharmacology*
- **Design:** Multiple-dose IV infusion, dose-escalation
- **Population:** Alzheimer's disease patients
- **Outcome:** Transient improvements in specific cognitive domains; verbal ability at lower doses, attention/visuospatial at higher doses; high individual variability; no significant improvement on most episodic learning tasks

### Raffaele et al. (1991) — *Psychopharmacology*
- **Design:** Open-label, continuous IV infusion up to 2 weeks
- **n = 9** (mild to moderate Alzheimer's)
- **Dose:** Individually optimized, range **4–16 mg/day continuous IV**
- **Outcome:**
  - 5 of 9 subjects (56%) showed significant, replicable improvement in verbal memory
  - Long-term recall improved from **11.8 → 20.1 words** (p < 0.05) at 4 mg/day
  - Cognitive response correlated with intact cholinergic system (EEG spectral analysis)
  - No cholinergic side effects at individually optimized low doses

### Soncrant & Raffaele et al. (1993) — *Psychopharmacology*
- **Design:** Dose-finding, continuous IV arecoline
- **n = 9** (mild to moderate Alzheimer's)
- **Dose:** **4 mg/day continuous IV** (optimal responder dose)
- **Outcome:**
  - Memory improvement in ~55% of subjects (5/9)
  - Verbal memory (selective reminding): **11.8 → 20.1 words** recalled (p < 0.05)
  - No neuroendocrine stress response (cortisol, ACTH, beta-endorphin unchanged)
  - No adverse effects at 4 mg/day
- **Safety:** Favorable profile at this dose

### Raffaele et al. (1996) — *Neuropsychopharmacology*
- **Design:** Dose-escalation IV infusion crossover
- **n = 15** subjects with Alzheimer's disease
- **Key finding:** Dose-modality interaction — verbal ability improves at LOW doses, attention/visuospatial at HIGHER doses
- **Dose range:** Optimal individual doses ranged **fourfold** across subjects (4–16 mg/day IV)
- **Pattern:** Inverted U dose-response curve; too high or too low doses ineffective
- **Significance:** Different cognitive domains have different optimal doses — individualized dosing required

### Pang et al. (1996) — *Clinical Pharmacology and Therapeutics* (PK study)
- **n = 15** Alzheimer's disease subjects
- **Design:** Single high dose (5 mg over 30 min) + chronic multiple-dose steady-state infusions up to 2 weeks
- **PK results:**
  - Bimodal plasma half-life: initial t½ = **0.95 ± 0.54 min**; terminal t½ = **9.3 ± 4.5 min**
  - Optimal therapeutic dose varied **fourfold** across subjects
  - No accumulation during chronic infusion at stable dose
  - Cholinergic side effects dose-limiting at higher infusion rates

## Areca Nut Cessation (BENIT Trial)

### Tsoh et al. (2023) — *Int J Environ Res Public Health*
- **Design:** Randomized, controlled, superiority trial (first known RCT for areca nut cessation)
- **n = 176** (88 intervention, 88 control); enrolled August 2016; Guam and Saipan
- **Intervention:** 5 in-person behavioral cessation sessions over 22 days
- **Comparator:** Cessation brochure only
- **Primary outcome:** 7-day point-prevalence abstinence at day 22
- **Results:**
  - Cessation rate: **38.6% vs 9.1%** (p < 0.001)
  - Estimated 71% reduction in chewing (95% CI: 41–88%) in intervention vs control
  - Salivary arecoline and cotinine used as objective biomarkers
- **Limitations:** Short follow-up (22 days); long-term abstinence rates unknown; no pharmacotherapy arm

## Antiparasitic (Anthelmintic)

### An et al. (2012) — *Acta Tropica* 124:152–157 — Human Treatment Study
- **Design:** Three-arm community-based treatment comparison in northwest Sichuan Province, China
- **Population:** Confirmed taeniasis cases (*Taenia* spp.)
- **Arms:** Pumpkin seeds alone vs areca nut extract alone vs combination
- **Results:**
  - Areca nut extract alone: **63.6% (7/11)** expelled whole tapeworms; mean expulsion time 6 h 27 min (range 1–22 h)
  - Pumpkin seeds alone: **75.0% (9/12)** expelled whole tapeworms; mean expulsion time 14 h 10 min
  - Combination (areca nut + pumpkin seed): synergistic — faster expulsion than either component alone (mean ~2 h)
  - Adverse effects in combination group: mild nausea and dizziness in ~46.3% of patients; all transient and self-limiting
- **Significance:** Human evidence confirming traditional TCM anthelmintic use; supports Chinese Pharmacopoeia antiparasitic indication

### Traditional Anthelmintic Use (Chinese Pharmacopoeia — No Modern RCTs for Standard Doses)

The Chinese Pharmacopoeia documents anthelmintic use since 1953. The An et al. (2012) study is the only identified controlled human treatment study.

- **Tapeworm (Taenia spp.):** 30–60 g dried seed decoction (single dose; pharmacopoeial)
- **General parasites (hookworm, pinworm, whipworm, fasciolopsiasis):** 3–10 g dried seed decoction
- **Mechanism:** Arecoline causes flaccid paralysis of cestode musculature at low mM concentrations
- **Veterinary evidence:** 98% tapeworm clearance in dogs at 40 mg arecoline hydrobromide orally (Wu et al., 2015)

## Schizophrenia — Observational (Muscarinic Pathway)

These are naturalistic observational studies, not trials. They document associations between habitual betel nut chewing and reduced schizophrenia symptoms, and contributed to the development of muscarinic M1/M4 agonists as antipsychotics (xanomeline-trospium / Cobenfy, FDA-approved 2024).

### Sullivan et al. (2000) — *British Journal of Psychiatry* 177:174–178
- **Design:** Cross-sectional observational
- **n = 70** (40 chewers, 30 non-chewers; all with diagnosed schizophrenia; Palau, Micronesia)
- **Assessment:** PANSS (Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale) + ESRS
- **Results:**
  - Chewers scored significantly lower on PANSS positive subscale (p = 0.001)
  - Chewers scored significantly lower on PANSS negative subscale (p = 0.002)
- **Limitations:** Cross-sectional; betel quid used (not isolated areca nut); possible confounding by tobacco

### Sullivan et al. (2007) — *American Journal of Psychiatry*
- **Design:** Longitudinal observational follow-up cohort; Palau, Micronesia
- **Results:** Male high-consumption chewers had significantly lower positive symptoms vs low consumers or non-users
- **Significance:** Longitudinal design strengthens cross-sectional 2000 findings

### Nepal cohort (2009) — *Longitudinal observational*
- **Population:** Schizophrenic patients in Nepal (independent South Asian cohort)
- **Results:** Lower PANSS scores associated with regular betel nut use; cross-cultural replication of Palau findings

**Mechanistic hypothesis:** Arecoline's muscarinic M1 agonism may reduce positive/negative schizophrenia symptoms via cholinergic-dopaminergic interaction — the same mechanism later exploited by xanomeline in Cobenfy.

| Indication | Evidence Level | Route | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Alzheimer's / memory | Small trials (n=9–15) | IV arecoline only | Not applicable to oral use |

| Anthelmintic (tapeworm) | Human treatment study (n=11) + pharmacopoeia | Oral extract/decoction | 63.6% expulsion in human study |
