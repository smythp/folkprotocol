# Areca Nut — Traditional Uses

*Areca catechu L. | Arecaceae*

---

## Overview

Areca nut is consumed by approximately 600 million people globally, making it the fourth most widely used psychoactive substance after tobacco, alcohol, and caffeine (Zain et al., 2021; IARC Vol. 85, 2004). Traditional use spans South Asia, Southeast Asia, and East Asia with distinct cultural and medicinal contexts across systems.

---

## Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)

**Chinese name:** Bing Lang (槟榔)
**Part used:** Ripe seed (Semen Arecae)
**First included in Chinese Pharmacopoeia:** 1953
**Ranking:** First among China's "four southern medicines" (Lin et al., 2024)

### Pharmacopoeial Actions (Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2020, 11th Edition)
- **Sha chong xiao ji** — kills parasites, reduces stagnation/accumulation
- **Xing qi li shui** — moves qi, promotes urination (resolves dampness)

### Indications
- Tapeworm, hookworm, pinworm, whipworm, fasciolopsiasis
- Qi stagnation (bloating, indigestion, constipation)
- Oedema
- Malaria (in combined formulas)

### Traditional Preparations
- **Standard decoction:** Water decoction 20–30 min
  - General use: 3–10 g dried seed
  - Tapeworm treatment: 30–60 g dried seed (single higher dose)
- Combined formulas for parasitic infections (typically paired with pumpkin seed for tapeworm)

### TCM Cautions
- Use with caution in pregnancy
- Avoid prolonged high-dose use

## Ayurveda

**Sanskrit name:** Kramuka (क्रमुक) / Puga
**System:** Ayurveda (Indian traditional medicine)

### Uses
- Digestive complaints and mouth freshening (primary modern use)
- Stimulant; mild euphoriant
- Betel quid preparation: areca nut + betel leaf (*Piper betle*) + slaked lime — a distinct combination with different phytochemistry from areca nut alone
- Oral hygiene: astringent properties attributed to tannin fraction

### Cultural Context
- Betel quid chewing is a ceremonial and social practice in South Asia
- Offered at weddings, religious ceremonies, and as gesture of hospitality
- Initiation predominantly in adolescence via peer influence and cultural norms (Zain et al., 2021)

---

## Southeast Asian Traditional Medicine

**Regional names:** Pinang (Malay/Indonesian), various local names
**Countries of primary use:** India, Bangladesh, Taiwan, China, Papua New Guinea, Pacific Islands, Myanmar, Thailand, Vietnam

### Uses
- Stimulant (wakefulness, mild euphoria)
- Antiparasitic (tapeworm, roundworm)
- Digestive aid
- Oral hygiene (astringent)

### Preparation Forms
- Fresh nut (highest alkaloid content; immature > mature nut)
- Dried/cured nut (20–45% reduction in alkaloids vs fresh)
- Boiled preparations (further alkaloid reduction)
- Lime (calcium hydroxide) is typically added to betel quid — raises pH, affects alkaloid absorption

## Traditional Use History Summary

| System | Centuries of Use | Primary Indications | Preparation |
|---|---|---|---|
| TCM | Documented since at least 7th c. CE; pharmacopoeia since 1953 | Anthelmintic, qi-moving, GI | Water decoction |
| Ayurveda | Classical texts; widespread | Digestive, stimulant, oral hygiene | Betel quid (nut + leaf + lime) |
| Southeast Asian | Pre-colonial; widespread | Stimulant, antiparasitic, social | Fresh/dried nut; betel quid |

---

## Key Distinction: Nut vs Betel Quid

The IARC Group 1 carcinogen classification applies to **areca nut itself** — not only to tobacco-containing betel quid. Betel leaf (*Piper betle*) is a separate plant with a different safety profile. Traditional preparations always combined these ingredients; modern research must be read with the distinction between the nut, the leaf, and the full betel quid in mind.

## Dependence and Social Context

- Dependence criteria met by ~34% of habitual users (DSM-IV; Dassanayake et al., 2011; n=800 Sri Lankan adults)
- Mean Severity of Dependence Score: 7.3 (comparable to moderate nicotine dependence)
- Withdrawal: mood swings, anxiety, irritability, reduced concentration, sleep disturbances, increased appetite
- No approved pharmacotherapy exists for areca nut dependence (unlike nicotine)
- Social/cultural factors dominant in initiation and maintenance: ceremonial use, availability, peer norms
