# Areca Nut — Preparations

*Areca catechu L. | Arecaceae*

---

## Important Context

No standardized oral therapeutic extract has been validated in clinical trials. All preparation data comes from traditional pharmacopoeia, historical practice, and analytical chemistry. The IARC Group 1 carcinogen classification applies to the nut itself — any preparation carries inherent risk.

---

## Traditional Decoction (TCM — Pharmacopoeial)

**Source:** Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2020 (11th Edition)
**Part used:** Ripe seed (Semen Arecae), dried

| Indication | Dose | Method |
|---|---|---|
| General (qi stagnation, oedema, minor parasites) | 3–10 g dried seed | Water decoction, 20–30 min |
| Tapeworm treatment (Taenia spp.) | 30–60 g dried seed | Single-dose decoction |

**Standardization marker:** Arecoline ≥ 0.20% (w/w) on dried basis by HPLC

**Preparation:** Standard water decoction
1. Weigh dried seed
2. Add water (typically 10× seed weight)
3. Bring to boil, simmer 20–30 min
4. Strain and drink

**Notes:**
- Tapeworm dose (30–60 g) is significantly higher than general dose and carries greater alkaloid load
- Traditionally paired with pumpkin seed (*Cucurbita moschata*) for tapeworm
- Processing (drying, boiling) reduces alkaloid content by 20–45% vs fresh nut

## Tincture

No pharmacopoeial or clinical tincture standard exists. Based on alkaloid content of dried seed:

- **Typical herb-to-menstruum ratio for alkaloid-containing seeds:** 1:5 (w/v) in 60–70% ethanol
- **Theoretical arecoline yield:** Given dried seed arecoline at 0.20–0.63% (pharmacopoeial minimum to typical range), a 1:5 tincture would yield approximately 0.4–1.26 mg/mL arecoline equivalent
- No validated clinical dose; no safety data for tincture form
- Ethanol may alter alkaloid extraction profile relative to water decoction

**Not recommended for therapeutic use** — no clinical validation, carcinogen status applies.

---

## Standardized Extract

No internationally validated standardized extract exists for areca nut.

Current state (Cheng et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024):
- No internationally recognized CoA requirements for areca nut preparations
- Batch-to-batch variability in commercial preparations: up to **3-fold difference** in arecoline content
- If standardization were applied, the Chinese Pharmacopoeia marker (arecoline ≥ 0.20%) is the only documented specification

**Key standardization challenges:**
- Alkaloid content varies with nut maturity, geographic origin, drying method, storage duration
- Polyphenol profile varies independently of alkaloid content
- No consensus on whether alkaloid or polyphenol fraction is the primary therapeutic target
- Immature nuts have significantly higher alkaloid content than mature nuts

## Arecoline Hydrobromide (Historical/Veterinary)

- Historical human anthelmintic use: **not established** (dose not defined in literature)
- Veterinary anthelmintic: **40 mg oral in dogs** — 98% tapeworm clearance (Wu et al., 2015)
- Historical animal dosing reference: **2–4 mg/kg** in animals (arecoline hydrobromide; Zhang et al., 2024)
- Human IV research dose: **4–16 mg/day continuous infusion** (Alzheimer studies; Raffaele et al., 1991, 1996)

## Betel Quid (Traditional Oral Preparation)

**Components:** Areca nut + Betel leaf (*Piper betle*) + Slaked lime (calcium hydroxide) ± tobacco
- Lime raises pH → increases extraction and absorption of arecoline from nut
- Betel leaf contains its own alkaloids (chavicol, eugenol) with distinct pharmacology
- Tobacco addition substantially increases cancer and dependence risk
- This is the primary traditional delivery route for ~600 million users globally

**Note:** Betel quid is a combination preparation — effects and risks differ from areca nut alone and from betel leaf alone.

## Analytical Quality Control

For any research or preparation quality control:

| Method | Application |
|---|---|
| HPLC-IMS (Li et al., 2022) | Simultaneous quantification of all 4 alkaloids (arecoline, arecaidine, guvacoline, guvacine); LOD 0.01–0.02 mg/kg |
| HPLC-MS/MS | Gold standard; best for trace metabolites and metabolite profiling |
| NIR spectroscopy | Field-level rapid screening for gallic acid, tannins, catechin, arecoline (Cheng et al., 2023) |
