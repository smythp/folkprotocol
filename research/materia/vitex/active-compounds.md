# Vitex agnus-castus - Active Compounds and Bioavailability

## Primary Active Compounds

### Diterpenes (Clerodadienols)

**Function**: Primary bioactive compounds responsible for clinical effects.

**Mechanism of Action**:
- Act as dopaminergic agents
- Bind to dopamine D2 receptors
- Inhibit prolactin release
- Possess prolactin-suppressive properties almost identical to dopamine itself [Wuttke et al. 2003]

**Clinical Significance**:
- Primary mechanism for PMS symptom relief
- Reduce elevated serum prolactin in mastalgia
- Normalize luteal phase in hyperprolactinemia
- Affect downstream sex hormone production

**Chemical Class**: Clerodadienols (specific clerodadiene-type diterpenes).

## Standardization Marker Compounds

### Casticin

**Role**: Primary standardization marker for quality control.

**Chemical Formula**: C19H18O8; Molecular Weight: 374.3

**Official Standards**:
- **European Pharmacopoeia**: Minimum 0.08% casticin in dried drug [Reference 01/2015:2147]
- **German Commission E**: 0.6% casticin in extract
- **Clinical Products**: Ze 440 standardized for casticin content

**Analytical Detection**:
- HPLC (High-Performance Liquid Chromatography) analysis
- Specific retention times and UV absorption profiles
- Used for product quality verification

**Quality Variability**:
- Sogame et al. 2019 study of 11 products (1 medicinal, 10 dietary supplements):
  - Medicinal product aligned with European reference standards
  - Dietary supplements showed significant variability
  - Casticin not detected in 2 dietary supplements
  - Wide fluctuations in marker compound concentrations across products

### Agnuside

**Role**: Secondary standardization marker; chemotaxonomic marker of genus Vitex.

**Chemical Characteristics**:
- Iridoid glycoside compound
- Suitable for quality control alongside casticin
- Used in HPLC fingerprinting

**Quality Control Application**:
- Dual-marker system (agnuside + casticin) provides better quality assurance
- Agnuside not detected in 2 dietary supplements [Sogame et al. 2019]
- Ratio of agnuside to casticin can indicate species adulteration (e.g., Vitex negundo contamination)

**German Commission E Standard**: 0.6% agnuside (alternative to casticin).

## Additional Marker Compounds

### Other Compounds Used in Analysis
[Sogame et al. 2019 UHPLC-DAD method]:

1. **Isovitexin**: Flavonoid compound
2. **5-hydroxykaempferol-3,6,7,4′-tetramethylether**: Methylated flavonoid
3. **Vitetrifolin D**: Diterpene compound

**Analytical Parameters**:
- Column: Zorbax Eclipse XDB-C18 (50mm × 2.1mm, 1.8μm particle size)
- Mobile phase: Gradient from 0.5% acetic acid to acetonitrile
- Flow rate: 0.6 mL/min

**Method Validation**:
- Intra-day RSD: <3.9%
- Inter-day RSD: <6.4%
- Demonstrated specificity, accuracy, precision, reproducibility

## Bioavailability and Pharmacokinetics

### Agnuside Pharmacokinetics in Mice

**Study Type**: Pharmacokinetic study in mice.

**Key Parameters**:
- **Oral bioavailability**: 0.7%
- **Tmax** (time to maximum concentration): 30-45 minutes

**Clinical Implication**:
- Very low oral bioavailability for agnuside specifically
- May explain need for relatively high doses of extract
- Other compounds (diterpenes) may have different bioavailability profiles

### Human Pharmacokinetics

**Data Available**: None.

**EMA Assessment**: No specific information on pharmacokinetics in humans.

**Research Gap**: This is a significant limitation acknowledged in multiple reviews.

### Bioavailability Enhancement Research

**Nanoemulsion Formulation Study** [2021, PMC]:
- **Rationale**: Many herbal extracts have low in-vivo activity due to:
  - Poor solubility
  - Poor absorption
  - Low bioavailability
- **Approach**: Novel nanoemulsion formulation to improve oral delivery
- **Goal**: Enhance bioavailability of Vitex agnus-castus extract
- **Status**: In development/research phase (not commercially available)

## Analytical Methods for Quality Control

### HPLC Fingerprinting

**Purpose**: Identify and quantify marker compounds for quality assurance.

**Method Details** [Sogame et al. 2019]:
- UHPLC-DAD (Ultra-High-Performance Liquid Chromatography with Diode-Array Detection)
- Simultaneous quantification of multiple markers
- Fingerprint comparison with European reference standards

**Applications**:
- Product quality verification
- Species authentication (detect Vitex negundo contamination)
- Standardization verification
- Batch-to-batch consistency

### Quality Issues Identified

**Study of 11 Commercial Products** [Sogame et al. 2019]:

**Findings**:
1. Medicinal product (1): Aligned with European reference standards
2. Dietary supplements (10):
   - Significant variability in marker compounds
   - 2 supplements: Agnuside and/or casticin not detected
   - Wide fluctuations in concentrations
   - 1 supplement: Possible contamination with Vitex negundo (based on marker ratios)
   - 2 supplements: Poor disintegration test results

**Quality Concerns**:
- Incorrect or poor-quality botanical origin
- Different contents of active ingredients than claimed
- Unauthorized manufacturing procedures
- Lack of proper standardization

**Clinical Implication**: Product quality is highly variable; medicinal-grade products with proper standardization are essential for clinical efficacy.

## Standardization Systems

### European Pharmacopoeia Standard

**Reference**: 01/2015:2147

**Botanical Definition**:
- Whole, ripe, dried fruit of Vitex agnus-castus L.
- Must meet minimum quality standards

**Chemical Standard**:
- Minimum 0.08% casticin (C19H18O8; Mr 374.3) in dried drug

**Application**: Baseline quality standard for raw material.

### German Commission E Standardization

**Extract Standard**:
- 0.6% casticin OR 0.6% agnuside
- Applies to dried fruit extract at 30-40mg daily dose

**Alternative Forms**:
- 2.6-4.2mg dry native extract (higher concentration)
- 40 drops tincture

**Philosophy**: Higher standardization (0.6%) than European Pharmacopoeia (0.08%) reflects concentration in extract vs. raw fruit.

### Clinical Product Standardization

**Ze 440**:
- Extraction: 60% ethanol
- Ratio: 6-12:1 (extract to raw material)
- Standardization marker: Casticin
- Dose equivalence: 20mg extract = 120-240mg dry weight
- Clinical evidence: Dose-finding study established optimal 20mg dose

**BNO 1095** (Agnucaston/Cyclodynon):
- Extraction: 70% ethanol, 30% water
- Ratio: 10:1
- Dose equivalence: 4mg extract = 40mg dry weight
- Clinical evidence: Included in meta-analysis (Csupor et al. 2019)

## Extraction Ratios and Active Compound Concentration

### Extract Concentration Factors

**Ze 440**: 6-12:1 ratio
- Meaning: 6-12 grams of dried fruit → 1 gram of extract
- 20mg extract ≈ 120-240mg dried fruit equivalent

**BNO 1095**: 10:1 ratio
- Meaning: 10 grams of dried fruit → 1 gram of extract
- 4mg extract ≈ 40mg dried fruit equivalent

### Solvent Systems

**Ethanol-Based Extraction**:
- Rationale: Diterpenes and casticin are lipophilic (fat-soluble)
- Ethanol concentration: 50-70% optimal
- Higher ethanol % extracts more lipophilic compounds
- Water component may extract water-soluble constituents (iridoids like agnuside)

**Clinical Products Use Ethanol**:
- Ze 440: 60% ethanol
- BNO 1095: 70% ethanol + 30% water
- EMA standard: 50-70% V/V ethanol
- Traditional tinctures: Alcohol-based

## Mechanism of Action Summary

### Dopaminergic Pathway

**Primary Mechanism** [Wuttke et al. 2003]:
1. Diterpenes (clerodadienols) bind to dopamine D2 receptors
2. Mimics dopamine action in anterior pituitary
3. Inhibits prolactin release from lactotroph cells
4. Reduced prolactin → downstream hormonal effects

**Clinical Effects of Prolactin Reduction**:
- Reduced breast pain (mastalgia)
- Normalized luteal phase
- Improved progesterone synthesis
- Reduced PMS symptoms
- Fertility enhancement (in hyperprolactinemia cases)

### Proposed Additional Mechanisms

**Hormonal Cascade Effects**:
- Normalization of luteal phase → increased progesterone
- Increased 17-beta-estradiol in luteal phase (observed in Milewicz et al. 1993)
- Restoration of hormone balance in follicular-luteal transition

**Possible Direct Effects** (less well-documented):
- Potential phytoestrogenic activity (suggested in Naseri et al. 2019 menopause study)
- Other receptor interactions (not well-characterized)

## Quality Control: Key Takeaways

### For Clinical Use
1. **Choose medicinal-grade products** over dietary supplements
2. **Verify standardization**: 0.6% casticin or agnuside
3. **Check for European Pharmacopoeia compliance** (minimum 0.08% casticin in raw material)
4. **Use researched extracts**: Ze 440, BNO 1095, or equivalent standardized products

### Red Flags for Poor Quality
- No standardization information
- Dietary supplements with vague labeling
- Products without HPLC testing
- Absence of casticin/agnuside content claims
- Very low prices (suggesting low-quality raw material)
- Poor disintegration (doesn't dissolve properly)

### Analytical Testing
- HPLC fingerprinting confirms identity and quality
- Dual-marker system (casticin + agnuside) better than single marker
- Comparison with reference standards essential

## Research Gaps and Future Directions

### Critical Unknowns

1. **Human Pharmacokinetics**:
   - No data on absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion (ADME)
   - Unknown bioavailability of diterpenes (only agnuside studied, 0.7% in mice)
   - Time to peak concentration unknown in humans
   - Half-life unknown

2. **Active Compound Identity**:
   - Which specific diterpenes are most active?
   - Is casticin itself active, or just a marker?
   - What is the contribution of agnuside vs. diterpenes?

3. **Dose-Response Relationships**:
   - Are marker compounds (casticin, agnuside) responsible for effects?
   - What minimum concentration of diterpenes is needed?
   - Can synthetic diterpenes replicate effects?

4. **Bioavailability Enhancement**:
   - Can nanoemulsion formulations improve efficacy?
   - Would higher bioavailability allow lower doses?

### Future Research Priorities

**Pharmacokinetic Studies**:
- Human ADME studies with standardized extracts
- Bioavailability studies of individual diterpenes
- Food effect studies
- Drug interaction pharmacokinetics

**Mechanistic Research**:
- Isolate and test individual diterpenes
- Receptor binding studies (D2 and others)
- Molecular mechanism elucidation
- Other potential targets beyond dopamine receptors

**Quality Improvement**:
- Better standardization methods
- International quality standards
- Supply chain verification
- Genetic authentication methods

## Summary: Active Compound Profile

### Primary Actives
**Diterpenes (clerodadienols)**: Dopamine D2 receptor agonists; inhibit prolactin; almost identical action to dopamine.

### Standardization Markers
- **Casticin**: Primary marker; 0.08-0.6% depending on form
- **Agnuside**: Secondary marker; iridoid glycoside

### Bioavailability
- **Very low** for agnuside (0.7% oral in mice)
- **Unknown** for diterpenes in humans
- **Enhancement strategies** under investigation (nanoemulsions)

### Quality Control
- **HPLC essential** for product verification
- **High variability** in commercial products
- **Medicinal-grade products** recommended

### Mechanism
- **Dopaminergic** (primary): D2 receptor activation → prolactin inhibition → hormone normalization
- **Additional effects** (secondary): Hormonal cascade effects on progesterone, estradiol
