## Official Monograph Recognition of Traditional Use

### WHO Monographs on Selected Medicinal Plants
- **Volume**: 4
- **Year**: 2004
- **Inclusion**: Vitex agnus-castus
- **Implication**: WHO inclusion indicates recognized traditional medicinal use across cultures

### German Commission E Monograph
- **Approval**: Pre-2000
- **Status**: Approved for traditional medicinal use in Germany
- **Approved traditional indications**:
  - Menstrual cycle irregularities
  - Premenstrual syndrome
  - Mastalgia (breast pain)
  - Menopausal disorders

### European Medicines Agency (EMA/HMPC)
- **Authority**: Herbal Medicinal Products Committee
- **Status**: Traditional herbal medicinal product based on long-standing use
- **Primary indication**: Treatment of premenstrual syndrome
- **Preparation**: Liquid or dried extracts with ethanol (50-70% V/V)
- **Daily dosage**: 30-40mg herbal substance

### European Pharmacopoeia
- **Reference**: 01/2015:2147
- **Official name**: Agnus Castus Fruit (Agni casti fructus)
- **Description**: Whole, ripe, dried fruit of Vitex agnus-castus L.
- **Standardization**: Minimum 0.08% casticin (C19H18O8; Mr 374.3) in dried drug
- **Implication**: Official pharmacopoeia inclusion indicates long-established traditional use in Europe

## Traditional Preparation Methods

### German Commission E Traditional Dosing
Based on long-standing European traditional use:

**Forms**:
- Dried fruit extract: 30-40mg daily
- Dry native extract: 2.6-4.2mg daily
- Tincture: 40 drops daily

**Administration**:
- Timing: Once in morning with liquid
- Duration: Several months consecutively
- Note: Considered long-term hormonal balancing herb rather than fast-acting remedy

### Traditional Standardization
- **Marker**: 0.6% casticin or agnuside
- **Rationale**: These compounds have been used traditionally to standardize preparations

## Historical Names and Cultural Context

### Common Names

**English**:
- Chaste tree
- Chasteberry
- Monk's pepper
- Agnus castus

**Latin botanical**:
- Vitex agnus-castus L.

### Etymological Implications

**"Chaste tree" and "Monk's pepper"**:
- These names suggest historical use related to sexual desire or reproductive function
- "Monk's pepper" implies traditional use by monks, possibly to reduce libido (though modern research shows opposite effects on hormones)
- Reflects European monastic herbal traditions

**"Agnus castus"** (Latin):
- "Agnus" = lamb (pure, chaste)
- "Castus" = chaste, pure
- Reinforces historical association with chastity and purity

## Traditional Preparation Details from Monographs

### Extraction Solvents (Traditional to Modern)
- **Ethanol-based**: 50-70% ethanol (EMA), 60% ethanol (Ze 440), 70% ethanol (BNO 1095)
- **Water component**: Some preparations include water (BNO 1095: 70% ethanol, 30% water)
- **Rationale**: Ethanol extraction captures diterpenes and other active compounds

### Plant Part Used
- **Fruit**: Whole, ripe, dried fruit (European Pharmacopoeia)
- **Not used**: Leaves, stems, roots (at least not in official preparations)

### Traditional Duration of Use
- **Minimum**: 3 menstrual cycles (for PMS)
- **Typical**: Several months consecutively
- **Pattern**: Long-term use rather than acute treatment
- **Philosophy**: Gradual hormonal rebalancing rather than symptom suppression

## Summary: Traditional Use Profile

### Primary Traditional System
European herbal medicine, particularly German phytotherapy tradition.

### Traditional Forms
- Dried fruit
- Tincture (alcohol extract)
- Native extract

### Traditional Dosing Pattern
- Daily use
- Morning administration
- Long-term treatment (months)
- Continuous rather than cyclical

### Traditional Philosophy
Gradual hormonal rebalancing herb rather than acute symptom treatment; considered a "tonic" for women's reproductive health.

### Historical Names
Reflect traditional beliefs about effects on chastity and purity, though modern research shows different mechanisms (dopaminergic prolactin reduction).
