# Vitex agnus-castus - Clinical Uses

## Premenstrual Syndrome (PMS)

### Evidence Quality
Multiple RCTs and meta-analyses with substantial pooled effects.

### Effect Sizes and Response Rates
- Hedges g = -1.21 (95% CI: -1.53 to -0.88) in systematic review [Verkaik et al. 2017]
- 2.57x more likely to achieve symptom remission vs placebo (95% CI 1.52-4.35) [Csupor et al. 2019, meta-analysis of 3 RCTs, 520 participants]
- 52% responder rate vs 24% placebo in Ze 440 trial [Schellenberg 2001, 170 participants]
- 91% responder rate by cycle 3 in Japanese population [Momoeda et al. 2014, 69 participants]

### Clinical Trial Results

**Ze 440 Dose-Finding Study** [Schellenberg et al. 2012]
- Sample: 162 women, ages 18-45
- Duration: 3 menstrual cycles
- Doses tested: Placebo, 8mg, 20mg, 30mg
- Result: 20mg demonstrated significantly greater improvement vs placebo and 8mg
- Note: 30mg showed no additional benefit over 20mg
- Optimal dose: 20mg daily (equivalent to 120-240mg dry weight)

**BMJ Placebo-Controlled Trial** [Schellenberg 2001]
- Sample: 170 women, mean age 36 (86 active, 84 placebo)
- Duration: 3 consecutive menstrual cycles
- Dose: One tablet daily of Ze 440 dry extract
- Results: Main efficacy improvement significantly greater in active group (P<0.001)
- All three global impression measures showed significant superiority (P<0.001)
- Adverse events: Mild and rare (4 active, 3 placebo), none causing discontinuation

**Comparison with Pyridoxine** [Lauritzen et al. 1997]
- Sample: 175 women (90 VAC, 85 pyridoxine)
- Duration: 3 treatment cycles
- Results:
  - PMTS scale scores: VAC decreased 15.2 to 5.1 (-47.4%); pyridoxine 11.9 to 5.1 (-48%)
  - VAC showed considerably more marked alleviation of: breast tenderness, edema, inner tension, headache, constipation, depression
  - Investigators rated VAC 'excellent' in 24.5% vs 12.1% for pyridoxine
  - Patient complaint-free rates: 36.1% (VAC) vs 21.3% (pyridoxine)
  - Adverse events: 12 VAC patients, 5 pyridoxine patients; no serious events

**Japanese Population Study** [Momoeda et al. 2014]
- Sample: 69 women, ages 18-44
- Design: Multi-center, prospective, open-label, single-arm phase 3
- Dose: 20mg Vitex extract (Prefemin®) once daily
- Duration: 3 menstrual cycles
- Results:
  - After first cycle: statistically significant decrease in total VAS score (P<0.001)
  - Symptom improvement continued across all three cycles
  - 91% responder rate by cycle 3
  - Almost all patients without symptoms or exhibited only mild symptoms
  - 8 patients experienced minor adverse events; one allergic dermatitis case had uncertain causal relationship

### Dosing Protocols
- **Optimal dose**: 20mg Ze 440 daily OR 4mg BNO 1095 daily
- **Duration**: Minimum 3 menstrual cycles
- **Timing**: Once in morning with liquid
- **Standardization**: 0.6% casticin or agnuside

### Comparative Efficacy
Superior to:
- Pyridoxine (vitamin B6)
- Magnesium oxide

## Cyclic Mastalgia (Breast Pain)

### Evidence Quality
Systematic review of 25 studies (17 RCTs, 8 nonrandomized trials); meta-analysis of 6 studies with 718 participants.

### Effect Size
- Moderate effect size: SMD 0.67 (95% CI: 0.5-0.85) favoring VAC over placebo [Ooi et al. 2020]

### Clinical Trial Results

**Placebo-Controlled Double-Blind Study** [Halaska et al. 1999]
- Sample: 97 women (48 VACS, 49 placebo)
- Dose: 2 x 30 drops/day
- Duration: 3 treatment cycles with baseline assessment
- Results:
  - After 1 cycle: VACS 21.4mm pain reduction vs 10.6mm placebo
  - After 2 cycles: VACS 33.7mm reduction vs 20.3mm placebo (p=0.006)
  - After 3 cycles: VACS 34.3mm reduction vs 25.7mm placebo (p=0.064)
  - Adverse events comparable: 5 in VACS, 4 in placebo

### Mechanism
Reduces elevated serum prolactin levels in reproductive-age women (18-45 years).

### Comparative Efficacy
Seven trials demonstrated VAC as noninferior alternative to:
- Dopamine agonists
- NSAIDs
- SSRIs
- Hormonal contraceptives

### Dosing Protocols
- **Dose**: 20-40mg daily OR 2 x 30 drops/day
- **Duration**: 3 treatment cycles
- **Population**: Effective in reproductive-age women (18-45 years)

### Safety
Safe and associated with only mild and reversible adverse events.

## Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD)

### Evidence Quality
Limited RCT data; mixed results in systematic reviews.

### Clinical Trial Results

**Comparison with Fluoxetine** [Atmaca et al. 2003]
- Sample: 41 women with PMDD per DSM-IV
- Design: Randomized controlled, single-blind, rater-blinded, prospective comparison
- Screening: 2 months
- Treatment duration: 2 months
- Outcome measures: Penn daily symptom report, Hamilton depression rating scale, Clinical Global Impression scales
- Results:
  - Fluoxetine response rate: 68.4% (n=13)
  - Vitex response rate: 57.9% (n=11)
  - No statistically significant difference between groups
  - Fluoxetine more effective for psychological symptoms
  - Vitex showed better effects on physical symptoms
  - Both treatments well-tolerated

### Differential Effects
- **Vitex**: Better for physical symptoms
- **SSRIs (fluoxetine)**: Better for psychological symptoms

### Systematic Review Findings
- One study showed equivalence to fluoxetine
- Another found fluoxetine outperformed herbal extract
- Overall: Mixed results [van Die et al. 2013, review of 12 RCTs]

## Hyperprolactinemia and Luteal Phase Defects

### Evidence Quality
Small RCTs; recent systematic review (2023) of 9 papers (6 prospective studies, 3 case reports).

### Mechanism
Diterpenes interact with dopamine D2 receptors to inhibit prolactin release [Puglia et al. 2023].

### Clinical Trial Results

**Latent Hyperprolactinemia RCT** [Milewicz et al. 1993]
- Sample: 52 women (37 complete cases: 20 placebo, 17 treatment)
- Design: Randomized placebo-controlled double-blind
- Dose: 20mg Vitex agnus castus preparation daily
- Duration: 3 months
- Results:
  - Prolactin release reduced after 3 months in treatment group
  - Shortened luteal phases normalized
  - Deficits in luteal progesterone synthesis eliminated
  - 17-beta-estradiol rose in luteal phase in patients receiving verum
  - No adverse effects observed
  - **2 women in treatment group became pregnant during study**

### Comparison with Bromocriptine

**RCT Comparing VAC with Bromocriptine** [Irum et al. 2020]
- Sample: 88 women (2 treatment groups)
- Results:
  - No significant difference (p>0.05) between groups for prolactin and LH levels after treatment
  - Higher rate of side effects in bromocriptine group vs Vitex group
  - **Vitex has efficacy equal to bromocriptine but with fewer side effects**
  - Comparable for reducing serum prolactin levels and ameliorating cyclic mastalgia

### Systematic Review Conclusion [Puglia et al. 2023]
- Some evidence suggests possible role for management of hyperprolactinemia in selected patients
- Not any large randomized controlled trials supporting this application
- May represent potentially useful and safe phytotherapic option for selected patients with mild hyperprolactinemia
- Need for larger, well-designed studies

### Dosing Protocols
- **Dose**: 20mg daily
- **Duration**: 3 months minimum

### Clinical Effects
- Reduces prolactin secretion
- Normalizes shortened luteal phase
- Eliminates progesterone synthesis deficits
- Increases 17-beta-estradiol in luteal phase
- Comparable to bromocriptine in effectiveness

## Menopausal Symptoms

### Evidence Quality
Limited RCT data; one well-designed double-blind study.

### Clinical Trial Results

**Comparison with Placebo** [Naseri et al. 2019]
- Sample: 52 women (26 Vitex, 26 placebo)
- Design: Randomized controlled double-blind clinical trial
- Duration: 8 weeks
- Results:
  - Mean scores for total menopausal disorder significantly lower in Vitex group (P<0.05)
  - **Anxiety**: Significantly reduced (P<0.05)
  - **Vasomotor dysfunction**: Significantly reduced (P<0.05)
  - Somatic complications: No significant difference (P>0.05)
  - Depression: No significant difference (P>0.05)
  - Sexual dysfunction: No significant difference (P>0.05)
- Conclusion: Phytoestrogenic medicine can alleviate menopausal symptoms

### Effective Symptoms
- Anxiety
- Vasomotor dysfunction (hot flashes, night sweats)

### Ineffective Symptoms
- Somatic complications
- Depression
- Sexual dysfunction

### Dosing
- Duration: 8 weeks minimum
- Dose: Not specified in this study; standard recommendations (30-40mg daily) likely apply

## Menstrual Bleeding Disorders

### Evidence Quality
Systematic review and meta-analysis [Mollazadeh et al. 2019].

### Search Results
- Initial searches: 8905 papers
- Studies included: 5

### Key Findings
- VAC did not have significant effect on amount of menstrual bleeding compared to placebo in first or second menstrual cycles
- When compared to mefenamic acid, VAC showed higher bleeding scores in first cycle but no significant difference in second cycle
- Reported side effects limited to nausea and abdominal pain
- Higher-quality clinical trials needed due to relatively low quality of existing evidence

### Clinical Implication
**Not recommended** for reducing menstrual bleeding volume based on current evidence.

## General Dosing Guidelines

### German Commission E Recommendations
- **Dose**: 30-40mg dried fruit extract daily
- **Standardization**: 0.6% casticin or agnuside
- **Alternative forms**:
  - 2.6-4.2mg dry native extract
  - 40 drops tincture
- **Timing**: Once in morning with liquid
- **Duration**: Several months consecutively
- **Note**: Considered long-term hormonal balancing herb rather than fast-acting remedy

### Extract Equivalencies
- **Ze 440**: 20mg extract = 120-240mg dry weight (6-12:1 ratio, 60% ethanol)
- **BNO 1095**: 4mg extract = 40mg dry weight (10:1 ratio, 70% ethanol/30% water)

## Research Quality Considerations

### Strengths
- Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses
- Several well-designed double-blind RCTs
- Dose-finding studies available
- Comparative studies with pharmaceutical treatments
- Long history of traditional use supporting safety

### Limitations
- High heterogeneity in some meta-analyses
- Risk of publication bias
- Many studies have high risk of bias
- Incomplete product characterization in many trials
- Lack of large-scale RCTs for hyperprolactinemia
- Quality variability in commercial products

### Future Research Needs
- Large, well-designed RCTs with proper product characterization
- Long-term safety studies
- Standardization and quality control improvement
- More research on specific conditions (PMDD, menopause, hyperprolactinemia)
