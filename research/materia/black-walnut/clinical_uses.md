# Black Walnut - Clinical Uses

## Critical Evidence Gap

**No human RCTs exist for any therapeutic indication of Juglans nigra (black walnut).**

This distinguishes black walnut from most other medicinal herbs with regulated status. The only official approval is a traditional use claim from Health Canada (2022) for an anthelmintic indication — no clinical trial evidence was required or provided.

All mechanistic and efficacy evidence is in vitro (cell-based or animal models only). Clinical applications are based exclusively on traditional use and extrapolation from in vitro phytochemistry.

---

## Approved Indication: Anthelmintic / Vermifuge

### Health Canada NHPID Traditional Use Claim (2022)
- **Approved claim**: "Used in Herbal Medicine (as a vermifuge/as an anthelmintic) to help expel intestinal worms"
- **Evidence basis**: Traditional use only; no clinical trial evidence required for traditional use designation
- **Approved preparation**: Unripe hull (pericarp) — dry, powder, or non-standardized ethanolic extract
- **Approved dose**: 0.65–2.75 g/day
- **Population**: Adults 18 years and older
- **Duration**: Occasional use; consult practitioner if use exceeds 14 days
- **Source**: Health Canada Natural Health Products Ingredients Database (NHPID) monograph, April 29, 2022

**Note**: This is the ONLY official regulatory monograph for Juglans nigra globally. No WHO, Commission E, EMA/HMPC, ESCOP, or USP monograph exists for this species.

## Allergen Characterization (Human Sera Studies)

These are not therapeutic trials; they characterize black walnut as a food allergen using human immune data.

### Zhang et al. 2017 (PMID 27936684)
- **Design**: Immunological characterization using sera from DBPCFC-confirmed walnut allergy patients
- **Participants**: 25 adults with double-blind, placebo-controlled food challenge (DBPCFC)-confirmed walnut allergy
- **Key finding**: Jug n 4 (11S globulin, legumin-type storage protein) bound IgE in **32% of 25 confirmed allergy patients**
- **Significance**: Establishes Jug n 4 as a clinically relevant black walnut allergen; relevant to safety screening
- **Limitations**: Small serum panel (n=25); immunological characterization only, no dose-response data

### Costa et al. 2014 (PMID 24382327)
- **Design**: Systematic narrative review of walnut allergen molecular characterization
- **Scope**: Both Juglans regia and Juglans nigra
- **Key findings**:
  - Walnut allergy prevalence: 1–2% of general population; one of the most common tree nut allergies
  - Jug r 1 (English walnut) can elicit reactions at threshold doses as low as 1–14 mg walnut protein in sensitized individuals
  - Jug n allergens (Jug n 1, 2, 4) are structurally homologous to J. regia equivalents — high cross-reactivity expected
- **Clinical implication**: Patients with Juglans regia allergy should be considered at risk for Juglans nigra reactions

## In Vitro Evidence (Preclinical Only)

The following in vitro findings represent the mechanistic basis for traditional therapeutic applications. None have been confirmed in human clinical trials.

### Antimicrobial / Antifungal (In Vitro)
- Juglone demonstrates broad-spectrum antimicrobial and antifungal activity in cell and culture-based assays
- Documented in multiple in vitro studies (reviewed: Dos Santos Moreira et al. 2021, PMID 34348624)
- **No clinical trials** on black walnut for bacterial or fungal infections in humans

### Antiparasitic (In Vitro)
- Juglone and tannins have demonstrated anthelmintic activity in vitro / animal models
- Forms the mechanistic basis for the traditional vermifuge claim
- **No human dose-finding or efficacy RCTs** for intestinal parasites

### Anticancer / Cytotoxic (In Vitro Only)
- **Karki et al. 2020 (PMID 32610056)**: Juglone kills MIA PaCa-2 (human pancreatic carcinoma) cells via ferroptosis (iron-dependent lipid peroxidation)
  - Juglone IC50 (combination with thymoquinone): 24.75 μM
  - Synergistic with thymoquinone at 95% effect level (combination index favoring moderate synergism)
  - **Preclinical only; no human data; no therapeutic implication**

### Anti-inflammatory (In Vitro)
- **Ho et al. 2019 (PMID 31607915)**: Kernel extracts from 'Surprise' and 'Sparrow' cultivars significantly inhibited production of all 6 detected proinflammatory cytokines/chemokines (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α, and others) in LPS-stimulated human U-937 promonocytic cells
  - Effect is **cultivar-dependent**: 'Daniel', 'Mystry', and 'Sparks' cultivars showed weak or no significant inhibition
  - Active compounds: specific phenolic/polyphenolic compounds varying by cultivar (identified via metabolomic profiling)
  - Cell model: PMA-differentiated U-937 human promonocytic cells — a legitimate human cell line (not primary cells)
  - **Preclinical only; no human trial data**

### Pin1 Inhibition (In Vitro)
- Juglone is a well-characterized covalent inhibitor of Pin1 (peptidyl-prolyl isomerase) with IC50 in low micromolar range
- Pin1 is implicated in cancer and Alzheimer's disease; juglone inhibition is mechanistically interesting
- **No clinical translation; no human trials**

## Summary: Clinical Evidence Assessment

| Indication | Human Evidence | Quality | Approval Status |
|------------|---------------|---------|-----------------|
| Anthelmintic / vermifuge | None (traditional use only) | N/A | Health Canada traditional use claim (2022) |
| Antimicrobial / antifungal | None | N/A | No regulatory approval |
| Antiparasitic | None | N/A | No regulatory approval |
| Anti-inflammatory | None (human cell line in vitro only) | N/A | No regulatory approval |
| Cancer | None | N/A | No regulatory approval |
| Cardiovascular | None | N/A | No regulatory approval |

**Bottom line**: Black walnut hull should not be used as a first-line treatment for any indication where clinically validated alternatives exist. Its use is supported only by traditional practice and in vitro pharmacology. The anthelmintic use supported by Health Canada is plausible mechanistically but unvalidated in human trials.

---

## Research Gaps

1. No human dose-finding trials for anthelmintic / antiparasitic indication
2. No human clinical data for antimicrobial or antifungal applications despite substantial in vitro evidence
3. No head-to-head comparison with approved anthelmintics
4. No human pharmacokinetic (PK) data; plasma juglone concentrations after oral dosing unknown
5. No long-term safety data in humans; carcinogenicity risk from juglone not quantified at therapeutic exposures
