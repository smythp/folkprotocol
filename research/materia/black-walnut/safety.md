# Black Walnut - Safety

## Overview

Black walnut hull has a significant safety profile that distinguishes it from most other herbal medicines. The key concern is **juglone mutagenicity** (Ames-positive with metabolic activation), which places black walnut in a category requiring cautious short-term use with practitioner oversight.

---

## Critical Safety Finding: Juglone Mutagenicity

**Source**: Erisen et al. 2020 (PMID 32597139) — Archives of Industrial Hygiene and Toxicology

**Finding**: Free juglone is **Ames test positive** (mutagenic) in the standard bacterial reverse mutation assay (Salmonella typhimurium TA98) **with S9 metabolic activation** at concentrations of **1.25–2.5 μmol/L**.

### Key Details
- Test strain: S. typhimurium TA98 (frameshift mutagen detector)
- Metabolic activation required: Yes (S9 fraction necessary — suggests bioactivation by CYP450 enzymes to mutagenic metabolites)
- Concentration range positive: 1.25–2.5 μmol/L with S9
- Without S9: not mutagenic in this study (activation-dependent mutagenicity)
- IC50 in mouse fibroblasts (L929): **60 μmol/L at 48h** (cytotoxic threshold)
- Nano-encapsulated juglone: **no mutagenicity** in Ames test at equivalent concentrations

### Clinical Significance
- S9-dependent Ames positivity indicates bioactivation by hepatic CYP450 enzymes to mutagenic intermediates
- This creates theoretical genotoxic and carcinogenic risk with long-term internal use
- Human exposure from typical hull preparations is not quantified (no PK data); the relationship between Ames-positive concentrations in vitro and achievable human plasma concentrations is unknown
- **All regulatory and traditional sources consensus: maximum 14 days use without practitioner oversight**
- The nano-encapsulation finding (eliminating mutagenicity) is mechanistically interesting but not used in any commercial preparation

## Contraindications

### Absolute Contraindications (Cross-Source Consensus)
1. **Pregnancy** — contraindicated across all regulatory and traditional sources; specific teratogenicity/embryotoxicity data absent but precautionary principle applies given juglone mutagenicity and tannin load
2. **Lactation** — contraindicated; potential excretion of juglone or tannin metabolites in breast milk
3. **Confirmed walnut allergy** — Jug n allergens (Jug n 1, 2, 4) cross-react with J. regia allergens; IgE-mediated reactions possible

### Relative Contraindications / Cautions
4. **Chronic GI inflammatory conditions** (Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis, IBS) — high tannin content and potential juglone GI irritation may worsen symptoms
5. **Oral medication regimens** — tannin-drug binding significantly reduces absorption of many oral drugs; separation by ≥1 hour required (see Drug Interactions)
6. **Pre-existing kidney or liver disease** — high tannin intake associated with impaired renal/hepatic function; theoretical risk of hepatotoxic juglone accumulation
7. **Long-term use without practitioner oversight** — mutagenicity risk; all sources specify maximum 14 days for self-directed use

## Known Adverse Reactions

### Gastrointestinal Effects
- **Nausea, vomiting**: Dose-dependent; more common at higher doses
- **Watery diarrhea / cathartic effect**: Juglone irritant + tannin cathartic action; can be significant at higher doses
- **GI cramping**: Related to high tannin astringency and juglone irritation of GI mucosa
- **Mechanism**: Juglone causes direct mucosal irritation; tannins alter protein and enzyme activity in GI tract

### Contact Dermatitis / Skin Reactions
- **Well-documented** occupational contact dermatitis from fresh hull handling
- Fresh hull juice stains skin brown/black (juglone oxidation); irritation and sensitization in susceptible individuals
- **Internal use**: Topical allergic contact dermatitis is a separate concern from internal use reactions
- Persons who develop contact dermatitis from hull handling should use preparations cautiously internally

### Cardiovascular Effects (High Dose)
- Large doses may cause cardiovascular depression / sedation of cardiac function
- Documented in pharmacological review contexts at doses well above traditional use levels
- Not a concern at Health Canada approved dosing (0.65–2.75 g/day)

### Staining
- Not a safety concern but a practical one: juglone in hull preparations stains teeth, mucous membranes, and GI tract brown — darkening of urine and stools possible

## Drug Interactions

### Tannin-Mediated Absorption Reduction (Clinically Significant)
**Mechanism**: High tannin content (~45% in hull/bark preparations) binds oral medications via protein complexation, reducing their bioavailability.

**Affected drug classes** (general tannin-drug interaction class — not black walnut-specific human data):
- Iron supplements (tannins reduce non-heme iron absorption significantly)
- Antibiotics (tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones, ciprofloxacin) — chelation and tannin binding
- Cardiac glycosides (digoxin) — reduced absorption reported with tannin-rich preparations
- Antipsychotics, antidepressants — potential for reduced bioavailability
- Antacids and alkaline medications — tannin precipitation possible

**Recommendation**: Separate black walnut hull preparations by **≥1 hour** (preferably 2 hours) from all oral medications.

### Renal Accumulation (Animal Data)
- **Chen et al. 2006 (PMID 16393859)**: In rats, juglone preferentially accumulates in kidney via **covalent binding to renal cytosolic proteins** — the proposed mechanism for nephrotoxicity at high doses
- Dermal absorption measured at <20% (relevant: occupational/topical handling exposure is lower than oral)
- **No human renal toxicity cases documented**; animal finding warrants caution in patients with pre-existing renal impairment
- Clinical significance at Health Canada approved doses (0.65–2.75 g/day) is unknown — no human PK data to translate animal findings to human exposure levels

### Potential CYP Interactions
- Juglone undergoes metabolic activation by CYP450 enzymes (implied by S9-dependent Ames test positivity)
- Theoretical CYP450 inhibition or induction is possible at sufficient juglone exposure
- No human PK data; specific CYP interaction studies not published for black walnut preparations
- **Clinical significance unknown** but warrants caution with narrow therapeutic index drugs

### Anticoagulant / Antiplatelet Caution
- High-flavonoid preparations may theoretically interact with anticoagulants
- Not specifically documented for black walnut; precautionary note applies given flavonoid content
- Vitamin K interactions (possible with high tannin/polyphenol intake affecting gut flora)

## Hepatotoxicity Assessment

| Parameter | Status |
|-----------|--------|
| DILI (drug-induced liver injury) cases | None reported in literature |
| Hepatotoxicity risk | Theoretical; not documented in humans |
| High-dose animal hepatotoxicity | Not identified in bibliography |
| Tannin-mediated hepatotoxicity | Theoretical with excessive prolonged intake |

Despite juglone's mutagenicity in vitro, no hepatotoxicity case reports were identified. This may reflect the short-term use convention, low bioavailability of juglone from whole-hull preparations, or underreporting.

## Use Duration Consensus

| Source | Maximum Duration Without Practitioner Oversight |
|--------|------------------------------------------------|
| Health Canada NHPID (2022) | 14 days |
| Naturopathic herbalism tradition | 2 weeks on / 2 weeks off cycling |
| SCU monograph | 14 days to 6 weeks maximum |
| General safety consensus | 14 days self-directed; longer with qualified practitioner |

**All sources agree**: Black walnut hull is not appropriate for continuous long-term use without professional oversight. The juglone mutagenicity finding provides a clear scientific rationale for this consensus.

---

## Pregnancy and Lactation Summary

- **Pregnancy**: CONTRAINDICATED — all traditional and regulatory sources agree; no exception cited
  - Juglone mutagenicity creates theoretical teratogenic risk
  - Tannin load and potential uterine stimulation (traditional abortifacient use in high doses in some sources)
  - Health Canada explicitly states: "Consult before use if pregnant or breastfeeding"
- **Lactation**: CONTRAINDICATED — precautionary; potential transfer of bioactive compounds to breast milk
- **Evidence basis for contraindication**: Precautionary / traditional; no human teratogenicity or reproductive toxicity studies have been conducted

---

## Allergy and Cross-Reactivity

- Black walnut (Jug n allergens) cross-reacts with English walnut (Jug r allergens) and other tree nuts with homologous 2S albumin, 7S vicilin, and 11S legumin allergens
- Persons with tree nut allergy (especially Juglans regia) should avoid black walnut preparations
- **Jug n 4** (11S globulin): IgE-binding in 32% of DBPCFC-confirmed walnut allergy patients (Zhang 2017, n=25)
- Walnut allergy prevalence: 1–2% of general population (Costa 2014)
- Both internal use and topical (contact) routes can elicit IgE-mediated reactions in sensitized individuals
