# Black Walnut - Traditional Uses

## Overview

Black walnut (Juglans nigra L.) has a primary home in North American folk medicine, with particular depth in Indigenous North American traditions and early American botanical medicine (19th–early 20th century Eclectic physicians). It lacks the multi-tradition depth of herbs with Ayurvedic, TCM, and European histories simultaneously — its traditional record is strongest in one region.

---

## North American Indigenous Traditions

**Era**: Pre-contact through 19th century (ongoing in some communities)

### Anthelmintic / Parasite Expulsion
- Unripe green hull preparations used traditionally to expel intestinal worms (roundworms, tapeworms, pinworms)
- The "wormy" season in autumn (when hulls ripen) coincided with traditional harvest for this purpose
- **Preparation**: Fresh or dried green hull, typically as a tea or infusion
- **This use underlies the only approved regulatory claim (Health Canada NHPID 2022)**

### Skin Conditions
- Hull juice applied topically to ringworm (tinea), eczema, and skin parasites (scabies)
- Contact with fresh hull causes brown/black staining of skin — a property associated with "drawing out" disease in folk theory
- **Preparation**: Fresh hull juice or poultice of green hull

### Antiseptic / Wound Treatment
- Astringent properties of high-tannin hull preparations used for wound cleansing
- Bark decoctions applied to mouth sores, gum disease, and sore throats
- **Preparation**: Bark decoction (gargle/rinse)

### Laxative / Digestive
- Hull preparations used as purgatives for constipation and intestinal cleansing
- Action attributed to high tannin content (cathartic at higher doses) and juglone irritant effect
- **Preparation**: Hull tea or decoction

## Eclectic Physicians (United States, 19th–Early 20th Century)

**Era**: ~1820s–1930s

The Eclectic school of medicine, prominent in 19th-century America, incorporated black walnut from Indigenous practice into systematic botanical medicine.

### Uses Documented by Eclectics
- **Anthelmintic**: Primary use; hull preparations prescribed for intestinal worms (lumbricoides, oxyuris)
- **Skin diseases**: Topical application for herpetic eruptions, eczema, impetigo, favus, ringworm
- **Syphilis / alterative**: Hull tinctures used as an "alterative" (blood purifier) in syphilis — a characteristic Eclectic application reflecting pre-antibiotic practice
- **Scrofula**: Used for enlarged lymph nodes and chronic skin ulcers

### Eclectic Preparation Preferences
- Green (unripe) hull extract in dilute alcohol
- Bark decoctions for external application
- Often combined with other "alteratives" (yellow dock, sarsaparilla) for chronic skin conditions

## North American Folk Herbalism (20th Century)

**Era**: 1900s–present

Folk herbalism in the United States and Canada continued and expanded Eclectic applications, particularly:

### Candida / Yeast Overgrowth
- Black walnut hull tincture widely used in folk tradition for Candida albicans overgrowth and "systemic yeast"
- Mechanistic basis: juglone's documented antifungal activity in vitro
- **Preparation**: Green hull tincture (1:4 at 25% ethanol), typically 5–10 drops 3x daily
- **Duration**: 2 weeks on / 2 weeks off cycling
- **Note**: No human clinical evidence; traditional use not supported by RCTs

### Parasite Cleansing Protocols
- Black walnut often combined with wormwood (Artemisia absinthium) and cloves in traditional "parasite cleanse" protocols (popularized by Hulda Clark in the 1990s)
- No clinical evidence for these combination protocols
- **Preparation**: Liquid extract 1:2 (green hull), 1.5–5.5 mL/day

### Skin Fungal Infections
- Topical tincture for athlete's foot (tinea pedis), nail fungus, jock itch
- Mechanistic basis: juglone's antifungal properties in vitro
- **Preparation**: Undiluted tincture applied topically

## British / European Herbal Tradition

**Extent**: Limited; Juglans regia (English/Persian walnut) predominates in European traditions.

- British herbalism adopted black walnut secondarily through American imports in the 20th century
- No classical European monograph for J. nigra (Commission E, ESCOP, and EMA all evaluated J. regia leaf only)
- Uses in British herbal practice largely mirror North American folk applications: anthelmintic, skin conditions, and as an alterative

## Traditional Preparation Methods

| Preparation | Material | Method | Traditional Dose |
|-------------|----------|--------|-----------------|
| Green hull tincture | Fresh/unripe hull | Macerated in 25% ethanol (1:4 ratio) | 5–10 drops, 3× daily |
| Liquid extract 1:2 | Unripe hull | 1:2 with water/ethanol | 1.5–5.5 mL/day |
| Fresh plant liquid extract 1:1 | Green hull | 1:1 extraction (fresh plant) | 1–10 drops, 1–3× daily in water |
| Hull tea / decoction | Dried hull | 30 g in 500 mL water, decoct 15–20 min | 200 mL 3× daily |
| Bark decoction | Inner bark | 30 g in 500 mL water | 200 mL 3× daily (topical or gargle) |
| Topical poultice | Fresh hull | Crushed fresh hull applied directly | As needed |

## Key Traditional-to-Regulatory Alignment

The only regulatory monograph (Health Canada 2022) directly validates the anthelmintic/vermifuge use — the most consistently documented traditional application across Indigenous, Eclectic, and folk traditions. Other traditional uses (Candida, skin fungi, alterative) remain outside any regulatory framework and lack clinical validation.

---

## Note on Species Distinctiveness

Traditional applications documented for "black walnut" in historical texts must be verified to refer specifically to Juglans nigra rather than Juglans regia (English/Persian walnut). European historical texts often refer to Juglans regia. North American texts from the Eclectic period and later generally specify J. nigra, the native American species.

Juglone content is the key distinguishing factor: J. nigra hull contains significantly higher juglone concentrations than J. regia, making traditional preparations non-interchangeable for pharmacologically-driven effects.
