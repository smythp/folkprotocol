# Black Walnut - Active Compounds

## Primary Part Used

**Unripe green hull (pericarp)** is the therapeutically relevant preparation. The nut meat, inner shell, leaves, and bark have distinct phytochemical profiles and are not interchangeable with hull preparations.

---

## Principal Bioactive: Juglone

**Chemical name**: 5-hydroxy-1,4-naphthoquinone
**CAS**: 481-39-0
**MW**: 174.15 g/mol

### Biological Activities (In Vitro)
- **Antimicrobial**: Active against gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria; mechanisms include membrane disruption and redox cycling
- **Antifungal**: Active against Candida species and dermatophytes in vitro; documented in multiple studies
- **Anthelmintic**: Proposed mechanism for traditional vermifuge use; oxidative stress on parasite biology
- **Antiproliferative / cytotoxic**: IC50 in mouse fibroblasts (L929): **60 μmol/L at 48h** (Erisen 2020, PMID 32597139)
  - Kills pancreatic cancer cells via **ferroptosis** (iron-dependent lipid peroxidation) (Karki 2020, PMID 32610056)
  - Antiproliferative activity against three human tumor cell lines comparable to clinical reference standards (Osztie 2024, PMID 39000038)
- **Pin1 inhibition**: Covalent cysteine-targeting inhibitor of Pin1 (peptidyl-prolyl isomerase); IC50 in low micromolar range. Pin1 is implicated in cancer cell proliferation and Alzheimer's pathology
- **ROS generation**: Dual behavior — antioxidant at low doses, pro-oxidant at high doses via redox cycling of the naphthoquinone moiety
- **Sedative / antihypertensive**: Reported in pharmacological review at doses above normal therapeutic range (Dos Santos Moreira et al. 2021, PMID 34348624)

### Bioavailability
- **PAMPA data (Osztie 2024, PMID 39000038)**: Juglone shows **positive GI epithelial membrane permeability** and **BBB penetration potential** via passive diffusion in vitro
  - Log Pe values for juglone indicate capacity to cross both GI epithelial and blood-brain-barrier membranes
  - This is the first published PAMPA bioavailability data for Juglans nigra compounds
  - **Limitation**: PAMPA is an artificial membrane model; human in vivo confirmation absent
- **EMA data (J. regia context)**: Juglone degrades in acidic gastric environments in aqueous preparations, potentially limiting systemic bioavailability from tea/infusion forms
- **Tannin complexation**: High tannin content in whole hull preparations may bind juglone and reduce its absorption from GI tract
- **No human pharmacokinetic data**: Plasma Cmax, Tmax, or half-life of juglone after oral black walnut hull dosing has never been measured

### Animal In Vivo Pharmacokinetics (No Human Data Available)

**Chen et al. 2006 (PMID 16393859)** — Male F344 rats, 14C-radiolabelled juglone, oral/IV/dermal routes:
- **Oral bioavailability**: ~40–50% of oral dose absorbed within 24 hours
- **Dermal absorption**: <20% within 24 hours (relevant to topical exposure)
- **Tissue distribution**: Highest juglone-derived radioactivity in **kidney** — attributed to covalent binding to renal cytosolic proteins; potential mechanism for nephrotoxicity at high doses
- **5 urinary metabolites identified**: 1,4,5-trihydroxynaphthalene di-glucuronide; 1,4,5-trihydroxynaphthalene mono-glucuronide mono-sulfate; 2-sulfo-2,3-dihydrojuglone; 4,8-dihydroxy-1-tetralone mono-glucuronide; 1,4,5-trihydroxynaphthalene mono-glucuronide
- **Primary metabolic pathways**: Glucuronidation and reduction (liver microsomal)
- *Only available in vivo PK dataset for juglone; rat-to-human extrapolation requires caution*

**Aithal et al. 2011 (PMID 21523783)** — Free vs. PEGylated liposomal juglone in mouse/rat:
- **Free juglone plasma half-life**: ~**2 hours** (short; rapid clearance)
- **Liposomal juglone**: **12-fold increase** in plasma half-life vs. free juglone; significantly improved biodistribution and reduced acute toxicity
- *Commercial black walnut hull preparations contain free juglone — systemic exposure limited by short t½ and gastric acid degradation*

### Juglone in Commercial Preparations
- Commercial black walnut hull products are **not standardized to juglone content**
- Reasons: juglone instability during processing, oxidation of the naphthoquinone moiety, and toxicity concerns limiting standardization targets
- Health Canada NHPID explicitly permits only "non-standardized" ethanolic extracts

## Secondary Bioactives: Ellagitannins and Gallotannins

**Typical hull/bark tannin content**: ~45% (high tannin matrix)

### Key Tannin Classes
- **Ellagitannins**: Pedunculagin, casuarictin, strictinin, and related compounds; hydrolyze to release ellagic acid
- **Gallotannins**: Hydrolyze to release gallic acid
- **Ellagic acid**: Polyphenol released from ellagitannins; bioavailable as urolithins via gut microbiome conversion

### Biological Activities of Tannins
- **Anthelmintic**: Tannin-protein complexation in parasite GI mucosa may contribute to vermifuge effect
- **Astringent**: Protein precipitation; basis for traditional use in wound care, gum disease, skin conditions
- **Antimicrobial**: Broad activity via protein and membrane binding
- **Drug binding**: Tannins bind oral medications and reduce their absorption — major drug interaction concern (see Safety)

### Bioavailability of Tannins
- Intact ellagitannins have poor oral bioavailability; ellagic acid is released by hydrolysis
- Urolithins (gut microbiome metabolites of ellagic acid) vary by individual microbiome composition
- No human PK data for black walnut tannin metabolites specifically

## Flavonoids and Phenolics

Identified by UHPLC-HR-MS/MS analysis (Osztie 2024, PMID 39000038) — 161 compounds tentatively identified across bark, leaf, and pericarp extracts.

### Major Compound Classes Identified
- **Quercetin and derivatives**: Quercetin-3-glucoside, quercetin-3-rutinoside, and related flavonols
- **Gallic acid compounds**: Methyl gallate, ethyl gallate (ethyl gallate also showed positive PAMPA BBB permeability in Osztie 2024)
- **Myricetin derivatives**
- **Caffeic acid derivatives**
- **Diarylheptanoids** (also noted in ESCOP J. regia monograph; present in J. nigra by compositional similarity)

### Flavonoid Bioactivity
- Anti-inflammatory, antioxidant activities documented for quercetin class (general; not black walnut-specific trials)
- Contribute to overall polyphenol antioxidant capacity of hull preparations

## Essential Oil Constituents (Leaf, Not Hull)

From Paudel et al. 2013 (PMID 24354208) — GC-MS of J. nigra leaf essential oil:
- Major volatile: **(E)-caryophyllene (17.3–20.4%)**
- Germacrene D (~7%)
- Other sesquiterpenes (beta-elemene, alpha-humulene)
- **Important note**: Juglone is **non-volatile** and NOT present in the leaf essential oil fraction
- Leaf essential oil data are NOT applicable to hull or bark preparations used therapeutically

## Standardization Status

**No pharmacopoeial standardization exists for black walnut hull preparations.**

- Primary proposed markers: juglone content, ellagic acid, total tannin content
- No validated analytical method for juglone in commercial products established in pharmacopoeia
- Juglone instability during drying, extraction, and storage complicates standardization
- Health Canada explicitly permits only "non-standardized" ethanolic extracts in its NHPID approval

---

## Research on Derivatives

Juglone is a **privileged scaffold** in medicinal chemistry — its naphthoquinone core allows diverse chemical modification. Multiple juglone derivatives have been synthesized with:
- Enhanced potency against specific targets
- Reduced cytotoxicity vs. free juglone
- Targeted antimicrobial or anticancer activity

(Reviewed: Dos Santos Moreira et al. 2021, PMID 34348624)

None of these derivatives are present in commercial preparations or in clinical trials. They represent future drug discovery directions, not current therapeutic compounds.
