# Ginger (Zingiber officinale) - Clinical Uses

## Evidence-Based Clinical Applications

### 1. Chemotherapy-Induced Nausea and Vomiting (CINV)

**Evidence Quality:** Strong (35 RCTs analyzed)

**Dosing Protocols:**
- 500-2000 mg/day ginger capsules or extract
- Timing: Initiate before chemotherapy with standard antiemetics
- Duration: Throughout chemotherapy cycle

**Clinical Outcomes:**

*With standard antiemetics (22 trials):*
- Grade 3 acute nausea: 81% reduction (RR 0.19, p < 0.001)
- High-grade vomiting: 53% reduction (RR 0.47, p = 0.01)

*Ginger-partitioned moxibustion (13 trials):*
- Mild vomiting: 44% reduction (RR 0.56, p = 0.001)
- Severe vomiting: 61% reduction (RR 0.39, p < 0.00001)

**Safety:** Only 2.8% of patients experienced dizziness

**Source:** Lin et al., Cancer Nursing 2025 (PMID: 38625733)

---

### 2. Body Weight and Composition Management

**Evidence Quality:** Moderate (27 RCTs, n=1309)

**Optimal Protocol:**
- Dose: 2 g/day ginger powder
- Duration: >8 weeks
- Dose-response: Nonlinear relationship

**Clinical Outcomes:**
- Body weight: -1.52 kg reduction
- BMI: -0.58 kg/m² reduction
- Waist circumference: -1.04 cm reduction
- Body fat percentage: -0.87% reduction

**Source:** Rafieipour et al., Nutrition Reviews 2024 (PMID: 38261398)

---

### 3. Inflammatory Markers Reduction

**Evidence Quality:** Good (16 RCTs, n=1010)

**Dosing:** Variable across studies (examined multiple protocols)

**Clinical Outcomes:**

*C-Reactive Protein (CRP):*
- SMD: -5.11 (95% CI: -7.91 to -2.30)
- Interpretation: Significant reduction

*High-Sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP):*
- SMD: -0.88 (95% CI: -1.63 to -0.12)
- Interpretation: Substantial decrease

*TNF-α (Tumor Necrosis Factor-alpha):*
- SMD: -0.85 (95% CI: -1.48 to -0.21)
- Interpretation: Notable reduction

*IL-6 (Interleukin-6):*
- SMD: -0.45 (95% CI: -1.29 to 0.38)
- Interpretation: No significant impact

**Additional Findings (25 studies analyzed):**
- Total antioxidant capacity (TAC): Increased
- Malondialdehyde (MDA): Reduced
- Prostaglandin E2 (PGE2): Marginally significant reduction

**Sources:**
- Morvaridzadeh et al., Cytokine 2020 (PMID: 32763761)
- Jalali et al., Phytotherapy Research 2020 (PMID: 32147845)

---

### 4. Primary Dysmenorrhea (Menstrual Pain)

**Evidence Quality:** Strong (7 studies, 4 RCTs analyzed)

**Dosing Protocols:**
- Dose: 750-2000 mg ginger powder/day
- Frequency: Three times daily (typically 500-1000 mg per dose)
- Timing:
  - Protocol 1: Starting 2 days before menstruation through day 3 (5 days total)
  - Protocol 2: First 3 days of menstruation only

**Clinical Outcomes:**

*Pain Severity Reduction:*
- Risk Ratio: -1.85 (95% CI: -2.87 to -0.84, p = 0.0003)
- Protocol 1: Significant difference (p = 0.015)
- Protocol 2: Significant difference (p = 0.029)

*Pain Duration Reduction:*
- Protocol 1: Significant difference (p = 0.017)

**Specific RCT Data (n=120):**
- Placebo group: 46 participants
- Ginger group: 56 participants
- Dose tested: 500 mg capsules, three times daily
- Quality: Low to moderate risk of bias in 6 of 7 RCTs

**Sources:**
- Daily et al., Pain Medicine 2015 (PMID: 26177393)
- Rahnama et al., BMC Complementary Medicine 2012 (PMID: 22781186)

---

### 5. Blood Pressure Reduction

**Evidence Quality:** Moderate (6 RCTs, n=345)

**Optimal Protocol:**
- Dose: ≥3 g/day
- Duration: ≤8 weeks
- Patient profile: Age ≤50 years

**Clinical Outcomes:**
- Systolic BP: -6.36 mmHg (95% CI: -11.27 to -1.46)
- Diastolic BP: -2.12 mmHg (95% CI: -3.92 to -0.31)

**Source:** Hasani et al., Phytotherapy Research 2019 (PMID: 30972845)

---

### 6. Osteoarthritis

**Evidence Quality:** Moderate (5 trials, n=593 meta-analysis; additional RCTs)

**Dosing Protocols:**
- Range: 480-2000 mg/day
- Forms: Steamed ginger extract or ginger powder
- Duration: 12 weeks

**Clinical Outcomes:**

*Meta-Analysis Results:*
- Pain reduction: SMD -0.30 (95% CI: -0.50 to -0.09, p = 0.005)
- Disability reduction: SMD -0.22 (95% CI: -0.39 to -0.04, p = 0.01)
- Both outcomes: Statistically significant

*Specific RCT (n=100, 12 weeks):*
- Dose: 480 mg/day steamed ginger extract (GGE03) in 1.6 g/day total product
- Outcomes: Pain VAS, K-WOMAC (total and sub-scores), and PGA all decreased significantly vs placebo
- Safety: No significant changes in safety endpoints

**Important Safety Note:**
Discontinuation rate was higher with ginger than placebo (RR 2.33, 95% CI: 1.04-5.22, p=0.04). Patients given ginger were more than twice as likely to discontinue treatment compared to placebo.

**Conclusion:** Modestly efficacious and reasonably safe, with moderate quality evidence

**Sources:**
- Bartels et al., Osteoarthritis & Cartilage 2015 (PMID: 25300574)
- Baek et al., Food & Function 2024 (PMID: 39212491)

### 7. Migraine Treatment

**Evidence Quality:** Moderate (3 RCTs analyzed for meta-analysis; individual trials)

**Dosing Protocols:**

*Acute Treatment:*
- Dose: 400 mg ginger extract (5% active ingredient) + standard NSAID
- Timing: At onset of migraine attack
- Comparison: Ginger powder comparable to sumatriptan

*Prophylaxis (Limited Efficacy):*
- Dose: 200 mg dry extract (5% active ingredient), three times daily
- Duration: 3 months
- Note: Limited efficacy vs placebo

**Clinical Outcomes (Acute Treatment):**

*Pain-Free at 2 Hours:*
- RR: 1.79 (95% CI: 1.04-3.09, p = 0.04)
- Interpretation: Substantially improved

*Pain Score Reduction at 2 Hours:*
- MD: -1.27 (95% CI: -1.46 to -1.07, p < 0.00001)

*Specific Trial Data (n=60):*
- 400 mg ginger extract + 100 mg ketoprofen vs placebo + 100 mg ketoprofen
- Significant improvement at 1 hour (p = 0.04), 1.5 hours (p = 0.01), and 2 hours (p = 0.04)
- Better clinical response, pain reduction, and functional status at all time points

*Comparative Efficacy (n=100):*
- Ginger powder vs sumatriptan for acute migraine without aura
- Efficacy: Comparable effectiveness at 2 hours
- Adverse effects: Ginger had fewer adverse effects than sumatriptan
- Patient satisfaction: No difference between groups

**Additional Benefits:** Obviously lower incidence of nausea/vomiting in ginger group

**Safety:** No significant differences in total adverse events

**Sources:**
- Chen & Cai, American Journal of Emergency Medicine 2021 (PMID: 33293189)
- Martins et al., Cephalalgia 2019 (PMID: 29768938)
- Maghbooli et al., Phytotherapy Research 2014 (PMID: 23657930)

### 8. Pregnancy-Induced Nausea and Vomiting

**Evidence Quality:** Good (4 trials analyzed)

**Dosing:**
- Range: 750-2000 mg/day ginger powder
- Form: Divided doses
- Timing: As needed during pregnancy

**Clinical Outcomes:**
- Significantly more effective than placebo in reducing:
  - Frequency of vomiting
  - Intensity of nausea

**Safety Profile:**
- Adverse events: Generally mild and infrequent
- Considered safe and effective for pregnancy-related nausea and vomiting

**Important Considerations:**
- Maximum safe dosage not fully established
- Appropriate duration of treatment uncertain
- Consequences of over-dosage unknown
- Potential drug-herb interactions require further study

**Source:** Ding et al., Women Birth 2013 (PMID: 22951628)

## Summary of Evidence Quality

### Strong Evidence (Multiple High-Quality Studies)
1. Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting
2. Primary dysmenorrhea
3. Inflammatory marker reduction
4. Pregnancy-induced nausea

### Moderate Evidence (Multiple Studies, Some Limitations)
1. Body weight and composition management
2. Blood pressure reduction
3. Osteoarthritis
4. Migraine acute treatment

### Limited Evidence
1. Migraine prophylaxis
2. Long-term safety in pregnancy

---

## Overall Systematic Review Findings

**Comprehensive Review (109 RCTs analyzed, 2020):**
- Strong evidence for: Nausea/vomiting in pregnancy, inflammation, metabolic syndromes, digestive function, colorectal cancer markers
- Quality assessment: Only 39.4% of trials met high quality criteria
- Main limitations: Small sample sizes, unstandardized evaluation systems

**Source:** Anh et al., Nutrients 2020 (PMID: 31935866)

---

## Clinical Recommendations Summary

**Best Evidence For:**
1. CINV (with standard antiemetics): 500-2000 mg/day
2. Dysmenorrhea: 750-2000 mg/day during menstruation
3. Inflammatory conditions: Variable dosing, examine specific protocols

**Moderate Evidence For:**
1. Weight management: 2 g/day for >8 weeks
2. Blood pressure: ≥3 g/day for ≤8 weeks (age ≤50)
3. Osteoarthritis: 480-2000 mg/day (note higher discontinuation rates)
4. Migraine acute treatment: 400 mg + standard treatment

**Safety Considerations:**
- Generally well-tolerated
- Most common adverse effects: GI symptoms (bloating, heartburn, diarrhea)
- Higher discontinuation rates in osteoarthritis trials
- Pregnancy: Generally safe but maximum dosage not established
