# Maca (Lepidium meyenii) - Safety Profile

## Overall Safety Assessment

### Authoritative Reviews

#### USP (United States Pharmacopeia) Safety Review 2009
**Document**: DSC USP Safety Review of Maca
**Products Reviewed**:
- Maca root
- Maca root powder
- Maca root dry extract

**Findings**:
- ✅ **No serious adverse events** found for single-ingredient maca products
- Quality monographs in development for USP-NF
- Specifications for identity, composition, purity, and contaminant limits being established

#### LiverTox Database (NIH/NCBI)
**Database**: National Institutes of Health LiverTox
**Hepatotoxicity Assessment**:
- ✅ **Maca has NOT been implicated in causing liver injury**
- ✅ **No reports of transient serum enzyme elevations**
- ✅ **No clinically apparent liver injury** attributed to maca

**Clinical Significance**: **Excellent hepatic safety profile**

#### Comprehensive Review (Frontiers in Pharmacology 2024)
**Authors**: Ulloa del Carpio et al.

**Overall Assessment**:
- ✅ **Good safety profile**
- ✅ **No fatal adverse effects reported**
- ✅ **Scientific research found consumption very safe**
- ✅ **Most adverse effects mild and transient**

## Common Mild Adverse Effects

### Gastrointestinal Effects
**Frequency**: Most common category of adverse effects

**Reported Effects**:
- **Gastrointestinal upset** with 3 g/day for 12 weeks
- **Mild GI disturbances** with ~5 g/day gelatinized maca for 30 days

**Severity**: Mild
**Duration**: Transient (resolves during or after treatment)

---

### Neurological Effects
**Reported Effects**:
- **Headache** (transient, mild)
- **Mild headache and flushing** in some unpublished studies
- **Irritability** (transient)

**Severity**: Mild
**Duration**: Transient

---

## Rare or Isolated Adverse Events

### Case Reports

#### 1. Vaginal Bleeding
- **Population**: One 24-year-old woman
- **Status**: Case report (single isolated case)
- **Causality**: Unclear; temporal association only
- **Clinical Significance**: Extremely rare; no pattern across studies

#### 2. Manic Episode
- **Population**: One 27-year-old man with no psychiatric history
- **Status**: Case report (single isolated case)
- **Causality**: Unclear; could be coincidental
- **Clinical Significance**: Extremely rare; no replication

#### 3. Liver and Blood Pressure Changes
- **Dose**: 0.6 g/kg/day for 90 days (very high dose in animal study context)
- **Effects**:
  - Moderate increase in liver transaminase (AST)
  - Moderate increase in diastolic blood pressure
- **Context**: Likely animal study; dose far exceeds typical human use
- **Clinical Relevance**: Not observed in human trials at standard doses

---

## Doses with No Adverse Effects

### Well-Tolerated Dosing Ranges

**Studies Reporting No Adverse Effects**:
- **3.3 g/day maca glucosinolates**: No adverse effects or toxicity reported
- **2.0 g/day for 4 months** (Meissner 2006): Well tolerated
- **2.4 g/day for 12 weeks** (Zenico 2009): No significant adverse effects
- **3.0 g/day for 12 weeks** (Shin 2023): Well tolerated, no significant changes in laboratory tests or vital signs

**Conclusion**: **2.0-3.5 g/day well tolerated** in clinical trials up to 12 weeks

---

## Preclinical Toxicity

### Acute Toxicity (Animal Studies)
- **No acute toxicity** in animals at ≤17 g dried hypocotyls/kg body weight
- **Significance**: Very high safety margin; human equivalent dose far exceeds therapeutic range

## Contraindications

### Formal Contraindications
**Status**: **No formal contraindications established** in reviewed literature

### Theoretical Concerns (Not Formally Contraindicated)

#### 1. Hormone-Sensitive Conditions
**Rationale**:
- Meissner 2006b showed hormone axis modulation in postmenopausal women
  - Increased estradiol (P<0.05)
  - Suppressed FSH, LH
- **However**: Brooks 2008 showed NO hormone changes in similar population

**Evidence Quality**: **Conflicting**
- Some studies show hormone changes (Meissner 2006b)
- Other studies show NO hormone changes (Gonzales 2002, Brooks 2008)

**Clinical Approach**:
- **Caution** in hormone-sensitive conditions (breast cancer, endometrial cancer, ovarian cancer) until more data available
- **Not a formal contraindication** based on available evidence
- Effects appear variable/context-dependent

#### 2. Thyroid Conditions
**Rationale**:
- Glucosinolates can theoretically interfere with thyroid function (goitrogenic compounds)
- Meissner 2006b showed suppressed T3 (P<0.05)

**Evidence**:
- **No clinical reports** of thyroid dysfunction in maca trials
- Glucosinolate content (benzyl glucosinolate) may have lower goitrogenic potential than other glucosinolates

**Clinical Approach**:
- **Theoretical concern only**; no documented cases
- Monitor thyroid function in patients with existing thyroid conditions
- **Not a formal contraindication**

#### 3. Bipolar Disorder or Mania Risk
**Rationale**:
- One case report of manic episode in 27-year-old man

**Evidence Quality**: **Single case report**; causality uncertain

**Clinical Approach**:
- **Use caution** in patients with bipolar disorder
- Monitor for mood changes
- **Not a formal contraindication**; extremely rare event

## Drug Interactions

### Documented Interactions
**Status**: **No documented drug interactions** in clinical trials

### Theoretical Interactions (Unproven)

#### 1. Anticoagulants/Antiplatelets
**Rationale**: Theoretical only; no documented cases
**Evidence**: None
**Recommendation**: No special precautions based on available data

#### 2. Antihypertensive Medications
**Rationale**:
- Meissner 2006 showed **blood pressure reduction** (systolic and diastolic, P<0.05)
- Could theoretically potentiate antihypertensive drugs

**Evidence**: Beneficial effect in studies; no adverse interactions reported
**Recommendation**: Monitor blood pressure; interaction likely beneficial, not harmful

#### 3. Hormone Therapy
**Rationale**:
- Variable effects on hormone levels
- Could theoretically interact with HRT, oral contraceptives, or hormone-sensitive treatments

**Evidence**:
- Meissner 2006b: Maca described as having "hormone-balancing effects distinct from HRT"
- No documented adverse interactions

**Recommendation**: **Use with caution**; theoretical concern only

#### 4. Diabetes Medications
**Rationale**:
- Black maca showed **blood glucose reduction** (Gonzales-Arimborgo 2016)
- Could theoretically potentiate hypoglycemic medications

**Evidence**: Beneficial effect; no documented hypoglycemia in trials
**Recommendation**: Monitor blood glucose; interaction likely beneficial

## Pregnancy and Lactation

**Evidence**: **No human studies** in pregnant women

**Traditional Use**: Used traditionally in Peru for fertility; no documented harm
**Animal Data**: No acute toxicity at high doses; no teratogenicity studies found

**Clinical Recommendation**:
- **Insufficient data** to recommend use during pregnancy
- **Caution advised**: Avoid use during pregnancy due to lack of safety data
- **Not a proven hazard**; simply unstudied

### Lactation

**Excretion in Breast Milk**: Unknown

**Clinical Recommendation**:

- **Not a proven hazard**; simply unstudied

## Special Populations

### Postmenopausal Women
**Evidence**: **Well-studied population**

**Safety**:
- ✅ Multiple RCTs with excellent safety (Brooks 2008, Meissner 2006a/b)
- ✅ Beneficial effects on cardiovascular markers (blood pressure, HDL cholesterol)
- ✅ No serious adverse effects

**Clinical Recommendation**: **Safe and effective** in this population

---

### Men with Sexual Dysfunction
**Evidence**: Multiple RCTs (Dording 2008, Zenico 2009, Gonzales 2002, Shin 2023)

**Safety**:
- ✅ Well tolerated across studies
- ✅ No changes in testosterone (not androgenic)
- ✅ No serious adverse effects

**Clinical Recommendation**: **Safe and effective** in this population

---

### Athletes/Performance Enhancement
**Evidence**: Meta-analysis (Ojeda 2024) of physical performance studies

**Safety**:
- ✅ No reported adverse effects in performance studies
- ✅ No doping concerns (not on WADA prohibited list)
- ✅ Dose-dependent benefits without safety signals

**Clinical Recommendation**: **Safe** for athletic performance enhancement

## Laboratory Monitoring

### Recommended Monitoring
**Based on available evidence**: **No routine monitoring required**

### Optional Monitoring (Theoretical)
**In specific populations**:

1. **Thyroid function** (TSH, free T3, free T4):
   - If pre-existing thyroid condition
   - If symptoms of thyroid dysfunction develop
   - **Rationale**: Glucosinolate content; Meissner 2006b showed T3 suppression

2. **Liver function tests** (AST, ALT):
   - **Not routinely needed** (LiverTox: no liver injury)
   - Consider if very high doses or prolonged use
   - **Rationale**: One animal study showed AST elevation at very high dose

3. **Blood pressure**:
   - If on antihypertensive medications
   - **Rationale**: Meissner 2006 showed BP reduction (beneficial)

4. **Blood glucose**:
   - If diabetic or on hypoglycemic medications
   - **Rationale**: Black maca showed glucose reduction (beneficial)

## Overdose

### Documented Cases
**Status**: **No documented overdose cases** in literature

### Maximum Studied Doses (Humans)
- **6.0 g/day** (Shin 2023, though description unclear; likely 3.0 g/day)
- **3.5 g/day** (Brooks 2008) - well tolerated
- **~5 g/day** - mild GI disturbances reported

### Animal Acute Toxicity
- **≤17 g dried hypocotyls/kg body weight**: No acute toxicity
- **Human equivalent**: Far exceeds any therapeutic dose

**Clinical Recommendation**: Very wide safety margin; overdose unlikely to cause serious harm

## Quality and Contamination Concerns

### Product Variability
**Issue**: Commercial products show **69-2738 μg/g macamide variation**

**Safety Implications**:
- Inconsistent products may have unpredictable effects
- Some products may be ineffective (low bioactive content)
- Over-concentrated products theoretically higher risk (unlikely to cause harm based on safety data)

### Contaminants
**USP Safety Review Focus**:
- **Heavy metals**: Specifications being developed
- **Microbial contaminants**: Standards in development
- **Pesticide residues**: Limits being established

**Clinical Recommendation**: Use **reputable sources** with third-party testing (USP, NSF, ConsumerLab)

## Summary: Safety Profile

### Favorable Safety Features
✅ **No serious adverse events** in clinical trials
✅ **No liver toxicity** (LiverTox database)
✅ **Centuries of traditional use** without documented harm
✅ **Well tolerated** at 2.0-3.5 g/day for 12 weeks
✅ **No documented drug interactions**
✅ **Very high acute toxicity threshold** in animals
✅ **No fatal adverse effects** reported

### Mild/Transient Effects (Common)
- Gastrointestinal upset (most common)
- Headache
- Irritability
- Flushing

### Precautions (Theoretical, Not Contraindications)
⚠️ **Hormone-sensitive conditions**: Caution due to variable hormone effects (conflicting data)
⚠️ **Thyroid conditions**: Monitor due to glucosinolate content (no documented cases)
⚠️ **Bipolar disorder**: Caution due to one case report of mania
⚠️ **Pregnancy/lactation**: Insufficient data; avoid due to lack of safety studies
⚠️ **Drug interactions**: Theoretical only; monitor blood pressure, glucose if on related medications

### Evidence Gaps
❌ Long-term safety studies (>12 weeks)
❌ Pregnancy and lactation safety
❌ Pediatric safety
❌ Formal drug interaction studies

### Clinical Conclusion
**Maca has an excellent safety profile** for short-term use (≤12 weeks) at therapeutic doses (2.0-3.5 g/day). Most adverse effects are mild and transient. Traditional use suggests long-term safety, though formal studies are lacking. No formal contraindications exist, though theoretical caution advised in hormone-sensitive conditions, pregnancy/lactation, and bipolar disorder.
