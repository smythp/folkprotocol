# Lavandula angustifolia (Lavender) - Safety Profile

## General Safety Assessment

**Regulatory Status:**
- **FDA:** Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) for food use
- **EMA:** Recognized for traditional medicinal use with established safety profile
- **WHO:** Included in medicinal plants with established traditional uses
- **Commission E (1990):** "None known" for both contraindications and side effects

**Overall Assessment:** "In general, lavender is well tolerated" (FDA note)

## Adverse Effects

### Common Adverse Effects (Oral Silexan)

**Primary Adverse Event:** Eructation (belching)

**Study Evidence:**

**Kasper et al., 2014 (PMID: 24456909)** - GAD trial with 539 participants
- **Adverse event incidence densities per day:**
  - Silexan 160mg: 0.006
  - Silexan 80mg: 0.008
  - Paroxetine 20mg: 0.011
  - Placebo: 0.008

**Interpretation:** Silexan showed adverse event rates similar to or lower than placebo, and notably lower than the SSRI comparator.

**Kasper et al., 2016 (PMID: 26718792)** - Mixed anxiety-depression trial with 318 participants
- Eructation was primary adverse event with higher incidence in Silexan group
- Overall well-tolerated

**Kasper et al., 2025 (PMID: 38558147)** - Depression trial with 498 participants
- Eructation most frequent adverse effect
- Both Silexan and sertraline well tolerated

### Gastrointestinal Effects

**Study:** Kasper et al., 2018 (PMID: 28511598) - Clinical pharmacology review
- **Adverse effects:** Mild gastrointestinal symptoms only
- **Dose range:** 80-160 mg daily
- **Safety profile:** No sedation or withdrawal symptoms

**Summary:** Mild GI symptoms (primarily eructation) are the most common adverse effects with oral Silexan. These are generally well-tolerated and do not lead to treatment discontinuation in clinical trials.

### Sedation and Withdrawal

**Evidence:** Multiple trials (Kasper et al., 2010, 2014, 2016, 2018, 2025; Woelk & Schläfke, 2010)

**Key Findings:**
- **No sedative effects** at therapeutic doses (80-160 mg daily oral Silexan)
- **No withdrawal symptoms** upon discontinuation
- **No potential for drug abuse** (Woelk & Schläfke, 2010, PMID: 19962288)

**Comparison to Benzodiazepines:** Unlike lorazepam and other benzodiazepines, lavender (Silexan) does not produce sedation, dependence, or withdrawal symptoms despite comparable anxiolytic efficacy.

### Aromatherapy/Inhalation Adverse Effects

**Lillehei et al., 2015 (PMID: 26133206)** - Sleep study with 79 college students
- **Adverse events:** None reported
- **Duration:** 5 nights with 55 μl lavender essential oil patches

**Wang et al., 2025 (PMID: 41177368)** - Hypertension/anxiety study with 58 participants
- **Adverse events:** None reported
- **Duration:** 7 consecutive days, 15 minutes daily inhalation
- **No declarations of interest**

**Summary:** Aromatherapy/inhalation route shows excellent safety profile with no reported adverse events in clinical trials.

## Drug Interactions

### CYP450 Enzyme System

**Study:** Doroshyenko et al., 2013 (PMID: 23401474)
- **Study design:** Double-blind, randomized, 2-fold crossover trial
- **Sample size:** 16 healthy male and female Caucasians
- **Intervention:** Silexan 160 mg once daily for 11 days
- **Enzymes tested:** CYP1A2, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, CYP2D6, CYP3A4
- **Probe drugs:** Cocktail of probe substrates for each CYP enzyme

**Key Findings:**
- **No clinically relevant inhibitory or inducing effects** on CYP1A2, 2C9, 2C19, 2D6, and 3A4 enzymes in vivo
- No significant interactions with most enzyme phenotyping metrics
- Omeprazole (CYP2C19) showed minor variability, likely inherent pharmacokinetic fluctuation

**Clinical Significance:**
- **Low risk of drug-drug interactions** via major CYP pathways
- Safe co-administration with medications metabolized by CYP450 enzymes
- No dose adjustments needed for co-administered drugs

### Clinical Trial Evidence

**Kasper et al., 2018 (PMID: 28511598)**
- **Review conclusion:** "Did not cause drug interactions"
- Based on 7 clinical trials and 3 preclinical publications

**Implications:**
- Safe to combine with SSRIs, benzodiazepines, and other psychotropic medications
- No interactions documented with oral contraceptives
- Compatible with most conventional medications

## Contraindications

### Established Contraindications

**German Commission E (1990):** "None known"

**Contact Allergy/Sensitization:**
- While not listed as an absolute contraindication, documented contact allergy represents a relative contraindication for topical use

**Study:** Chiang, Zirwas & Warshaw, 2019 (PMID: 30779160)
- **Study type:** Retrospective clinical study from Australian clinic
- **Findings:**
  - Lavender identified as contact sensitizer
  - Can cause allergic contact dermatitis (ACD)
  - Lavender is uncommon cause of ACD overall

**Recommendation:** Patch testing recommended before widespread topical application in individuals with history of contact allergies or sensitive skin.

### Pregnancy and Lactation

**Note:** The bibliography does not include specific studies on pregnancy and lactation safety. Traditional regulatory monographs (Commission E, EMA, WHO) do not list specific pregnancy/lactation contraindications, but this represents absence of evidence rather than evidence of safety.

**Clinical Practice Guidance:**
- **Pregnancy:** Insufficient evidence; caution advised
- **Lactation:** Insufficient evidence; caution advised
- **Topical use around breast area:** Historically cautioned due to early endocrine concerns (see Endocrine Effects section)

**Best Practice:** Pregnant and lactating women should consult healthcare providers before using therapeutic doses of lavender, though culinary/food-grade uses are generally recognized as safe (FDA GRAS status).

## Special Populations

### Children - Endocrine Effects

**Historical Concern:**
**Henley et al., 2007** - Case report
- Prepubertal gynecomastia linked to lavender and tea tree oils
- Estrogenic and antiandrogenic activity reported
- This case report raised significant concerns about endocrine disruption

**Modern Evidence:**

**Tao et al., 2022 (PMID: 35663791)**
- **Study type:** Population-based cohort study (JAMA Dermatology)
- **Findings:**
  - Children regularly exposed to lavender or tea tree essential oils experienced **same risk of endocrine disorders** as those not exposed
  - **Little to no evidence** substantiating proposed link between lavender essential oil and endocrine disruption in children
- **Quality:** Large population-based study, 2022 publication

**Nataf et al., 2023 (PMID: 37906319)**
- **Study type:** In vitro endocrine activity assessment
- **Components tested:** Linalool and linalyl acetate (main lavender components)
- **Findings:** **No significant estrogenic or androgenic activity detected**
- **Quality:** 2023 publication, proactive testing strategy, critical assessment

**Summary:**
- **Historical concerns (2007)** about gynecomastia have been **refuted by recent large population studies (2022)** and mechanistic testing (2023)
- **No increased risk** of endocrine disorders in children exposed to lavender
- Main active compounds (linalool, linalyl acetate) show **no estrogenic or androgenic activity**

**Clinical Recommendation:** Based on current evidence (2022-2023), lavender does not pose endocrine disruption risk in children. Historical concerns have not been substantiated by rigorous population-based and mechanistic studies.

### Topical Use Around Breast Area

**Historical Caution:** Topical use around breast area was historically advised against due to the Henley et al. (2007) gynecomastia case report.

**Current Evidence:** Recent studies (Tao et al., 2022; Nataf et al., 2023) show no endocrine activity, suggesting this caution may be unnecessary.

**Clinical Practice:** While evidence suggests low risk, some practitioners may still exercise caution due to the historical concerns. This represents a conservative approach rather than evidence-based contraindication.

## Cytotoxicity and Dermatological Safety

### In Vitro Cytotoxicity

**Study:** Prashar, Locke & Evans, 2004 (PMID: 15144499)
- **Study type:** In vitro cytotoxicity study
- **Cell types:** Human endothelial cells and fibroblasts
- **Findings:**
  - Lavender oil cytotoxic to human skin cells at **0.25% (v/v) concentration** in all cell types tested
  - Linalool may be the active component responsible for cytotoxicity
  - **In vitro findings; clinical relevance unclear**

**Implications:**
- Concerns about potential irritant or allergenic skin reactions with topical use
- **Clinical formulations** typically use 1-3% dilution in carrier oil
- Most clinical studies have not reported significant skin irritation at therapeutic concentrations

**Clinical Recommendation:** Use appropriately diluted lavender oil (1-3% in carrier) for topical applications. Patch test recommended for sensitive individuals.

### Contact Dermatitis

**Study:** Chiang, Zirwas & Warshaw, 2019 (PMID: 30779160)
- **Study type:** Retrospective clinical study
- **Findings:**
  - Lavender identified as contact sensitizer
  - Can cause allergic contact dermatitis (ACD)
  - Lavender is **uncommon cause of ACD overall**

**Clinical Recommendation:**
- Patch testing recommended for individuals with history of contact allergies
- Discontinue use if signs of contact dermatitis develop (redness, itching, rash)
- Uncommon overall; most individuals tolerate topical lavender without issue

## Toxicity

### Acute Toxicity

**No documented acute toxicity** at therapeutic doses in clinical trials.

**Silexan clinical trials:** Doses up to 160 mg daily for 10 weeks showed no serious adverse events.

**FDA GRAS Status:** Generally Recognized as Safe for food use suggests low acute toxicity risk.

### Chronic Toxicity

**Clinical trial evidence:** 6-10 week treatment courses with Silexan (80-160 mg daily) showed:
- No accumulation of adverse effects over time
- No withdrawal symptoms upon discontinuation
- No evidence of organ toxicity

**Long-term safety:** While most trials are 6-10 weeks, the favorable safety profile and traditional use history suggest low chronic toxicity risk. However, studies >10 weeks are limited.

### Overdose/Excessive Consumption

**Case Report Note:** One case report of anticholinergic syndrome from excessive lavender tea consumption exists in the literature (not detailed in this bibliography).

**Clinical Recommendation:** Adhere to established therapeutic doses. Excessive consumption beyond therapeutic ranges is not recommended.

## Comparative Safety

### Lavender vs. Conventional Anxiolytics/Antidepressants

**Firoozeei et al., 2021 (PMID: 33549687)** - Depression meta-analysis
- **Conclusion:** "Lavender has less serious side effects compared to conventional antidepressants"

**Kasper et al., 2014 (PMID: 24456909)** - GAD trial comparing Silexan to paroxetine
- **Adverse event rates:**
  - Paroxetine: 0.011 per day
  - Silexan 160mg: 0.006 per day
  - Silexan 80mg: 0.008 per day
  - Placebo: 0.008 per day

**Woelk & Schläfke, 2010 (PMID: 19962288)** - Silexan vs. lorazepam
- **Silexan advantages:**
  - No sedative effects
  - No potential for drug abuse
  - No withdrawal symptoms
- **Comparable efficacy** to lorazepam without benzodiazepine-associated risks

**Summary:** Lavender (Silexan) demonstrates a **superior safety profile** compared to conventional anxiolytics (benzodiazepines) and antidepressants (SSRIs) while maintaining comparable efficacy.

## Safety Summary by Route of Administration

### Oral (Silexan)
**Dose range:** 80-160 mg daily for 6-10 weeks
**Common adverse effects:** Mild gastrointestinal symptoms (eructation)
**Serious adverse effects:** None documented in clinical trials
**Drug interactions:** None (no CYP450 interactions)
**Contraindications:** None established (Commission E: "none known")
**Pregnancy/Lactation:** Insufficient evidence; caution advised
**Children:** No endocrine disruption risk (recent population studies)

### Inhalation/Aromatherapy
**Dose range:** 55 μl patches or 15-minute sessions daily
**Adverse effects:** None reported in clinical trials
**Drug interactions:** None documented
**Contraindications:** None established
**Pregnancy/Lactation:** Insufficient evidence; generally considered lower risk than oral
**Children:** No endocrine disruption risk

### Topical/Massage
**Typical concentration:** 1-3% dilution in carrier oil
**Adverse effects:** Contact dermatitis (uncommon but documented)
**Cytotoxicity:** In vitro concerns at 0.25% (v/v); clinical relevance unclear
**Contraindications:** Known contact allergy to lavender
**Recommendation:** Patch test for sensitive individuals or those with allergy history
**Pregnancy/Lactation:** Topical use around breast area historically cautioned (though recent evidence suggests low risk)

## Clinical Practice Recommendations

### Pre-Treatment Assessment
1. Screen for history of contact allergies (if topical use planned)
2. Review current medications (though drug interactions unlikely)
3. Pregnancy/lactation status (counsel on insufficient evidence)
4. Consider patch test for topical applications in sensitive individuals

### During Treatment
1. Monitor for GI symptoms (eructation) with oral Silexan (generally mild and tolerable)
2. Assess for contact dermatitis if using topical preparations
3. No routine monitoring required for systemic toxicity
4. No concerns about sedation, dependence, or withdrawal

### Discontinuation
1. No tapering required
2. No withdrawal symptoms expected
3. Can discontinue abruptly without concern

### Special Considerations
1. **Children:** Safe based on recent evidence (no endocrine disruption)
2. **Elderly:** No specific contraindications; may prefer over benzodiazepines due to lack of sedation
3. **Pregnancy/Lactation:** Insufficient evidence; caution advised for therapeutic doses
4. **Polypharmacy:** Safe; no significant drug interactions

## Quality Assurance and Contamination

**European Pharmacopoeia Standards:** Ensure use of pharmaceutical-grade or European Pharmacopoeia-compliant lavender preparations to minimize contamination and adulteration risks.

**Silexan:** Pharmaceutical-grade standardization provides highest quality assurance.

**Essential Oils:** Third-party testing for purity recommended when purchasing essential oils for therapeutic use.

## Summary of Safety Profile

**General:** Generally well tolerated across multiple routes of administration

**Common Adverse Effects:** Mild gastrointestinal symptoms (eructation) with oral use; otherwise minimal

**Drug Interactions:** None clinically significant (no CYP450 interactions)

**Contraindications:** None established by regulatory monographs; relative contraindication for topical use in individuals with known lavender contact allergy

**Special Populations:**
- **Children:** Safe; no endocrine disruption risk (refuted by recent studies)
- **Pregnancy/Lactation:** Insufficient evidence; caution advised for therapeutic doses
- **Elderly:** Safe; may be preferred over conventional anxiolytics due to lack of sedation

**Comparative Safety:** Superior safety profile compared to benzodiazepines (no sedation, dependence, withdrawal) and SSRIs (lower adverse event rates)

**Regulatory Status:** FDA GRAS (food use), EMA traditional medicinal use recognition, WHO medicinal plant status, Commission E approved with "none known" for contraindications and side effects

**Overall Assessment:** Lavender, particularly in standardized oral preparations (Silexan) and inhalation/aromatherapy applications, demonstrates an excellent safety profile suitable for clinical use in anxiety, depression, stress, and sleep disorders.
