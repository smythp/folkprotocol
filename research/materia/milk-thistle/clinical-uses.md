# Milk Thistle: Clinical Uses

## Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD/NASH)

### Meta-Analysis Evidence (Li et al. 2024)
- **Study Size**: 26 RCTs, 2,375 patients
- **PMID**: 38579127

#### Lipid Profile Improvements
- Total cholesterol decreased (SMD: -0.85)
- Triglycerides reduced (SMD: -0.62)
- LDL-C lowered (SMD: -0.81)
- HDL-C increased (SMD: 0.46)

#### Liver Enzyme Reductions
- ALT decreased by 12.39 units
- AST decreased by 10.97 units

#### Metabolic Markers
- Fasting insulin reduced (SMD: -0.59)
- Fatty liver index decreased by 6.64 points
- Fatty liver score lowered (SMD: -0.51)

#### Histological Outcomes
- Hepatic steatosis significantly improved (OR: 3.25)

### NASH Trial (Navarro et al. 2019)
- **Study Size**: 78 patients (25 placebo, 26 receiving 420mg, 27 receiving 700mg)
- **Duration**: 48 weeks
- **Dosage**: Legalon 420 mg or 700 mg three times daily (1260 mg or 2100 mg/day)
- **PMID**: 31536511

#### Results
- Primary endpoint (≥2 point NAS improvement):
  - 700 mg group: 15%
  - 420 mg group: 19%
  - Placebo group: 12%
  - Statistical significance: p = 0.79 (not significant)

**Note**: Study had methodological challenges—63% of enrolled participants failed entry criteria upon central pathology review. Results inconclusive due to enrollment and efficacy concerns.

### Liver Enzyme Impact Review (Calderon Martinez et al. 2023)
- **Study Size**: 29 RCTs, 3,846 participants
- **Dosage Range**: 140 mg to 420 mg
- **PMID**: 38021897

#### Findings
- 65.5% of studies: Reduced liver enzyme levels
- 20.7% of studies: No significant change
- 13.8% of studies: Elevated liver enzymes

**Clinical Implications**: Most pronounced benefits in NAFLD patients. Variations attributed to comorbidities, dosing, and underlying disease differences.

## Liver Cirrhosis

### Landmark Trial (Ferenci et al. 1989)
- **Study Size**: 170 patients (87 silymarin, 83 placebo)
- **Duration**: Mean 41 months; minimum 2 years for all patients
- **Dosage**: 140 mg three times daily (420 mg/day)
- **Study Type**: Double-blind, prospective RCT
- **PMID**: 2671116

#### Survival
- **4-year survival**: 58% (±9%) silymarin vs 39% (±9%) placebo (p = 0.036)

#### Subgroup Efficacy
- Effective in alcoholic cirrhosis patients (p = 0.01)
- Effective in Child A severity patients (p = 0.03)

#### Mortality
- Silymarin group: 24 deaths (18 liver-related)
- Placebo group: 37 deaths (31 liver-related)

**Adverse Effects**: None reported

### Meta-Analysis (Saller et al. 2008)
- **Study Size**: 19 double- or single-blind trials from 65 papers
- **PMID**: 18334810

#### Alcoholic Liver Disease
- Reduced aspartate aminotransferase vs placebo
- No effect on alkaline phosphatase

#### Liver Cirrhosis
- Modest mortality benefit
- Liver-related mortality: 10.0% (silymarin) vs 17.3% (placebo)

#### Recommended Use
- Supportive element for Amanita phalloides poisoning
- Supportive treatment for alcoholic cirrhosis

### Earlier Meta-Analysis (Jacobs et al. 2002)
- **Study Size**: 14 placebo-controlled RCTs, 433 patients
- **PMID**: 12427501

#### Findings
- Safe and well tolerated
- No reduction in mortality (OR: 0.8)
- No improvements in liver biopsy histology
- No significant differences in liver enzymes or albumin
- Minor reduction in ALT (-9 IU/L) without clinical significance

**Conclusion**: Data too limited to exclude substantial benefit or harm. Insufficient evidence to recommend for liver disease treatment.

## Drug-Induced Liver Injury

### Anti-Tuberculosis Drug Hepatoprotection (Tao et al. 2019)
- **Study Size**: 5 RCTs, 1,198 patients (585 silymarin, 613 placebo)
- **PMID**: 30733935

#### Hepatoprotection
- Reduced anti-TB drug-induced liver injury at week 4 (RR: 0.33, 95% CI: 0.15-0.75)
- Protective effects on ALT, AST, and ALP (statistically significant)

#### Safety
- Adverse events comparable to placebo (OR: 1.09, 95% CI: 0.86-1.39, p = 0.47)
- Significantly reduced risk of liver injury within 4 weeks of treatment

## Nephroprotective Effects

### Meta-Analysis (Frounchi et al. 2025)
- **Study Size**: 7 clinical trials included in meta-analysis from 10 identified
- **PMID**: 40886393

#### Overall Nephroprotective Effect
- Significant reduction in serum creatinine (Hedges' g = -1.23; 95% CI: -2.02 to -0.43; p = 0.0024)

#### Subgroup Analysis by Condition
- **Drug-induced acute kidney injury**: Effective (p = 0.003)
- **Chronic kidney disease**: Not effective (p = 0.3065)

#### Dosage Analysis
- Daily dose of 280 mg: Ineffective (p = 0.2375)

**Safety**: No significant adverse events reported

**Clinical Implication**: Silymarin shows promise for acute kidney protection (particularly drug-induced AKI) but not for chronic kidney disease.

## Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus

### Meta-Analysis (Voroneanu et al. 2016)
- **Study Size**: 5 RCTs, 270 patients
- **PMID**: 27340676

#### Glycemic Control
- Fasting blood glucose decreased by 26.86 mg/dL (95% CI: -35.42 to -18.30)
- HbA1c decreased by 1.07% (95% CI: -1.73 to -0.40)

#### Lipid Profile
- No significant effect on lipid profile

#### Renal Effects
- Benefits for proteinuria and CKD progression uncertain (only single small studies)

#### Limitations
- Low quality evidence
- Substantial heterogeneity across studies
- No clinical recommendation can be made at this time

**Safety**: Well tolerated in included studies

## Chronic Hepatitis C

### Phase I Dose-Escalation (Hawke et al. 2010)
- **Study Size**: 32 patients (four cohorts of 8 patients each, 3:1 silymarin:placebo)
- **Duration**: 7 days
- **Dosage**: 140 mg, 280 mg, 560 mg, or 700 mg every 8 hours (420 mg, 840 mg, 1680 mg, or 2100 mg/day)
- **PMID**: 19841158

#### Pharmacokinetics (Nonlinear)
- Silybin A: 11-fold increase in steady-state exposure with 5-fold dose increase
- Silybin B: 38-fold increase in steady-state exposure with 5-fold dose increase

#### Safety
- No drug-related adverse events reported
- Doses up to 2.1 grams daily well tolerated
- No clinically meaningful changes in liver enzymes or viral load

### Evidence Gap (Saller et al. 2008)
- **PMID**: 18334810

**Finding**: No evidence of benefit for treatment of viral hepatitis C in systematic review

## Summary of Evidence Quality

### Strong Evidence
- **NAFLD/NASH**: Meta-analysis of 26 RCTs showing improvements in liver enzymes, lipid profiles, and hepatic steatosis
- **Alcoholic cirrhosis**: Long-term RCT showing survival benefit
- **Anti-TB drug liver injury**: Meta-analysis showing protective effects

### Moderate Evidence
- **Drug-induced acute kidney injury**: Meta-analysis showing benefit
- **Type 2 diabetes**: Small meta-analysis showing glycemic control improvements (low quality evidence)

### Weak/Conflicting Evidence
- **Chronic hepatitis C**: No benefit found in systematic review; dose-escalation showed safety but no efficacy
- **Chronic kidney disease**: Meta-analysis found no benefit
- **General liver cirrhosis**: Mixed results across meta-analyses; earlier studies found insufficient evidence

### Evidence Gaps
- **Toxic liver diseases**: Evidence scarce (Saller et al. 2008)
- **Liver biopsy histology**: No improvements found (Jacobs et al. 2002)

## Dosing Summary from Clinical Trials

### Standard Doses

#### 420 mg/day (140 mg three times daily)
- **Studies**: Ferenci 1989 cirrhosis trial; Hawke 2010 phase I
- **Duration**: Long-term (months to years)
- **Indication**: Cirrhosis, chronic liver disease
- **Evidence**: Strong survival benefit in cirrhosis

#### 840 mg/day (280 mg three times daily)
- **Studies**: Hawke 2010 phase I
- **Duration**: 7 days in pharmacokinetic study
- **Indication**: Chronic hepatitis C
- **Evidence**: Safety established; efficacy unclear

#### 1260 mg/day (420 mg three times daily)
- **Studies**: Navarro 2019 NASH trial
- **Duration**: 48 weeks
- **Indication**: Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis
- **Evidence**: Inconclusive due to methodological issues

#### 2100 mg/day (700 mg three times daily)
- **Studies**: Navarro 2019 NASH trial; Hawke 2010 phase I
- **Duration**: 7 days to 48 weeks
- **Indication**: NASH, hepatitis C; maximum tolerated dose
- **Safety**: Well tolerated even at this high dose for 24 weeks

### Bioavailability Considerations
- Standard silymarin extracts have poor bioavailability (<50 μg/mL water solubility)
- Extensive first-pass metabolism reduces systemic availability
- Enhanced formulations (phytosome, micellar) may allow for lower effective doses
- Nonlinear pharmacokinetics: dose increases result in disproportionate plasma level increases
