# Skullcap - Safety and Drug Interactions

**Overall rating:** LiverTox Likelihood Score **B** — very likely but rare cause of clinically apparent liver injury.

---

## Hepatotoxicity: Core Safety Concern

Skullcap is the primary documented safety concern for this herb. Both species have been associated with liver injury, though the evidence base and severity patterns differ.

### Epidemiology (Soldera 2024 — Systematic Review)
- **Database:** 936 total herb-induced liver injury (HILI) cases from 446 references; 79 herb types identified
- **Skullcap profile:**
  - Demographics: predominantly female; mean age 54 years
  - Common indications for use: anxiety, stress, insomnia; also arthritis
  - Dose range documented in cases: 400 mg to 16 g daily
  - Onset to liver injury: 1 week to 3 months
  - Injury pattern: hepatocellular (occasionally mixed)
  - Severity: mild-to-moderate; normalization within ~12 weeks after discontinuation
  - Mortality: 14.2% mortality across all HILI cases (not skullcap-specific rate)
  - Some severe cases: liver transplantation required
  - Species differentiation: not consistently reported in case literature

### LiverTox Profile (NIH/NCBI NBK548757)
- **Likelihood score:** B (very likely but rare cause of clinically apparent liver injury)
- **Documented case:** 53-year-old woman, sleep aid containing skullcap + valerian, 4 weeks exposure
  - ALT: 1208 U/L
  - AST: 1082 U/L
  - Alkaline phosphatase: 298 U/L
  - Bilirubin: 9.0 mg/dL
  - Recovery: complete normalization within 3 months of discontinuation
- **Onset:** typically 1–12 weeks (most commonly 1–3 months for Chinese skullcap)
- **Injury pattern:** hepatocellular
- **Severity:** usually mild-to-moderate; at least one acute liver failure case documented
- **Outcomes:** self-limiting; no chronic disease or cirrhosis reported in typical cases
- **Mechanism:** unknown
- **Species note:** most DILI cases attributed to *S. baicalensis*; *S. lateriflora* association less robust

## Case Reports

### Case 1: Drug-Induced Autoimmune Hepatitis (Thakral et al. 2023)
- **Species:** *S. lateriflora* (North American skullcap supplement)
- **Patient:** 62-year-old Caucasian female; history of Sjögren's syndrome, anxiety, insomnia
- **Exposure:** 1–2 months before onset
- **Presentation:** Generalized fatigue, arthralgias, pruritus, new-onset jaundice

**Laboratory values (initial/readmission):**
- AST: 1091 → 1222 IU/L
- ALT: 980 → 540 IU/L
- Total bilirubin: 9.5 → peaked at 11.8 mg/dL
- INR: 2.4
- Alkaline phosphatase: 164 → 619 IU/L
- IgG: 2573 mg/dL (elevated)
- Biopsy: extensive plasma cells consistent with new-onset autoimmune hepatitis

**Outcome:** Jaundice resolved after protracted course; patient remained dialysis-dependent and was listed for liver-kidney transplant.

**Significance:** Rare presentation of drug-induced autoimmune hepatitis with severe outcome; one of the most serious reported cases for *S. lateriflora*.

---

### Case 2: S. baicalensis Hepatotoxicity — Positive Rechallenge (Yang et al. 2012)
- **Species:** *S. baicalensis* (via Move Free Advanced supplement — glucosamine, chondroitin, Chinese skullcap, Black Catechu)
- **Patient:** Elderly woman
- **Course:** Developed significant cholestasis and hepatitis → recovered after stopping → self-rechallenged → hepatotoxicity recurred → biopsy confirmed acute DILI → recovered after second discontinuation

**Significance:** Positive rechallenge provides stronger causality evidence (Roussel Uclaf Causality Assessment). This is the most robust causal link documented for *S. baicalensis* hepatotoxicity.

---

### Case 3: Hepatotoxicity + Pulmonary Infiltrates (Dhanasekaran et al. 2013)
- **Species:** *S. baicalensis* (Move Free Advanced supplement)
- **Patient:** 62-year-old Caucasian female with diabetes mellitus
- **Presentation:** Acute shortness of breath, hypoxia, jaundice

**Laboratory values:**
- AST: 893 → 668 IU/L (2 weeks post-discontinuation)
- ALT: 1247 → 1144 IU/L (2 weeks post-discontinuation)
- Complete normalization by 8 weeks

**Pulmonary findings:**
- Bilateral interstitial infiltrates on chest X-ray
- Bilateral ground glass opacities upper lobes on CT
- Required oxygen supplementation
- Both hepatic and pulmonary complications resolved after discontinuation

**Significance:** Documents rare pulmonary toxicity in addition to hepatotoxicity — a unique multi-organ presentation.

## Hepatic Safety Data: Controlled Setting

### Puri et al. 2019 — S. baicalensis Long-Term LFT Monitoring
- **Design:** Prospective observational pre-post assessment (uncontrolled)
- **Participants:** 17 (16 female, mean age 38.6 years)
- **Dose:** 1335 mg dried *S. baicalensis* root daily
- **Duration:** Average 444 days (~14.8 months)

**Liver function tests (mean pre → post):**
- ALT: 25.7 → 25.1 IU/L (non-significant decrease)
- AST: 22.1 → 23.5 IU/L (non-significant increase)
- Alkaline phosphatase: 63.7 → 63.3 IU/L (non-significant decrease)
- Bilirubin: 6.1 → 6.0 μM (non-significant decrease)

**Conclusion:** No statistically significant LFT changes; no clinical or biochemical hepatic dysfunction during 14.8 months of supplementation.

**Limitations:** Small sample (n=17), uncontrolled design, no comparison group. Cannot rule out idiosyncratic reactions in a larger population.

## Adulteration: Germander (Teucrium canadense)

A critical confounding factor in skullcap hepatotoxicity attribution:

- **Problem:** Germander (*Teucrium canadense*) has historically been used to adulterate *S. lateriflora* products
- **Germander hepatotoxicity:** Well-documented; pyrrolizidine-like diterpene hepatotoxins (neoclerodane diterpenes) are the likely cause
- **Implication:** Some "skullcap" DILI cases may actually be germander toxicity
- **Current status:** LiverTox and regulatory bodies acknowledge this confound
- **Mitigation:** Authentication testing (AHP HPLC method; ajugapitin marker) required for *S. lateriflora* quality assurance
- **Consumer risk:** Products without authentication certificates may carry germander adulteration risk

## Drug Interactions

### CYP2C9 Inhibition — Clinically Significant (Yi et al. 2009)
- **Species:** *Scutellariae radix* (*S. baicalensis* root)
- **Design:** Parallel-group clinical trial; probe drug cocktail; 24 healthy male volunteers (8 per herbal group); 13 days
- **Result:** CYP2C9 activity reduced to **71% of baseline** (losartan metabolic ratio)
- **Clinical implication:** Moderate CYP2C9 inhibition may affect metabolism of:
  - **Warfarin** (narrow therapeutic index — most clinically significant)
  - **Phenytoin** (narrow therapeutic index)
  - **NSAIDs** (ibuprofen, diclofenac, naproxen, celecoxib)
  - Other CYP2C9 substrates

### CYP2E1 Induction (Yi et al. 2009)
- **Result:** CYP2E1 activity increased **1.42-fold**
- **Clinical implication:** May increase toxicity of CYP2E1-substrates (acetaminophen, ethanol metabolism)
- Other CYP isoforms (CYP1A2, CYP2C19, CYP2D6, CYP3A4) not significantly affected in this study

### Bidirectional CYP3A4 Effects (Zhou et al. 2021 — Review)
- Baicalin (0.01–1 µM) in HeLa cells:
  - **Increased** CYP3A4, CYP2C9, CYP2C19 expression at 24–36h
  - **Decreased** CYP3A4 and CYP2C9 at 48h
- Effect appears time- and concentration-dependent
- Clinical significance unclear; warrants monitoring with CYP3A4-sensitive drugs

### Documented Interaction Categories (Zhou et al. 2021 — Review)
- **Cancer drugs:** Interactions documented; potential for altered chemotherapy efficacy/toxicity
- **Diabetes medications:** Interaction potential documented
- **Antimicrobials:** Interaction potential documented
- **Most clinically relevant:** Warfarin, chemotherapy agents, immunosuppressants

## Pregnancy and Lactation

**Recommendation:** Contraindicated

**Basis:**
- Insufficient safety data in humans
- Traditional TCM use of *S. baicalensis* to "calm the fetus" does not constitute safety evidence by modern standards
- LiverTox and regulatory sources uniformly advise avoidance during pregnancy and lactation
- Given documented hepatotoxicity potential, risk-benefit does not support use in pregnancy

---

## Contraindications

1. **Pre-existing liver disease** — documented hepatotoxicity risk
2. **Concurrent hepatotoxic medications** — additive liver risk
3. **Warfarin / anticoagulant therapy** — CYP2C9 inhibition may increase warfarin levels
4. **Pregnancy and lactation** — insufficient safety data
5. **Autoimmune conditions** (relative, S. lateriflora) — one documented case of drug-induced autoimmune hepatitis; patients with pre-existing autoimmune conditions (as in the Thakral 2023 case with Sjögren's syndrome) may be at higher risk

---

## Monitoring Recommendations

For patients using skullcap preparations:
- **Baseline LFTs** before starting
- **Repeat LFTs** at 4–8 weeks of use
- **Discontinue immediately** if jaundice, fatigue, right upper quadrant pain, or dark urine develop
- **Drug interaction review** if on warfarin, phenytoin, NSAIDs, chemotherapy, or immunosuppressants

---

## Safety Summary

| Risk | Evidence Level | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Hepatotoxicity | Case reports + systematic review | Rare but potentially severe; monitor LFTs |
| Drug-induced autoimmune hepatitis | 1 case report (S. lateriflora) | Rare; severe outcome possible |
| Pulmonary toxicity | 1 case report (S. baicalensis) | Very rare |
| CYP2C9 inhibition | 1 human clinical trial | Moderate; monitor warfarin, phenytoin |
| CYP2E1 induction | 1 human clinical trial | Moderate; caution with acetaminophen |
| Pregnancy risk | No data | Contraindicated |
| Germander adulteration | Historical documentation | Complicates causality; authentication required |

**Bottom line:** Rare but potentially serious hepatotoxicity is the primary safety concern. Risk is likely higher with *S. baicalensis* than *S. lateriflora*, but species differentiation in cases is inconsistent. Germander adulteration may account for a subset of reported toxicity. Drug interactions via CYP2C9 are clinically relevant for warfarin users.
