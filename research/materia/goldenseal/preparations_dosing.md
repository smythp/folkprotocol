# Goldenseal - Preparations and Dosing

## Overview

No therapeutic dosing has been established by clinical trial. All dosing information derives from traditional practice (Eclectic and Native American), pharmacokinetic studies (where dose is used to achieve measurable CYP inhibition, not therapeutic effect), and NCCIH/Commission E assessments.

**Commission E**: Negative monograph — no approved dose for any indication.
**WHO**: No monograph — no WHO-endorsed dose.
**NCCIH**: Short-term use (~3 g/day) appears tolerated in healthy adults; no therapeutic dose established.

---

## Traditional Dosing (Eclectic Medicine, 1850–1930)

### Dried Root/Rhizome (Oral)
- **Dose range**: 0.5–10 g dried root
- **Frequency**: Three times daily
- **Common practical dose**: 1–2 g TID (3–6 g/day total)
- **Source**: Felter & Lloyd, *King's American Dispensatory* (1898); USP entries

### Fluid Extract (1:1 in 60% ethanol)
- **Dose range**: 0.3–10 mL
- **Frequency**: Three times daily
- **Standard Eclectic dose**: 1–2 mL TID
- **Note**: High potency; 1 mL = ~1 g dried herb equivalent

### Tincture (1:10 in 60% ethanol)
- **Dose range**: 2–4 mL
- **Frequency**: Three times daily
- **Resulting herb equivalent**: 0.2–0.4 g per dose at 1:10 ratio

### Powdered Root Extract (Standardized)
- **Typical historical dose**: 1–4 grains (65–260 mg) dried extract
- **Frequency**: Three times daily

## Pharmacokinetic Study Dosing (Reference for CYP Inhibition)

| Study | Dose Used | Duration | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gurley 2005 | ~1 g extract TID (~3 g/day) | 28 days | CYP phenotype mapping |
| Gurley 2007 | Not specified (commercial product) | 14 days | Digoxin/P-gp |
| Gurley 2008 (CYP3A) | Not specified (commercial product) | 14 days | Midazolam/CYP3A |
| Gurley 2008 (CYP2D6) | Not specified (commercial product) | 14 days | DURR/CYP2D6 |
| Sandhu 2003 | 1140 mg root BID (2280 mg/day) | 14 days | Indinavir/CYP3A |
| Gupta 2015 | 2.7 g single dose (78 mg hydrastine + 132 mg berberine) | Single dose | Hydrastine PK |
| Nguyen 2021 | Not specified (commercial extract) | Acute and 14 days | Probe cocktail |

**Key observation**: CYP inhibition (40–62% for CYP3A; 40–50% for CYP2D6) was achieved consistently across multiple commercial goldenseal products and dosing protocols. The inhibition appears to occur at doses commonly sold commercially, not only at high experimental doses.

## Contemporary Commercial Preparations

### Capsules/Tablets (Dried Root Powder)
- **Typical commercial dose**: 500–1000 mg per capsule
- **Product labeling**: Usually 1–2 capsules two to three times daily
- **Resulting daily dose**: 1–6 g/day depending on product
- **Quality variable**: Only 59% of products met proposed USP standards in 2003 survey (Edwards & Draper)
- **Standardization requirement**: ≥2.0% hydrastine and ≥2.5% berberine (USP draft)

### Standardized Root Extracts (Concentrated)
- **Concentration**: Typically 10:1 or 4:1 extract ratios
- **Dose range**: 50–300 mg/day (lower doses in divided doses, reflecting concentration)
- **Standardization target**: ≥2% hydrastine and ≥2.5% berberine
- **Note**: Products advertised as "standardized" should specify both markers

### Tinctures (Commercial)
- **Standard ratio**: 1:10 in 60–70% ethanol (traditional), or 1:5 with similar alcohol content
- **Typical dose**: 2–4 mL, three times daily
- **Alkaloid content (validated by AOAC HPLC method)**: ~4,000–5,000 μg/mL per alkaloid in well-prepared tinctures
- **Note**: Some commercial tinctures have been found with negligible alkaloid content

### Topical Preparations
- **Wound wash**: 1/4 teaspoon dried root powder in 1 cup of boiled, cooled water; strain through cloth
- **Salve**: Ground root combined with beeswax and carrier oil (traditional preparation)
- **Eye wash (traditional, not recommended)**: Very dilute decoction for conjunctivitis; risk of contamination and inadequate sterility; safer alternatives exist

## Preparation Quality Considerations

### Selecting a Quality Product
1. **Ask for certification of analysis (COA)**: Should specify % hydrastine (≥2.0%) and % berberine (≥2.5%)
2. **USP compliance**: Products meeting proposed USP standards more likely to contain adequate alkaloid levels
3. **Root vs. "herb"**: "Goldenseal root" products average 4× higher alkaloid content than "goldenseal herb" products (Edwards & Draper 2003)
4. **Analytical method**: HPLC-UV per AOAC Official First Action (Brown & Roman 2008) is the gold standard
5. **Adulterant risk**: Common adulterants include Coptis (goldthread), Oregon grape (*Mahonia aquifolium*), barberry (*Berberis vulgaris*), and celandine — all contain berberine but at different ratios and with different alkaloid profiles

### Wild-Harvested vs. Cultivated
- Wild-harvested goldenseal is ecologically threatened; listed on CITES Appendix II and United Plant Savers at-risk list
- Cultivated goldenseal is environmentally preferred; quality should be comparable if grown under appropriate conditions
- Some traditional herbalists prefer wild-crafted for potency concerns, but evidence for potency difference is anecdotal

## Dosing in Special Contexts

### Acute Short-Term Use (Contemporary Practice)
- Some practitioners use goldenseal for 7–14 days maximum for acute mucosal conditions
- Typical dose: 500 mg–1 g dried root, 2–3 times daily (1–3 g/day total)
- **Note**: No RCTs support efficacy for any acute indication

### Drug Interaction Risk Dosing Threshold
- CYP inhibition appears at commercially available doses (14-day supplementation studies used standard products)
- There is no established "safe" dose that avoids CYP inhibition; assume meaningful CYP2D6/CYP3A inhibition at any dose exceeding minimal use
- Goldenseal should be discontinued at least 2–4 weeks before any surgery or procedure requiring CYP3A or CYP2D6-metabolized anesthetics

### Avoiding Use
The following populations should generally **avoid goldenseal entirely**:
- Pregnant women (see Safety)
- Breastfeeding mothers
- Neonates and infants
- Patients on narrow therapeutic index drugs metabolized by CYP2D6 or CYP3A4 (e.g., warfarin, digoxin/caution, antidepressants, antipsychotics, immunosuppressants, antiretrovirals)
- Patients on metformin (glycemic control may be impaired)
- Patients with hepatic disease

## AOAC Validated Analytical Method

**Brown & Roman 2008 (AOAC Official First Action)**:
- **Method**: HPLC with UV detection
- **Matrices tested**: Powdered botanical materials, whole root, finished dietary supplements, liquid tinctures
- **Reproducibility**: RSDR 2.68–6.65% (hydrastine); 5.66–7.68% (berberine)
- **HorRat values**: 0.77–2.12 (acceptable range)
- **Quantitative ranges**: 0.2–4% w/w in powders; ~4,000–5,000 μg/mL in liquid tinctures
- **Status**: AOAC Official First Action; recommended method for USP compliance testing

**Weber et al. 2003 single-laboratory validation**:
- Quantifies berberine, hydrastine, canadine, and palmatine
- Recovery: 92.3% (palmatine) to 101.9% (hydrastine)
- Precision: % RSD ≤ 1.6% across 10 replicates
- Differentiates goldenseal from Coptis, Oregon grape root, barberry bark, and celandine by chromatographic profile
