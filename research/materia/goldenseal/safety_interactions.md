# Goldenseal - Safety and Drug Interactions

## Safety Classification Summary

**Risk level**: HIGH — significant drug interaction risk and concerning genotoxicity/carcinogenicity data
**Regulatory status**: Commission E negative (unapproved) monograph (1991); no WHO monograph; DSHEA dietary supplement (US)
**NCCIH guidance**: Short-term use (~3 g/day) appears tolerated in healthy adults; pregnancy and neonatal use contraindicated

---

## Carcinogenicity (Rodent Bioassay)

**NTP Technical Report 562 (2010)** — 2-year carcinogenesis study
- **Design**: 2-week, 3-month, and 2-year feed studies in F344/N rats and B6C3F1 mice
- **Composition tested**: 3.89% berberine, 2.8% hydrastine, 0.17% canadine
- **Male rats**: **Clear evidence of carcinogenic activity** — increased hepatocellular adenoma and carcinoma
- **Female rats**: **Clear evidence of carcinogenic activity** — hepatocellular tumors
- **Male mice**: **Some evidence of carcinogenic activity**
- **Female mice**: No evidence at tested doses
- **Nonneoplastic lesions**: Increased hepatocyte changes and liver weight across multiple dose groups
- **Genotoxicity (Ames test)**: NOT mutagenic in Salmonella typhimurium or E. coli tester strains (with or without S9)
- **Micronucleus test**: No increase in micronucleated erythrocytes (3-month mouse study)
- **Mechanism**: Not fully characterized in 2010 report (see Chen et al. 2013)
- **Human relevance**: Not directly established; rodent carcinogenicity has not been formally extrapolated to human cancer risk
- **Source**: PMID 21372858

**Human relevance note**: The NTP "clear evidence" finding in male and female rats is among the strongest carcinogenicity findings possible in standard rodent bioassays. While direct human extrapolation is uncertain, this finding substantially elevates concern about long-term goldenseal use.

## Genotoxicity Mechanism

**Chen et al. 2013** — Topoisomerase II inhibition and DNA damage
- **Design**: In vitro mechanistic study in HepG2 human hepatoma cells
- **Institution**: National Center for Toxicological Research, U.S. FDA
- **Primary agent**: Berberine is the most potent DNA damage inducer; palmatine also contributes
- **Mechanism**: Berberine suppresses topoisomerase II activity → DNA double-strand breaks
- **Confirmation**: Topoisomerase II gene knockdown significantly reduced berberine-induced DNA damage
- **Commercial products**: Multiple commercial goldenseal products demonstrated dose-dependent DNA damage correlating with berberine concentration
- **Assays**: Comet assay and γ-H2AX expression (histone marker of double-strand breaks)
- **Conclusion**: Berberine-mediated topoisomerase II inhibition explains goldenseal hepatocarcinogenicity
- **Source**: PMID 23747414

## Contraindications

### Absolute Contraindications

**1. Pregnancy**
- Berberine crosses the placenta
- Animal data suggest teratogenicity at high doses
- Eclectic physicians noted uterotonic (labor-stimulating) effects — explicitly warned against use in pregnancy
- Berberine displaces bilirubin from albumin → fetal hyperbilirubinemia risk
- **Rating**: Absolutely contraindicated; no safe dose established

**2. Neonates and Infants**
- Berberine competitively displaces bilirubin from albumin binding sites
- In neonates with physiologic jaundice (immature hepatic bilirubin conjugation), displaced bilirubin can cross the blood-brain barrier
- **Kernicterus** (bilirubin encephalopathy): irreversible brain damage or death
- Risk exists via breast milk if nursing mother ingests goldenseal
- **Rating**: Absolutely contraindicated

**3. Breastfeeding**
- Berberine may transfer into breast milk; neonatal kernicterus risk
- **Rating**: Contraindicated

### Relative Contraindications

**4. Known hepatic disease**
- Rodent hepatocarcinogenicity data; berberine-mediated DNA damage in HepG2 cells
- Avoid in patients with hepatic impairment, hepatitis, or hepatic neoplasia
- Goldenseal's protective effects against APAP hepatotoxicity (animal data) do NOT offset carcinogenicity concerns

**5. Concurrent medications metabolized by CYP2D6 or CYP3A4**
- See Drug Interactions section
- Clinically significant inhibition documented in multiple human trials

**6. Diabetes (patients on metformin)**
- Goldenseal decreased metformin exposure GMR 0.77 (90% CI: 0.71–0.83)
- May impair glycemic control in patients relying on metformin
- Source: Nguyen et al. 2021 (PMID 33174626)

**7. Severe renal impairment**
- Clearance data insufficient; caution advised

## Drug Interactions (Documented in Human Clinical Trials)

### CYP3A4/5 Substrates — SIGNIFICANT INTERACTION

**Magnitude**: 40–62% inhibition of intestinal CYP3A activity in vivo
**Mechanism**: Both reversible competitive inhibition (hydrastine) and mechanism-based irreversible inhibition (MI complex formation)
**Onset**: Evident after 14 days supplementation
**Recovery**: Requires weeks (irreversible component requires new enzyme synthesis)

**Clinically significant CYP3A4 substrate examples**:
- Benzodiazepines (midazolam, triazolam, alprazolam) — demonstrated 43–62% AUC increase
- Calcium channel blockers (felodipine, nifedipine, amlodipine)
- Immunosuppressants (cyclosporine, tacrolimus, sirolimus)
- HIV protease inhibitors — hepatically metabolized substrates (indinavir) less affected than intestinal substrates
- Statins (simvastatin, lovastatin — NOT rosuvastatin which uses different pathway)
- Erectile dysfunction drugs (sildenafil, vardenafil)
- Opioids (fentanyl, oxycodone, codeine)
- Chemotherapy agents (docetaxel, vincristine, imatinib — see PBPK below)

**PBPK modeling prediction (Adiwidjaja et al. 2022)**:
- Bosutinib (cancer drug): ~2-fold AUC increase predicted (5th–95th %ile: 1.4–3.1× increase) — clinically significant; dose adjustment may be needed
- Imatinib: AUC ratio 1.0–1.2 — unlikely clinically important
- Source: PMID 35048143

### CYP2D6 Substrates — SIGNIFICANT INTERACTION

**Magnitude**: ~40–50% inhibition in vivo
**Evidence**: Gurley 2005 (n=12, ~40%), Gurley 2008 (n=54, ~50%); strongest effect among six herbs tested

**Clinically significant CYP2D6 substrate examples**:
- Antidepressants: Tricyclics (amitriptyline, nortriptyline), SSRIs (fluoxetine, paroxetine — also inhibitors themselves)
- Antipsychotics: Haloperidol, risperidone, aripiprazole
- Beta-blockers: Metoprolol, carvedilol, propranolol
- Opioids: Codeine (reduced conversion to morphine → reduced analgesia), tramadol, oxycodone
- Cardiovascular: Flecainide, propafenone
- Tamoxifen (reduced conversion to active endoxifen → potentially reduced efficacy in breast cancer)

### Metformin — REDUCED EXPOSURE

**Magnitude**: GMR 0.77 (90% CI: 0.71–0.83) — 23% reduction in exposure
**Mechanism (Oyanna et al. 2023, PMID 37562957)**: Goldenseal extract inhibits intestinal apical uptake transporters (OCT3 IC50 = 4.9 μM; PMAT IC50 = 13.1 μM; THTR2 IC50 = 5.8 μM) — 7.4× more potent than isolated berberine for OCT3. Mouse IV studies confirmed the effect is intestinal (not hepatic). Notably, isolated berberine and hydrastine alone showed no effect on metformin PK, indicating unidentified constituent(s) are responsible.
**Clinical concern**: May impair glycemic control in patients with type 2 diabetes relying on metformin
**Source**: Nguyen et al. 2021 (PMID 33174626); Oyanna et al. 2023 (PMID 37562957)

### CYP2C9 Substrates — POTENTIAL (IN VITRO EVIDENCE ONLY)

**Evidence**: In vitro data suggesting inhibition; no human clinical trial data
**Relevant substrates**: Warfarin (S-warfarin), NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen), phenytoin, glipizide
**Clinical status**: Cannot rule out interaction; warfarin patients should be monitored

### P-Glycoprotein Substrates — NO SIGNIFICANT INTERACTION

**Evidence**: Digoxin study (Gurley 2007, n=20) showed only 14% Cmax increase; no significant effects on AUC, clearance, or t½
**Conclusion**: Goldenseal is NOT a clinically significant P-gp modulator at typical oral doses
**Source**: PMID 17079360

### CYP2E1 Substrates — IN VITRO SIGNAL, NO HUMAN DATA

**In vitro**: IC50 = 4.32 μg/mL (Yamaura et al. 2011, rat studies)
**Animal**: Hepatoprotective vs. acetaminophen in rats via CYP2E1 inhibition
**Human**: No clinical studies; relevance uncertain
**Relevant substrates**: Acetaminophen (APAP), ethanol, halothane

### NOT Clinically Significant

- CYP1A2 substrates: No significant in vivo inhibition (Gurley 2005)
- OAT1/3 transporter substrates (furosemide probe): No effect (Nguyen 2021)
- BCRP/OATP1B1/3 substrates (rosuvastatin probe): No effect (Nguyen 2021)

## Adverse Effects

### Reported at Standard Doses
- Generally well tolerated short-term at ~3 g/day (NCCIH)
- No systematic adverse event data from controlled trials

### Dose-Dependent and High-Dose Effects
- Nausea, vomiting (irritant to GI mucosa at high doses)
- Reduced intestinal motility (may worsen constipation)
- Hypotension (berberine has vasodilatory properties in vitro; clinical significance unclear)
- Neurotoxicity concerns at very high doses (animal data); not documented at standard human doses

### Special Populations
- **Newborns exposed via mother**: Kernicterus risk (see Contraindications)
- **Pregnant women**: Uterotonic effects; potential teratogenicity
- **Individuals with G6PD deficiency**: Theoretical hemolytic risk (berberine — class effect of certain alkaloids)

## Drug Test Masking — Myth

**Status**: No scientific basis. Goldenseal does not mask THC metabolites, cocaine metabolites, or other illicit drug markers in urine immunoassays or confirmatory GC-MS testing.

Multiple controlled studies and manufacturer testing have confirmed goldenseal has no masking effect. The myth originates from fictional 1900 literature (Lloyd's *Stringtown on the Pike*). Use of goldenseal specifically to pass drug tests has no pharmacological rationale and exposes users to goldenseal's known risks without any benefit.

---

## Long-Term Safety

**Status**: Not established in humans. The 2-year NTP rodent bioassay demonstrating hepatocarcinogenicity raises substantial concerns about prolonged use. Long-term human observational data are unavailable. Traditional use patterns typically involved short-term, acute use (not daily maintenance supplementation for years), which is the predominant contemporary marketing pattern.
