# Goldenseal - Active Compounds

## Primary Alkaloids

### (-)-β-Hydrastine

**Chemical class**: Phthalide isoquinoline alkaloid
**Content in dried root**: 1.5–4.0% (w/w)
**USP draft standard**: ≥2.0% dry basis
**CAS number**: 118-08-1

**Pharmacokinetics (Gupta et al. 2015, n=11; 2.7 g dose containing 78 mg hydrastine)**:
- Cmax: 225 ± 100 ng/mL
- Tmax: 1.5 ± 0.3 hours
- t½: 4.8 ± 1.4 hours
- AUC: 6.4 ± 4.1 ng·h/mL·kg
- **Oral bioavailability**: Substantially better than berberine; rapid GI absorption
- **Metabolism**: Rapid and extensive phase I (reduction, demethylation, hydroxylation) + phase II (glucuronidation >> sulfation); multiple pharmacologically active metabolites possible
- **Source**: PMID 25609220

**Pharmacological activities (preclinical)**:
- CYP inhibition: IC50 = 25–30 μM for testosterone metabolism (CYP3A4); forms metabolic intermediate (MI) complex with CYP2C9, CYP2D6, CYP3A4 suggesting mechanism-based (irreversible) inhibition (Chatterjee & Franklin 2003)
- Uterotonic activity (historical Eclectic observation; not confirmed in modern RCTs)
- Hemostatic properties (traditional; unverified mechanism)
- Better absorbed than berberine — likely the dominant systemic alkaloid from whole-root supplements

**Distribution in plant**:
- Concentrated in rhizome/root
- Lower concentrations in leaves
- Absent from aerial parts grown separately

### Berberine

**Chemical class**: Isoquinoline alkaloid (protoberberine)
**Content in dried root**: 2.5–5.8% (w/w)
**USP draft standard**: ≥2.5% dry basis
**CAS number**: 2086-83-1

**Pharmacokinetics (Gupta et al. 2015, n=11; 2.7 g dose containing 132 mg berberine)**:
- Cmax: 1.1 ± 1.2 ng/mL (**extremely low — ~200× lower than hydrastine despite similar dose**)
- Tmax: 3.0 ± 3.3 hours
- AUC: 0.15 ± 0.09 ng·h/mL·kg
- **Oral bioavailability**: Very poor from whole-root goldenseal preparations; extensive intestinal and hepatic first-pass metabolism
- **Key implication**: The large literature on isolated berberine (from various plant sources) may not translate to goldenseal — berberine blood levels from goldenseal are negligible
- **Source**: PMID 25609220

**Pharmacological activities (preclinical/in vitro)**:
- Antimicrobial: Broad spectrum activity; MIC for S. aureus ~150 μg/mL (isolated berberine); whole-extract 38 μg/mL (synergism with flavonoids)
- Anti-inflammatory: TNF-α, IL-6 reduction in LPS-stimulated macrophages
- Hypoglycemic: Documented in multiple RCTs as isolated berberine (not goldenseal)
- AMPK activation: Mechanism for metabolic effects of isolated berberine
- Hypolipidemic: Isolated berberine has clinical evidence; not goldenseal specifically
- CYP inhibition: IC50 = 45 μM for CYP2D6 (bufuralol assay) — reversible competitive inhibition (Chatterjee & Franklin 2003)

**Safety concern — DNA damage**:
- Berberine suppresses topoisomerase II activity → DNA double-strand breaks
- Comet assay and γ-H2AX expression in HepG2 cells show dose-dependent damage
- Berberine is the most potent DNA damage inducer identified in goldenseal
- Palmatine also contributes; damage correlates with berberine concentration in commercial products
- Source: Chen et al. 2013 (PMID 23747414), FDA/NCTR research

**Bilirubin displacement**:
- Berberine displaces bilirubin from albumin binding sites
- Basis for absolute contraindication in pregnancy and neonates (kernicterus risk)
- Clinically significant at doses achievable with goldenseal use

### Canadine (Tetrahydroberberine, (-)-Corydaline)

**Chemical class**: Protoberberine alkaloid
**Content in dried root**: 0.5–1.0% (w/w)
**Status**: Secondary alkaloid; less studied than berberine or hydrastine

**Pharmacological activities (limited data)**:
- Weak sedative/muscle relaxant activity (related compounds)
- MAO inhibitory activity (weak)
- Not a primary pharmacological driver in goldenseal preparations

---

### Palmatine

**Chemical class**: Protoberberine alkaloid
**Content**: Minor alkaloid; < 0.5%
**Note**: Contributes to DNA damage per Chen et al. 2013; also found in Oregon grape, barberry

## Synergistic Flavonoids (Primarily in Leaves)

**Identification (Junio et al. 2011; Cech et al. 2012):**
- Sideroxylin
- 8-Desmethyl-sideroxylin
- 6-Desmethyl-sideroxylin

**Mechanism**: These flavonoids have **no inherent antimicrobial activity** against S. aureus alone. Their activity is entirely synergistic — they inhibit NorA multidrug resistance efflux pump in S. aureus, preventing bacterial expulsion of berberine.

**Consequence of synergy**:
- Whole goldenseal leaf extract: MIC ~38 μg/mL vs MRSA (standardized to berberine)
- Isolated berberine alone: MIC ~150 μg/mL (4× less potent)
- Explains why traditional whole-plant preparations may outperform isolated berberine
- Also explains why roots (berberine-rich) + leaves (flavonoid-rich) may be complementary

**Distribution**: Synergistic flavonoids concentrate in leaves; commercial preparations typically use root only, missing this synergism.

## CYP Inhibition Mechanisms

**Dual inhibition mechanisms identified (Chatterjee & Franklin 2003, in vitro):**

1. **Reversible competitive inhibition**
   - Berberine: IC50 = 45 μM for CYP2D6
   - Hydrastine: IC50 = 25–30 μM for CYP3A4

2. **Mechanism-based (irreversible) inhibition** via metabolic intermediate (MI) complex
   - Hydrastine forms MI complex with CYP2C9, CYP2D6, and CYP3A4
   - Time-dependent inhibition pattern confirmed
   - Irreversible component means enzyme activity recovery requires new protein synthesis (~2 weeks)
   - This partly explains the persistent inhibition observed in clinical studies after 14 days supplementation

**Primary mechanism established (Nguyen et al. 2023, PMID 37541764)**: Time-dependent inhibition of intestinal CYP3A by (−)-β-hydrastine is the predominant driver of the goldenseal-midazolam interaction. Integrated PBPK models incorporating hydrastine TDI predicted clinical observations within 1.2-fold. Acute single-dose (3 g) produced greater CYP3A inhibition (AUC GMR 1.57) than chronic dosing (1 g TID × 6 days; GMR 1.39), suggesting possible autoinduction or adaptive changes with chronic exposure.

**In vivo clinical inhibition summary:**
- CYP3A4/5: ~40–62% inhibition (intestinal predominant; mechanism is hydrastine TDI)
- CYP2D6: ~40–50% inhibition
- CYP2C9: Inhibition demonstrated in vitro; clinical significance unstudied
- CYP1A2: No significant in vivo inhibition
- CYP2E1: IC50 = 4.32 μg/mL in vitro (hepatoprotective vs APAP in rats); no clinical human data
- P-glycoprotein: Not significantly inhibited at oral doses (digoxin study)

## Standardization Markers

| Alkaloid | USP Draft Minimum | Typical Commercial Range | AOAC Method |
|---|---|---|---|
| (-)-β-Hydrastine | ≥2.0% dry weight | 0–2.93% (survey 2003) | HPLC-UV (Brown & Roman 2008) |
| Berberine | ≥2.5% dry weight | 0.82–5.86% (survey 2003) | HPLC-UV (Brown & Roman 2008) |
| Canadine | Not specified | 0.5–1.0% | HPLC-UV (Weber et al. 2003) |

**Quality issues (Edwards & Draper 2003, n=20 commercial products)**:
- Some products contained **zero detectable hydrastine**
- 7 of 17 goldenseal root products failed proposed USP standards (~41% non-compliance)
- Products labeled "goldenseal herb" contained ~4× less alkaloid than products labeled "goldenseal root"
- Tinctures: ~4,000–5,000 μg/mL per alkaloid in validated liquid samples

**Validated analytical method**: HPLC-UV per AOAC Official First Action (Brown & Roman 2008)
- Reproducibility: RSDR 2.68–6.65% (hydrastine); 5.66–7.68% (berberine)
- Differentiates goldenseal from common adulterants: Coptis (goldthread), Oregon grape root, barberry bark, celandine (Weber et al. 2003)

## Phytochemical Profile (Full)

**Root/Rhizome alkaloids** (by content):
1. Berberine (~2.5–5.8%) — dominant by mass
2. (-)-β-Hydrastine (~1.5–4.0%) — dominant by systemic exposure
3. Canadine (~0.5–1.0%)
4. Palmatine (minor)
5. Berberastine (minor)

**Leaf compounds** (distinct from root):
- Synergistic flavonoids (sideroxylin series) — absent in root
- Lower alkaloid concentrations than root
- Often omitted from commercial preparations

**NTP TR562 composition** (batch used in 2-year carcinogenesis study):
- 3.89% berberine, 2.8% hydrastine, 0.17% canadine
