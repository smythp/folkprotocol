# Goldenseal - Clinical Uses

## Overview

**Critical finding:** No RCTs have ever tested goldenseal for any therapeutic indication (respiratory infections, UTIs, digestive disorders, eye infections, wound healing). All human clinical trials involve pharmacokinetic drug interaction endpoints only. The entire therapeutic evidence base is preclinical (in vitro, animal) and traditional.

**NCCIH conclusion (2026):** "Not enough evidence to determine whether goldenseal is useful for any health condition."

**Commission E (1991):** Negative (unapproved) monograph — use in medicinal products not supported. No WHO monograph exists.

## Pharmacokinetic / Drug Interaction Studies (All Human RCTs)

### CYP3A4/5 Inhibition

**Gurley et al. 2008** — CYP3A inhibition (midazolam probe)
- **Design**: Randomized controlled crossover trial
- **Sample size**: 16 healthy adult volunteers
- **Intervention**: Goldenseal supplementation 14 days; midazolam as CYP3A probe
- **Midazolam AUC**: 107.9 → 175.3 ng·h/mL (62% increase)
- **Clearance (Cl/F/kg)**: Decreased from 1.26 → 0.81 L/h/kg
- **Half-life**: Extended 2.01 → 3.15 hours
- **Cmax**: 50.6 → 71.2 ng/mL
- **Comparison**: Kava kava had no significant effect on CYP3A
- **Conclusion**: Significant herb-drug interaction risk with CYP3A substrates
- **Source**: PMID 17495878

**Nguyen et al. 2023 (NaPDI Center)** — Mechanistic/PBPK: hydrastine TDI as primary driver
- **Design**: Integrated in vitro + in vivo + PBPK modeling study; two clinical arms (chronic and acute)
- **Sample size**: 16 healthy adults (8M/8F) in chronic arm; 8 participants in acute arm
- **Chronic intervention**: 1 g goldenseal three times daily × 6 days; 2.5 mg oral midazolam
- **Acute intervention**: Single 3 g goldenseal dose; 2.5 mg oral midazolam
- **Chronic result**: Midazolam AUC GMR 1.39 (90% CI: 1.30–1.48); Cmax GMR 1.31 (90% CI: 1.16–1.48)
- **Acute result**: Midazolam AUC GMR 1.57 (90% CI: 1.39–1.77); Cmax GMR 1.40 (90% CI: 1.12–1.74) — **greater inhibition than chronic**
- **Mechanism established**: Time-dependent (mechanism-based) inhibition of intestinal CYP3A by (−)-β-hydrastine is the predominant driver
- **PBPK performance**: Integrated model predicted observed interaction within 1.2-fold for both arms
- **Funding**: NaPDI Center (NCCIH/NIH)
- **Source**: PMID 37541764

**Nguyen et al. 2021 (NaPDI Center)** — Probe cocktail, intestinal vs. hepatic CYP3A
- **Design**: Crossover clinical study; probe cocktail (midazolam, rosuvastatin, furosemide, metformin)
- **Sample size**: 16 healthy adult volunteers
- **Funding**: NaPDI Center (NCCIH/NIH)
- **Midazolam AUC (CYP3A)**: GMR 1.43 (90% CI: 1.35–1.53) — 43% increase; no significant change in t½
- **Mechanistic finding**: Primarily intestinal CYP3A inhibition (not hepatic)
- **Rosuvastatin**: No significant effect (BCRP/OATP1B1/3 transporters not inhibited)
- **Furosemide**: No significant effect (OAT1/3 not inhibited)
- **Metformin**: Decreased exposure — GMR 0.77 (90% CI: 0.71–0.83); may impair glycemic control in diabetic patients
- **Conclusion**: Goldenseal inhibits intestinal CYP3A; transporter interactions overpredicted by in vitro models
- **Source**: PMID 33174626

**Sandhu et al. 2003** — Indinavir (CYP3A4 hepatic substrate)
- **Design**: Crossover study
- **Sample size**: 10 healthy volunteers
- **Intervention**: Goldenseal root 1140 mg twice daily (2280 mg/day) × 14 days; single 800-mg oral indinavir dose
- **Indinavir Cmax**: No significant change (11.6 vs 11.9 mg/L)
- **Oral clearance**: No significant difference (26.8 vs 23.9 mg·h/L)
- **t½, Tmax**: Both unchanged
- **Interpretation**: Confirms goldenseal inhibits intestinal (not hepatic) CYP3A4; indinavir is primarily hepatically metabolized
- **Source**: PMID 14551183

### CYP2D6 Inhibition

**Gurley et al. 2005** — Four-herb CYP phenotype comparison
- **Design**: Crossover pharmacokinetic study
- **Sample size**: 12 healthy adult volunteers (6 female, 6 male)
- **Intervention**: ~1 g goldenseal extract three times daily × 28 days; probe cocktail for CYP1A2, 2D6, 2E1, 3A4/5
- **CYP2D6 inhibition**: ~40%
- **CYP3A4/5 inhibition**: ~40%
- **CYP1A2**: No significant inhibition
- **CYP2E1**: No significant inhibition
- **Comparison**: Goldenseal had highest interaction risk among four herbs tested (vs. kava, black cohosh, valerian)
- **Source**: PMID 15900287

**Gurley et al. 2008** — CYP2D6 assessment across six herbs
- **Design**: Three separate randomized open-label studies with 30-day washout periods
- **Sample size**: 54 healthy volunteers total (18 per sub-study, 9 female each)
- **Intervention**: 14 days supplementation; debrisoquine urinary recovery ratio (DURR) as CYP2D6 phenotype marker
- **Goldenseal CYP2D6 result**: ~50% reduction in DURR (p < 0.05)
- **Key finding**: Only goldenseal produced significant CYP2D6 inhibition among all six herbs tested
- **Comparators with no effect**: Milk thistle, black cohosh, kava kava, St. John's wort, Echinacea
- **Source**: PMC2562884

### P-Glycoprotein (Non-Interaction)

**Gurley et al. 2007** — Digoxin/P-gp study
- **Design**: Randomized crossover with positive controls (rifampin, clarithromycin)
- **Sample size**: 20 healthy volunteers
- **Intervention**: 14 days goldenseal supplementation; digoxin pharmacokinetics assessed
- **Goldenseal effect**: Only 14% increase in Cmax — the sole statistically significant finding; no significant effects on AUC, CL/F, or t½
- **Positive controls confirmed**: Rifampin reduced digoxin AUC (P-gp induction confirmed); clarithromycin increased digoxin (P-gp inhibition confirmed)
- **Conclusion**: Goldenseal is NOT a potent P-glycoprotein modulator at oral doses tested
- **Source**: PMID 17079360

## Absence of Therapeutic Efficacy Trials

The following folk indications have **never been evaluated in a human RCT**:

| Indication | Evidence Status |
|---|---|
| Upper respiratory infection (treatment) | No human trials |
| UTI | No human trials |
| Digestive disorders (dyspepsia, gastritis) | No human trials |
| Eye infections (conjunctivitis) | No human trials |
| Wound healing / topical antiseptic | No human trials |
| Traveler's diarrhea | No human trials |
| Sinusitis | No human trials |

### Preclinical Activity (No Clinical Translation Yet)
- **Antimicrobial**: Berberine in vitro antibacterial activity documented; goldenseal leaf extract vs. MRSA (MIC 38 μg/mL standardized to berberine); synergistic with flavonoid efflux-pump inhibitors (sideroxylin). No clinical trials.
- **Anti-inflammatory**: Reduced TNF-α, IL-6, IL-10, IL-12 in LPS-stimulated macrophages (in vitro). No clinical trials.
- **Hypoglycemic**: Berberine (as isolated alkaloid from multiple sources) has RCT evidence for T2DM — not from goldenseal specifically.
- **Drug test masking**: No scientific basis; goldenseal does not mask illicit drugs in urinalysis (persistent myth, definitively disproven).

## Dosing in Clinical Studies

| Study | Dose | Duration | Endpoint |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gurley 2005 | ~1 g extract TID (3 g/day) | 28 days | CYP phenotypes |
| Gurley 2007 | Not specified | 14 days | Digoxin PK |
| Gurley 2008 (CYP3A) | Not specified | 14 days | Midazolam PK |
| Gurley 2008 (CYP2D6) | Not specified | 14 days | DURR/CYP2D6 |
| Sandhu 2003 | 1140 mg twice daily (2280 mg/day) | 14 days | Indinavir PK |
| Gupta 2015 | 2.7 g single dose (78 mg hydrastine, 132 mg berberine) | Single dose | Hydrastine PK |
| Nguyen 2021 | Extract (acute and 14-day chronic regimens) | Acute and 14 days | Probe cocktail |

**Short-term tolerance**: Doses of ~3 g/day for short periods appear well tolerated in healthy adults (NCCIH). No systematic adverse event data from controlled trials.
