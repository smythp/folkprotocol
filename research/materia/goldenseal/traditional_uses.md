# Goldenseal - Traditional Uses

## Overview

Goldenseal (*Hydrastis canadensis* L.) is a North American woodland perennial endemic to the deciduous forests of the eastern United States and Canada. It is one of the most commercially important and heavily harvested North American medicinal plants, now endangered in much of its native range due to overharvesting.

---

## Native American Traditions (Primary Historical Source)

### Cherokee
- **Mucosal conditions**: Decoctions of root used for mouth sores, sore throats, and inflamed mucous membranes
- **Eye infections**: Root tea applied topically for conjunctivitis and eye inflammation — the origin of the folk name "eye root"
- **Wound healing**: Crushed root applied topically as antiseptic wound dressing
- **Digestive complaints**: Root preparations for nausea, dyspepsia, and liver complaints
- **Dye**: Yellow rhizome dye for baskets, clothing, and as war paint — origin of the name "Indian dye" and "yellow root"
- **Century**: Pre-contact practices extending through 18th–19th century documentation

### Iroquois (Haudenosaunee)
- **General tonic**: Root preparations as a bitter digestive tonic and liver support
- **Skin conditions**: Topical applications for skin eruptions and infections

### Crow Nation
- **Eye wash**: Berberine-containing root preparations used as eye washes; documented in 19th-century ethnobotanical records

### Other Nations (Delaware, Micmac, Ojibwe)
- Root used for general infections, digestive complaints, and fever reduction
- Yellow dye applications documented across multiple cultures

**Key ethnobotanical source**: Moerman's Native American Ethnobotany Database and 19th-century field reports from explorers and physicians working with Native populations.

## Eclectic Medicine (Late 19th – Early 20th Century)

The Eclectic Physicians of North America elevated goldenseal to one of their primary botanical medicines. This period (1850–1920) represents the most detailed historical documentation of goldenseal therapeutic use.

### Felter & Lloyd, *King's American Dispensatory* (1898)
The definitive Eclectic reference. Primary indications:
- **Mucous membrane inflammation**: Considered a specific for catarrhal states of all mucous surfaces — respiratory, GI, urinary, and ophthalmic
- **Gastric catarrh**: Dyspepsia, gastritis, constipation — regarded as specific for atonic states of the stomach
- **Hepatic congestion**: Used as a "hepatic tonic" for sluggish liver function and biliary insufficiency
- **Topical antiseptic**: Wound washes, vaginal douches, eye washes — especially as berberine sulfate preparations
- **Dose**: 1–4 grains (60–240 mg) dried extract; fluid extract 5–20 minims; tincture 1–2 drachms
- **Caution noted**: "Should not be given in full doses during pregnancy"

### John Uri Lloyd (1849–1936)
- Developed and popularized "Hydrastis canadensis fluid extract" as a commercial pharmaceutical standard
- Emphasized goldenseal as a substitute for expensive quinine in malarial regions
- Documented its use in gonorrhea and urinary tract infections (topical irrigation)

### Specific Eclectic Indications
1. **Respiratory**: Chronic catarrh, sinusitis, post-nasal drip, laryngitis — "the mucosal tonic"
2. **Digestive**: Dyspepsia, gastric ulcers (protective mucilage effect postulated), intestinal catarrh
3. **Genitourinary**: UTIs, gonorrhea (local irrigation), leucorrhea
4. **Ophthalmic**: Conjunctivitis — berberine sulfate eye drops widely used pre-antibiotics
5. **Dermatological**: Eczema, impetigo, infected wounds
6. **Uterine**: Used to stimulate uterine contractions (labor induction — now a contraindication)

## American Homeopathic Tradition

- **Preparation**: Hydrastis 3X to 30C for catarrhal conditions
- **Key symptom picture**: Thick, ropy yellow-green mucus from any mucous membrane
- **Indicated conditions**: Sinusitis, post-nasal drip, gonorrhea, ulcers
- **Liver affinity**: Used for liver congestion with gastric symptoms
- **Constitutional use**: Cachectic individuals, debilitated states

## Early Allopathic/Pharmacopoeia Recognition

### United States Pharmacopeia
- Listed in the USP from **1831 to 1936**
- Removed when plant demand outstripped sustainable supply
- USP entry documented uses for: mucosal inflammation, gastric catarrh, uterine hemorrhage

### National Formulary
- Listed in the NF from **1936 to 1960**
- Applications similar to USP; used in compound preparations

### Early Pharmaceutical Products (Late 19th – Early 20th Century)
- Berberine sulfate: pharmaceutical-grade eye drops (ophthalmic preparations)
- Hydrastis fluid extract: standardized by alkaloid content
- Compound goldenseal preparations: combinations with other bitters for digestive formulas

## Persistent Folk Uses (20th Century – Present)

### Urban Myth: Drug Test Masking
- **Widely circulated belief** (1980s–present): Goldenseal tea will mask THC or other drugs in urine drug tests
- **Origin**: Popularized in John Uri Lloyd's 1900 novel *Stringtown on the Pike* where a character discusses goldenseal and drug testing
- **Scientific status**: **No scientific basis**. Multiple studies confirm goldenseal does not mask illicit drug metabolites in urinalysis (Mahady 2001, NCCIH). The myth has led to massive commercial goldenseal consumption with no pharmacological basis.

### Contemporary Folk Application Patterns
1. **Immune support / cold prevention**: Combined with echinacea in widely marketed "immune formulas" — despite zero clinical evidence
2. **Traveler's diarrhea**: Used preemptively or acutely for GI infections — berberine has in vitro activity against enteropathogens; no goldenseal clinical trials exist
3. **Sinusitis and congestion**: Neti pot rinses with goldenseal tea
4. **Topical wound care**: Goldenseal powder applied directly to wounds or made into salve

## Traditional Preparation Methods

| Preparation | Traditional Method | Strength/Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Root decoction | Simmer 1–2 tsp dried root in 2 cups water 15–20 min | Extract ratio approximately 1:20 |
| Cold infusion | Macerate root in cool water overnight | Gentler; preserves thermolabile compounds |
| Tincture (traditional) | 1:10 in 60% ethanol; macerate 2–4 weeks | Standard Eclectic preparation |
| Fluid extract | 1:1 in 60% ethanol (pharmacopeial standard) | High potency; Eclectic formulas |
| Poultice | Fresh or dried root ground and applied topically | Direct antimicrobial application |
| Eye wash | 1/4 tsp dried root in 1 cup boiled, cooled water; strain carefully | Traditional but risk of contamination |
| Powder capsule | Dried ground root | Most common contemporary form |

## Conservation Note

Goldenseal is listed on **CITES Appendix II** (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species), requiring documentation of sustainable harvest. Wild-harvested goldenseal is preferred by consumers but is ecologically threatened. The United Plant Savers lists goldenseal as an "at-risk" species. Most commercial goldenseal is now cultivated, though quality standards are poorly enforced.
