# Tone Critique: peppermint.md vs ashwagandha.md Gold Standard

## 1. OVERALL ASSESSMENT

**Verdict: Clinical-leaning. Better than ginger.md, worse than ashwagandha.md.**

The practical sections ("How To Try It," "What To Track," "The Bottom Line") are genuinely good—imperative commands, "you" framing, real stakes. The "Cooling Sensation" section is excellent narrative.

The problem is the lead: "What The Evidence Says" opens with dense RR/MD notation and a data-listing style that sets a clinical tone the rest of the page struggles to escape. "Who This Is/Isn't For" has good structure but lacks ashwagandha's humanizing "What they report" testimonials. The emotional stakes—why IBS matters, what headache relief means in daily life—are mostly absent.

The page tells you what the evidence shows. Ashwagandha tells you what it's like to use it.

## 2. SPECIFIC PASSAGES TO FIX

---

### PASSAGE 1

**Section:** What The Evidence Says — IBS bullet (line ~158-159)

**Current text:**
> "A 2019 meta-analysis of 12 RCTs (835 participants) found 58% of patients responded to peppermint oil vs 29% on placebo—a number needed to treat of 3. A 2014 meta-analysis of 9 RCTs (726 participants) found global symptom improvement RR 2.23. Eight of twelve placebo-controlled studies in a major dosing review showed statistically significant benefit."

**Problem:** Third-person clinical voice, data-dump pattern, "patients responded" not "you get relief." The NNT = 3 is actually good plain language but buried in RR notation.

**Suggested direction:** Lead with "that's 2-in-3 people getting real relief" or frame around what 58% vs 29% means in practical terms. Cut the RR 2.23—it adds nothing after the plain-language version. Use "If 10 people with IBS try peppermint, about 6 get meaningful relief. On placebo, it's about 3." Or pull a user voice in ("People describe finally having predictable days again").

---

### PASSAGE 2

**Section:** What The Evidence Says — IBS abdominal pain bullet (line ~159)

**Current text:**
> "IBS abdominal pain: Consistent effect across three independent meta-analyses (9-12 RCTs each), with risk ratios of 1.78-2.14 for meaningful pain reduction."

**Problem:** "risk ratios of 1.78-2.14" is pure statistical notation with no reader-facing meaning.

**Suggested direction:** Replace with plain interpretation. "Your odds of significant pain reduction are roughly twice what they'd be on placebo—replicated across three separate independent analyses. That level of consistency is rare in herbal medicine."

---

### PASSAGE 3

**Section:** What The Evidence Says — Nausea bullet (line ~167)

**Current text:**
> "A 2025 meta-analysis of 19 RCTs found inhaled peppermint oil reduced PONV (MD -0.60), pregnancy nausea (MD -0.51 to -0.68), and chemotherapy-induced nausea (MD -2.23 to -2.41 at 48-72 hours). Effects were statistically significant but often below clinically meaningful thresholds."

**Problem:** MD notation throughout. "MD -2.23 to -2.41 at 48-72 hours" is meaningless without knowing the scale. This is a pure data-dump.

**Suggested direction:** The last sentence is actually the most important framing—lead with it. "Peppermint inhalation reduces nausea, but don't expect a dramatic effect—the benefit is real but modest, particularly helpful as a complement to other treatments rather than a standalone fix." Then a sentence on what "modest" looks like in practice.

---

### PASSAGE 4

**Section:** What The Evidence Says — Cognitive function (line ~168)

**Current text:**
> "A 24-person double-blind crossover study found 100µL encapsulated peppermint oil improved performance on demanding attention tasks (Rapid Visual Information Processing) and attenuated mental fatigue at 1-3 hours post-dose—with a plausible mechanism: menthol inhibits acetylcholinesterase and modulates acetylcholine receptors."

**Problem:** "100µL" is a lab measurement that readers can't translate. "Rapid Visual Information Processing" is opaque jargon. The mechanism sentence is a gear-shift into academic voice.

**Suggested direction:** "One study found peppermint oil capsules helped people sustain attention during demanding tasks and reduced mental fatigue—effects that peaked around 1-3 hours after taking it. The mechanism may have to do with how menthol affects neurotransmitter activity." Pull the µL measurement; readers can't act on it.

---

### PASSAGE 5

**Section:** How To Try It — Functional dyspepsia paragraph (line ~205)

**Current text:**
> "For functional dyspepsia (upper GI symptoms, post-meal discomfort, bloating with nausea), consider a peppermint + caraway oil combination (Menthacarin-type products). A meta-analysis of 249 patients found pain reduction SMD 0.80 and global improvement RR 2.65—stronger evidence than peppermint alone for this indication."

**Problem:** "SMD 0.80 and global improvement RR 2.65" in a practical How To Try section. This is where readers are deciding to buy something.

**Suggested direction:** "For upper GI symptoms—the post-meal bloating, nausea, and discomfort pattern—a peppermint + caraway combination actually has stronger evidence than peppermint alone. Look for Menthacarin-type products. The trial data here is genuinely impressive."

---

### PASSAGE 6

**Section:** Who This Is/Isn't For — IBS section (lines ~263-264)

**Current text:**
> "IBS-D or IBS-mixed (diarrhea-predominant or alternating): Strongest evidence here. Peppermint's antispasmodic effect on colonic smooth muscle, visceral sensitivity modulation via TRP channels, and motility effects address core IBS pathophysiology. 58% response vs 29% placebo across multiple meta-analyses is clinically meaningful. One review concluded it may be the 'drug of first choice' for non-serious IBS presentations."

**Problem:** Missing "What they report" human voice. Mechanism paragraph is appropriate background but needs a human anchor. No "you" framing.

**Suggested direction:** Add a "What people notice" line: "What they report: unpredictable bowel days becoming manageable, post-meal cramping fading, the constant background anxiety of IBS easing." This is ashwagandha's pattern—claim, then human testimony.

---

### PASSAGE 7

**Section:** Who This Is/Isn't For — all "Clear benefit likely" entries

**Problem:** None of the "clear benefit" entries have a "What they report" equivalent. Ashwagandha's power comes from this pattern:
> "Strong Responders (likely to benefit): ... What they report: 'Stress doesn't hit me the same way,' deeper sleep, sustained energy, mental clarity, profound stress relief."

Peppermint gives clinical case but no human voice for any of the benefit categories.

**Suggested direction:** Add short "What people notice:" lines to each category: IBS ("unpredictable days becoming predictable"), headache ("the familiar tension headache dissipating without reaching for ibuprofen"), PONV ("nausea dialing back within minutes of inhaling").

---

### PASSAGE 8

**Section:** What The Evidence Says — Good evidence bullet, antispasmodic for endoscopy (line ~164)

**Current text:**
> "A meta-analysis of 14 RCTs (1,002 patients) found L-menthol suppressed peristalsis in 55.9% of cases (OR 3.88) with adverse events comparable to placebo—a safer profile than traditional anticholinergic antispasmodics."

**Problem:** "OR 3.88" is technical notation. "1,002 patients" is fine. The finding (safer than anticholinergics) is genuinely meaningful but the stat notation buries it.

**Suggested direction:** Drop "OR 3.88"—the 55.9% is sufficient and plain. Let the safety comparison do the work: "safer than the standard drug class" is the headline.

## 4. VOICE AUDIT

**"You/your" instances in body text (below frontmatter):**
Approximately 10-12 instances, mostly concentrated in:
- "How To Try It" section (good)
- "What To Track" section (good)
- "Who This Is/Isn't For" (some)
- "The Cooling Sensation" (one instance)
- "The Bottom Line" (good)

The "What The Evidence Says" section has **zero** "you" instances. This is the biggest structural issue.

**"Participants/patients" instances:**
- Line ~158: "patients responded"
- Line ~163: "35 patients, 118 migraine attacks"
- Line ~164: "1,002 patients"
- Line ~167: (implied patients in study descriptions)
- Line ~168: "144 volunteers"
- Line ~267: "30 children"

That's approximately 6 instances of third-person study-subject language. Not egregious, but the evidence section reads as written for clinicians.

**Questions to reader:** Near zero. The page makes statements at the reader rather than questions to the reader. Ashwagandha has implicit questions embedded ("Is this working for you?" is implied by the tracking framework; "Are you a strong responder or non-responder?" drives the whole Who This Is For section).

**Verdict:** Frequency of "you" is acceptable in total, but it's concentrated in the wrong places. The evidence section—where readers decide whether to try something—is clinical. The practical sections are good.

## 5. SECTIONS NEEDING NARRATIVE

### "What The Evidence Says" (highest priority)

Currently: Study-study-study bullet list. No reader in the story. No "what this means for you." No emotional stakes.

What's missing: Why does this evidence matter to someone standing in their kitchen with IBS symptoms? What does "58% response" feel like from the inside? The ashwagandha equivalent tells us: "Life-changing stress resilience, deep sleep, sustained energy." The peppermint version needs: something like "for IBS, this is the difference between planning your life around bathroom access and not."

### "Who This Is/Isn't For" — IBS section

Currently: Clinical indication + mechanism + stat. No user testimony.

What's missing: The "What they report:" pattern from ashwagandha. Even one sentence of voice.

### Nausea section (throughout)

Currently: Meta-analysis data delivery. No "here's what to expect" framing.

What's missing: "Peppermint inhalation is a 5-minute trial with nothing to lose. Keep a bottle in your bag. When nausea hits—whether from a bumpy car ride, morning sickness, or post-surgery recovery room—open the bottle and breathe normally. About half the time, the edge comes off within 10 minutes."

### Opening of "What The Evidence Says"

Currently starts with: "Peppermint is unusual among herbs in having multiple independent meta-analyses for a single condition—IBS—all pointing the same direction."

This is actually good framing—preserve it. The problem is what follows immediately pivots to study listings. The opening sentence creates an expectation of "and here's what that means for you" that the next paragraph doesn't deliver.
