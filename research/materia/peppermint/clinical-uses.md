# Peppermint (Mentha piperita) - Clinical Uses

## Evidence-Based Clinical Applications

### Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

#### Global Symptom Improvement

**Ingrosso 2022 Meta-Analysis** (10 RCTs, 1,030 participants)
- Effect size: RR 0.65 (95% CI 0.43-0.98) for symptom improvement
- Number needed to treat (NNT): 4 patients for global symptom improvement
- Quality: Very low quality of evidence
- Reference: PMID 35942669

**Alammar 2019 Meta-Analysis** (12 RCTs, 835 participants)
- Effect size: RR 2.39 (95% CI 1.93-2.97, p<0.00001) for global symptom improvement
- Study breakdown: 253 treatment vs 254 placebo participants across 7 studies
- Number needed to treat: 3 patients for persistent symptoms
- Treatment response rate: 58% (peppermint oil) vs 29% (placebo)
- Placebo response range: 10-52% across studies
- Reference: PMID 30654773

**Khanna 2014 Meta-Analysis** (9 RCTs, 726 participants)
- Effect size: RR 2.23 (95% CI 1.78-2.81) for global IBS symptom improvement
- Study population: 392 patients across 5 studies
- Minimum treatment duration: 2 weeks
- Formulation: Enteric-coated peppermint oil capsules
- Adverse events: Mild and transient; heartburn most common
- Conclusion: Safe and effective short-term treatment for IBS
- Reference: PMID 24100754

#### Abdominal Pain Relief

**Ingrosso 2022 Meta-Analysis**
- Effect size: RR 0.76 (95% CI 0.62-0.93) for abdominal pain improvement
- Number needed to treat: 7 patients for pain relief
- Reference: PMID 35942669

**Alammar 2019 Meta-Analysis**
- Effect size: RR 1.78 (95% CI 1.43-2.20, p<0.00001) for abdominal pain
- Study population: 278 patients per group across 6 studies
- Number needed to treat: 4 patients for pain improvement
- Reference: PMID 30654773

**Khanna 2014 Meta-Analysis**
- Effect size: RR 2.14 (95% CI 1.64-2.79) for abdominal pain improvement
- Study population: 357 patients across 5 studies
- Reference: PMID 24100754

#### Phase III Clinical Trial (Weerts 2020)

**Study Design**: Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, multicenter trial
- Total participants: 189 enrolled, 178 completed
- Treatment duration: 8 weeks
- Formulations tested: Two different release mechanisms

**Dosing Protocols**:
1. Small-intestinal-release: 182 mg peppermint oil per capsule
2. Ileocolonic-release: 182 mg peppermint oil per capsule
3. Placebo

**Primary Outcome Results**:
- Small-intestinal group: 46.8% abdominal pain response (29/62 patients; P=0.170 vs placebo)
- Ileocolonic group: 41.3% response (26/63 patients; P=0.385 vs placebo)
- Placebo: 34.4% response (22/64 patients)
- Neither formulation met primary endpoints using regulatory criteria
- Secondary outcomes showed modest improvements

**Adverse Events**: More common in both peppermint oil groups (P<0.005), but described as mild
- Reference: PMID 31470006

#### Novel Delivery System Trial (Cash 2016)

**Study Design**: Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled
- Total participants: 72 patients
- Peppermint group: 35 patients
- Placebo group: 37 patients
- Demographics: Mean age 40.7 years, 75% female
- Treatment duration: 4 weeks

**Formulation**: Triple-coated microsphere
- Average diameter: <1.5 mm
- Core: Microcrystalline cellulose with enteric polymer coating
- Delivery mechanism: Sustained release in small intestine

**Dosing**: 3 times daily

**Efficacy Results**:
- Total IBS Symptom Score (TISS) reduction: 40% (peppermint) vs 24.3% (placebo)
- At 24 hours: 19.6% reduction (peppermint) vs 10.3% (placebo)
- Greater improvement in individual GI symptoms vs placebo
- Greater improvement in severe/unbearable symptoms vs placebo

**Safety**: Well tolerated with few adverse events
- Reference: PMID 26319955

#### Recommended IBS Dosing (Grigoleit 2005 Review)

**Standard Protocol**: 180-200 mg enteric-coated peppermint oil
- Frequency: 1-2 capsules three times daily (TID)
- Treatment duration: 24 weeks maximum studied
- Efficacy: 8 of 12 placebo-controlled studies showed statistically significant benefits

**Clinical Recommendation**: May be drug of first choice in IBS patients with non-serious constipation or diarrhea
- Reference: PMID 16121521

### Functional Dyspepsia

**Menthacarin Combination (Madisch 2023)**

**Study Design**: Systematic review and meta-analysis
- Total trials: 12 studies reviewed, 5 included in meta-analysis
- Total participants: 580 across all studies
- Meta-analysis participants: 249 patients

**Formulation**: Menthacarin (proprietary peppermint oil and caraway oil combination)

**Efficacy Results**:
- Pain reduction: Standardized mean difference 0.80 (95% CI 0.39-1.21)
- Global improvement: RR 2.65 (95% CI 1.81-3.87) on Clinical Global Impression Scale
- Effective for functional dyspepsia
- Promising for IBS symptoms

**Safety**: Effective and safe
- Reference: PMID 36502789

### Pediatric Functional Abdominal Pain

**Shulman 2022 Pharmacokinetic Study**

**Study Design**: Randomized controlled trial
- Total participants: 30 children (29 with evaluable PK data)
- Age range: 7-12 years
- Condition: Functional abdominal pain
- Formulation: Enteric-coated peppermint oil

**Dosing Protocols**:
1. 180 mg (single dose)
2. 360 mg (180 mg twice daily)
3. 540 mg (180 mg three times daily)

**Pharmacokinetic Findings**:
- Apparent dose-proportionality for systemic menthol exposure
- Elimination half-lives: 2.1 hours, 3.5 hours, and 4.6 hours across dose groups
- Weight-adjusted dosing explained 59% of variability in menthol exposure

**Gut Motility Effects**:
- Colonic transit time inversely related to AUClast (P=0.003)
- Faster colonic transit at higher menthol exposures
- Stomach, small bowel, and whole gut contractility positively correlated with menthol exposure

**Safety**: No specific adverse events documented in pediatric population
- Reference: PMID 34528282

### Antispasmodic Use During Colonoscopy/Endoscopy

**L-Menthol for Endoscopy (Gergő 2025)**

**Study Design**: Systematic review and meta-analysis
- Total trials: 14 RCTs
- Total participants: 1,002 patients

**Formulation**: L-menthol (derived from peppermint oil)
**Mechanism**: Calcium channel blockade promotes GI smooth muscle relaxation

**Efficacy Results**:
- Peristalsis suppression: 55.9% success rate (OR 3.88)
- Improved ease of examination: OR 2.53
- No significant impact on adenoma detection rates: OR 1.06
- No prolonged withdrawal time: 3.24 seconds difference (not clinically significant)

**Adverse Events**: Low and comparable to placebo (OR 0.97)
**Conclusion**: Safer alternative to traditional antispasmodics
- Reference: PMID 40566063

**Aziz 2020 Colonoscopy Meta-Analysis**

**Study Design**: Systematic review and meta-analysis
- Total trials: 6 studies

**Efficacy Results**:
- Reduced spasticity: Risk difference -0.39 (p=0.02)
- Severe spasticity decreased: Risk difference -0.15 (p=0.04)
- Peristalsis lowered during examination: p≤0.001
- Improved adenoma detection rate: RR 1.31 (p=0.01) in 2 studies

**Limitations**: Significant heterogeneity across studies
**Recommendations**: Larger studies with standardized dosing protocols needed
- Reference: PMID 31994371

### Other Clinical Applications (Chumpitazi 2018 Review)

**Mechanisms of Action**:
- Smooth muscle relaxation via calcium channel blockade or direct enteric nervous system effects
- Visceral sensitivity modulation through transient receptor potential cation channels
- Anti-microbial activity
- Anti-inflammatory activity
- Psychosocial distress modulation

**Gastrointestinal Effects**: Affects entire digestive tract
- Esophageal motility
- Gastric emptying
- Small bowel transit
- Gallbladder function
- Colonic motility

**Clinical Efficacy Evidence For**:
- Irritable bowel syndrome
- Functional dyspepsia
- Childhood functional abdominal pain
- Post-operative nausea

**Safety Profile**: Few adverse effects reported in peppermint oil trials
**Conclusion**: Good safety profile with effective treatment for multiple GI disorders
- Reference: PMID 29372567

## Standard Dosing Summary

### Adults - IBS Treatment
- **Standard dose**: 180-200 mg enteric-coated peppermint oil
- **Frequency**: 1-2 capsules three times daily (TID)
- **Treatment duration**: Typically 2-24 weeks
- **Single dose range**: 0.1-0.24 ml per subject in clinical studies
- **Novel formulations**: Triple-coated microsphere, 3 times daily for 4 weeks

### Pediatric Dosing (Ages 7-12)
- **Low dose**: 180 mg daily (single dose or divided)
- **Medium dose**: 360 mg daily (180 mg twice daily)
- **High dose**: 540 mg daily (180 mg three times daily)
- **Formulation**: Enteric-coated peppermint oil
- **Safety**: Well-tolerated in clinical trials

### Combination Formulations
- **Menthacarin**: Proprietary peppermint oil and caraway oil combination
- **Indication**: Functional dyspepsia and IBS
- **Dosing**: As per manufacturer specifications (not specified in reviewed studies)

## Adverse Event Rates

### Alammar 2019 Meta-Analysis
- Peppermint oil group: 9.3% (32/344 patients)
- Placebo group: 6.1% (20/327 patients)
- No statistically significant difference (p=0.16)
- Conclusion: Similar adverse event rates to placebo

### Ingrosso 2022 Meta-Analysis
- Risk ratio of any adverse event: 1.57 (95% CI 1.04-2.37)
- Adverse events more frequent with peppermint oil than placebo
- Nature of events: Generally mild and transient

### Common Adverse Effects (Grigoleit 2005)
- **Peppermint oil specific**: Heartburn, anal/perianal burning or discomfort
- **Anticholinergic comparator effects**: Dry mouth, blurred vision
- **Characterization**: Generally mild and transient

### Cash 2016 Novel Delivery System
- Well tolerated with few adverse events
- No serious adverse events reported

### Weerts 2020 Phase III Trial
- More common in both peppermint oil groups (P<0.005)
- Described as mild
- Did not lead to significant discontinuation

## Clinical Recommendations

### When to Use Peppermint Oil
1. **IBS with non-serious constipation or diarrhea** (Grigoleit 2005)
   - May be drug of first choice
   - Comparable effectiveness to smooth muscle relaxants

2. **Functional dyspepsia** (Madisch 2023)
   - Consider combination formulations with caraway oil

3. **Pediatric functional abdominal pain** (Shulman 2022, Chumpitazi 2018)
   - Weight-adjusted dosing recommended
   - Well-tolerated in ages 7-12

4. **Antispasmodic for endoscopy/colonoscopy** (Gergő 2025, Aziz 2020)
   - L-menthol preferred for procedural use
   - Safer alternative to traditional antispasmodics

### Formulation Considerations
- **Enteric-coated formulations strongly preferred** for GI conditions
- Extended release over 10-12 hours in small intestine
- pH-triggered coating targets colon as primary site of action
- Novel delivery systems (triple-coated microspheres) show promise

### Treatment Duration
- **Short-term**: 2-4 weeks minimum for symptom improvement
- **Standard**: 8 weeks for full therapeutic trial
- **Extended**: Up to 24 weeks studied safely

### Efficacy Expectations
- **NNT for global IBS symptoms**: 3-4 patients
- **NNT for abdominal pain**: 4-7 patients
- **Average response rate**: 58% vs 29% placebo
- **Placebo response**: Highly variable (10-52% across studies)

## Research Quality and Gaps

### Evidence Quality (Ingrosso 2022)
- Overall quality: Very low quality of evidence
- Recommendation: Better-designed trials needed

### Standardization Issues
- Dosing protocols vary across studies
- Formulation differences complicate comparisons
- Release mechanisms (small intestinal vs ileocolonic) show different efficacy profiles

### Research Needs
- Larger, well-designed RCTs
- Standardized dosing protocols
- Long-term safety data (>24 weeks)
- Optimal formulation and delivery system determination
- Head-to-head comparisons with standard treatments
- Pediatric safety and efficacy data expansion
