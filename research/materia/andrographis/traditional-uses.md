# Andrographis - Traditional Uses

## Botanical Identity

**Scientific name**: Andrographis paniculata (Burm.f.) Wall. ex Nees
**Family**: Acanthaceae
**Common names**:
- Kalmegh (Indian tradition)
- King of Bitters (English)
- Chuan Xin Lian (Traditional Chinese Medicine)

## WHO Monograph (2002)

**Source**: World Health Organization monographs on selected medicinal plants, Volume 2
**Publication**: Geneva, Switzerland, 2002
**Approval**: 120+ experts in 50+ countries
**ISBN**: 9789241545372

### Definition
Dried aerial parts of Andrographis paniculata (Burm. f.) Nees (Acanthaceae)

### Traditional Uses Supported by Clinical Data

1. **Upper Respiratory Infections**:
   - Prophylaxis and symptomatic treatment of common cold
   - Uncomplicated sinusitis
   - Bronchitis
   - Pharyngotonsillitis

2. **Lower Urinary Tract Infections**

3. **Acute Diarrhea**

### Traditional Dosing from WHO Monograph

**For Pyrexia**:
- 3g crude drug decoction, twice daily

**For Common Cold**:
- 1.5-3.0g powdered crude drug, three times daily
- Administration: After meals and at bedtime

**For Diarrhea**:
- 3-9g crude drug decoction as single dose as needed
- Alternative: Two 500mg tablets, four times daily after meals

## Traditional Medicine Systems

### Asian Traditional Medicine
**Primary origins**: Andrographis paniculata is primarily an Asian medicinal plant, with established use in:
- **Ayurvedic medicine** (Indian tradition) - Known as Kalmegh
- **Traditional Chinese Medicine** (TCM) - Known as Chuan Xin Lian

### European Traditional Medicine
**Commission E Status**: No monograph published by German Commission E
**Reason**: Commission E primarily assessed herbs used in German phytotherapy. Andrographis paniculata was not traditionally used in German herbal medicine, being instead an Asian medicinal plant.

## Nordic Traditional Use

**Melchior 1997 Study Note**:
- Standardized Andrographis paniculata extract (Kanjang®) had been used in Nordic countries for over 12 years prior to 1997 publication
- Used for common cold and sinusitis
- Positive results reported throughout this period

## Traditional Preparation Methods

Based on WHO monograph and historical use:

### Decoction
- **Method**: Boiling crude dried herb in water
- **Typical dose**: 3-9g crude drug
- **Applications**: Pyrexia, diarrhea

### Powdered Herb
- **Method**: Dried aerial parts ground to powder
- **Typical dose**: 1.5-3.0g
- **Frequency**: Three times daily
- **Timing**: After meals and at bedtime
- **Applications**: Common cold, upper respiratory infections

### Traditional Tablet Form
- **Composition**: Compressed powdered herb or crude extract
- **Typical strength**: 500mg per tablet
- **Dosing**: 2 tablets, four times daily after meals
- **Applications**: Diarrhea, digestive complaints

## Traditional Indications Summary

**Primary traditional uses**:
1. Fever reduction (pyrexia)
2. Respiratory infections (common cold, sinusitis, bronchitis, pharyngitis)
3. Urinary tract infections
4. Acute diarrhea and digestive disorders

**Common name significance**: "King of Bitters" reflects the herb's intensely bitter taste, traditionally associated with fever-reducing and digestive properties in both Ayurvedic and TCM traditions.

## Historical Context

- **WHO recognition**: Included in WHO Selected Medicinal Plants Volume 2 (2002), indicating global recognition of traditional use and clinical evidence
- **Pharmacopoeial status**: Official in United States Pharmacopeia (USP) Dietary Supplements Compendium
- **International use**: Crossed from Asian traditional medicine into Western herbal medicine practice by late 20th century, particularly in Nordic countries
- **Modern adoption**: Transitioned from crude traditional preparations to standardized extracts while maintaining traditional indication profile (primarily respiratory infections)

## Traditional vs. Modern Use

**Continuity**: Modern clinical research has focused predominantly on the same conditions emphasized in traditional use - particularly upper respiratory tract infections and fever.

**Evolution**: While traditional use relied on crude herb decoctions and powders, modern practice increasingly employs standardized extracts (typically to 30% andrographolides) with more precise dosing.

**Safety note**: Traditional use forms (oral herbal preparations) have maintained favorable safety profiles, while modern injectable derivative forms (andrographolide sulfonate, potassium dehydroandrographolide succinate) have shown serious adverse events not associated with traditional preparations.
