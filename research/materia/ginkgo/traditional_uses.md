## CONTRAST: TRADITIONAL VS MODERN USE

### Key Differences

**Traditional Use (Hypothetical - Not in Current Bibliography):**
- Whole plant material (leaves, seeds)
- Non-standardized preparations (decoctions, teas, powders)
- Variety of indications based on traditional systems (TCM patterns, etc.)
- Dosing based on traditional measures (grams of herb, cups of tea)
- Limited documentation of efficacy and safety

**Modern Use (Well-Documented in Current Bibliography):**
- Standardized extracts (22-27% flavonoids, 5.4-6.6% terpene lactones, <5 ppm ginkgolic acids)
- Pharmaceutical-grade tablets/capsules
- Evidence-based indications (cognitive impairment, dementia, cerebrovascular insufficiency)
- Precise dosing (120-240 mg daily)
- Extensive RCT data on efficacy and safety (18+ trials, 2,500+ participants)

## HISTORICAL NOTE: GINKGO SEEDS VS LEAVES

### Likely Traditional Pattern (Not in Current Bibliography)

**Ginkgo Seeds (Bai Guo in TCM):**
- Traditionally used in Chinese medicine
- Indications likely include respiratory and urinary conditions
- Contain ginkgotoxin (4-O-methylpyridoxine), a neurotoxin
- Can cause seizures if consumed in large quantities
- Require processing to reduce toxicity

**Ginkgo Leaves:**
- Modern pharmaceutical use focuses on leaves (not seeds)
- Standardized leaf extracts have extensive clinical trial evidence
- Traditional use of leaves less well documented than seeds in early Chinese texts
- Leaves contain flavonoids and terpene lactones (therapeutic)
- Leaves contain ginkgolic acids (allergenic, removed in standardized extracts)

**Why This Matters:**
- Most traditional sources likely discuss seeds, not leaves
- Modern clinical evidence exclusively uses standardized leaf extracts
- Seeds and leaves have different phytochemical profiles and traditional uses
