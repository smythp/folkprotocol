# Magnolia Bark - Traditional Uses

## Chinese Pharmacopoeia

**Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2015 (and 2010 editions)**
- **Official name**: Magnoliae Officinalis Cortex
- **Chinese name**: Houpo (厚朴) / Houpu / Hou Po
- **Definition**: Dried bark of *Magnolia officinalis* Rehd. et wils and *M. officinalis* Rehd. et wils var. biloba Rehd. et wils
- **Historical use**: "Used in Chinese pharmacopoeia for thousands of years"

### Traditional Properties
- **Temperature**: Warm
- **Flavor**: Bitter and acrid

### Traditional Indications (Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2015)
1. Abdominal distention
2. Vomiting and diarrhea
3. Food accumulation and Qi stagnation
4. Constipation
5. Phlegm and fluid retention
6. Cough resulting from asthma

### Active Constituents (Natural Bark)
- Honokiol: 1-5% in natural bark
- Magnolol: 2-10% in natural bark

### Pharmacopoeial Standards
- **Minimum quality standard**: 2.0% total honokiol and magnolol (combined)
- **Also official in**: European Pharmacopoeia (same 2.0% standard)

## Chinese and Japanese Traditional Medicine

**Poivre 2017 (PMID: 28271656) - Comprehensive Review**

### Traditional Uses Documented
- Anxiety
- Asthma
- Depression
- Gastrointestinal disorders
- Headache

### Traditional Medicine Systems
- **Chinese traditional medicine**: Primary historical use
- **Japanese traditional medicine** (Kampo): Integrated into classical formulas

### Duration of Use
- "Thousands of years" in Chinese and Japanese traditional medicine

## Regulatory Status

### No Western Monographs
- **WHO (World Health Organization)**: No specific monograph published for *Magnolia officinalis*
- **German Commission E**: No monograph published
  - Reason: "Commission E did not evaluate Magnolia officinalis; primarily assessed herbs used in German phytotherapy; M. officinalis is primarily an Asian medicinal plant"

### Official Recognition
- ✓ **Chinese Pharmacopoeia** (multiple editions: 2010, 2015)
- ✓ **European Pharmacopoeia** (2.0% minimum honokiol + magnolol)
- ✗ **WHO Monographs**
- ✗ **Commission E**

## Comparison: Traditional vs Modern Evidence

| Traditional Use | Modern Clinical Evidence | Status |
|----------------|--------------------------|--------|
| Anxiety | ✓ 3 RCTs showing anxiety reduction | **Well-supported** |
| Asthma | Limited modern studies; one Kampo formula study | Partially supported |
| Depression | ✓ 2 RCTs for depressed mood in menopause; 1 RCT postpartum depression | **Well-supported** |
| GI disorders | Not evaluated in modern RCTs | Unsupported in modern literature |
| Headache | Not evaluated in modern RCTs | Unsupported in modern literature |
| Sleep (implied by "phlegm retention, cough from asthma") | ✓ Preclinical GABA receptor studies; 2 menopausal RCTs for insomnia | **Partially supported** |

## Summary

**Well-Documented Traditional Uses**:
- ✓ Thousands of years of use in Chinese and Japanese traditional medicine
- ✓ Official in Chinese Pharmacopoeia (multiple editions) and European Pharmacopoeia
- ✓ Traditional indications for anxiety, depression, asthma, GI disorders, headache
- ✓ Traditional properties: warm temperature, bitter and acrid flavor

**Limitations in Modern Documentation**:
- Most traditional knowledge in Chinese/Japanese language sources
- Western monographs (WHO, Commission E) absent
- Traditional multi-herb formulas not well-studied in modern trials
- Gap between traditional whole-plant decoctions and modern standardized extracts

**Modern Validation**:
- Anxiety and depression: **Well-supported** by modern RCTs
- Asthma: Limited modern evidence
- GI disorders and headache: Not evaluated in modern clinical trials
- Sleep (related to traditional "phlegm and cough" indications): Supported by preclinical GABA studies and menopausal insomnia trials
