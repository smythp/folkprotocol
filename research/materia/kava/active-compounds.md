# Kava - Active Compounds and Bioavailability

## Major Kavalactones (Kavapyrones)

The primary active constituents of kava are kavalactones (also called kavapyrones), a group of lipid-soluble lactone compounds.

### Six Major Kavalactones:
1. **Kavain**
2. **Dihydrokavain**
3. **Methysticin**
4. **Dihydromethysticin**
5. **Yangonin**
6. **Desmethoxyyangonin**

These compounds are found in varying proportions depending on cultivar, plant part, age, and growing conditions.

## Standardization

### Clinical Trial Standardization

**Common Standardization Approaches:**
- Products standardized to total kavalactone content
- Typical standardization range: 30-70% kavalactones
- Individual kavalactone profile varies by cultivar

**Specific Extract Standardizations:**

**WS1490 (Acetone Extract):**
- Most studied pharmaceutical extract
- Standardized German pharmaceutical preparation
- Used in multiple clinical trials
- Dosing: 50-300 mg/day of extract

**Aqueous Extracts:**
- Standardized to specific kavalactone content (mg)
- Various formulations: 120-250 mg kavalactones per dose
- Traditional preparation method adapted for standardization

### Standardization Markers

**Total Kavalactones:**
- Primary marker for product standardization
- Sum of all six major kavalactones
- Clinical dosing typically expressed as mg total kavalactones

**Clinical Trial Doses (as Total Kavalactones):**
- 120 mg/day (divided doses)
- 150-200 mg/day (WS1490)
- 240 mg/day (120 mg twice daily) - common in recent trials
- 250 mg/day (KADSS trial)
- 300 mg/day (maximum in some studies)

**Recommended Maximum:**
- ≤250 mg kavalactones/day for medicinal use (Teschke & Lebot 2011)

## Pharmacokinetics

### Absorption

**Kanumuri et al. 2022** - Clinical pharmacokinetic study, 10 participants:

**Time to Maximum Plasma Concentration:**
- **Tmax: 1-3 hours** after oral administration

**Dosing Regimens Studied:**
- Single dose: 225 mg kavalactones
- Multiple dose: 75 mg three times daily (3 × 75 mg)

**Key Findings:**
- Fast absorption with predictable pharmacokinetics
- **Food significantly reduced absorption extent** in multiple-dose study
- Higher overall exposure with single 225 mg dose vs distributed 3 × 75 mg dosing
- Dose proportionality established from 75-225 mg kavalactone doses

**Caco-2 Permeability Study (2007):**
- All kavalactones readily crossed Caco-2 monolayers
- Apparent permeabilities starting at 42 × 10⁻⁶ cm/s
- Most exhibited >70% crossing within 90 minutes
- Extraction method (ethanol or water) influences total but not relative concentrations
- Extraction method does not markedly affect bioavailability

### Systemic Exposure and Bioavailability

**Kanumuri et al. 2022** - Bioavailability ranking:
- **Dihydrokavain > Dihydromethysticin > Kavain > Methysticin > Yangonin**

This ranking indicates which kavalactones achieve highest blood levels after oral administration.

**Kawain Pharmacokinetics (2005 study):**
- Kawain well absorbed
- **>90% of dose eliminated within 72 hours**, chiefly in urine
- **Coadministration with kava extract caused tripling of kawain AUC(0-8h)**
- **Coadministration caused doubling of kawain Cmax** compared to kawain alone
- Significant interactions between kavalactones when taken together

**Clinical Implication:**
- Whole kava extract may have different pharmacokinetics than isolated kavalactones
- Synergistic or interactive effects among kavalactones affect bioavailability
- This supports use of whole-plant extracts over isolated compounds

### Distribution

Limited specific data on tissue distribution in humans.

**Jussofie et al. 1994** - In vitro research on brain tissue:
- Kavapyrones cross blood-brain barrier
- Regional effects in brain:
  - Strongest effect in **hippocampus** (358% increase in GABA binding)
  - Also active in **amygdala** and **medulla oblongata**

### Metabolism

**Cytochrome P450 Involvement:**

**White et al. 2018** review:
- Kavapyrones induce pharmacologic effects and drug interactions through **cytochrome P450** and **P-glycoprotein systems**

**2004 Review** - CYP450 enzyme interactions:
- Several kavalactones are **potent inhibitors** of CYP450 enzymes:
  - **CYP1A2**
  - **CYP2C9**
  - **CYP2C19**
  - **CYP2D6**
  - **CYP3A4**
  - **CYP4A9/11**
- Drug interaction potential through cytochrome P450 system

### Elimination

**Kawain elimination (2005 study):**
- >90% eliminated within 72 hours
- Chiefly excreted in urine
- Suggests relatively rapid clearance

**Hu et al. 2024** - NNAL glucuronidation study:
- Kava enhanced glucuronidation (Phase II metabolism)
- Increased both N-glucuronidation and O-glucuronidation
- Suggests kava may modulate UGT (UDP-glucuronosyltransferase) enzymes
- Individuals with UGT2B10 genetic variants showed altered response

## Mechanisms of Action by Compound Class

### GABA-A Receptor Modulation

**Jussofie et al. 1994** - In vitro research:
- Kavapyrone extract enhanced [³H]muscimol binding in concentration-dependent manner
- **Strongest potentiation in hippocampus (358%)**, followed by amygdala and medulla oblongata
- Effects due to **increase in binding sites (Bmax)** rather than affinity change
- Synergistic effects with pentobarbital or steroids
- Kavapyrones act on GABA-A receptor binding

**Key Point:**
- Positive allosteric modulation **outside benzodiazepine binding site**
- Increases number of receptor binding sites
- Does not bind at same site as benzodiazepines

### Voltage-Gated Ion Channels

**Sodium Channel Blockade:**
- Singh & Singh 2002: Proposed mechanism for anxiolytic effects
- May contribute to muscle relaxant and local anesthetic effects
- Mechanism similar to local anesthetics

**Calcium Channel Blockade:**
- Reduces excitatory neurotransmitter release
- Contributes to overall calming effect

### Monoamine Systems

**MAO-B Inhibition:**
- Reversible monoamine oxidase B inhibition
- Sarris et al. 2011: Identified as one mechanism

**Noradrenaline Reuptake Inhibition:**
- May contribute to cognitive effects
- LaPorte et al. 2011: Proposed cognitive improvements from noradrenaline re-uptake inhibition in prefrontal cortex

**Dopamine Reuptake Inhibition:**
- Contributes to mood effects
- Sarris et al. 2011: Identified as mechanism

### Gene Expression Effects

**Cribb et al. 2023** - Gene expression analysis (71 baseline, 40 at 8 weeks):
- Kava decreased expression of:
  - **GABA receptor gene (GABRR2)**
  - **Catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT)**
- Results did not withstand correction for multiple comparisons
- Supports kava mechanism involving GABAergic and catecholaminergic systems
- Baseline gene expression did not predict treatment response

### Neuroimaging Evidence

**Savage et al. 2023** - MRI study (37 participants, 8 weeks, 240 mg/day):
- Kava group showed **significant reduction in dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC) GABA** vs placebo (p = 0.049)
- Baseline anxiety scores correlated positively with GABA levels
- dACC GABA could serve as biomarker for diagnosis and treatment response
- Well-tolerated with no serious adverse events

**Clinical Implication:**
- Paradoxical finding: kava enhances GABA binding in vitro but reduces GABA levels in anxious brain regions
- May normalize GABA dysregulation rather than simply increasing GABA
- Different from mechanism of benzodiazepines

## Summary of Proposed Mechanisms

**Sarris et al. 2011** comprehensive review identified multiple mechanisms:

1. **GABA-A receptor modulation**
   - Positive allosteric modulation outside benzodiazepine binding site
   - Increases receptor binding sites (Bmax)
   - Alters lipid membrane structure affecting receptor function

2. **Voltage-gated sodium channel blockade**
   - Contributes to muscle relaxant effects
   - Local anesthetic properties

3. **Voltage-gated calcium channel blockade**
   - Reduces excitatory neurotransmitter release

4. **Noradrenaline reuptake inhibition**
   - May contribute to cognitive effects
   - Prefrontal cortex activity

5. **MAO-B inhibition (reversible)**
   - Monoamine oxidase B inhibition

6. **Dopamine reuptake inhibition**
   - Contributes to mood effects

**Multi-Target Activity:**
Unlike single-target pharmaceuticals, kava appears to work through multiple complementary mechanisms. This polypharmacology may explain:
- Broad anxiolytic effects
- Individual variation in response
- Difficulty replicating effects with isolated compounds

## Extraction Method Effects on Composition

**White et al. 2018** review:
- Significant product variability depending on extraction methods and plant parts used
- **Acetone extract most commonly studied** formulation for anxiety treatment
- Adverse outcomes may stem from kavapyrones themselves or substandard product formulation

**Extraction Methods Used in Clinical Trials:**

1. **Aqueous (Water) Extraction**
   - Traditional preparation method
   - Recommended for safety (Sarris et al. 2011)
   - Used in several positive RCTs
   - Example: Sarris et al. 2013, Sarris et al. 2009 (KADSS)

2. **Acetone Extraction (WS1490)**
   - Most studied pharmaceutical extract
   - Used in multiple German clinical trials
   - Standardized preparation
   - Examples: Volz & Kieser 1997, Gastpar & Klimm 2003, Malsch & Kieser 2001

3. **Ethanolic Extraction**
   - Common in commercial products
   - May have higher hepatotoxicity risk (debated)
   - Less studied in clinical trials

**Caco-2 Permeability Study (2007):**
- Extraction method (ethanol or water) influences total but not relative concentrations
- Extraction method does not markedly affect bioavailability of individual kavalactones
- Suggests differences in safety/efficacy may relate to:
  - Total kavalactone content
  - Presence of contaminants
  - Non-kavalactone constituents

## Quality Variables Affecting Compound Composition

**Teschke & Lebot 2011** - Six-point standardization plan:

1. **Cultivar Selection:**
   - Noble kava cultivars (e.g., Borogu) vs "tudei" varieties
   - Different kavalactone profiles
   - Noble varieties preferred for safety

2. **Plant Age:**
   - At least 5 years old at harvest
   - Affects kavalactone content and profile

3. **Plant Parts:**
   - Peeled and dried rhizomes and roots only
   - Aerial parts (leaves, stems) contain different compounds associated with toxicity
   - Should be avoided

4. **Contamination:**
   - Mould hepatotoxins (Aspergillus aflatoxins, ochratoxin A)
   - Bacterial contamination (Bacillus cereus)
   - Can occur in any extraction type
   - Critical quality control issue

**Teschke et al. 2012** findings:
- Poor quality raw material is primary safety concern
- Hepatotoxicity occurred regardless of extraction solvent
- Quality of starting material more important than extraction method

## Genetic Variation in Response

**Sarris et al. 2013:**
- GABA transporter polymorphisms predicted treatment response
- Pharmacogenetic approach may identify responders

**Hu et al. 2024:**
- UGT2B10 genetic variants affected NNAL glucuronidation response to kava
- Genetic status may predict individual response to kava intervention

**Clinical Implication:**
- Individual genetic variation affects kava response
- Future personalized medicine approaches may optimize treatment
- May explain mixed results in clinical trials

## Pharmacokinetic and Pharmacodynamic Interactions

**Kavalactone-Kavalactone Interactions:**
- Coadministration of kavalactones increases bioavailability of individual compounds
- Whole extract has different pharmacokinetics than isolated kavalactones
- Supports traditional use of whole plant rather than isolated compounds

**Drug Interactions via CYP450:**
- Multiple CYP450 enzymes inhibited by kavalactones
- Potential for interactions with medications metabolized by:
  - CYP1A2 (e.g., caffeine, clozapine, theophylline)
  - CYP2C9 (e.g., warfarin, NSAIDs)
  - CYP2C19 (e.g., omeprazole, citalopram)
  - CYP2D6 (e.g., codeine, many antidepressants)
  - CYP3A4 (e.g., statins, many medications)

**Clinical Implication:**
- Caution with concurrent medications
- Monitor for drug interactions
- Potential for altered drug metabolism

## Onset of Action and Duration

**Clinical Trial Evidence:**

**Kinzler et al. 1991:**
- Improvements evident already after **one week** of treatment
- Treatment advantage expanded throughout 4-week duration

**Volz & Kieser 1997:**
- Significant superiority starting from **week 8** on Hamilton Anxiety Scale
- 25-week study showed sustained effect

**General Pattern:**
- Onset typically **1-8 weeks** after starting treatment
- Not immediate like benzodiazepines
- Suggests mechanism involves adaptive changes, not just acute receptor effects
- Consistent with gene expression changes (Cribb et al. 2023)

## Conclusion: Active Compounds

**Key Findings:**

1. **Multiple Active Constituents:**
   - Six major kavalactones with varying bioavailability
   - Whole extract more bioavailable than isolated compounds
   - Synergistic/interactive effects

2. **Standardization Critical:**
   - Total kavalactone content primary marker
   - Clinical doses: 120-250 mg/day
   - Quality of raw material critical

3. **Multi-Target Pharmacology:**
   - GABA-A receptor modulation (primary)
   - Ion channel effects
   - Monoamine modulation
   - Polypharmacology may explain broad effects

4. **Bioavailability:**
   - Rapid absorption (Tmax 1-3 hours)
   - Food reduces absorption
   - Most eliminated within 72 hours
   - Ranking: dihydrokavain > dihydromethysticin > kavain > methysticin > yangonin

5. **Drug Interactions:**
   - CYP450 enzyme inhibition
   - Potential interactions with many medications
   - Caution with concurrent drugs

6. **Genetic Variation:**
   - GABA transporter polymorphisms affect response
   - UGT2B10 variants affect metabolism
   - Personalized medicine approaches promising

7. **Delayed Onset:**
   - 1-8 weeks typical for anxiolytic effects
   - Suggests adaptive mechanisms, not just acute effects
   - Consistent with gene expression and neuroplastic changes
