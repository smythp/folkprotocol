# Kava (Piper methysticum) - Clinical Uses

## Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

### Sarris et al. 2013 - Positive Results
- **Sample:** 75 participants, 58 completers
- **Duration:** 6 weeks
- **Dosing:** Aqueous kava extract providing 120-240 mg kavalactones per day (response-adjusted dosing)
- **Administration:** Divided doses throughout the day
- **Results:**
  - Statistically significant anxiety reduction vs placebo (p = 0.046, Cohen's d = 0.62)
  - Among moderate-to-severe GAD cases: stronger effect (p = 0.02, d = 0.82)
  - Remission rates: 26% kava vs 6% placebo (p = 0.04)
  - GABA transporter polymorphisms predicted treatment response
- **Tolerability:** Well-tolerated with more headaches in treatment group (p = 0.05)
- **Safety:** No significant liver function test abnormalities

### Sarris et al. 2020 - Negative Results
- **Sample:** 171 participants
- **Duration:** 16 weeks
- **Dosing:** Aqueous extract of dried kava root, 120 mg kavalactones twice daily (240 mg/day total)
- **Administration:** Twice daily
- **Results:**
  - No significant advantage over placebo (p = 0.25)
  - Remission: 17.4% kava vs 23.8% placebo
- **Adverse Events:**
  - Memory impairment and tremor more frequent in kava group
  - Liver function abnormalities significantly more frequent in kava group
  - No participant developed clinically significant hepatic injury
- **Conclusion:** May help with situational anxiety short-term but not effective for diagnosed GAD with this extract

### KADSS Trial 2009 - Strong Positive Results
- **Sample:** 60 participants
- **Duration:** 3 weeks per phase (crossover design)
- **Dosing:** Five kava tablets daily containing 250 mg kavalactones per day
- **Results:**
  - Hamilton Anxiety Scale reduction: -9.9 (kava) vs -0.8 (placebo) in phase one
  - Hamilton Anxiety Scale reduction: -10.3 (kava) vs +3.3 (placebo) in phase two
  - Pooled effect highly significant (p < 0.0001, Cohen's d = 2.24)
  - Significant reductions in Beck Anxiety Inventory and Montgomery-Asberg Depression Rating Scale scores
  - Equivalent efficacy for anxiety with comorbid depression
- **Safety:** No serious adverse effects or clinical hepatotoxicity observed

## Non-Psychotic Anxiety Disorders (WS1490 Extract)

### Gastpar & Klimm 2003 - Multicenter Trial
- **Sample:** 141 participants
- **Duration:** 4 weeks
- **Dosing:** WS 1490 extract: 3 × 50 mg/day (150 mg/day total)
- **Administration:** Three divided doses daily
- **Results:**
  - 73% of patients on WS 1490 showed anxiety score improvements exceeding 5 points vs 56% in placebo group
  - Analysis of variance demonstrated superiority of WS 1490 over placebo (p < 0.01)
  - Improvements observed on well-being and Clinical Global Impressions scales
- **Safety:** Well-tolerated with no adverse liver function effects and minimal side effects

### Volz & Kieser 1997 - Long-Term Efficacy
- **Sample:** 101 participants
- **Duration:** 25 weeks
- **Dosing:** WS 1490 (dose not specified in abstract)
- **Results:**
  - Significant superiority of test drug starting from week 8 on Hamilton Anxiety Scale
  - Superior across multiple secondary measures including HAMA subscores for somatic and psychic anxiety, Clinical Global Impression
- **Safety:** Adverse events rare and distributed evenly in both groups
- **Conclusion:** Treatment alternative to tricyclic antidepressants and benzodiazepines with proven long-term efficacy and no tolerance problems

## Sleep Disturbances Associated with Anxiety

### Lehrl 2004 - WS1490 for Sleep
- **Sample:** 61 participants
- **Duration:** 4 weeks
- **Dosing:** WS 1490: 200 mg daily
- **Results:**
  - Quality of sleep significantly improved (p = 0.007)
  - Recuperative effect after sleep significantly improved (p = 0.018)
  - Hamilton Anxiety Scale psychic anxiety subscale significant reduction (p = 0.002)
- **Safety:** Good tolerability with no drug-related adverse events
- **Conclusion:** Sleep disturbances associated with non-psychotic anxiety disorders can be effectively and safely treated

## Meta-Analysis Evidence

### Pittler & Ernst 2000
- **Studies Included:** 7 systematic review, 3 in meta-analysis
- **Effect Size:** Weighted mean difference of 9.69 points on Hamilton Rating Scale for Anxiety (95% CI: 3.54-15.83)
- **Conclusion:** Kava extract superior to placebo, represents herbal treatment option worthy of consideration

### Witte et al. 2005 - WS1490 Specific
- **Studies Included:** 6 studies
- **Effect Size:**
  - Effective success rate OR=3.3 (95% CI: 2.09-5.22)
  - Treatment group improved by approximately 5.94 points on Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale compared to controls
  - Greater response rates in females and younger patients
- **Quality:** No publication bias, no remarkable heterogeneity, high methodological standards
- **Conclusion:** WS1490 represents viable alternative to conventional pharmaceutical treatments

### Sarris et al. 2011 - Comprehensive Review
- **Studies Reviewed:** 6 studies
- **Effect Size:** Significant result in 4 of 6 studies, mean Cohen's d = 1.1
- **Mechanisms:** Modulation of GABA activity via alteration of lipid membrane structure and sodium channel function, MAO-B inhibition, noradrenaline and dopamine re-uptake inhibition

### Smith & Leiras 2018
- **Studies Included:** 11 studies, 5 in pooled analysis
- **Total Participants:** 330
- **Effect Size:** Risk ratio of 1.50 in favor of intervention from responder rates (95% CI: 1.12-2.01)
- **Safety:** Adverse events matched placebo levels (p = 0.574)
- **Hepatotoxicity:** No hepatotoxicity marker elevation compared to baseline except in two studies
- **Conclusion:** Suitable as short-term treatment for anxiety, not replacement for prolonged anti-anxiety use

## Neurocognitive Effects

### LaPorte et al. 2011 - Systematic Review
- **Studies Included:** 10 studies (7 acute, 3 chronic)
- **Positive Findings:**
  - One acute study showed significantly improved visual attention and working memory processes
  - Proposed mechanism: cognitive improvements from noradrenaline re-uptake inhibition in prefrontal cortex
- **Negative Findings:**
  - One acute study showed increased body sway
  - One chronic study showed significantly impaired visual attention during high-cognitive demand
  - Proposed mechanism: balance effects from GABA pathway modulation
- **Overall Conclusion:** Majority of evidence suggests kava has no replicated significant negative effects on cognition

## Dosing Guidelines from Clinical Evidence

### Standard Therapeutic Doses
- **Common Range:** 120-250 mg kavalactones per day
- **Anxiety Treatment Range:** 150-300 mg kavalactones per day
- **Maximum Recommended:** ≤250 mg kavalactones per day (Teschke & Lebot 2011)
- **WS1490 Pharmaceutical Extract:** 150-200 mg per day

### Duration Recommendations
- **Maximum Continuous Use:** ≤3 months (based on hepatotoxicity prevention)
- **Typical Trial Duration:** 4-6 weeks
- **Onset of Effect:** Typically after 1-8 weeks of use

### Administration
- **Frequency:** Typically divided into 2-3 doses per day
- **Food Interaction:** Food reduces absorption - consider taking on empty stomach
- **Extract Type:** Aqueous extracts preferred over ethanolic or acetone extracts for safety

## Clinical Interpretation

### Evidence Quality
- **Mixed Results:** High-quality trials show both positive and negative results
- **Factors Affecting Outcomes:** Extraction method, dosing, duration, patient population
- **Strongest Evidence:** WS1490 acetone extract has most consistent positive results
- **Aqueous Extracts:** Show variable efficacy but best safety profile

### Patient Selection
- **Best Response:** Moderate-to-severe anxiety (stronger effect sizes)
- **Comorbid Depression:** Evidence supports efficacy for anxiety with comorbid depression
- **Genetic Factors:** GABA transporter polymorphisms may predict treatment response
- **Demographics:** Greater response rates in females and younger patients

### Treatment Context
- **Short-Term Use:** Most appropriate for short-term anxiety treatment (4-6 weeks)
- **Long-Term Efficacy:** One 25-week trial showed sustained efficacy without tolerance
- **Not First-Line:** Suitable as alternative when conventional treatments not desired or tolerated
- **Situational vs. Diagnosed GAD:** May be more effective for situational anxiety than diagnosed GAD
