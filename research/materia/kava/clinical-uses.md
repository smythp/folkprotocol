# Kava - Clinical Uses

## Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

### Efficacy Evidence

**Meta-Analysis Results:**
- **Pittler & Ernst 2000**: 7 studies reviewed, 3 in meta-analysis
  - Weighted mean difference: 9.69 points reduction on Hamilton Anxiety Scale vs placebo (95% CI: 3.54-15.83)

- **Witte et al. 2005**: 6 studies, WS1490 extract
  - Effective success rate OR=3.3 (95% CI: 2.09-5.22)
  - Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale improvement: 5.94 points vs controls
  - Greater response in females and younger patients

- **Sarris et al. 2011**: Review of 6 studies
  - 4 of 6 showed significant results
  - Mean effect size: Cohen's d = 1.1

- **Ooi et al. 2018**: Meta-analysis of 3 placebo-controlled trials (130 participants)
  - Effect sizes: 0.59-0.99 (not statistically significant)
  - Conclusion: Current evidence insufficient to confirm effect beyond placebo

- **Barić et al. 2018**: Network meta-analysis
  - 4 quality trials, n=233 participants (139 kava, 359 placebo)
  - Hamilton Anxiety scale difference: -3.24 (95% CI: -6.65, 0.17, p = 0.059)

### Individual RCT Results

**Positive Trials:**

1. **Sarris et al. 2009 (KADSS)**: 60 participants, 3 weeks, 250 mg kavalactones/day
   - Hamilton Anxiety Scale reduction: -9.9 (kava) vs -0.8 (placebo) in phase one
   - Phase two: -10.3 (kava) vs +3.3 (placebo)
   - Pooled effect: p < 0.0001, Cohen's d = 2.24
   - Also reduced Beck Anxiety Inventory and Montgomery-Asberg Depression Rating Scale scores
   - No serious adverse effects or hepatotoxicity

2. **Sarris et al. 2013**: 75 participants, 58 completers, 6 weeks, 120-240 mg kavalactones/day
   - Significant anxiety reduction vs placebo: p = 0.046, Cohen's d = 0.62
   - Moderate-to-severe GAD cases: p = 0.02, d = 0.82
   - Remission rates: 26% (kava) vs 6% (placebo), p = 0.04
   - GABA transporter polymorphisms predicted treatment response
   - Well-tolerated, more headaches in treatment group (p = 0.05)
   - No significant liver function test abnormalities

3. **Gastpar & Klimm 2003**: 141 participants, 4 weeks, WS 1490 150 mg/day
   - 73% on WS 1490 showed >5 point anxiety score improvement vs 56% placebo
   - Analysis of variance: p < 0.01
   - Improvements in well-being and Clinical Global Impressions
   - Well-tolerated, no adverse liver effects

4. **Volz & Kieser 1997**: 101 participants, 25 weeks
   - Significant superiority from week 8 on Hamilton Anxiety Scale
   - Superior across somatic anxiety, psychic anxiety, Clinical Global Impression
   - Adverse events rare and evenly distributed
   - Demonstrated long-term efficacy without tolerance

5. **Malsch & Kieser 2001**: 40 participants, 5 weeks, WS1490 50-300 mg/day
   - Post-benzodiazepine treatment study
   - Superior to placebo on Hamilton Anxiety Scale (p = 0.01)
   - Superior on subjective well-being (p = 0.002)
   - Further symptom reduction possible after benzodiazepine change-over

6. **Kinzler et al. 1991**: 58 participants, 4 weeks, WS 1490 300 mg/day
   - Significant anxiety reduction on Hamilton Anxiety Scale
   - Improvements evident after one week
   - Treatment advantage expanded throughout duration
   - No adverse effects reported

**Negative Trials:**

1. **Sarris et al. 2020**: 171 participants, 16 weeks, 240 mg kavalactones/day
   - No significant advantage over placebo (p = 0.25)
   - Remission rates: 17.4% (kava) vs 23.8% (placebo)
   - Memory impairment and tremor more frequent in kava group
   - Liver function abnormalities more frequent (but no clinically significant hepatic injury)
   - Conclusion: May help situational anxiety short-term but not effective for diagnosed GAD with this extract

2. **Connor & Davidson 2002**: 37 participants, 4 weeks
   - Primary analysis: no significant difference between kava and placebo
   - Secondary analysis: kava superior on SARA scale in patients with lower baseline anxiety
   - Placebo outperformed kava in patients with higher baseline anxiety
   - Both well-tolerated

### Dosing for GAD

**Effective Doses:**
- 120-250 mg kavalactones/day (most common range)
- 240 mg/day (120 mg twice daily) - used in multiple studies
- 250 mg/day - KADSS trial showing large effect (d = 2.24)
- WS1490 extract: 150-200 mg/day

**Duration:**
- Short-term use: 4-8 weeks appears safe and potentially effective
- Onset of effect: typically 1-8 weeks
- Long-term use (25 weeks) demonstrated in Volz & Kieser 1997 without tolerance

**Administration:**
- Typically divided into 2-3 doses per day
- Food reduces absorption - consider empty stomach
- Aqueous extracts preferred over ethanolic/acetone for safety

## Sleep Disturbances with Anxiety

**Lehrl 2004**: 61 participants, 4 weeks, WS 1490 200 mg/day
- Quality of sleep significantly improved (p = 0.007)
- Recuperative effect after sleep improved (p = 0.018)
- Hamilton Anxiety Scale psychic anxiety subscale reduction (p = 0.002)
- Good tolerability with no drug-related adverse events
- Indication: Sleep disturbances associated with non-psychotic anxiety disorders

## Perimenopausal and Postmenopausal Anxiety

**Cagnacci et al. 2003**: 68 participants (34 control, 15 kava 100mg, 19 kava 200mg), 3 months
- Anxiety declined at 1 and 3 months with kava (p < 0.001)
- Depression decreased significantly at 3 months
- Climacteric symptom scores improved
- Anxiety reduction with kava significantly greater than spontaneous control improvement (p < 0.009)

**De Leo et al. 2001**: Postmenopausal women (menopause 1-12 years), 6 months
- Significant HAMA score reduction in all four treatment groups
- Reduction more pronounced in groups receiving kava vs hormone-only groups
- Kava accelerates resolution of psychological symptoms
- Combination with HRT appears effective

## Anxiety with Comorbid Depression

**Sarris et al. 2009 (KADSS)**: 60 participants, 250 mg kavalactones/day
- Equivalent efficacy for anxiety with comorbid depression
- Significant reductions in both Beck Anxiety Inventory and Montgomery-Asberg Depression Rating Scale

## Motivation During High-Stress Training

**Smith et al. 2024**: 15 military special operations candidates, 14-day loading periods, 225 mg kavalactones nightly
- Between-group differences in 'Move' motivation emerged after 1 week (p = 0.044)
- Differences persisted through loading period (p = 0.022)
- Kavalactone-first group maintained stable motivation scores (21.0-28.6)
- Cortisol, sleep, and mood remained within normal parameters
- Rest motivation unaffected

## Nervous Tension and Restlessness

**Kuchta et al. 2021**: Observational multicenter study, 156 participants, median 28 days
- All individual symptoms showed significant and clinically relevant improvements
- Most effective for nervous tension and restlessness
- Better outcomes in patients with acute vs chronic complaints
- Excellent safety profile including laboratory data
- Confirms effective and safe short-term use for Commission E-defined indication

## Mechanism-Based Evidence

### Neuroimaging Findings

**Savage et al. 2023**: 37 participants (20 kava, 17 placebo), 8 weeks, 240 mg kavalactones/day
- Kava group showed significant reduction in dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC) GABA vs placebo (p = 0.049)
- Baseline anxiety scores correlated positively with GABA levels
- dACC GABA could serve as biomarker for diagnosis and treatment response
- Well-tolerated with no serious adverse events

### Gene Expression

**Cribb et al. 2023**: 71 baseline, 40 at 8 weeks
- Kava decreased expression of GABA receptor gene (GABRR2) and catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT)
- Results did not withstand correction for multiple comparisons
- Supports kava mechanism involving GABAergic and catecholaminergic systems
- Baseline gene expression did not predict treatment response

### Mechanisms of Action

**Sarris et al. 2011** comprehensive review identified:
- Modulation of GABA activity via alteration of lipid membrane structure and sodium channel function
- MAO-B inhibition (reversible)
- Noradrenaline re-uptake inhibition
- Dopamine re-uptake inhibition

**Jussofie et al. 1994** - In vitro research:
- Kavapyrone extract enhanced [³H]muscimol binding in concentration-dependent manner
- Strongest potentiation in hippocampus (358%), followed by amygdala and medulla oblongata
- Effects due to increase in binding sites (Bmax) rather than affinity change
- Synergistic effects with pentobarbital or steroids
- Kavapyrones produce sedative effects through GABA-A receptor binding

**Singh & Singh 2002** review:
- Kavalactones produce sedative, anxiolytic, antistress, analgesic, local anaesthetic effects
- Proposed mechanisms: sodium channel blockade and GABA receptor enhancement

### Multiple Proposed Mechanisms:
1. **GABA-A receptor modulation**: Positive allosteric modulation outside benzodiazepine binding site; increases receptor binding sites
2. **Voltage-gated sodium channel blockade**: May contribute to muscle relaxant and local anesthetic effects
3. **Voltage-gated calcium channel blockade**: Reduces excitatory neurotransmitter release
4. **Noradrenaline reuptake inhibition**: May contribute to cognitive effects
5. **MAO-B inhibition (reversible)**: Monoamine oxidase B inhibition
6. **Dopamine reuptake inhibition**: Contributes to mood effects

## Neurocognitive Effects

**LaPorte et al. 2011**: Systematic review of 10 studies (7 acute, 3 chronic)
- One acute study: significantly improved visual attention and working memory
- Another acute study: increased body sway
- One chronic study: significantly impaired visual attention during high-cognitive demand
- Majority of evidence: kava has no replicated significant negative effects on cognition
- Proposed mechanisms: cognitive improvements from noradrenaline re-uptake inhibition in prefrontal cortex, balance effects from GABA pathway modulation

**Aporosa et al. 2022**: 39 participants (20 kava users, 19 controls), 3.6 liters beverage kava during 6-hour sessions
- Kava group demonstrated improved Focus scores at final testing
- Significant impairment in Temporal Order Judgement (executive function, decision-making)
- No substantial deficits in Speed, Accuracy, Timing Perception, Plasticity
- Traditional kava use may present driver safety concerns through mechanisms distinct from alcohol/cannabis

## Tobacco Carcinogen Detoxification

**Hu et al. 2024**: One week supplementation among smokers
- Kava enhanced detoxification of NNAL (tobacco carcinogen metabolite)
- Increased both N-glucuronidation and O-glucuronidation in urine
- Individuals with UGT2B10 genetic variants showed reduced basal NNAL-N-glucuronidation capacity
- Genetic status may predict individual response to kava intervention

## Clinical Summary

**Best Evidence For:**
- Short-term (4-8 weeks) treatment of mild-to-moderate anxiety
- Nervous tension and restlessness
- Sleep disturbances associated with anxiety
- Perimenopausal/postmenopausal anxiety
- Post-benzodiazepine anxiety treatment

**Mixed/Insufficient Evidence:**
- Long-term treatment of diagnosed GAD (conflicting results)
- Anxiety with high baseline severity

**Typical Clinical Dosing:**
- 120-250 mg kavalactones per day
- Divided into 2-3 doses
- Duration: 4-8 weeks initially
- Maximum recommended: ≤250 mg/day for ≤3 months

**Safety Considerations:**
- Short-term use (4-8 weeks) generally well-tolerated
- Adverse events typically match placebo levels
- Hepatotoxicity rare when recommendations followed
- More headaches reported in some studies
- Memory impairment and tremor reported in one long-term study
- Routine liver function tests recommended for regular users
