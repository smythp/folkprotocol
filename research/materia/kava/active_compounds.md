# Kava (Piper methysticum) - Active Compounds

## Major Kavalactones

The six major active compounds in kava:

1. **Kavain** (also spelled Kawain)
2. **Dihydrokavain**
3. **Methysticin**
4. **Dihydromethysticin**
5. **Yangonin**
6. **Desmethoxyyangonin**

These compounds are collectively referred to as kavalactones or kavapyrones.

## Pharmacokinetics

### Absorption and Time to Peak Concentration

**Kanumuri et al. 2022** - Clinical PK study in 10 healthy volunteers:
- **Time to Maximum Plasma Concentration:** 1-3 hours after oral administration
- **Absorption Profile:** Fast absorption with predictable pharmacokinetics
- **Dose Proportionality:** Established from 75-225 mg kavalactone doses

### Bioavailability Ranking

**Systemic Exposure Ranking** (highest to lowest):
1. Dihydrokavain (highest systemic exposure)
2. Dihydromethysticin
3. Kavain
4. Methysticin
5. Yangonin (lowest systemic exposure)

### Food Effects

**Kanumuri et al. 2022** findings:
- **Food Impact:** Food significantly reduced absorption extent in multiple-dose study
- **Clinical Implication:** Consider taking on empty stomach for optimal absorption
- **Dosing Pattern Effect:** Higher overall exposure observed with single 225 mg dose versus distributed 3 × 75 mg dosing

### Permeability Studies

**Caco-2 Cell Monolayer Study (2007)**:
- **All kavalactones readily crossed Caco-2 monolayers**
- **Apparent Permeabilities:** Starting at 42 × 10⁻⁶ cm/s
- **Rapid Crossing:** Most exhibited more than 70% crossing within 90 minutes
- **Extraction Method Impact:** Ethanol or water extraction influences total but not relative concentrations
- **Bioavailability Conclusion:** Extraction method does not markedly affect bioavailability

### Elimination

**Kawain Disposition Study (2005)**:
- **Elimination:** >90% of kawain dose eliminated within 72 hours
- **Primary Route:** Chiefly eliminated in urine
- **Absorption:** Kawain well absorbed

## Kavalactone Interactions

### Synergistic Effects

**Drug Metabolism and Disposition Study (2005)**:
- **Interaction Effect:** Coadministration of kawain with kava extract caused tripling of kawain AUC(0-8h)
- **Peak Concentration:** Coadministration caused doubling of kawain C(max) compared to kawain alone
- **Clinical Significance:** Significant interactions between kavalactones when taken together
- **Implication:** Whole extract may be more effective than isolated kavalactones due to synergistic pharmacokinetic interactions

## Standardization Markers

### Clinical Trial Standardization

**Common Standardization Approaches:**

1. **Total Kavalactone Content:**
   - Typical range: 30-70% kavalactones
   - Products should be standardized to total kavalactone content
   - Individual kavalactone profile varies by cultivar

2. **WS1490 Pharmaceutical Extract:**
   - Acetone extraction method
   - Most studied pharmaceutical formulation
   - Standardized kavalactone content
   - Used in multiple RCTs showing efficacy

3. **Aqueous Extracts:**
   - Water-soluble extraction
   - Traditional preparation method
   - Recommended for safety (Sarris et al. 2011)
   - Variable kavalactone content depending on preparation

### Dosing Standardization in Trials

Clinical trials standardized to kavalactone content:

- **Sarris et al. 2013:** 120-240 mg kavalactones/day
- **Sarris et al. 2020:** 240 mg kavalactones/day (120 mg twice daily)
- **KADSS 2009:** 250 mg kavalactones/day
- **Gastpar & Klimm 2003:** 150 mg kavalactones/day (WS1490)
- **Lehrl 2004:** 200 mg WS1490/day

## Mechanisms of Action

### GABAergic Effects

**Sarris et al. 2011** comprehensive review:
- **Primary Mechanism:** Modulation of GABA activity via alteration of lipid membrane structure and sodium channel function
- **GABA Transporter:** Polymorphisms in GABA transporter genes predict treatment response (Sarris et al. 2013)
- **Balance Effects:** GABA pathway modulation may cause increased body sway (LaPorte et al. 2011)

### Enzyme Inhibition

**MAO-B Inhibition:**
- Monoamine oxidase B inhibition contributes to anxiolytic effects

**Noradrenaline and Dopamine Effects:**
- Noradrenaline re-uptake inhibition
- Dopamine re-uptake inhibition
- Cognitive improvements potentially from noradrenaline re-uptake inhibition in prefrontal cortex (LaPorte et al. 2011)

### Sodium Channel Function

- Alteration of sodium channel function contributes to anxiolytic effects
- Mechanism distinct from benzodiazepines

## Drug Interactions via Cytochrome P450

### CYP450 Enzyme Inhibition

**White 2018 review:**
- Kavapyrones induce pharmacologic effects and drug interactions through cytochrome P450 and P-glycoprotein systems

**Specific CYP450 Enzymes Affected (2004 review):**
Several kavalactones are potent inhibitors of:
- CYP1A2
- CYP2C9
- CYP2C19
- CYP2D6
- CYP3A4
- CYP4A9/11

**Clinical Significance:**
- Drug interaction potential through cytochrome P450 system
- Caution with concurrent use of medications metabolized by these enzymes
- May affect levels of other drugs taken concurrently

### P-Glycoprotein Effects

- Kavalactones affect P-glycoprotein systems
- May influence drug absorption and distribution
- Potential for drug interactions

## Extraction Method Effects

### Acetone Extraction (WS1490)

**Characteristics:**
- Most commonly studied formulation for anxiety treatment (White 2018)
- Standardized pharmaceutical preparation
- Consistent kavalactone profile
- Strong clinical evidence base

**Clinical Evidence:**
- Witte et al. 2005 meta-analysis: OR=3.3 (95% CI: 2.09-5.22)
- Gastpar & Klimm 2003: 73% response vs 56% placebo
- Multiple positive RCTs

### Aqueous (Water) Extraction

**Characteristics:**
- Traditional preparation method
- Recommended for safety (Sarris et al. 2011, Teschke & Lebot 2011)
- Water-soluble kavalactones extracted
- Variable kavalactone content

**Clinical Evidence:**
- Sarris et al. 2013: Positive results (d=0.62, 26% remission vs 6% placebo)
- KADSS 2009: Strong positive results (d=2.24, p<0.0001)
- Sarris et al. 2020: Negative results (no advantage over placebo)

**Safety Profile:**
- Best safety profile compared to other extraction methods
- Lower hepatotoxicity risk
- Aligns with traditional preparation methods

### Ethanolic Extraction

**Characteristics:**
- Common in commercial products
- May extract broader range of compounds including potentially hepatotoxic constituents

**Safety Concerns:**
- May have higher hepatotoxicity risk compared to aqueous extracts
- Less aligned with traditional preparation methods

## Quality Factors Affecting Active Compound Profile

### Cultivar Selection

**Noble Kava Cultivars:**
- Example: Borogu cultivar
- Recommended for medicinal use (Teschke & Lebot 2011)
- Different cultivars have different kavalactone profiles
- Individual kavalactone profile varies by cultivar

### Plant Age and Parts

**Optimal Material:**
- At least 5 years old at harvest (Teschke & Lebot 2011)
- Peeled and dried rhizomes and roots only
- Avoid aerial parts (leaves, stems) - associated with toxicity

**Quality Impact:**
- Plant age affects kavalactone concentration
- Aerial parts contain different compound profiles, potentially hepatotoxic
- Peeling removes outer bark compounds

### Processing and Storage

**Factors:**
- Drying method affects compound stability
- Storage conditions affect compound degradation
- Extraction method determines which compounds are present in final product

## Standardization Recommendations

### For Safety (Teschke & Lebot 2011)

1. **Source Material:**
   - Noble kava cultivar (e.g., Borogu)
   - At least 5 years old at harvest
   - Peeled and dried rhizomes and roots

2. **Extraction:**
   - Aqueous extraction recommended
   - Hepatotoxicity occurred independently of solvent, but aqueous extraction aligns with traditional safe use

3. **Dosage:**
   - ≤250 mg kavalactones per day for medicinal use
   - Higher doses associated with increased adverse event risk

### For Efficacy

**Based on Clinical Evidence:**
- Standardize to total kavalactone content
- 120-250 mg kavalactones per day most common therapeutic range
- WS1490 acetone extract has most consistent positive clinical results
- Aqueous extracts show variable efficacy but better safety profile

## Product Variability

### White 2018 Review Findings

**Quality Issues:**
- Significant product variability depending on extraction methods and plant parts used
- Adverse outcomes stem from kavapyrones themselves or substandard product formulation
- Modern commercial extracts vary widely in quality and composition

**Standardization Challenges:**
- Individual kavalactone profile varies by cultivar
- Different extraction methods yield different compound profiles
- No universal standardization across products

## Clinical Significance of Compound Profile

### Response Variability

**Factors Affecting Response:**
- Extraction method (acetone vs. aqueous vs. ethanol)
- Kavalactone content and profile
- Source cultivar quality
- Patient genetics (GABA transporter polymorphisms)

### Onset of Effect

**Pharmacodynamic Profile:**
- Results don't appear immediately (White 2018)
- Typical onset: 1-8 weeks of use
- Suggests mechanism beyond immediate pharmacological effects
- May involve neuroadaptive changes

## Summary: Active Compound Key Points

1. **Six Major Kavalactones:** Kavain, dihydrokavain, methysticin, dihydromethysticin, yangonin, desmethoxyyangonin
2. **Bioavailability Ranking:** Dihydrokavain > dihydromethysticin > kavain > methysticin > yangonin
3. **Time to Peak:** 1-3 hours after oral administration
4. **Food Effects:** Food significantly reduces absorption
5. **Synergy:** Kavalactones interact when taken together (whole extract more effective than isolated compounds)
6. **Mechanisms:** GABA modulation, MAO-B inhibition, noradrenaline/dopamine re-uptake inhibition, sodium channel effects
7. **Drug Interactions:** Inhibits multiple CYP450 enzymes and affects P-glycoprotein
8. **Standardization:** Products should be standardized to 30-70% total kavalactones
9. **Extraction Matters:** Aqueous extraction safest, acetone extraction (WS1490) most studied and consistent
10. **Quality Critical:** Cultivar, plant age, plant parts, and extraction method all affect compound profile and clinical outcomes
