# Astragalus - Preparations and Dosing

## Traditional Chinese Medicine Preparations

### 1. Decoction (Tang) - Most Traditional Method

**Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2020 Standard**:
- **Dose**: 9-30 g dried root daily
- **Preparation**: Water decoction
- **Method**:
  1. Use 9-30 g dried Radix Astragali (cut root pieces)
  2. Add to water (typically 3-4 cups per 30 g)
  3. Bring to boil, then simmer for 30-60 minutes
  4. Strain and consume as tea
  5. Can be consumed throughout the day

**Physical Characteristics of Quality Root**:
- Shape: Cylindrical pieces
- Length: 30-90 cm (whole roots)
- Diameter: 1-3.5 cm
- Surface color: Light brownish yellow or light brownish
- Characteristics: Irregular longitudinal wrinkles or grooves
- Fracture surface: Strong fiber texture with powdery appearance

**Advantages**:
- Traditional method with 2,000+ year history
- Multi-constituent extraction (polysaccharides, saponins, flavonoids)
- Whole extract may have superior bioavailability vs. isolated compounds
- Cost-effective

**Disadvantages**:
- Time-consuming preparation
- Taste can be challenging (earthy, slightly sweet)
- Difficult to standardize exact dose
- Requires quality dried root source

### 2. Powdered Root

**Preparation**:
- Dried root ground into fine powder
- Dose: 9-30 g daily (similar to decoction)
- **Administration methods**:
  - Mixed with water or juice
  - Mixed with honey to form paste
  - Added to smoothies or food
  - Encapsulated (if using smaller doses)

**Advantages**:
- No preparation time
- Easier to transport
- Can be added to various foods/drinks

**Disadvantages**:
- Texture can be gritty
- Taste may be less palatable than tea
- Requires quality dried root source
- Difficult to verify quality once powdered

### 3. Wine Extract (Traditional Tonic)

**Traditional Preparation**:
- Dried root macerated in rice wine or spirits
- Ratio: Typically 1:5 to 1:10 (herb:alcohol by weight)
- Maceration time: 2-4 weeks, shaken periodically
- Strain and consume in small amounts daily

**Dose**:
- Typically 5-30 mL daily of the wine extract
- Based on traditional use; no clinical trial data

**Advantages**:
- Long shelf life
- Alcohol may extract different constituents than water
- Traditional tonic form
- Convenient dosing

**Disadvantages**:
- Contains alcohol (not suitable for all individuals)
- No clinical trial data on this preparation
- Requires weeks to prepare
- Difficult to standardize

### 4. Combined Formulas (Classical TCM)

Astragalus is frequently combined with other herbs in traditional formulas:

**Common Classical Combinations**:
- **With Angelica sinensis (Dang Gui)**: For blood deficiency patterns
- **With Panax ginseng**: For severe Qi deficiency
- **With Rehmannia**: For kidney support
- **Bu Zhong Yi Qi Tang** (Tonify the Middle and Augment Qi Decoction): Classic formula containing astragalus

**Dose in formulas**: Typically 9-15 g astragalus per formula (total formula may be 30-60 g)

## Modern Standardized Extract Preparations

### 1. Isoflavonoid-Standardized Capsules

**Example: Latour et al. 2021 Study**
- **Standardization**: 0.5% 3-hydroxy 7-methoxy isoflavonoids
- **Dose**: 500 mg daily
- **Form**: Capsules
- **Application**: Exercise-induced immunosuppression
- **Duration**: 6 weeks
- **Results**: Maintained NK cells and Tregs; stabilized immune system

**Advantages**:
- Consistent standardization
- Convenient dosing
- Clinically validated in RCT
- Easy to take

**Disadvantages**:
- More expensive than dried root
- Standardization to isoflavonoids may not capture all active constituents
- Relies on quality manufacturer

### 2. Non-Standardized Root Extract Capsules

**Example: Li et al. 2018 Diastolic Dysfunction Study**
- **Form**: Astragalus capsules (non-standardized)
- **Dose**: 5-10 g/day
- **Duration**: 12 months
- **Results**: Improved diastolic function at 10 g/day dose

**Example: Banihashemi et al. 2025 Post-COVID Fatigue Study**
- **Form**: Astragalus root extract
- **Dose**: 500 mg twice daily (1,000 mg/day total)
- **Duration**: Variable; measurements at end and 1-month follow-up
- **Results**: 17.2% fatigue reduction at 1-month follow-up

**Advantages**:
- Convenient capsule form
- Clinical trial validation
- May contain broader spectrum of constituents than single-standardized extracts

**Disadvantages**:
- Lack of standardization makes product comparison difficult
- Quality varies by manufacturer
- No guarantee of active constituent levels

### 3. Multi-Herb Standardized Mixtures

**Example: HT042 - Lee et al. 2018 Pediatric Growth Study**
- **Composition**: Astragalus membranaceus + Eleutherococcus senticosus + Phlomis umbrosa
- **Form**: Extract mixture (specific dose not provided for astragalus component alone)
- **Duration**: 24 weeks
- **Age group**: 6-8 years
- **Results**: +0.29 cm height gain (all children); +0.45 cm (shorter children)

**Safety**: Song et al. 2017
- LD50 >5,000 mg/kg
- NOAEL 4,000 mg/kg/day
- Favorable safety profile

**Advantages**:
- Synergistic herbal combination
- Clinically validated for specific application (pediatric growth)
- Good safety profile

**Disadvantages**:
- Cannot isolate astragalus effects from other herbs
- Specific to this proprietary mixture
- May not generalize to other applications

### 4. Polysaccharide-Rich Preparations

**Applications**: Cancer immunotherapy research
- **Form**: Astragalus polysaccharide (APS) preparations
- **Standardization**: Variable; focus on polysaccharide content
- **Clinical evidence**: Li et al. 2025 meta-analysis (31 trials, 2,648 participants)
- **Results**: Risk ratio 1.30 for treatment efficacy; significant immune improvements

**Advantages**:
- Targeted immune-active constituent
- Clinical evidence in cancer populations
- High safety profile

**Disadvantages**:
- Standardization methods vary
- Poor oral bioavailability (works via gut-immune interactions)
- Product quality highly variable

## Tincture Preparations (Western Herbalism)

### Typical Tincture Ratios

**Standard Ratios**:
- **1:3 ratio** (1 part herb to 3 parts menstruum): Stronger tincture
- **1:5 ratio** (1 part herb to 5 parts menstruum): Most common

**Menstruum** (Solvent):
- Typically 25-40% alcohol + water
- Some use glycerin-based for alcohol-free option

### Dosing

**Typical tincture dose** (based on Western herbalism practice, not clinical trials):
- 2-4 mL (40-80 drops), 2-3 times daily
- Equivalent to approximately 0.4-0.8 g dried root per mL at 1:5 ratio
- Daily dose: ~2.4-9.6 g dried herb equivalent

**Note**: No clinical trial validation for tincture preparations specifically

**Advantages**:
- Convenient dosing
- Long shelf life
- Rapid absorption (some constituents)
- Easy to adjust dose

**Disadvantages**:
- Contains alcohol (not suitable for all)
- No clinical trial validation
- May not extract polysaccharides efficiently
- Difficult to standardize

## Tea Preparations (Western/Modern Adaptation)

### Simple Tea Method

**Preparation**:
1. Use 3-9 g dried astragalus root (sliced)
2. Simmer in 2-3 cups water for 20-30 minutes
3. Strain and drink as tea

**Dose**: 1-3 cups daily

**Note**: This is a simplified version of the traditional decoction; lower dose and shorter decoction time than traditional TCM approach

**Advantages**:
- Easy to prepare
- Pleasant mild taste
- Cost-effective
- Can combine with other herbs (e.g., ginger, licorice)

**Disadvantages**:
- Lower dose than traditional or clinical trial dosing
- Shorter decoction may extract fewer constituents
- No clinical trial validation at this dose

## Choosing a Preparation

### For Evidence-Based Clinical Applications:

**Immune support (athletes, general)**:
- **Best evidence**: 500 mg/day standardized extract (0.5% isoflavonoids) - Latour 2021
- **Alternative**: 9-30 g traditional decoction (Chinese Pharmacopoeia)

**Post-COVID fatigue**:
- **Best evidence**: 500 mg twice daily (1,000 mg/day) astragalus root extract - Banihashemi 2025

**Type 2 diabetes (adjuvant)**:
- **Best evidence**: Variable in meta-analysis; 5-10 g/day capsules or traditional decoction
- **Refer to**: Hong et al. 2024 meta-analysis (20 studies, varied dosing)

**Cardiovascular (diastolic dysfunction)**:
- **Best evidence**: 10 g/day astragalus capsules - Li 2018

**Cancer support (adjuvant)**:
- **Best evidence**: Astragalus polysaccharide preparations (variable dosing)
- **Refer to**: Li et al. 2025 meta-analysis

### For Traditional Use:
- **Traditional decoction**: 9-30 g/day (Chinese Pharmacopoeia standard)
- **Powder**: 9-30 g/day (equivalent to decoction)

### For Convenience:
- **Standardized capsules**: 500-1,000 mg/day (look for isoflavonoid or astragaloside standardization)
- **Tincture**: 2-4 mL, 2-3× daily (note: no clinical trial validation)

## Quality Considerations

### When Selecting Products:

1. **Botanical verification**:
   - Ensure Astragalus membranaceus or A. membranaceus var. mongholicus
   - Avoid adulteration with other Astragalus species

2. **Standardization** (if applicable):
   - Look for % isoflavonoids or % astragalosides
   - Example: 0.5% 3-hydroxy 7-methoxy isoflavonoids (Latour 2021)

3. **Third-party testing**:
   - Heavy metals (lead, arsenic, cadmium, mercury)
   - Pesticide residues
   - Microbial contamination

4. **Form integrity**:
   - Dried root: Should match Pharmacopoeia physical description
   - Capsules/tablets: Should list mg of extract and ratio
   - Tinctures: Should list herb:menstruum ratio and alcohol %

5. **Reputable sources**:
   - GMP-certified manufacturers
   - Companies with quality control documentation
   - Products with batch testing

## Bioavailability Considerations

### Whole Extract vs. Isolated Compounds

**Key finding** (Gu et al. 2004):
- Absorption from whole Radix Astragali extract significantly higher than isolated astragaloside IV
- Suggests synergistic effects or co-factors improve bioavailability

**Implication**: Whole root preparations (decoction, powder) may be superior to single-compound extracts

### Polysaccharide Preparations

**Bioavailability challenge**:
- Poor oral absorption due to molecular weight, structure
- Likely work through gut-immune interactions

**Implication**: Systemic bioavailability less critical for immune effects

## Practical Recommendations

### For Traditional Use (TCM Approach):
- **Preparation**: Decoction
- **Dose**: 9-30 g dried root
- **Method**: Simmer 30-60 minutes in 3-4 cups water
- **Frequency**: Daily

### For Evidence-Based Modern Use:
- **Preparation**: Standardized capsules or root extract
- **Dose**: 500-1,000 mg/day (or 5-10 g for higher therapeutic dose)
- **Standardization**: Look for 0.5% isoflavonoids or astragaloside content listed
- **Quality**: Choose GMP-certified, third-party tested products
- **Duration**: Based on clinical trials - typically 6 weeks to 12 months

### For Convenience with Traditional Principles:
- **Preparation**: Powdered root in capsules
- **Dose**: 3-9 g/day (moderate dose between tea and full therapeutic)
- **Quality**: Verify botanical source and purity
- **Method**: Take with meals

## Summary

- **Most traditional**: Decoction at 9-30 g/day (Chinese Pharmacopoeia standard)
- **Most convenient**: Standardized capsules at 500-1,000 mg/day
- **Best clinical evidence**: Varies by condition (see dosing summary table)
- **Quality matters**: Botanical verification, standardization, third-party testing essential
- **Bioavailability**: Whole extracts may be superior to isolated compounds
- **No clinical data**: Tinctures, Western-style teas (lower doses)
