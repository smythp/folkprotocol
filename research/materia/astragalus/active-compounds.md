# Astragalus - Active Compounds and Bioavailability

## Primary Active Constituents

### 1. Astragalosides (Saponins)

**Main Compound: Astragaloside IV**

Astragaloside IV is considered a primary active constituent and standardization marker, despite its poor bioavailability.

#### Bioavailability Data

**Gu et al. 2004** - Comparative pharmacokinetic study in rats:
- **Oral bioavailability**: 2.2% (extremely poor)
- **Permeability** (Caco-2 cell model): Mean Papp = 6.7 ± 1.0 × 10⁻⁸ cm/sec (very low)
- **Absorption from aqueous solution**: Minimal
- **Absorption from whole Radix Astragali extract**: Significantly higher than aqueous solution
- **Bile duct ligation effect**: Did not affect absorption
- **P-glycoprotein**: Not affected by P-gp inhibitors; does not rely on active efflux transporters
- **Conclusion**: In vitro absorption studies effectively predict bioavailability; whole extract superior to isolated compound

**Zhang et al. 2006** - Preclinical pharmacokinetic study:
- **Oral bioavailability in rats**: 2.2%
- **Oral bioavailability in beagle dogs**: 7.4%
- **Half-life in male rats**:
  - 0.75 mg/kg dose: 98.1 minutes
  - 1.5 mg/kg dose: 67.2 minutes
  - 3.0 mg/kg dose: 71.8 minutes
- **Half-life in female rats**:
  - 0.75 mg/kg dose: 34.0 minutes
  - 1.5 mg/kg dose: 66.9 minutes
  - 3.0 mg/kg dose: 131.6 minutes
- **Systemic clearance (dogs)**: ~0.004 L/kg/min (slow hepatic clearance)
- **Plasma protein binding**: Approximately 83%
- **Tissue distribution**: Highest concentrations in lung and liver
- **Brain distribution**: Limited (does not readily cross blood-brain barrier)
- **Elimination**: Only 50% recovered in urine and feces combined
- **Human study note**: One human study attempted to measure astragalosides in serum but could not detect any

#### Cycloastragenol

A derivative/metabolite of astragaloside IV:
- **Safety**: Well-studied (Szabo 2014)
- **NOAEL**: >150 mg/kg/day in rats
- **Genotoxicity**: Not mutagenic at ≤5,000 μg/plate in bacterial strains
- **Applications**: Sometimes marketed for anti-aging properties
- **Note**: Derivative compound, not found in whole root extracts in significant amounts

### 2. Astragalus Polysaccharides (APS)

**Characteristics**:
- High molecular weight complex carbohydrates
- Primary immunomodulatory compounds in many studies
- Used in cancer immunotherapy research

**Bioavailability Challenges**:
- Low bioavailability due to:
  - Excessive molecular weight
  - Bulky structure
  - Low solubility
  - Negatively charged characteristics
- Despite poor oral bioavailability, demonstrate clinical effects (likely through gut-immune interactions)

**Clinical Evidence**:
- Li et al. 2025 meta-analysis: 31 trials, 2,648 participants with malignant tumors
- Significant improvements in immune markers (CD3, CD4, CD4/CD8 ratio)
- Risk ratio 1.30 for treatment efficacy [95% CI: 1.21, 1.39], p < 0.01

### 3. Flavonoids (Isoflavonoids)

**Key Compounds**:
- 3-hydroxy 7-methoxy isoflavonoids
- Used as standardization markers in some extracts

**Standardization Example**:
- Latour et al. 2021 study used standardized extract: 0.5% 3-hydroxy 7-methoxy isoflavonoids
- Dose: 500 mg daily
- Application: Exercise-induced immunosuppression in athletes
- Results: Significant immune system stabilization (maintained NK cells, Tregs)

**Bioavailability**: Limited data on flavonoid bioavailability from astragalus specifically

## Standardization Approaches

### Chinese Pharmacopoeia Standards

**Physical Specifications**:
- Botanical source: A. membranaceus var. mongholicus or A. membranaceus
- Shape: Cylindrical
- Length: 30-90 cm
- Diameter: 1-3.5 cm
- Surface color: Light brownish yellow or light brownish
- Characteristics: Irregular longitudinal wrinkles; fracture surface with strong fiber texture, powdery appearance
- Daily dosage: 9-30 g

### Commercial Standardized Products

**1. Isoflavonoid-Standardized Extracts**
- **Example**: Latour et al. 2021 study
- **Standardization**: 0.5% 3-hydroxy 7-methoxy isoflavonoids
- **Dose**: 500 mg daily
- **Application**: Athletic immunosuppression

**2. Multi-Herb Standardized Mixtures**
- **Product**: HT042
- **Composition**: Astragalus membranaceus + Eleutherococcus senticosus + Phlomis umbrosa
- **Studies**: Lee et al. 2018 (pediatric growth), Song et al. 2017 (safety)
- **Safety**: LD50 >5,000 mg/kg; NOAEL 4,000 mg/kg/day

**3. Polysaccharide-Rich Preparations**
- Used in cancer immunotherapy studies
- Standardization methods vary
- Focus on immune-active polysaccharide content

### Challenges in Standardization

1. **Multiple active constituent classes**: Saponins, polysaccharides, flavonoids
2. **Synergistic effects**: Whole extract more effective than isolated compounds
3. **Poor bioavailability of marker compounds**: Astragaloside IV barely detectable in human serum
4. **Variation between species**: A. membranaceus vs A. membranaceus var. mongholicus
5. **Lack of harmonization**: Different studies use different standardization approaches

## Bioavailability Enhancement Strategies

### Whole Extract vs. Isolated Compounds

**Key Finding** (Gu et al. 2004):
- Absorption from whole Radix Astragali extract significantly higher than isolated astragaloside IV in aqueous solution
- Suggests synergistic effects or co-factors in whole extract improve bioavailability

### Formulation Considerations

1. **Traditional decoction**: 9-30 g dried root
   - Water extraction
   - Contains multiple constituent classes
   - Traditional method may optimize bioavailability through synergy

2. **Standardized extracts**: 500-1,000 mg daily
   - Concentrated to specific marker compounds
   - More convenient dosing
   - Variable bioavailability depending on standardization method

3. **Polysaccharide preparations**: Variable dosing
   - May work through gut-immune interactions rather than systemic absorption
   - Bioavailability less critical for mechanism of action

## Pharmacokinetic Summary

### Astragaloside IV
- **Oral bioavailability**: 2.2-7.4% (very poor)
- **Permeability**: Very low (Papp 6.7 × 10⁻⁸ cm/sec)
- **Half-life**: 34-131 minutes (dose and sex dependent)
- **Protein binding**: ~83%
- **Tissue distribution**: Lung > liver >> brain
- **Clearance**: Slow hepatic clearance (~0.004 L/kg/min in dogs)
- **Elimination**: Only 50% recovered in urine/feces; remaining fate unknown
- **Human detectability**: One study could not detect astragalosides in human serum

### Astragalus Polysaccharides
- **Oral bioavailability**: Low (due to molecular weight, structure, solubility)
- **Mechanism**: Likely gut-immune interactions rather than systemic absorption
- **Clinical efficacy**: Demonstrated despite poor bioavailability

### Isoflavonoids
- **Bioavailability data**: Limited
- **Standardization marker**: 3-hydroxy 7-methoxy isoflavonoids (0.5% in Latour 2021)
- **Clinical efficacy**: Demonstrated in immunomodulation study

## Clinical Implications

### Paradox of Efficacy Despite Poor Bioavailability

**Observations**:
1. Astragaloside IV has extremely poor bioavailability (2.2-7.4%)
2. One human study could not detect astragalosides in serum
3. Yet multiple clinical trials demonstrate significant effects

**Possible Explanations**:
1. **Gut-immune interactions**: Polysaccharides may work locally in gut-associated lymphoid tissue
2. **Multiple active constituents**: Effects not solely dependent on astragaloside IV
3. **Metabolites**: Active metabolites not measured in bioavailability studies
4. **Synergistic effects**: Whole extract components enhance absorption/activity
5. **Low threshold for activity**: Even minimal systemic absorption sufficient for effects

### Practical Recommendations

1. **Prefer whole extracts over isolated compounds**: Evidence suggests synergistic benefits
2. **Traditional preparations retain value**: Decoctions may optimize bioavailability naturally
3. **Standardization useful but not definitive**: Marker compounds may not predict efficacy
4. **Dosing matters**: Clinical trials use 500-1,000 mg standardized extracts or 5-30 g traditional preparations
5. **Multi-constituent activity**: Don't assume single compound responsible for effects

## Research Gaps

1. **Human pharmacokinetics**: Very limited data; one study couldn't detect astragalosides in serum
2. **Active metabolites**: Unknown if astragaloside metabolites contribute to effects
3. **Absorption enhancement**: Need formulation strategies to improve bioavailability
4. **Polysaccharide mechanisms**: How do poorly absorbed polysaccharides exert systemic immune effects?
5. **Synergy studies**: Which combinations of constituents optimize effects?
6. **Tissue-specific distribution in humans**: Animal data limited; no human data
7. **Optimal standardization**: Which markers best predict clinical efficacy?
8. **Species differences**: A. membranaceus vs A. membranaceus var. mongholicus constituent profiles

## Summary

- **Primary compounds**: Astragalosides (saponins), astragalus polysaccharides, isoflavonoids
- **Standardization markers**: Astragaloside IV, 3-hydroxy 7-methoxy isoflavonoids
- **Bioavailability paradox**: Astragaloside IV has 2.2-7.4% oral bioavailability, barely detectable in humans, yet clinical efficacy demonstrated
- **Whole extract superiority**: Whole extracts more effective than isolated compounds
- **Clinical dosing**: 500-1,000 mg standardized extracts or 9-30 g traditional decoctions
- **Mechanisms unclear**: Clinical effects may involve gut-immune interactions, synergistic constituents, or unmeasured metabolites
- **Research needed**: Human pharmacokinetics, absorption enhancement, mechanism of action studies
