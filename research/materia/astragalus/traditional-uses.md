# Astragalus - Traditional Uses

## Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)

### Official Recognition

**Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2020**
- **Official Name**: Radix Astragali (Huangqi/Huang Qi)
- **Botanical Definition**: Dried root of Astragalus membranaceus (Fisch.) Bge. var. mongholicus (Bge.) Hsiao or Astragalus membranaceus (Fisch.) Bge.
- **Status**: Official monograph in Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2020 edition
- **Prevalence**: Listed in 204 species of patented traditional Chinese medicines, accounting for approximately 14% of total number of traditional medicines (2015 edition)

### Physical Characteristics (Pharmacopoeia Standards)

- **Shape**: Cylindrical
- **Length**: 30-90 cm
- **Diameter**: 1-3.5 cm
- **Surface color**: Light brownish yellow or light brownish
- **Characteristics**: Irregular longitudinal wrinkles or grooves; fracture surface with strong fiber texture showing powdery appearance

### Traditional Dosing

**Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2020 Recommendations**:
- **Dose**: 9-30 g daily
- **Preparation**: Decoction (water extraction)
- **Administration**: Oral

This traditional dosage range (9-30 g) aligns with clinical trial dosing:
- Clinical studies have used 5-10 g/day in capsule form
- Traditional decoctions typically use the full 9-30 g range
- Some studies examined doses up to 60 g for up to 4 months with no major safety concerns

### Primary Traditional Applications

Based on Traditional Chinese Medicine principles:

1. **Qi Tonification**
   - Astragalus is considered a primary "Qi tonic" in TCM
   - Used to strengthen vital energy and combat fatigue
   - Traditional indication for "Qi deficiency" patterns

2. **Immune Support**
   - Traditional use for "strengthening the exterior" (Wei Qi)
   - Used to prevent recurrent infections
   - Traditional application in those prone to colds and flu

3. **Cardiovascular Support**
   - Traditional use for heart Qi deficiency
   - Applied in patterns of poor circulation
   - Used for cardiovascular weakness in TCM diagnosis

4. **Metabolic/Endocrine**
   - Traditional application in "wasting and thirsting" disorders (correlates with modern diabetes)
   - Used in patterns of fluid metabolism dysfunction

5. **Kidney Support**
   - Traditional application in kidney Qi deficiency
   - Used for edema and fluid retention patterns

### Traditional Preparation Methods

**Classical TCM Preparations**:

1. **Decoction (Tang)**
   - Most common traditional preparation
   - Dried root pieces boiled in water
   - Typically 9-30 g of dried root per decoction
   - Boiled for 30-60 minutes
   - Consumed as tea throughout the day

2. **Powdered Form**
   - Dried root ground into powder
   - Mixed with water or honey
   - Traditional dose similar to decoction (9-30 g)

3. **Wine Extract**
   - Root macerated in rice wine or other spirits
   - Traditional tonic preparation
   - Used for long-term supplementation

4. **Combined Formulas**
   - Frequently combined with other herbs in TCM formulas
   - Classic combinations include:
     - With Angelica sinensis (Dang Gui) for blood deficiency
     - With Ginseng for severe Qi deficiency
     - With Rehmannia for kidney support

## European/Western Herbal Medicine

### Regulatory Status

**World Health Organization (WHO)**:
- **Status**: No monograph published as of 2026
- **Note**: Astragalus has not been included in WHO Selected Medicinal Plants monograph series (Volumes 1-4)
- **Reason**: Primarily used in Traditional Chinese Medicine systems rather than European/Western herbal medicine where WHO monographs focused

**German Commission E**:
- **Status**: No monograph published
- **Note**: Commission E did not evaluate Astragalus membranaceus
- **Reason**: Primarily assessed herbs used in German phytotherapy; Astragalus is primarily an Asian medicinal plant

### Western Adoption

While Astragalus lacks European traditional use and regulatory monographs, it has been adopted into Western herbal practice in recent decades, primarily based on:

1. TCM traditional knowledge transfer
2. Growing clinical research evidence
3. Integration into functional medicine practices
4. Use in integrative oncology

**Modern Western Applications** (adapted from TCM):
- Immune system support
- Adaptogenic herb for stress
- Cardiovascular health
- Adjuvant cancer support
- Chronic fatigue

## Other Traditional Medicine Systems

### Ayurveda
- **Status**: Not traditionally used in Ayurvedic medicine
- **Note**: Astragalus membranaceus is native to China, Mongolia, and Korea; not part of the Indian materia medica
- **Confusion**: Sometimes confused with other Astragalus species or similar-sounding herbs, but A. membranaceus is not an Ayurvedic herb

### Traditional Korean Medicine
- **Status**: Used similarly to TCM
- **Common Name**: Hwanggi (황기)
- **Applications**: Parallel to Chinese traditional uses
- **Source**: Korea is within the native range of A. membranaceus var. mongholicus

### Traditional Mongolian Medicine
- **Status**: Used in traditional Mongolian medicine
- **Note**: Mongolia is part of the native range for A. membranaceus var. mongholicus
- **Applications**: Similar to TCM applications

## Historical Context

### Geographic Origin
- **Native Range**: China, Mongolia, Korea
- **Species**: Two recognized species
  - Astragalus membranaceus (Fisch.) Bge.
  - Astragalus membranaceus (Fisch.) Bge. var. mongholicus (Bge.) Hsiao

### Historical Documentation
- **Long history of use in TCM**: Documented in classical Chinese medical texts for over 2,000 years
- **Classical texts**: Referenced in major TCM pharmacopeias throughout Chinese history
- **Modern recognition**: Official inclusion in Chinese Pharmacopoeia (2020 edition)

### Traditional vs. Modern Use Alignment

The traditional TCM applications show remarkable alignment with modern clinical evidence:

| Traditional Use | Modern Clinical Evidence |
|----------------|--------------------------|
| Qi tonification | Improved energy, reduced fatigue (post-COVID, cancer-related) |
| Immune strengthening | Immunomodulation (CD3, CD4, NK cells, cytokines) |
| Cardiovascular support | Diastolic dysfunction improvement |
| "Wasting and thirsting" | Type 2 diabetes glycemic control |
| Kidney support | Diabetic nephropathy improvements |

## Traditional Contraindications

Traditional Chinese Medicine identifies certain patterns where Astragalus should be used with caution or avoided:

1. **Acute infections**: TCM traditionally avoids "tonifying" during active acute illness
2. **"Excess" patterns**: Not appropriate for "excess heat" or "yin deficiency with heat"
3. **Pregnancy**: Caution advised (modern data: insufficient safety information)

## Summary

- **Primary Traditional System**: Traditional Chinese Medicine
- **Official Recognition**: Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2020
- **Traditional Dose**: 9-30 g daily (decoction)
- **Western Regulatory Status**: No WHO or Commission E monographs (not a European traditional herb)
- **Historical Use**: Over 2,000 years in TCM
- **Modern Adoption**: Increasingly used in Western integrative medicine based on TCM knowledge and clinical research
- **Traditional-Modern Alignment**: Strong correspondence between traditional applications and clinical evidence
