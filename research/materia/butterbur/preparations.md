# Butterbur (Petasites hybridus) - Preparations & Dosing

## Critical Safety Notice

**ONLY PA-FREE, STANDARDIZED EXTRACTS SHOULD BE USED CLINICALLY**

Raw plant material and non-standardized preparations contain hepatotoxic pyrrolizidine alkaloids (PAs). All dosing recommendations below refer to pharmaceutical-grade, PA-free, standardized extracts only.

---

## Standardized Commercial Extracts

### Petadolex® (Root Extract)

**Extraction method:** Carbon dioxide (CO₂) supercritical extraction

**Plant part:** Root (rhizome)

**Standardization:**
- Minimum 7.5 mg petasin and isopetasin per dose
- Each capsule typically contains 50 mg or 75 mg of standardized extract

**Pyrrolizidine alkaloids:**
- Below limits of detection
- Verified PA-free through analytical testing

**Dosage forms:**
- Capsules: 50 mg, 75 mg

**Primary indication:** Migraine prophylaxis

**Clinical evidence:**
- Used in major RCTs (Lipton 2004, Grossmann 2000)
- Approximately 2.3 million patient-months of exposure (1992-2016)
- Only butterbur extract with proven clinical efficacy in migraine prevention (per Diener 2018)

---

### Ze 339 (Leaf Extract)

**Extraction method:** Carbon dioxide (CO₂) extract

**Plant part:** Leaves (not root)

**Standardization:**
- 8 mg petasines per tablet
- Petasines used as analytical marker

**Pyrrolizidine alkaloids:**
- PA-free (verified absence)

**Dosage forms:**
- Tablets: 8 mg petasines each

**Primary indication:** Allergic rhinitis (seasonal)

**Clinical evidence:**
- Used in comparative trial vs. cetirizine (Schapowal 2002)
- Anti-inflammatory activity documented (Thomet 2002)
- Large postmarketing surveillance (Käufeler 2006: 580 patients; Blosa 2021: 226 patients)

## Clinical Dosing by Indication

### Migraine Prophylaxis

#### Adults - Evidence-Based Dosing

**Optimal dose (Lipton 2004):**
- **75 mg twice daily (150 mg/day total)**
- Timing: Morning and evening
- Duration: 4 months in clinical trials
- Evidence level: Strongest - statistically significant vs. placebo (p=0.0012)
- Expected outcome: 48% reduction in attack frequency; 68% of patients achieve ≥50% reduction

**Lower dose (Grossmann 2000, Agosti 2006):**
- **50 mg twice daily (100 mg/day total)**
- Duration: 3-4 months
- Evidence level: Some evidence, not statistically significant vs. placebo (p=0.127)
- Expected outcome: 36% reduction in attack frequency (less than 150 mg dose)
- Note: May be considered as starting dose or for patients unable to tolerate higher dose

**Practical administration:**
- Take with food to minimize gastrointestinal effects
- Consistent timing (e.g., 8 AM and 8 PM)
- Petadolex capsules: Take 75 mg capsule twice daily, or 50 mg capsule twice daily for lower dose

#### Pediatric Dosing (Ages 6-17)

**Evidence from Pothmann 2005:**
- **50-150 mg daily** (not per dose, but total daily amount)
- Duration: 4 months
- Flexible dosing based on age/weight (specific weight-based guidelines not provided in study)
- Expected outcome: 77% achieve ≥50% reduction in attack frequency
- Safety: Excellent (7.4% adverse event rate, primarily burping)

**Practical considerations:**
- Start at lower end of range (50 mg) for younger/smaller children
- Titrate up based on response and tolerability
- Medical supervision recommended
- Not studied in children <6 years

#### Treatment Duration

**Initial trial:**
- 4 months standard duration based on RCT evidence
- Some trials used 12 weeks (3 months) with good results

**Reassessment:**
- Evaluate efficacy after 12-16 weeks
- If effective, discuss risk-benefit of continuation
- Safety data extends to 16 weeks; longer-term safety not well-established

**Discontinuation:**
- No evidence of rebound headaches upon discontinuation
- Can likely be stopped without taper (but not specifically studied)

### Allergic Rhinitis (Seasonal)

#### Standard Dosing (Ze 339)

**Evidence-based dose:**
- **One tablet (8 mg petasines) 3-4 times daily**
- Total daily dose: 24-32 mg petasines
- Duration: Typically 2 weeks, can be used throughout allergy season

**Studies supporting this dose:**
- Schapowal 2002: One Ze 339 tablet four times daily for 2 weeks (compared favorably to cetirizine)
- Thomet 2002: Three times daily for one week (significant symptom improvement by day 5)
- Käufeler 2006: Average of 2 tablets daily for 2 weeks (90% symptom improvement)
- Blosa 2021: 1-3 tablets daily, mean duration 63 days

**Flexible dosing:**
- Can adjust from 1-4 tablets daily based on symptom severity
- Higher doses (3-4 tablets) for more severe symptoms
- Lower maintenance doses (1-2 tablets) as symptoms improve

**Practical administration:**
- Timing: Spread doses throughout day (e.g., breakfast, lunch, dinner, bedtime for 4x daily)
- Can be taken with or without food
- Start at onset of allergy season or when symptoms begin

#### Combination Therapy

**Blosa 2021 findings:**
- 41.5% of patients used Ze 339 with other antiallergic/sympathomimetic drugs
- Combination therapy did not exceed monotherapy benefits
- Can be safely combined if needed, but monotherapy often sufficient

**Käufeler 2006 findings:**
- 44% received additional antiallergic medication
- Combination therapy did not exceed monotherapy benefits

**Implication:** Ze 339 effective as monotherapy; adding antihistamines may not provide additional benefit

---

### Asthma

#### Dosing from Danesch 2004

**Dose:** 50 mg/day (Petadolex)
- Duration: 2 months in study (with optional 2 additional months)
- Sample: 80 participants (64 adults, 16 children/adolescents)
- Study design: Open trial, concomitant asthma medications permitted

**Outcomes:**
- Asthma attack frequency, duration, and severity decreased
- Peak flow and FEV1 improved
- >40% of patients reduced baseline asthma medication use

**Note:**
- Limited evidence for asthma indication
- Not a primary indication for butterbur
- Should not replace standard asthma controller medications
- Consider only as adjunctive therapy under medical supervision

## Preparation Forms NOT Recommended

### Crude Herb (Leaves, Root)

**Safety concerns:**
- Contains pyrrolizidine alkaloids (hepatotoxic)
- Variable potency (inconsistent therapeutic compounds)
- No standardization
- Cannot guarantee PA removal

**Regulatory status:**
- German Commission E could not justify use of leaf for traditional indications due to safety concerns
- Risk of veno-occlusive liver disease
- Risk of hepatotoxicity-related liver failure

**Recommendation:** Do not use crude plant material

---

### Traditional Tea Preparations

**Why not recommended:**
- Water extraction does not remove pyrrolizidine alkaloids effectively
- PAs are somewhat water-soluble and will be present in tea
- Petasin content unpredictable
- No way to standardize dose
- Risk of PA exposure with each cup

**Historical context:**
- Traditional use in Europe included teas
- Modern understanding of PA toxicity makes this preparation unsafe
- Historical practitioners were unaware of PA risks

**Recommendation:** Do not use butterbur tea

---

### Traditional Tincture Preparations

**Alcohol extraction concerns:**
- Alcohol extracts both therapeutic compounds (petasins) and toxic compounds (PAs)
- No selective removal of PAs
- Traditional tincture ratios (e.g., 1:5, 1:10) not studied clinically
- Dose standardization impossible without petasin quantification

**Why CO₂ extraction is superior:**
- Selective for sesquiterpenes (petasins)
- Removes or minimizes pyrrolizidine alkaloids
- Reproducible and standardizable
- Pharmaceutical-grade purity

**Recommendation:** Only use CO₂-extracted, standardized preparations

---

### Homeopathic Preparations

**Safety:** Likely safe due to extreme dilution (no meaningful PA content)

**Efficacy:** No evidence of efficacy for homeopathic butterbur preparations

**Note:** Homeopathic preparations are pharmacologically distinct from herbal extracts and lack the clinical evidence base of standardized extracts like Petadolex or Ze 339.

## Quality Verification Checklist

### Before Using Any Butterbur Product

**Essential criteria:**
1. ☐ Product explicitly states "PA-free" or "Pyrrolizidine alkaloid-free"
2. ☐ Standardized to petasin/isopetasin content (mg per dose stated)
3. ☐ CO₂ extraction method used
4. ☐ Manufacturer provides certificate of analysis (COA)
5. ☐ Third-party testing verification available
6. ☐ GMP (Good Manufacturing Practices) certified facility
7. ☐ Product matches clinical trial specifications (Petadolex or Ze 339 equivalent)

**Red flags (do not use if any present):**
- ☐ No mention of PA content/removal
- ☐ "Wild-crafted" or "whole herb" without PA testing
- ☐ No standardization to petasins
- ☐ Claims like "natural" or "traditional preparation" without safety data
- ☐ Crude herb, tea, or non-CO₂ extracted products
- ☐ Manufacturer cannot provide COA

## Dosing Considerations

### Timing Optimization

**Migraine prophylaxis (BID dosing):**
- Morning dose: 7-9 AM (with breakfast)
- Evening dose: 7-9 PM (with dinner)
- Consistent timing helps maintain steady therapeutic levels
- Taking with food may reduce GI side effects (burping)

**Allergic rhinitis (TID or QID dosing):**
- Spread throughout day for continuous symptom control
- Examples:
  - TID: 8 AM, 2 PM, 8 PM
  - QID: 8 AM, 12 PM, 5 PM, 9 PM
- Timing flexibility acceptable (not as critical as migraine dosing)

### Missed Dose

**General guidance:**
- Take missed dose as soon as remembered
- If near time of next dose, skip missed dose (do not double)
- Do not take extra to "catch up"

### Dose Titration

**Starting therapy:**
- Can begin at target dose (no titration required based on evidence)
- Some patients may prefer starting at lower dose (50 mg BID for migraine) and increasing after 1-2 weeks if well-tolerated

**Discontinuing therapy:**
- No evidence requiring taper
- Can likely discontinue abruptly without rebound (but not specifically studied)
- If using for extended period, discuss discontinuation plan with provider

## Storage

**Optimal conditions:**
- Room temperature (15-30°C / 59-86°F)
- Protect from light
- Protect from moisture
- Keep in original container until use

**Stability considerations:**
- CO₂ extracts are generally stable when properly stored
- Check expiration date on product
- Discard if capsules/tablets show discoloration or degradation

## Administration Tips

### Minimizing GI Side Effects (Burping)

**Strategies:**
1. Take with meals (food in stomach)
2. Start with lower dose if concerned about tolerance
3. Swallow with adequate water
4. Avoid lying down immediately after taking
5. Consider taking evening dose earlier (e.g., 6 PM vs 9 PM)

**If burping persists:**
- Usually mild and tolerable (most common adverse effect in trials)
- Typically improves after first few weeks
- Rarely requires discontinuation (no discontinuations due to AEs in most trials)

### For Pediatric Patients

**Practical tips:**
1. Capsules may be opened and mixed with food if child cannot swallow pills (check with manufacturer about stability)
2. Consistent daily routine helps with adherence
3. Parent/guardian should supervise administration
4. Track migraine frequency in diary to assess response

## Monitoring During Treatment

### Self-Monitoring

**Migraine patients should track:**
- Migraine attack frequency (number per month)
- Attack severity (0-10 scale)
- Duration of attacks (hours)
- Acute medication use (e.g., triptans, NSAIDs)
- Adverse effects (especially GI symptoms)

**Allergic rhinitis patients should track:**
- Symptom severity (rhinorrhea, congestion, sneezing, itching)
- Rescue medication use (antihistamines, decongestants)
- Quality of life impact
- Adverse effects

### Medical Monitoring

**Baseline (before starting):**
- Liver function tests: AST, ALT, ALP, GGT, bilirubin
- Medical history review (especially liver disease, pregnancy, medications)

**During treatment:**
- Liver function tests: Repeat periodically
  - Suggested: At 4-6 weeks, then at 3 months
  - More frequent if baseline abnormalities or symptoms
- Clinical assessment of efficacy (at 4 weeks, 8 weeks, 12 weeks)
- Adverse effect assessment at each visit

**When to stop and seek medical attention:**
- Jaundice (yellowing of skin or eyes)
- Dark urine
- Light-colored stools
- Severe fatigue
- Abdominal pain (especially right upper quadrant)
- Significant liver enzyme elevations on testing

## Switching Between Products

### From Another Migraine Prophylactic to Butterbur

**Considerations:**
- May need to taper prior prophylactic (e.g., beta-blockers, anticonvulsants)
- Can start butterbur during taper or after discontinuation
- Allow 4 months to assess butterbur efficacy before concluding it's ineffective
- Keep diary to compare frequency before vs. during butterbur treatment

### From Antihistamine to Butterbur (for Allergic Rhinitis)

**Considerations:**
- Can switch abruptly (no taper needed for most antihistamines)
- May start butterbur while discontinuing antihistamine
- Assess response after 5-7 days (Thomet 2002 showed improvement by day 5)
- Can use rescue antihistamine if needed during transition

### Between Butterbur Products

**If switching brands:**
- Verify new product is PA-free and standardized
- Ensure equivalent petasin/isopetasin content per dose
- Monitor for any change in efficacy or adverse effects
- If possible, avoid switching if current product is effective and well-tolerated (consistency preferred)

## Special Populations Dosing

### Pediatric (Ages 6-17)

**Migraine:**
- Dose: 50-150 mg daily
- Start low (50 mg), increase based on response
- Evidence: Pothmann 2005 (108 children)

**Allergic rhinitis:**
- No specific pediatric dosing studies for Ze 339
- Danesch 2004 included 16 children/adolescents for asthma (50 mg/day)
- Consider lower end of adult dose range with medical supervision

### Elderly

**No specific studies in elderly populations**

**General considerations:**
- Start at standard adult dose (no evidence requiring reduction)
- May have more comorbidities (liver disease, polypharmacy)
- Closer monitoring may be prudent
- No age-based contraindication if liver function normal

### Renal Impairment

**No specific data**
- Petasins are not primarily renally eliminated (presumed hepatic metabolism)
- No dose adjustment likely needed
- No specific studies to confirm safety

### Hepatic Impairment

**Contraindicated**
- Avoid use in patients with pre-existing liver disease
- Theoretical increased risk even with PA-free products
- Two cases of reversible liver enzyme elevations with Petadolex (Diener 2018)

## Treatment Failure: What to Consider

### If Butterbur is Ineffective

**For migraine (after 4 months adequate trial):**
1. Verify product quality (PA-free, standardized, proper dose)
2. Confirm adherence (missing doses?)
3. Consider if dose was optimal (150 mg/day is most effective)
4. Reassess diagnosis (is it truly migraine?)
5. Evaluate other contributing factors (medication overuse headache, triggers)
6. Consider alternative prophylactic agent

**For allergic rhinitis (after 2 weeks):**
1. Verify adequate dosing (3-4 tablets daily may be needed)
2. Consider if allergy season is particularly severe this year
3. Assess for non-allergic rhinitis component
4. Consider addition of nasal corticosteroid (different mechanism)
5. May trial antihistamine or alternative therapy

## Summary: Practical Prescribing

### Migraine Prophylaxis (First-Line Approach)

1. **Product:** Petadolex or equivalent PA-free, CO₂-extracted, standardized root extract
2. **Dose:** 75 mg BID (morning and evening with food)
3. **Duration:** 4-month trial
4. **Monitoring:** Baseline LFTs, repeat at 4-6 weeks and 3 months
5. **Efficacy assessment:** Track migraine frequency; expect 40-50% reduction
6. **Safety:** Excellent if PA-free product used; main AE is burping

### Allergic Rhinitis (Seasonal)

1. **Product:** Ze 339 or equivalent PA-free, CO₂-extracted, standardized leaf extract
2. **Dose:** 1 tablet (8 mg petasines) TID-QID
3. **Duration:** Throughout allergy season (2 weeks to several months)
4. **Monitoring:** Baseline LFTs if using >2 weeks; clinical symptom assessment
5. **Efficacy assessment:** Expect improvement in symptoms by 5-7 days
6. **Safety:** Excellent; fewer side effects than sedating antihistamines

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Only use PA-free, standardized extracts** - Crude herb, teas, and non-standardized products are unsafe
2. **Petadolex for migraine (150 mg/day), Ze 339 for allergic rhinitis (24-32 mg petasines/day)** - These products have clinical evidence
3. **Duration matters** - Allow 4 months for migraine, 2 weeks for allergic rhinitis
4. **Monitoring is essential** - Baseline and periodic liver function tests recommended
5. **Common adverse effect (burping) is mild** - Rarely requires discontinuation
6. **Product quality varies** - Verify PA-free status and standardization before purchase
7. **Not for pregnancy, lactation, or liver disease** - Absolute contraindications
8. **Effective alternative to conventional therapies** - Similar efficacy with different side effect profile
