# Feverfew (Tanacetum parthenium) - Active Compounds

## Primary Bioactive Compound: Parthenolide

### Chemical Classification
- **Class**: Germacranolide-type sesquiterpene lactone
- **Status**: Most potent bioactive ingredient in feverfew
- **Primary Marker**: Used for standardization and quality control of feverfew preparations
- **Source**: Kashkooe et al. 2024 (Biomedicines. 2024;12(10):2297)

### Mechanism of Action

#### NF-κB Pathway Inhibition
- **Primary Mechanism**: Inhibition of nuclear factor kappa B (NF-κB) pathway
- **Effect**: Reduction in pro-inflammatory cytokines, especially tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α)
- **Therapeutic Relevance**: Anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties relevant to migraine prevention
- **Source**: Kashkooe et al. 2024

#### MAPK Pathway
- **Pathway**: Mitogen-Activated Protein Kinase (MAPK) pathway
- **Effect**: Anticancer effects (preclinical)
- **Clinical Status**: Limited by poor bioavailability (see below)
- **Source**: Kashkooe et al. 2024

#### Antiplatelet Activity
- **Effect**: Antisecretory effects on blood platelets
- **Active Compound**: Parthenolide appears primarily responsible for this activity
- **Source**: Heptinstall et al. 1992 (J Pharm Pharmacol. 1992 May;44(5):391-5)

## Bioavailability - Critical Limitation

### Poor Oral Bioavailability

#### Curry et al. 2004 Phase I Study
- **Study Type**: Phase I dose escalation trial in cancer patients
- **Preparation**: Feverfew oral capsules with standardized parthenolide
- **Doses Tested**: 1, 2, 3, and 4 mg daily
- **Schedule**: 28-day cycle
- **Detection Method**: Solid phase extraction and mass spectrometry
- **Detection Limit**: 0.5 ng/ml

**Key Finding**: **Parthenolide administered at doses up to 4 mg daily had no detectable concentration in plasma**

#### Implications
1. **Therapeutic Limitation**: Poor bioavailability limits systemic therapeutic applications
2. **Migraine Efficacy Paradox**: Despite poor systemic bioavailability, feverfew demonstrates clinical efficacy for migraine prevention
   - May suggest local effects, gut-mediated mechanisms, or active metabolites not measured
3. **Anticancer Applications**: Not clinically viable due to lack of systemic exposure
4. **Dose Requirements**: Higher doses may be needed, but absorption appears to plateau

#### Future Development
- **Synthetic Analog**: Dimethylamino-parthenolide (DMAPT) developed as more hydrophilic form
- **Improved Bioavailability**: DMAPT designed with greater bioavailability than native parthenolide
- **Status**: Under development for potential therapeutic applications requiring systemic exposure
- **Source**: Curry et al. 2004 (Invest New Drugs. 2004 Aug;22(3):299-305)

## Additional Bioactive Compounds

### Sesquiterpene Lactones
- **Compound Class**: Multiple sesquiterpene lactones present
- **Primary Marker**: Parthenolide (most studied)
- **Activity**: Anti-inflammatory, antiplatelet
- **Source**: Pareek et al. 2011 (Pharmacogn Rev. 2011;5(9):103-110)

### Flavonoid Glycosides
- **Presence**: Contains various flavonoid glycosides
- **Activity**: Likely contribute to antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects
- **Specifics**: Not detailed in clinical literature reviewed
- **Source**: Pareek et al. 2011

### Pinenes
- **Presence**: Contains pinenes (volatile compounds)
- **Activity**: May contribute to overall pharmacological profile
- **Specifics**: Limited detail in clinical literature
- **Source**: Pareek et al. 2011

## Standardization Requirements

### Regulatory Standards

#### Minimum Parthenolide Content
- **Standard**: ≥0.2% parthenolide
- **Source**: Multiple pharmacopoeia and regulatory standards
- **Purpose**: Ensure therapeutic efficacy and product consistency

#### Pharmacopoeia Specifications

**British Pharmacopoeia 2009**
- Official monograph for feverfew
- Specifies quality standards including parthenolide content

**European Pharmacopoeia 2008**
- 6th edition includes feverfew monograph
- Quality standards for medicinal use

**United States Pharmacopeia 2007**
- 30th edition botanical monograph
- Published by United States Pharmacopeial Convention (USP)

**American Herbal Pharmacopoeia 2011**
- Botanical Pharmacognosy on Microscopic Characterization of Botanical Medicines
- Includes feverfew specifications

#### Canadian Regulatory Standard
- **Requirement**: 125 mg of dried feverfew leaf preparation containing at least 0.2% parthenolide daily
- **Source**: Health Protection Branch Drug Identification Number (DIN) for feverfew product

### Stability Requirements

#### Parthenolide Degradation
- **Recommendation**: Feverfew should be dispensed in properly stabilized form
- **Standard**: Parthenolide content must not degrade to less than 90% of original content
- **Importance**: Ensures consistent therapeutic effect throughout product shelf life
- **Source**: Summary recommendations from clinical literature

## Quality Control Issues - Major Clinical Concern

### Heptinstall et al. 1992 - Foundational Standardization Study
- **Study Type**: Analytical study of parthenolide content in authenticated and commercial feverfew
- **Key Findings**:
  - Authenticated Tanacetum parthenium grown in UK: **High parthenolide levels in leaves, flowering tops, and seeds**
  - **Low parthenolide levels in stalks and roots**
  - **Commercial feverfew products varied widely in parthenolide content**
  - **In some commercial products, parthenolide was not detected**
- **Recommendation**: Manufacturers should use measurements of parthenolide as means of standardization and quality control
- **Reference**: J Pharm Pharmacol. 1992 May;44(5):391-5. PMID: 1359053

### Nelson et al. 2002 - Commercial Product Analysis
- **Study Type**: Quality control analysis of commercial feverfew products
- **Reference**: Am J Health Syst Pharm. 2002 Aug 15;59(16):1527-1531. PMID: 12185827

#### Parthenolide Content Variations

**Per Dosage Form**:
- **Range**: 0.02 to 3.0 mg parthenolide per capsule/tablet
- **Variation**: **150-fold difference**
- **Clinical Significance**: Enormous variation in therapeutic dose between products

**Percent Parthenolide**:
- **Range**: 0.14% to 0.74%
- **Variation**: 5.3-fold difference
- **Clinical Significance**: Inconsistent standardization across manufacturers

**Daily Parthenolide Intake** (based on label recommendations):
- **Range**: 0.06 to 9.7 mg/day
- **Variation**: **160-fold difference**
- **Clinical Significance**: Patients following label directions could receive wildly different therapeutic doses

**Dried Leaf Intake**:
- **Range**: 225 to 2,246 mg/day
- **Variation**: 10-fold difference

**Feverfew Leaf per Capsule**:
- **Range**: 25 to 500 mg
- **Label Accuracy**: Generally matched label claims (unlike parthenolide content)

#### Critical Finding: Extract Products
- **Finding**: **NO commercial feverfew extracts tested contained the label-claimed parthenolide content**
- **Implication**: Severe quality control and truth-in-labeling issues

#### Batch-to-Batch Variation
- **Finding**: Different batches from the same manufacturer showed significantly different parthenolide content
- **Implication**: Even brand consistency cannot be assumed

### Clinical Implications of Quality Issues

#### Implications for Treatment
1. **Efficacy Variability**: Patients may experience inconsistent results due to variable parthenolide content
2. **Dose Uncertainty**: Following label directions does not ensure therapeutic dose
3. **Study Replication**: Difficulty comparing clinical studies using different commercial products
4. **Therapeutic Failure**: Negative clinical trials (e.g., De Weerdt 1996 with only 0.5 mg parthenolide) may reflect inadequate dosing rather than inefficacy of properly standardized feverfew

#### Recommendations for Clinical Use
1. **Use Pharmaceutical-Grade Products**: Prefer products with third-party verification of parthenolide content
2. **Standardized CO2-Extract**: MIG-99 extract used in positive clinical trials (Diener 2005, Pfaffenrath 2002) represents gold standard
3. **Verify Parthenolide Content**: Confirm actual parthenolide content, not just leaf weight
4. **Avoid Generic "Feverfew" Products**: Without standardization guarantees, therapeutic efficacy cannot be assured

## Distribution in Plant

### High Parthenolide Content
- Leaves (primary source)
- Flowering tops
- Seeds

### Low Parthenolide Content
- Stalks
- Roots

**Clinical Relevance**: Only aerial parts (especially leaves and flowering tops) should be used for medicinal preparations

**Source**: Heptinstall et al. 1992

## Recommended Analytical Methods

### Detection Method
- **Technique**: Solid phase extraction and mass spectrometry
- **Detection Limit**: 0.5 ng/ml (for plasma measurements)
- **Purpose**: Verify parthenolide content in preparations
- **Source**: Curry et al. 2004

### Quality Control Marker
- **Primary Marker**: Parthenolide (sesquiterpene lactone)
- **Justification**:
  - Correlates with antisecretory effects on blood platelets
  - Responsible for anti-inflammatory mechanisms
  - Stable marker for standardization
- **Source**: Heptinstall et al. 1992

## Summary

### Key Active Compound
- **Parthenolide**: Germacranolide-type sesquiterpene lactone
- **Mechanism**: NF-κB pathway inhibition → reduced TNF-α and inflammatory mediators
- **Additional Compounds**: Flavonoid glycosides, other sesquiterpene lactones, pinenes

### Critical Issue: Bioavailability
- **Major Limitation**: Parthenolide not detectable in plasma at oral doses up to 4 mg daily
- **Paradox**: Despite poor bioavailability, clinical efficacy demonstrated for migraine prevention
- **Future**: Synthetic analog DMAPT under development with improved bioavailability

### Standardization Essential
- **Minimum Standard**: ≥0.2% parthenolide
- **Quality Crisis**: 150-fold variation in commercial products (0.02-3.0 mg per dose)
- **Clinical Impact**: Many commercial products may be therapeutically inadequate
- **Gold Standard**: MIG-99 CO2-extract (6.25 mg t.i.d.) with verified parthenolide content

### Recommendations
1. Use only pharmaceutical-grade standardized extracts with verified parthenolide content
2. Avoid unstandardized "feverfew leaf" products without parthenolide verification
3. Prefer products with third-party testing certificates
4. Be aware that label claims for parthenolide may not reflect actual content
5. Consider MIG-99 CO2-extract or equivalent standardized preparation with clinical evidence
