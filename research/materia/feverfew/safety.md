# Feverfew (Tanacetum parthenium) - Safety Profile

## Overall Safety Assessment

### Systematic Review Conclusions

#### Cochrane Review 2015 (Wider et al.)
- **Overall Assessment**: "No major safety problems reported with feverfew use"
- **Tolerability**: "Feverfew generally well tolerated"
- **Adverse Effects**: "Adverse effects usually mild and reversible"
- **Conclusion**: "Feverfew not associated with any major safety concerns"
- **Reference**: Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2015;(4):CD002286. PMID: 25892430

#### Ernst & Pittler 2000
- **Assessment**: "Feverfew is associated with only mild and transient adverse effects"
- **Major Safety Issues**: "No major safety problems"
- **Reference**: Public Health Nutr. 2000;3(4A):509-514. PMID: 11276299

## Adverse Effects

### Common Adverse Effects

#### Gastrointestinal Symptoms
- **Frequency**: Most frequently reported adverse effect category
- **Types**: Abdominal discomfort, nausea, other GI symptoms
- **Severity**: Generally mild
- **Management**: Usually self-limiting; may improve with food intake
- **Source**: Wider et al. 2015 Cochrane review

#### Oral/Mucosal Effects

**Mouth Ulceration**
- **Incidence**: 11% in larger series
- **Severity**: Described as "most serious" adverse effect in the 18% who reported any adverse effects
- **Source**: Wider et al. 2015 Cochrane review

**Widespread Oral Inflammation**
- **Presentation**: Inflammation of oral mucosa and tongue
- **Associated Symptoms**:
  - Lip swelling
  - Loss of taste
- **Frequency**: Reported but specific incidence not quantified
- **Source**: Wider et al. 2015 Cochrane review

#### Dermatological Effects

**Dermatitis**
- **Association**: Specifically associated with feverfew plant
- **Mechanism**: Likely contact dermatitis from sesquiterpene lactones
- **Risk Group**: May be more common with fresh plant contact vs. processed preparations
- **Source**: Wider et al. 2015 Cochrane review

### Adverse Event Rates in Clinical Trials

#### Diener et al. 2005 (Phase III Trial)
- **MIG-99 Group**: 8.4% (9/107 patients) reported related adverse events
- **Placebo Group**: 10.2% (11/108 patients) reported related adverse events
- **Statistical Difference**: p = 0.654 (not significant)
- **Conclusion**: Related adverse events were non-specific and similarly distributed in both treatment groups
- **Reference**: Cephalalgia. 2005;25(11):1031-1041. PMID: 16232154

#### Pfaffenrath et al. 2002 (Dose-Response Study)
- **Overall Incidence**: 35% of all participants (52/147) reported at least one adverse event
- **Treatment Groups vs. Placebo**: Incidence of AEs similar across all treatment groups including placebo
- **Dose-Related Effects**: **No dose-related effect observed in any safety parameter**
- **Withdrawals**: Three withdrawals necessitated by adverse effects in feverfew groups
- **Comparative Finding**: Two studies reported higher incidence of adverse effects in placebo group; one study reported similar incidence in both groups
- **Reference**: Cephalalgia. 2002;22(7):523-532. PMID: 12230594

#### Murphy et al. 1988 (Classic RCT)
- **Serious Adverse Effects**: None reported
- **Overall Safety**: Good tolerability in 4-month treatment periods
- **Reference**: Lancet. 1988 Jul 23;2(8604):189-192. PMID: 2899663

### Clinical Trial Safety Summary
- Adverse event rates in feverfew groups generally similar to or lower than placebo
- No dose-related increase in adverse effects (tested up to 18.75 mg t.i.d. in dose-response study)
- Most adverse effects are mild and non-specific
- Serious adverse effects leading to withdrawal are rare (3/147 in largest dose-response study)

## Toxicology

### Genotoxicity Assessment
- **Study Type**: Chromosomal analysis in chronic feverfew users
- **Sample**: 30 migraine sufferers
- **Duration**: 11 months of feverfew ingestion
- **Tests Performed**:
  - Chromosomal aberrations in circulating lymphocytes
  - Sister chromatid exchanges
- **Results**: No unexpected aberrations found
- **Conclusion**: Feverfew does not induce chromosomal abnormalities
- **Source**: Pfaffenrath et al. 2002 (citing earlier genotoxicity study)

### Chronic Toxicity
- **Status**: **No studies of chronic toxicity have been performed on the plant**
- **Implication**: Safety of long-term use has not been formally established
- **Clinical Experience**: Multiple clinical trials ranging from 4-16 weeks suggest good short-term safety
- **Gap**: Long-term safety (>6 months) not rigorously studied
- **Source**: Pfaffenrath et al. 2002

### Maximum Tolerated Dose
- **Phase I Cancer Trial**: Maximum tolerated dose was not reached at doses up to 4 mg daily parthenolide
- **Toxicity**: No significant toxicity at tested doses
- **Note**: This was for isolated parthenolide in standardized capsules, not whole feverfew extract
- **Reference**: Curry et al. 2004 (Invest New Drugs. 2004 Aug;22(3):299-305)

## Contraindications

### Absolute Contraindications

#### Pregnancy
- **Contraindication**: **Pregnant women should NOT use feverfew**
- **Reason**: Potential emmenagogue activity (menstruation-promoting effects)
- **Mechanism**: May stimulate uterine contractions or affect pregnancy maintenance
- **Traditional Use Context**: Historically used for menstrual complications, suggesting reproductive effects
- **Evidence Level**: Precautionary (based on mechanism and traditional use rather than human pregnancy studies)
- **Source**: Pfaffenrath et al. 2002

#### Lactation
- **Contraindication**: Not recommended for lactating mothers
- **Reason**: Unknown whether feverfew constituents are excreted in breast milk
- **Evidence Level**: Precautionary (lack of safety data)
- **Source**: Pfaffenrath et al. 2002

#### Pediatric Use
- **Contraindication**: Not recommended for use in children
- **Reason**: Safety and efficacy not established in pediatric population
- **Evidence Level**: Lack of clinical trial data in children
- **Source**: Pfaffenrath et al. 2002

### Relative Contraindications and Precautions

#### Allergy to Asteraceae/Compositae Family
- **Plants in Family**: Ragweed, chrysanthemums, marigolds, daisies
- **Risk**: Cross-reactivity possible
- **Manifestation**: Contact dermatitis, allergic reactions
- **Recommendation**: Use with caution or avoid in patients with known allergies to related plants

#### Bleeding Disorders
- **Mechanism**: Antiplatelet effects demonstrated in vitro
- **Clinical Significance**: Theoretical increased bleeding risk
- **Evidence**: No clinical bleeding complications reported in trials
- **Recommendation**: Use with caution; theoretical risk based on mechanism

#### Surgery
- **Concern**: Potential antiplatelet effects could theoretically increase surgical bleeding
- **Recommendation**: Consider discontinuing 2 weeks before scheduled surgery
- **Evidence Level**: Precautionary (no case reports of surgical complications)

## Drug Interactions

### Note on Evidence
The clinical bibliography does NOT include dedicated drug interaction studies. The following are theoretical interactions based on mechanism of action and general herbal medicine principles.

### Potential Interactions (Theoretical)

#### Anticoagulants and Antiplatelet Agents
- **Drugs**: Warfarin, aspirin, clopidogrel, NSAIDs, heparin
- **Mechanism**: Feverfew demonstrates antisecretory effects on blood platelets (parthenolide)
- **Theoretical Risk**: Additive antiplatelet effects, increased bleeding risk
- **Clinical Evidence**: Not reported in clinical trials, but trials excluded patients on anticoagulants
- **Recommendation**: Use with caution; monitor for bleeding signs
- **Source**: Heptinstall et al. 1992 (antiplatelet mechanism)

#### Hepatically Metabolized Drugs
- **Concern**: Potential for cytochrome P450 interactions (theoretical)
- **Evidence**: No specific interaction studies identified in bibliography
- **Recommendation**: Exercise caution with drugs having narrow therapeutic indices

### Important Note
**The reviewed clinical literature does not include systematic drug interaction studies for feverfew.** The above interactions are inferred from mechanism of action. Comprehensive drug interaction screening would require additional specialized literature.

## Special Populations

### Pregnancy and Lactation
See Contraindications above - **NOT RECOMMENDED**

### Pediatric Population
- **Status**: Not recommended
- **Evidence**: No clinical trials in children
- **Safety**: Not established

### Geriatric Population
- **Evidence**: Many clinical trials included adult populations across age ranges
- **Safety**: No specific concerns identified
- **Note**: Elderly may be more likely to be on anticoagulants (see drug interactions)

### Hepatic Impairment
- **Evidence**: Not specifically studied
- **Consideration**: Metabolism of feverfew constituents may be affected
- **Recommendation**: Use with caution; no specific dosing adjustments available

### Renal Impairment
- **Evidence**: Not specifically studied
- **Consideration**: Excretion pathways not well characterized
- **Recommendation**: Use with caution; no specific dosing adjustments available

## Withdrawal Effects

### Post-Marketing Observation
- **Context**: Some users reported symptoms when discontinuing feverfew after prolonged use
- **Reported Symptoms**: Rebound headache, anxiety, sleep disturbance
- **Evidence Quality**: Anecdotal reports; not systematically studied in clinical trials
- **Recommendation**: Consider gradual tapering rather than abrupt discontinuation, particularly after prolonged use (>3 months)
- **Source**: General herbal medicine literature (specific reference not in clinical bibliography)

## Overdose

### Acute Overdose
- **Evidence**: No systematic studies or case reports in reviewed literature
- **Maximum Tested Dose**: 18.75 mg t.i.d. (56.25 mg/day) of MIG-99 extract in clinical trials without serious toxicity
- **Traditional Use**: Likely involves doses lower than tested in clinical trials
- **Concern**: Theoretical increased risk of GI symptoms and oral ulceration at high doses

### Management
- **Specific Antidote**: None
- **Treatment**: Supportive care
- **Expected Course**: Given mild nature of adverse effects at therapeutic doses, acute overdose likely to be self-limiting

## Quality and Contamination Issues

### Adulteration Risk
- **Context**: 150-fold variation in parthenolide content in commercial products (see active_compounds.md)
- **Safety Implication**: Products with inadequate active compound content may be therapeutically ineffective
- **Risk**: Substitution or adulteration with related Asteraceae species
- **Recommendation**: Use only verified, authenticated feverfew (Tanacetum parthenium) from reputable sources

### Heavy Metals and Contaminants
- **Status**: Not specifically addressed in clinical bibliography
- **General Concern**: Botanical products may contain heavy metals or pesticide residues depending on growing conditions
- **Recommendation**: Use products with third-party testing for contaminants

## Monitoring Recommendations

### For Migraine Prophylaxis Use
- **Duration to Assess Efficacy**: 12-16 weeks minimum
- **Clinical Monitoring**:
  - Migraine frequency and severity
  - Adverse effects, particularly oral/GI symptoms
  - If on anticoagulants: monitor for signs of bleeding
- **Laboratory Monitoring**: Not routinely required for healthy patients using feverfew alone

### Long-Term Use
- **Clinical Trials**: Most trials were 3-4 months duration
- **Long-Term Safety**: Not systematically studied beyond 16 weeks
- **Recommendation**: Periodic clinical reassessment if using >6 months
- **Discontinuation Trial**: Consider periodic discontinuation to reassess need for ongoing therapy

## Summary

### Overall Safety Profile
- **General Assessment**: Good safety profile with mild, reversible adverse effects
- **Comparative Safety**: Adverse event rates similar to placebo in controlled trials
- **Serious Adverse Events**: Rare (3/147 withdrawals in dose-response study)

### Most Common Adverse Effects
1. Gastrointestinal symptoms (most frequent)
2. Mouth ulceration (11% incidence, most serious common effect)
3. Oral mucosal inflammation with lip swelling and taste changes
4. Contact dermatitis (with plant exposure)

### Absolute Contraindications
1. **Pregnancy** (potential emmenagogue activity)
2. **Lactation** (safety not established)
3. **Pediatric use** (safety and efficacy not established)

### Key Safety Considerations
1. **No dose-related toxicity** observed in clinical trials (tested up to 56.25 mg/day)
2. **No genotoxicity** demonstrated in 11-month study
3. **Chronic toxicity studies lacking** - long-term safety not formally established
4. **Drug interactions not systematically studied** - theoretical antiplatelet interaction
5. **Quality control critical** - use only standardized, authenticated products
6. **Rebound effects possible** - consider gradual discontinuation after prolonged use

### Clinical Recommendation
Feverfew appears safe for short-term use (up to 16 weeks) in non-pregnant, non-lactating adults without bleeding disorders. Long-term safety (>6 months) has not been rigorously established. Use pharmaceutical-grade standardized extracts from reputable manufacturers to ensure quality and consistency.
