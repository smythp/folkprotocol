# Feverfew (Tanacetum parthenium) - Traditional Uses

## Note on Traditional Use Documentation

The clinical bibliography for feverfew focuses primarily on modern clinical trials and pharmacological research. Traditional use information is limited in the scientific literature reviewed. The sources below represent what is documented in peer-reviewed systematic reviews and monographs.

## Traditional Medicinal Uses

### European Herbal Tradition

#### Fever
- **Historical Name**: The common name "feverfew" derives from its traditional use for reducing fever
- **Etymology**: Likely from Latin "febrifugia" (fever reducer)
- **Source**: Traditional European herbalism (specific dates not documented in bibliography)

#### Migraine and Headache
- **Traditional Use**: Long-standing traditional use for headache and migraine
- **Modern Validation**: This traditional use is now supported by clinical evidence (see clinical_uses.md)
- **Historical Recognition**: German Commission E Monograph (1984-1994) evaluated feverfew for this indication

#### Menstrual Complications
- **Traditional Use**: Used for various menstrual problems
- **Potential Mechanism**: May relate to emmenagogue activity (menstruation-promoting)
- **Safety Note**: This potential emmenagogue activity is why feverfew is contraindicated in pregnancy
- **Source**: Pareek et al. 2011 systematic review (Pharmacogn Rev. 2011;5(9):103-110)

#### Rheumatoid Arthritis
- **Traditional Use**: Applied for inflammatory joint conditions
- **Potential Mechanism**: Anti-inflammatory properties via NF-κB pathway inhibition
- **Modern Research Status**: Limited clinical trial data for this indication
- **Source**: Pareek et al. 2011 systematic review

### Documented Traditional Indications

From **Pareek et al. 2011 Systematic Review** (Pharmacognosy Reviews):
- Fever (primary traditional use reflected in name)
- Migraine
- Rheumatoid arthritis
- Menstrual complications

## Regulatory Recognition of Traditional Use

### German Commission E (1984-1994)
- **Source**: German Federal Institute for Drugs and Medical Devices - Commission E
- **Publication**: Bundesanzeiger (1984-1994)
- **English Translation**: The Complete German Commission E Monographs: Therapeutic Guide to Herbal Medicines (American Botanical Council, 1998)
- **Significance**: Part of 380 monographs evaluating safety and efficacy of herbs for licensed medical prescribing in Germany
- **Status**: Monographs have not been updated since 1994 but are still considered valid
- **Recognition**: Feverfew was among the herbs evaluated, recognizing its traditional medicinal use

### ESCOP Monograph (2014)
- **Source**: European Scientific Cooperative on Phytotherapy
- **Year**: 2014
- **Title**: "Tanaceti parthenii herba (Feverfew)"
- **Therapeutic Indication**: Prevention of migraine headaches
- **URL**: https://www.escop.com/downloads/feverfew/
- **Significance**: Recognition of traditional use backed by modern clinical evidence

### European Medicines Agency (EMA)
- **Source**: Committee on Herbal Medicinal Products (HMPC)
- **Conclusion**: Feverfew herb preparations can be used for the prevention of migraine
- **URL**: https://www.ema.europa.eu/en/medicines/herbal/tanaceti-parthenii-herba
- **Significance**: Official European regulatory recognition

### Canadian Regulatory Recognition
- **Source**: Health Protection Branch
- **Status**: Drug Identification Number (DIN) granted for British feverfew product
- **Indication**: Migraine prevention
- **Recommended Dosage**: 125 mg of dried feverfew leaf preparation containing at least 0.2% parthenolide daily
- **Significance**: Official recognition by Canadian regulatory authority based on traditional use and modern evidence

## Pharmacological Basis for Traditional Uses

### Anti-inflammatory Properties
- **Mechanism**: Inhibition of NF-κB pathway
- **Effect**: Reduction in pro-inflammatory cytokines, especially tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α)
- **Relevance**: Provides mechanistic support for traditional uses in inflammatory conditions (rheumatoid arthritis, migraine)
- **Source**: Kashkooe et al. 2024 (Biomedicines. 2024 Oct 10;12(10):2297)

### Antispasmodic Properties
- **Traditional Relevance**: May relate to use for menstrual complications
- **Source**: Pareek et al. 2011

### Cardiotonic Properties
- **Mention**: Listed in pharmacological properties
- **Traditional Use Documentation**: Not clearly documented in reviewed literature
- **Source**: Pareek et al. 2011

## Traditional Preparations

### Note on Preparation Information
The clinical bibliography focuses on standardized pharmaceutical preparations used in clinical trials. Specific traditional preparation methods (tincture ratios, decoction methods, etc.) are not documented in the peer-reviewed clinical literature reviewed.

### Historical Preparation Forms
Based on clinical trial methodology that attempted to replicate traditional use:
- **Dried Leaves**: Used in capsule form in early clinical trials (Murphy et al. 1988, De Weerdt et al. 1996)
  - Dose: 50-150 mg daily
  - Form: Encapsulated dried leaf material
- **Leaf Preparations**: Fresh or dried leaves
  - Traditional form likely involved whole leaf or leaf tea
  - Specific preparation methods not documented in reviewed literature
