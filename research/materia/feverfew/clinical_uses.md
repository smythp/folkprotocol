# Feverfew (Tanacetum parthenium) - Clinical Uses

## Primary Indication: Migraine Prevention

### Evidence Summary

Feverfew has demonstrated efficacy for migraine prevention in multiple clinical trials, with the most robust evidence from the 2025 meta-analysis by Shenoy et al. showing significant reductions in both migraine frequency and duration.

### Meta-Analyses and Systematic Reviews

#### Shenoy et al. 2025 Meta-Analysis
- **Study Design**: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 9 double-masked, placebo-controlled RCTs
- **Total Participants**: 899 participants
- **Databases**: PubMed, EMBASE, Google Scholar (through August 2025)
- **Key Findings**:
  - **Migraine Frequency Reduction**: 1.11 attacks per month reduction (95% CI: -1.23 to -0.99, p < 0.00001, I² = 28%)
  - **Migraine Duration Reduction**: 4.43 hours reduction (95% CI: -7.63 to -1.23, p = 0.007, I² = 98%)
- **Reference**: Natural Product Research, 2025. DOI: 10.1080/14786419.2025.2602038

#### Cochrane Review 2015 (Wider et al.)
- **Study Design**: Cochrane systematic review using Oxford Quality Scale and Cochrane risk-of-bias tool
- **Studies Included**: 6 trials
- **Total Participants**: 561 participants
- **Databases**: CENTRAL, MEDLINE, EMBASE, AMED (through January 2015)
- **Key Findings**:
  - **Migraine Frequency Reduction**: 0.6 headaches per month reduction (based on most rigorous trial, n=218)
  - **Baseline to Treatment Change (Feverfew Group)**: From 4.8 to 2.9 attacks/month (1.9 attack reduction)
  - **Baseline to Treatment Change (Placebo Group)**: From 4.8 to 3.5 attacks/month (1.3 attack reduction)
  - **Secondary Outcomes**: No significant differences for migraine intensity, duration, nausea, or vomiting
- **Quality Assessment**: Low quality evidence requiring confirmation in larger rigorous trials with stable feverfew extracts
- **Reference**: Cochrane Database Syst Rev. 2015;(4):CD002286. PMID: 25892430

#### Ernst & Pittler 2000 Systematic Review
- **Study Design**: Systematic review using Jadad score for quality assessment
- **Studies Included**: 6 randomized, placebo-controlled, double-blind trials
- **Databases**: Medline, Embase, Biosis, CISCOM, Cochrane Library (through December 1999)
- **Key Findings**:
  - Majority of trials favor feverfew over placebo for migraine prevention
  - Feverfew likely effective for migraine prevention
  - Mild and transient adverse effects only
  - No major safety problems
- **Reference**: Public Health Nutr. 2000;3(4A):509-514. PMID: 11276299

### Randomized Controlled Trials

#### Diener et al. 2005 - Phase III Confirmatory Trial
- **Study Design**: Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, multicenter, parallel-group trial
- **Sample Size**: 170 participants (ITT analysis: 89 feverfew, 81 placebo)
- **Preparation**: MIG-99 feverfew CO2-extract
- **Dosing**: 6.25 mg three times daily (18.75 mg total daily dose)
- **Treatment Duration**: 16 weeks (plus 4-week baseline period)
- **Key Findings**:
  - **Feverfew Group**: Migraine frequency decreased by 1.9 attacks per month from baseline of 4.76 attacks/month (p = 0.0456)
  - **Placebo Group**: Migraine frequency decreased by 1.3 attacks per month
  - **Responder Rate**: Odds ratio of 3.4 in favor of MIG-99 (p = 0.0049)
  - **Adverse Events**: 8.4% in MIG-99 group (9/107 patients) vs 10.2% in placebo (11/108 patients, p = 0.654)
- **Conclusion**: Confirmed efficacy of CO2-based feverfew extract MIG-99 in migraine prophylaxis
- **Reference**: Cephalalgia. 2005;25(11):1031-1041. PMID: 16232154

#### Pfaffenrath et al. 2002 - Dose-Response Study
- **Study Design**: Randomized, double-blind, multicentre, placebo-controlled dose-response trial with adaptive design
- **Sample Size**: 147 total participants (49 in confirmatory subgroup with ≥4 baseline attacks)
- **Preparation**: MIG-99 feverfew CO2-extract
- **Dosing**: Three dose levels tested
  - 2.08 mg t.i.d. (6.24 mg/day)
  - 6.25 mg t.i.d. (18.75 mg/day)
  - 18.75 mg t.i.d. (56.25 mg/day)
- **Treatment Duration**: 12 weeks (plus 4-week baseline period)
- **Key Findings**:
  - **Overall Population**: Failed to show significant migraine prophylactic effect
  - **Subgroup with ≥4 Baseline Attacks**: Migraine frequency decreased in dose-dependent manner (p = 0.001)
  - **Optimal Dose (6.25 mg t.i.d.)**: Most favorable benefit-risk ratio
    - Mean reduction: 1.8 attacks per 28 days (feverfew) vs 0.3 attacks (placebo)
    - Statistical significance: p = 0.02
  - **Adverse Events**: 35% of all participants (52/147) reported at least one AE; incidence similar across all treatment groups including placebo
  - **Withdrawals**: Three withdrawals due to adverse effects in feverfew groups
- **Conclusion**: Optimal dosing is 6.25 mg t.i.d. for patients with frequent migraines (≥4 attacks/month)
- **Reference**: Cephalalgia. 2002;22(7):523-532. PMID: 12230594

#### Murphy et al. 1988 - Classic Crossover RCT
- **Study Design**: Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover trial
- **Sample Size**: 72 enrolled, 60 completed, 59 analyzed
- **Preparation**: Dried feverfew leaves
- **Dosing**: One capsule daily
- **Treatment Duration**: Two 4-month treatment periods (crossover design) with 1-month single-blind placebo run-in
- **Key Findings**:
  - Significant reduction in mean number and severity of migraine attacks in each 2-month period
  - Significant decrease in degree of vomiting
  - Duration of individual attacks remained unchanged
  - Visual analogue scores indicated significant improvement with feverfew
  - No serious adverse effects reported
- **Reference**: Lancet. 1988 Jul 23;2(8604):189-192. PMID: 2899663

#### De Weerdt et al. 1996 - Negative Study
- **Study Design**: Randomized double-blind placebo-controlled crossover trial
- **Sample Size**: 50 enrolled, 44 completed
- **Preparation**: Dried alcoholic extract of feverfew on microcrystalline cellulose
- **Dosing**: One capsule daily containing 0.5 mg parthenolide
- **Treatment Duration**: 9 months
- **Key Findings**:
  - **No Prophylactic Effect**: Both treatment groups suffered the same number of migraine attacks
  - Patients showed tendency to use fewer symptomatic drugs during feverfew treatment period
- **Likely Explanation**: Low parthenolide content (0.5 mg daily) may explain lack of efficacy compared to positive studies using 6.25 mg t.i.d. of standardized extract
- **Reference**: Phytomedicine. 1996;3(3):225-230. PMID: 23195074

## Optimal Dosing for Migraine Prevention

### Recommended Dosing from Clinical Evidence

**MIG-99 CO2-Extract (Standardized Extract)**
- **Dose**: 6.25 mg three times daily (18.75 mg total daily)
- **Evidence Level**: Highest - confirmed in Phase III trial (Diener 2005) and dose-response study (Pfaffenrath 2002)
- **Best Response**: Patients with ≥4 migraine attacks per month at baseline
- **Duration**: Minimum 12-16 weeks for full prophylactic effect

**Dried Feverfew Leaves**
- **Dose Range**: 50-150 mg daily
- **Duration**: Up to 4 months
- **Source**: Various clinical trials

**Standardized Extract (0.2-0.4% Parthenolide)**
- **Dose Range**: 100-300 mg up to 4 times daily
- **Source**: Clinical practice recommendations

### Patient Selection

**Best Candidates for Feverfew Therapy**:
- Patients with frequent migraines (≥4 attacks per month)
- Patients seeking prophylactic migraine treatment
- Patients who prefer herbal treatments or have contraindications to conventional prophylactic medications

**Expected Response**:
- Reduction of approximately 1.8-1.9 migraine attacks per month
- Effect size comparable to some conventional prophylactic medications
- Response may take 8-12 weeks to become evident

## Other Clinical Uses

### Anti-inflammatory Applications
- **Mechanism**: Inhibition of NF-κB pathway, reducing pro-inflammatory cytokines (especially TNF-α)
- **Traditional Uses**: Rheumatoid arthritis, menstrual complications
- **Clinical Evidence**: Limited RCT data; primarily preclinical and traditional use

### Emerging Applications (Limited Clinical Evidence)
- **Psoriasis**: Mentioned in systematic reviews as emerging application
- **Allergies**: Traditional and emerging use
- **Asthma**: Traditional and emerging use
- **Note**: These applications lack the robust RCT evidence available for migraine prevention

### Anticancer Activity
- **Status**: Investigated in Phase I trial (Curry et al. 2004)
- **Major Limitation**: Poor oral bioavailability of parthenolide
  - No detectable plasma concentrations at doses up to 4 mg daily
  - Detection limit: 0.5 ng/ml using solid phase extraction and mass spectrometry
- **Future Direction**: Synthetic analog dimethylamino-parthenolide (DMAPT) developed with greater bioavailability
- **Clinical Application**: Not currently viable for cancer treatment due to bioavailability issues
- **Reference**: Invest New Drugs. 2004 Aug;22(3):299-305. PMID: 15122077

## Summary of Clinical Evidence

### Strength of Evidence
- **Migraine Prevention**: Moderate to high quality evidence from multiple RCTs and meta-analyses
  - Most recent 2025 meta-analysis shows significant benefits (899 participants, 9 RCTs)
  - Cochrane review notes low quality evidence requiring larger confirmatory trials
  - Best evidence for standardized CO2-extract (MIG-99) at 6.25 mg t.i.d.

### Effect Sizes
- **Migraine Frequency**: 1.11-1.9 attacks per month reduction
- **Migraine Duration**: 4.43 hours reduction
- **Responder Rates**: Odds ratio 3.4 favoring feverfew over placebo

### Critical Factors for Efficacy
1. **Standardization**: Products must contain adequate parthenolide (≥0.2%)
2. **Dosing**: Optimal dose is 6.25 mg t.i.d. of standardized CO2-extract
3. **Patient Selection**: Best response in patients with ≥4 migraines per month
4. **Duration**: Minimum 12-16 weeks for full prophylactic effect
5. **Quality Control**: Commercial products vary 150-fold in parthenolide content; use pharmaceutical-grade standardized extracts

### Limitations
- **Bioavailability**: Major limitation for therapeutic applications beyond migraine
- **Study Heterogeneity**: Difficult to pool results due to varying outcome measures
- **Quality Control**: Severe variability in commercial products necessitates careful product selection
