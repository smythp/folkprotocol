# Feverfew (Tanacetum parthenium) - Preparations and Dosing

## Note on Preparation Information

The clinical bibliography emphasizes **pharmaceutical-grade standardized extracts** used in clinical trials. Traditional preparation methods (tincture ratios, decoction instructions, etc.) are **not documented** in the peer-reviewed clinical literature reviewed. The information below focuses on clinically validated preparations.

## Standardized CO2-Extract: MIG-99 (Gold Standard)

### Clinical Evidence
MIG-99 represents the **most extensively studied** and **clinically validated** feverfew preparation with consistent positive results in rigorous clinical trials.

### Preparation Characteristics
- **Extraction Method**: Supercritical CO2 extraction
- **Standardization**: Standardized for parthenolide content
- **Form**: Oral capsules/tablets
- **Quality**: Pharmaceutical-grade with consistent batch-to-batch parthenolide content

### Dosing (Evidence-Based)

#### Standard Dose for Migraine Prevention
- **Dose**: 6.25 mg three times daily (t.i.d.)
- **Total Daily Dose**: 18.75 mg
- **Timing**: Divided doses throughout the day
- **Duration**: Minimum 12-16 weeks for full prophylactic effect
- **Evidence**: Phase III RCT (Diener et al. 2005), Dose-response study (Pfaffenrath et al. 2002)

#### Clinical Trial Results at This Dose
- **Migraine Frequency Reduction**: 1.9 attacks per month (from baseline of 4.76 attacks/month)
- **Responder Rate**: Odds ratio 3.4 vs. placebo (p = 0.0049)
- **Adverse Events**: 8.4% (similar to placebo at 10.2%)
- **Best Response**: Patients with ≥4 migraine attacks per month at baseline

#### Dose-Response Findings (Pfaffenrath et al. 2002)
Three doses tested in patients with frequent migraines:
- **2.08 mg t.i.d. (6.24 mg/day)**: Less effective
- **6.25 mg t.i.d. (18.75 mg/day)**: **Optimal benefit-risk ratio**
  - Mean reduction: 1.8 attacks per 28 days vs 0.3 for placebo (p = 0.02)
- **18.75 mg t.i.d. (56.25 mg/day)**: No additional benefit over 6.25 mg t.i.d., no increase in adverse effects

**Clinical Recommendation**: 6.25 mg t.i.d. provides optimal efficacy without need for higher doses

### Administration Guidelines
- **Timing**: Take with meals to minimize gastrointestinal adverse effects
- **Consistency**: Take at regular intervals (e.g., morning, midday, evening)
- **Duration to Assess Efficacy**: Minimum 12 weeks before evaluating effectiveness
- **Long-Term Use**: Clinical trials up to 16 weeks; long-term safety (>6 months) not systematically studied

## Dried Feverfew Leaf Preparations

### Clinical Trial Dosing
- **Dose Range**: 50-150 mg daily of dried feverfew leaves
- **Duration**: Up to 4 months in clinical trials
- **Form**: Encapsulated dried leaf powder
- **Evidence**: Used in early RCTs (Murphy et al. 1988, De Weerdt et al. 1996)

### Critical Limitation: Variable Parthenolide Content
The major issue with dried leaf preparations is **inconsistent parthenolide content**:

#### Nelson et al. 2002 Analysis
- **Dried Leaf per Capsule**: 25 to 500 mg (matched label claims)
- **Parthenolide per Capsule**: 0.02 to 3.0 mg (**150-fold variation**)
- **Daily Parthenolide Intake**: 0.06 to 9.7 mg/day (**160-fold variation**)

#### Clinical Implication
- **Therapeutic Failure Risk**: Many commercial dried leaf products likely contain insufficient parthenolide for efficacy
- **Example**: De Weerdt et al. 1996 negative study used only 0.5 mg parthenolide daily (likely inadequate dose)
- **Recommendation**: If using dried leaf, verify parthenolide content ≥0.2% and ensure adequate daily parthenolide intake

### Dried Leaf Dosing Recommendations (IF Standardized)
- **Minimum Parthenolide Content**: ≥0.2% parthenolide
- **Target Daily Parthenolide Intake**: Approximately 1.8-18.75 mg based on clinical trials
- **Calculation Example**: For 0.2% parthenolide content:
  - To achieve ~2 mg parthenolide: Need 1,000 mg (1 g) dried leaf
  - To achieve ~18.75 mg parthenolide: Need ~9,375 mg dried leaf (not practical)
- **Practical Issue**: Achieving MIG-99-equivalent parthenolide doses (18.75 mg/day) would require impractically large amounts of dried leaf even at 0.2% standardization

## Standardized Extracts (General)

### Regulatory/Clinical Recommendations
- **Parthenolide Content**: 0.2-0.4% parthenolide
- **Dose Range**: 100-300 mg extract up to 4 times daily
- **Indication**: Migraine treatment and prevention
- **Source**: Clinical practice recommendations

### Canadian Regulatory Standard
- **Dose**: 125 mg of dried feverfew leaf preparation
- **Standardization**: Containing at least 0.2% parthenolide
- **Frequency**: Daily
- **Indication**: Migraine prevention
- **Source**: Health Protection Branch Drug Identification Number (DIN)

### Critical Quality Control Requirement
- **Finding**: NO commercial feverfew extracts in Nelson et al. 2002 study contained the label-claimed parthenolide content
- **Implication**: **Label claims cannot be trusted without third-party verification**
- **Recommendation**: Use only products with Certificate of Analysis (CoA) from independent laboratory

## Part of Plant Used

### Medicinal Parts (Pharmacopoeia Standards)
- **Primary**: Aerial parts (herba)
- **Specifically**: Leaves and flowering tops
- **Latin**: Tanaceti parthenii herba

### Parthenolide Distribution in Plant
From Heptinstall et al. 1992:
- **High parthenolide content**:
  - Leaves (primary medicinal part)
  - Flowering tops
  - Seeds
- **Low parthenolide content**:
  - Stalks
  - Roots

**Clinical Recommendation**: Only use leaves and flowering tops for medicinal preparations

## Stability and Storage

### Parthenolide Stability
- **Requirement**: Feverfew should be dispensed in properly stabilized form
- **Standard**: Parthenolide content must not degrade to less than 90% of original content
- **Implication**: Proper storage and formulation is critical for maintaining therapeutic efficacy

### Storage Recommendations (General Herbal Standards)
- **Light**: Protect from light (parthenolide may be photosensitive)
- **Temperature**: Store in cool, dry place
- **Container**: Airtight container to prevent oxidation
- **Duration**: Use before expiration date; parthenolide content may degrade over time

### Quality Indicators
- **Appearance**: Dried leaves should be green to greenish-brown, not brown or discolored
- **Odor**: Characteristic aromatic odor
- **Testing**: Third-party laboratory testing for parthenolide content

## Product Selection Guidelines

### Recommended Product Characteristics
1. **Standardization**: Verified parthenolide content ≥0.2%
2. **Extraction Method**: CO2-extraction preferred (MIG-99 gold standard)
3. **Third-Party Testing**: Certificate of Analysis from independent laboratory
4. **GMP Certification**: Good Manufacturing Practices certified
5. **Batch Consistency**: Manufacturer documentation of batch-to-batch consistency
6. **Clinical Evidence**: Products matching those used in positive clinical trials preferred

### Products to AVOID
1. **Unstandardized "feverfew leaf"** without parthenolide verification
2. Products with **only weight claims** (e.g., "500 mg feverfew") without parthenolide content
3. Products from manufacturers with history of **label claim discrepancies**
4. Products without **Certificate of Analysis** or third-party testing
5. Products with parthenolide content **below 0.2%**
6. Products stored in clear containers (light exposure may degrade parthenolide)

### Cost Considerations
- **Pharmaceutical-grade standardized extracts**: More expensive but assured quality and efficacy
- **Generic dried leaf products**: Less expensive but high risk of inadequate parthenolide content
- **Cost-Effectiveness**: Paying more for verified standardized products likely more cost-effective than using ineffective unstandardized products

## Combination Products

### Note on Combinations
The clinical trials reviewed used **feverfew as a mono-preparation**. Combination products with other herbs have not been systematically studied for migraine prevention.

### Considerations for Combinations
- **Evidence Base**: Lacking for combination products
- **Standardization**: More difficult to standardize multiple herbs
- **Interactions**: Potential herb-herb interactions not studied
- **Dose**: Feverfew dose in combination products may be subtherapeutic
- **Recommendation**: For evidence-based migraine prevention, use feverfew as mono-preparation

## Administration Schedule

### Optimal Timing
- **Frequency**: Three times daily (t.i.d.)
- **Timing Examples**:
  - Morning (with breakfast): 6.25 mg
  - Midday (with lunch): 6.25 mg
  - Evening (with dinner): 6.25 mg
- **With Food**: Recommended to minimize GI adverse effects

### Initiation and Titration
- **Starting Dose**: Begin with full therapeutic dose (6.25 mg t.i.d.)
- **Titration**: Not necessary based on clinical trial protocols
- **Assessment Period**: Allow 12-16 weeks before evaluating efficacy
- **Early Discontinuation**: If intolerable adverse effects occur (uncommon)

### Discontinuation
- **Method**: Consider gradual tapering rather than abrupt discontinuation
- **Reason**: Some anecdotal reports of rebound headache with abrupt discontinuation
- **Duration**: Taper over 1-2 weeks if discontinuing after prolonged use (>3 months)

## Comparison of Preparation Methods

### Evidence-Based Ranking

**1. MIG-99 CO2-Extract (6.25 mg t.i.d.)**
- **Evidence Level**: Highest (Phase III RCT, dose-response study)
- **Pros**: Consistent parthenolide content, proven efficacy, good safety profile
- **Cons**: More expensive, pharmaceutical preparation required
- **Recommendation**: **First-line choice for evidence-based migraine prevention**

**2. Other Standardized Extracts (≥0.2% parthenolide)**
- **Evidence Level**: Moderate (if truly standardized to match clinical trial parthenolide doses)
- **Pros**: May be more accessible than MIG-99
- **Cons**: Quality verification essential; label claims may not reflect actual content
- **Recommendation**: Acceptable if third-party tested for parthenolide content

**3. Dried Feverfew Leaf (verified ≥0.2% parthenolide)**
- **Evidence Level**: Low to moderate (early RCTs used dried leaf, but quality varied)
- **Pros**: Traditional form, may be less expensive
- **Cons**: Difficult to achieve therapeutic parthenolide dose; 150-fold quality variation in commercial products
- **Recommendation**: Only if parthenolide content verified and sufficient for therapeutic dose

**4. Unstandardized Products**
- **Evidence Level**: None
- **Pros**: May be inexpensive and accessible
- **Cons**: No assurance of therapeutic efficacy; may contain little or no parthenolide
- **Recommendation**: **Not recommended - high risk of therapeutic failure**

## Summary

### Evidence-Based Preparation
- **Gold Standard**: MIG-99 CO2-extract, 6.25 mg t.i.d. (18.75 mg total daily)
- **Duration**: Minimum 12-16 weeks for full prophylactic effect
- **Best Response**: Patients with ≥4 migraines per month

### Critical Quality Requirements
- **Minimum Parthenolide**: ≥0.2% (regulatory standard)
- **Therapeutic Parthenolide Dose**: Based on clinical evidence, approximately 2-18.75 mg/day
- **Verification Essential**: Third-party testing required due to severe quality variability

### Major Gap: Traditional Preparations
- **Tinctures, teas, and other traditional preparations**: Not documented in clinical literature
- **Source**: Consult traditional herbalist and ethnobotanical resources

### Clinical Recommendation
For evidence-based migraine prevention, use **pharmaceutical-grade MIG-99 CO2-extract (6.25 mg t.i.d.)** or equivalent standardized extract with verified parthenolide content matching clinical trial preparations. Avoid unstandardized commercial products due to severe quality control issues (150-fold variation in active constituent).
