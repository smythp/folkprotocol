# American Ginseng - Preparations and Dosing

## Clinical Trial Preparations

### Standardized Extracts

**Standard Concentration**: 10% total ginsenosides

#### Cereboost™
- **Standardization**: 10.65% ginsenosides
- **Clinical Use**: Neurocognitive function
- **Doses Tested**: 100 mg, 200 mg, 400 mg (acute dosing)
- **Evidence**: RCT with 32 participants showing working memory improvement
- **Source**: PMID: 20676609

#### CVT-E002
- **Type**: Proprietary standardized extract
- **Clinical Use**: Respiratory infection prevention
- **Doses Tested**: 400 mg/day, 800 mg/day
- **Duration**: 6 months
- **Evidence**: Multicenter RCT with 783 elderly participants showing 31-33% relative risk reduction for URIs
- **Source**: PMID: 23074661

#### Wisconsin Ginseng Extract
- **Form**: Whole root extract (various trials)
- **Clinical Uses**: Cancer-related fatigue
- **Doses**: 750-2000 mg/day
- **Standardization**: Not always specified; likely lower concentration than 10% extracts
- **Evidence**: Phase III trial (n=364) and dose-finding pilot (n=290)
- **Sources**: PMID: 23853057, 19415341

#### Diabetes Study Extracts
- **Standardization**: 10% ginsenosides
- **Form**: Capsules
- **Doses**: 500 mg capsules taken three times daily (3 g/day total)
- **Duration**: 8-12 weeks in various studies
- **Evidence**: Multiple RCTs showing glycemic and cardiovascular benefits
- **Sources**: PMID: 29478187, 24891873

## Formulation Types and Bioavailability

### Powder Forms

**Bioavailability Comparison** (2016 study in beagles):

| Formulation | Relative Bioavailability | Dissolution Rate |
|-------------|--------------------------|------------------|
| **Ultrafine granular powder** | Highest | Fastest |
| **Common powder** | Moderate | Moderate |
| **Traditional pieces** | Lowest | Slowest |

**Clinical Implication**: Particle size matters significantly. Ultrafine formulations show better absorption.

**Source**: J Ethnopharmacol. 2016; https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378874116301386

### Processing Effects

**Impact on Bioavailability**:
- Different processing methods affect pharmacokinetics and oral bioavailability
- Processing impacts tissue concentrations of Rg1, Re, Rd, and Rb1
- **Recommendation**: Processing method should be considered when selecting products

**Source**: PMID: 29348719

## Traditional Preparations

### Decoction (Traditional Tea)

**Traditional Method**:
- Dried root sliced
- Simmered in water for extended period (typically 20-45 minutes)
- Traditional dose: 3-9 grams of dried root daily

**Modern Evidence-Based Tea Preparation**:

**Based on traditional use and extraction efficiency**:
- **Dose**: 3-6 grams dried root
- **Preparation**:
  1. Slice root thinly or use pre-sliced root
  2. Add to 2-3 cups (500-750 mL) water
  3. Bring to boil, then reduce heat
  4. Simmer for 30-45 minutes
  5. Strain and drink
- **Frequency**: Divide into 2-3 doses throughout day
- **Timing for Glycemic Effects**: Drink 40 minutes before meals

**Advantages**:
- Traditional method with historical use
- No processing/extraction solvents
- Full spectrum of compounds

**Disadvantages**:
- Variable ginsenoside content
- Lower bioavailability than ultrafine powders
- Time-consuming preparation
- Difficult to standardize dose

### Whole Root Powder

**Traditional Use**:
- Dried root ground to fine powder
- Taken directly or in capsules

**Dosing**:
- **Traditional range**: 3-9 grams daily
- **Modern clinical equivalent**: 1-3 grams of 10% extract = 3-9 grams crude powder (approximately)

**Preparation**:
- Mix powder with water, juice, or food
- Can be encapsulated for convenience

**Consideration**: Bioavailability lower than standardized extracts; ultrafine powder preferred if available

## Tincture Preparations

### Alcohol Extraction

**Note**: Limited clinical trial data for tincture preparations; most evidence from standardized extracts or whole root.

**Traditional Tincture Ratios**:
- **1:5 ratio** (1 part herb to 5 parts menstruum)
  - Common commercial ratio
  - Menstruum: Typically 50-70% alcohol to extract ginsenosides

- **1:3 ratio** (stronger concentration)
  - Less common
  - Higher concentration of active compounds

**Dose Conversion Estimates**:

Based on 10% extract clinical doses and traditional conversion ratios:

**For 1:5 tincture (50-60% alcohol)**:
- **Low dose** (equivalent to 400 mg extract): ~2 mL (40 drops) 3x daily
- **Moderate dose** (equivalent to 1000 mg extract): ~5 mL (100 drops) 3x daily
- **High dose** (equivalent to 2000 mg extract): ~10 mL (200 drops) 3x daily

**For 1:3 tincture (60-70% alcohol)**:
- Reduce volume by approximately 40% from 1:5 doses

**Timing**:
- For glycemic effects: 40 minutes before meals
- For general use: Divided doses throughout day
- For cognitive effects: Single dose as needed

**Important Caveats**:
1. These are estimates based on traditional ratios, not clinical evidence
2. Ginsenoside extraction efficiency varies with alcohol percentage
3. No clinical trials specifically tested tincture preparations
4. Quality and potency vary significantly between manufacturers

**Recommendation**: If using tincture, look for products that specify ginsenoside content (ideally 10% in dry herb equivalent)

## Capsule and Tablet Preparations

### Standardized Extract Capsules

**Evidence-Based Dosing**:

#### Cancer-Related Fatigue
- **Dose**: 2000 mg daily
- **Form**: Whole root extract or standardized extract capsules
- **Frequency**: Divide into 2 doses (1000 mg twice daily)
- **Duration**: Minimum 8 weeks for effect
- **Evidence**: Phase III RCT (n=364)
- **Source**: PMID: 23853057

#### Type 2 Diabetes and Glycemic Control
- **Dose**: 3 grams daily (500 mg capsules x 6, or 1000 mg capsules x 3)
- **Standardization**: 10% ginsenosides
- **Timing**: **CRITICAL - Take 40 minutes before each meal**
  - 1 gram 40 min before breakfast
  - 1 gram 40 min before lunch
  - 1 gram 40 min before dinner
- **Duration**: 8-12 weeks minimum
- **Evidence**: Multiple RCTs showing HbA1c reduction, blood pressure lowering, cardiovascular benefits
- **Sources**: PMID: 29478187, 11273850, 24891873

#### Cognitive Function
- **Dose**: 100-400 mg (acute dosing)
- **Standardization**: 10.65% ginsenosides (Cereboost™ or equivalent)
- **Timing**: As needed, 1-6 hours before cognitive demand
- **Evidence**: RCT (n=32) showing working memory improvement
- **Source**: PMID: 20676609

#### Respiratory Infection Prevention
- **Dose**: 400-800 mg daily
- **Form**: Standardized extract (CVT-E002 or similar)
- **Frequency**: Once or twice daily
- **Duration**: 6 months (through cold/flu season)
- **Population**: Especially for adults ≥65 years
- **Evidence**: Multicenter RCT (n=783)
- **Source**: PMID: 23074661

#### Arterial Stiffness/Cardiovascular Support
- **Dose**: 3 grams daily
- **Form**: Standardized extract capsules
- **Frequency**: Divide into 3 doses with meals
- **Duration**: 12 weeks minimum
- **Population**: Diabetic hypertensive patients
- **Evidence**: RCT (n=64)
- **Source**: PMID: 23973636

## Special Preparation Considerations

### Timing for Glycemic Effects

**Critical Finding**: Time-dependent effects on blood glucose

**Optimal Pre-Meal Timing**: **40 minutes before glucose load**
- Significantly better than 20, 10, or 0 minutes before
- First demonstrated by Vuksan et al. (2001)
- Confirmed in multiple subsequent trials

**Clinical Application**:
- For diabetes management: Take 40 minutes before each meal
- For general use: Timing less critical; take with or between meals

**Source**: PMID: 11273850

### Duration for Effect

**Time to Clinical Benefit**:

| Indication | Initial Effects | Optimal Benefit |
|------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Glycemic Control** | Acute (within hours) | 8 weeks |
| **Cancer Fatigue** | 4 weeks (mild) | 8 weeks (significant) |
| **Cognitive Function** | 1-6 hours | Acute effect |
| **Blood Pressure** | 4+ weeks | 8-12 weeks |
| **Respiratory Prevention** | Throughout season | Cumulative over months |

## Product Selection Guide

### Quality Markers to Look For

**Essential**:
1. **Species verification**: Panax quinquefolius confirmed (not P. ginseng)
2. **Standardization**: 10% total ginsenosides (matches clinical trials)
3. **Marker compounds**: Rb1 and Re content specified

**Preferred**:
4. **Third-party testing**: USP, NSF, ConsumerLab certification
5. **Formulation**: Ultrafine powder or extract (better bioavailability)
6. **Manufacturing**: GMP certified facility
7. **Origin**: Wisconsin or other North American cultivated (quality control)

**Red Flags**:
- No standardization listed
- "Proprietary blend" without ginsenoside content
- Extremely low price (adulteration risk)
- Species not specified
- No third-party testing

**Source for Quality Concerns**: American Herbal Pharmacopoeia (2012) - addresses widespread adulteration

## Combination Products

### Single-Herb vs. Combinations

**Clinical Evidence**: All major clinical trials used **single-herb preparations**
- Cancer fatigue: American ginseng alone
- Diabetes: American ginseng alone
- Cognitive: American ginseng alone
- Respiratory: American ginseng alone

**Recommendation**: For evidence-based use matching clinical trials, choose single-herb standardized products

**Combination Considerations**:
- May be appropriate for traditional formulas
- Difficult to achieve clinical trial doses in combinations
- Interactions between herbs not well studied
- Standardization more challenging

## Homemade Preparations

### Tea from Whole Root

**Ingredients**:
- 3-6 grams American ginseng root (dried, sliced)
- 2-3 cups (500-750 mL) water

**Method**:
1. Add sliced root to cold water
2. Bring to boil
3. Reduce heat and simmer 30-45 minutes
4. Strain
5. Drink throughout day

**Notes**:
- Can re-simmer roots 2-3 times
- Add honey or lemon if desired
- Store refrigerated up to 2 days

### Powder in Food/Beverages

**Method**:
- Grind dried root to fine powder (coffee grinder)
- Use 1-3 grams per serving
- Mix into:
  - Smoothies
  - Oatmeal
  - Yogurt
  - Soups
  - Nut butter

**Advantages**: Easy to incorporate; full spectrum
**Disadvantages**: Lower bioavailability than ultrafine commercial powder; harder to standardize dose

## Special Population Dosing

### Elderly (≥65 years)
- **Dose**: 400-800 mg daily for respiratory support
- **Evidence**: Safe and effective in 783 participants
- **Adjustments**: No dose reduction needed
- **Source**: PMID: 23074661

### Diabetic Patients
- **Dose**: 1-3 grams daily, 40 minutes before meals
- **Monitoring**: Blood glucose monitoring essential
- **Adjustment**: May need to reduce diabetes medication
- **Evidence**: Safe in 74 diabetic patients for 12 weeks
- **Sources**: PMID: 29478187, 24891873

### Cancer Patients
- **Dose**: 2000 mg daily during and after treatment
- **Duration**: Minimum 8 weeks
- **Safety**: No significant toxicity in 364 patients
- **Source**: PMID: 23853057

### Pediatric
- **Evidence**: Limited safety data in children 3-12 years
- **Recommendation**: Weight-based dosing under healthcare supervision
- **Source**: PMID: 18676527

## Storage and Shelf Life

### Whole Root
- **Storage**: Cool, dry, dark location
- **Container**: Airtight
- **Shelf Life**: 2-3 years if properly stored

### Powdered Root
- **Storage**: Cool, dry, dark location; refrigeration optimal
- **Container**: Airtight, opaque
- **Shelf Life**: 1-2 years; degradation faster than whole root

### Extracts/Capsules
- **Storage**: Follow manufacturer recommendations
- **Typical**: Cool, dry location away from sunlight
- **Shelf Life**: 2-3 years unopened; check expiration dates

### Tinctures
- **Storage**: Cool, dark location
- **Shelf Life**: 3-5 years due to alcohol preservation
- **Note**: Alcohol percentage affects preservation

## Cost-Effectiveness Considerations

### Comparison by Preparation Type

**Approximate Relative Costs** (for equivalent clinical doses):

Most Expensive → Least Expensive:
1. **Proprietary standardized extracts** (Cereboost, CVT-E002)
2. **Quality 10% extract capsules** (third-party tested)
3. **Generic standardized extract capsules**
4. **Whole root powder** (commercial)
5. **Whole dried root** (for tea)

**Clinical Efficacy Consideration**:
- More expensive standardized extracts have clinical trial evidence
- Cheaper preparations may have lower bioavailability
- Quality control more rigorous in premium products
- Adulteration risk higher in low-cost products

**Recommendation**: Balance cost with quality/efficacy needs
- For specific clinical indications (diabetes, cancer fatigue): invest in standardized extracts
- For general wellness: quality whole root preparations may suffice
