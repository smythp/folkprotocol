# American Ginseng - Clinical Uses

## Cancer-Related Fatigue

### Phase III Trial (Barton et al., 2013)
- **Design**: Randomized, double-blind, multicenter Phase III trial
- **Sample**: 364 participants across 40 institutions
- **Dosage**: 2000 mg daily
- **Duration**: 8 weeks
- **Results**:
  - At 4 weeks: 14.4-point improvement (ginseng) vs 8.2 points (placebo), p=0.07 (not significant)
  - At 8 weeks: 20-point improvement (SD=27) vs 10.3 (SD=26.1) placebo, **p=0.003** (statistically significant)
  - Greater benefit observed in patients receiving active cancer treatment vs those who had completed treatment
  - No statistically significant toxicity differences between groups
- **Source**: J Natl Cancer Inst. 2013;105(16):1230-8. PMID: 23853057

### Dose-Finding Pilot Study (Barton et al., 2010)
- **Design**: Phase III pilot, randomized, double-blind, dose-finding
- **Sample**: 290 participants
- **Treatment Groups**:
  - Placebo
  - 750 mg/day
  - 1,000 mg/day
  - 2,000 mg/day
- **Dosing Frequency**: Twice daily
- **Duration**: 8 weeks
- **Results**:
  - Activity interference scores: 460-467 (placebo/750mg) vs 480-551 (1,000-2,000mg groups)
  - SF-36 vitality subscale change: 7.3-7.8 points (placebo/750mg) vs 10.5-14.6 points (higher-dose groups)
  - Over twice as many patients on ginseng perceived benefit and were satisfied compared to placebo
  - No significant toxicity differences between groups
  - Tolerable toxicity at 1,000-2,000 mg/day doses
- **Conclusion**: Established 2,000 mg/day for subsequent Phase III trial
- **Source**: Support Care Cancer. 2010;18(2):179-87. PMID: 19415341

## Type 2 Diabetes and Glycemic Control

### Long-Term Glycemic and Cardiovascular Effects (Vuksan et al., 2019)
- **Design**: Double-blind, randomized, crossover controlled trial
- **Sample**: 24 participants (11 female, 13 male)
- **Demographics**: Mean age 64±7 years
- **Dosage**: 1 gram per meal (3 grams daily)
- **Duration**: 8 weeks, followed by washout and crossover
- **Results**:
  - **HbA1c reduction**: -0.29% (p=0.041)
  - **Fasting blood glucose reduction**: -0.71 mmol/L (p=0.008)
  - **Systolic blood pressure reduction**: -5.6±2.7 mmHg (p<0.001)
  - **LDL-C reduction**: -12.3±3.9%
  - **Serum NOx increase**: +1.85±2.13 µmol/L (p<0.03)
- **Note**: Demonstrated cardiovascular benefits alongside glycemic control
- **Source**: Eur J Nutr. 2019;58(3):1237-1245. PMID: 29478187

### Postprandial Glycemic Effects - Time and Dose Study (Vuksan et al., 2001)
- **Design**: Randomized controlled trial
- **Sample**: 12 participants
- **Demographics**: Mean age 42±7 years; BMI 24.1±1.1 kg/m²
- **Treatments**: 16 treatments total
- **Dosages Tested**: 0 (placebo), 1, 2, or 3 g
- **Timing Tested**: 40, 20, 10, or 0 min before 25-g oral glucose challenge
- **Key Findings**:
  - All three ginseng doses reduced postprandial glycemia vs placebo during final 45 minutes
  - **Area under curve reductions**: 14.4±6.5% (1g), 10.6±4.0% (2g), 9.1±6% (3g)
  - No significant differences between doses
  - **Optimal timing**: 40 minutes before glucose challenge significantly better than 20, 10, or 0 minutes
- **Clinical Significance**: First study demonstrating time-dependent glycemic effects; established optimal pre-meal timing
- **Source**: Am J Clin Nutr. 2001;73(4):753-8. PMID: 11273850

### Long-Term Safety in Type 2 Diabetes (Mucalo et al., 2014)
- **Design**: Double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled, parallel design
- **Sample**: 74 participants (35 intervention, 39 control)
- **Demographics**: Mean age 63±9.5 years, BMI 32±5
- **Dosage**: 500 mg capsules three times daily with meals (3 g/day)
- **Standardization**: 10% ginsenosides
- **Duration**: 12 weeks
- **Safety Results**:
  - No significant changes in AST (P=0.107) or ALT (P=0.763) - **hepatic safety confirmed**
  - No changes in serum urates (P=0.498) or creatinine (P=0.808) - **renal safety confirmed**
  - No changes in prothrombin time (P=0.469) or INR (P=0.413) - **haemostatic safety confirmed**
  - Only one adverse event: stomach heaviness in one participant who continued treatment
- **Conclusion**: 12 weeks supplementation demonstrated clinical safety in diabetic patients
- **Source**: Evid Based Complement Alternat Med. 2014;2014:969168. PMID: 24891873

## Cardiovascular Effects

### Arterial Stiffness in Diabetic Hypertensive Patients (Mucalo et al., 2013)
- **Design**: Double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled, parallel design
- **Sample**: 64 participants (22 male, 42 female)
- **Demographics**: Mean age 63±9.3 years
- **Baseline Characteristics**: BP 145±10.8/84±8.0 mmHg; HbA1c 7.0±1.3%; fasting glucose 8.1±2.3 mmol/L
- **Dosage**: 3 grams daily
- **Duration**: 12 weeks as adjunct to existing therapy
- **Results**:
  - **Radial augmentation index reduced**: 5.3% compared to placebo (P=0.041)
  - **Systolic blood pressure decreased**: 11.7% (P<0.001)
  - No significant effect on diastolic blood pressure
- **Clinical Significance**: First study examining arterial stiffness effects of American ginseng in diabetic hypertensive patients
- **Source**: J Ethnopharmacol. 2013;150(1):148-53. PMID: 23973636

### Meta-Analysis: Blood Pressure Effects (2016)
- **Study Type**: Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Key Findings**:
  - No significant effect on systolic blood pressure (SBP), diastolic blood pressure (DBP), or mean arterial pressure (MAP) in general population
  - **Stratified analysis favored systolic BP improvement** in diabetes, metabolic syndrome, and obesity
  - Ginseng should not be discouraged for concern of increased blood pressure
- **Note**: Covered both Panax ginseng and Panax quinquefolius
- **Source**: PMID: 27074879

### Vascular Function Meta-Analysis (2025)
- **Study Type**: Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Database Search**: Through February 2025
- **Key Findings**:
  - Pooled analysis showed **significant increase in FMD** (flow-mediated dilation) level
  - **Significant increase in endothelium-derived nitric oxide levels**
  - Ginseng appears to have neutral vascular effects overall
- **Source**: BMC Complement Med Ther. https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12906-025-04936-5

## Neurocognitive Function

### Acute Cognitive Effects (Scholey et al., 2010)
- **Design**: Acute, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, crossover
- **Sample**: 32 healthy young adults
- **Extract**: Cereboost™ (standardized American ginseng extract, 10.65% ginsenosides)
- **Dosages**: 100 mg, 200 mg, 400 mg
- **Measurement Times**: 1, 3, and 6 hours post-administration
- **Results**:
  - **Significant improvement of working memory (WM) performance** associated with P. quinquefolius
  - **Corsi block performance improved** across all doses at all testing times
  - **Choice reaction time accuracy and calmness** significantly improved with 100 mg dose
  - No changes in blood glucose levels observed
- **Clinical Significance**: Demonstrated acute cognitive effects independent of glucose regulation
- **Source**: Psychopharmacology (Berl). 2010;212(3):345-56. PMID: 20676609

### Meta-Analysis: Cognitive Effects (Zeng et al., 2024)
- **Study Type**: Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Studies Included**: 15 randomized controlled trials
- **Total Sample**: 671 participants
- **Key Findings**:
  - **Significant effect on memory improvement**: SMD = 0.19 (95%CI: 0.02-0.36, p < 0.05)
  - **High-dose preparations showed stronger effects**: SMD = 0.33 (95%CI: 0.04-0.61)
  - No positive effects on overall cognition, attention, or executive function
  - **Lower doses (100-400 mg) enhanced attentional and working memory performance** in healthy adults
- **Note**: Meta-analysis included both Panax ginseng and Panax quinquefolius studies
- **Source**: Phytother Res. 2024;38(12):6023-6034. PMID: 39474788

## Respiratory Infection Prevention

### Large Trial in Elderly (McElhaney et al., 2011)
- **Design**: Multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial
- **Sample**: 783 participants (748 in primary analysis)
- **Population**: Community-dwelling adults aged ≥65 years
- **Treatment Groups**:
  - Placebo
  - 400 mg/day CVT-E002
  - 800 mg/day CVT-E002
- **Duration**: 6 months
- **Results**:
  - Laboratory-confirmed clinical URI incidence: 5.5% (placebo), 5.2% (400mg), 4.6% (800mg); P=0.89 (not significant)
  - **Jackson-confirmed URIs (study completers)**: 28.9% (placebo), 20.0% (400mg), 19.4% (800mg); **P<0.04** (significant)
  - **CVT-E002 reduced relative risk of Jackson-confirmed URIs by 31-33%**
  - **Number needed to treat**: 10.5-11.2
  - No significant differences in adverse events; well tolerated at both doses
- **Clinical Significance**: Demonstrated efficacy for reducing respiratory infection severity and duration in elderly population
- **Source**: Influenza Res Treat. 2011;2011:759051. PMID: 23074661

### Pediatric Safety Study (2008)
- **Design**: Phase II randomized controlled trial
- **Population**: Children 3 to 12 years of age
- **Treatment Groups**: Standard dose, Low dose, Placebo
- **Key Findings**: Documented safety and tolerability of 2 weight-based dosing schedules
- **Source**: PMID: 18676527

## Pharmacokinetics and Drug Interactions

### Interaction with HIV Protease Inhibitor (Andrade et al., 2008)
- **Design**: Controlled clinical trial
- **Sample**: 13 for pharmacokinetic analysis; 11 for insulin sensitivity assessment
- **Dosing Regimen**:
  - Indinavir: 800 mg every 8 hours for 3 days, then continued for 14 additional days
  - American ginseng: 1 gram every 8 hours for 14 days (co-administered with indinavir)
- **Results**:
  - **No significant difference in indinavir concentrations** with ginseng co-administration
  - Insulin sensitivity decreased 14.8% (±5.9%) after 3 days of indinavir alone (p=0.03)
  - **Insulin sensitivity remained unchanged** when ginseng was added to indinavir therapy
- **Clinical Significance**: No drug interaction with indinavir; American ginseng may counteract metabolic side effects of protease inhibitors
- **Source**: BMC Complement Altern Med. 2008;8:50. PMID: 18713456

## Research Quality Summary

**Strengths**:
- Multiple Phase III trials with large sample sizes (n=290, 364, 783)
- Dose-finding studies establishing optimal ranges
- Timing studies for glycemic effects
- Safety studies in vulnerable populations (diabetics, cancer patients, elderly)
- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses available
- Traditional use history supporting safety (300+ years in Chinese medicine)

**Key Limitations**:
- Low bioavailability of parent ginsenosides (<5% for most)
- Product quality and adulteration concerns
- Limited long-term cognitive function data
- Lack of pregnancy and lactation safety data
