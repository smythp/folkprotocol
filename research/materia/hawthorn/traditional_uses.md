# Hawthorn (Crataegus spp.) - Traditional Uses

## Historical Background

Hawthorn has been used traditionally across multiple healing systems, primarily for cardiovascular support. The evidence for traditional uses comes from historical monographs, pharmacopoeias, and ethnopharmacological reviews.

## Primary Traditional Uses

### Cardiovascular Support
- Heart failure
- Hypertension
- "Nervous heart complaints" (German Commission E terminology)
- General cardiovascular tonic

### Secondary Traditional Uses
- Indigestion
- Dysmenorrhea (menstrual pain)
- Osteoporosis

**Note:** The correlation between secondary traditional uses (indigestion, dysmenorrhea, osteoporosis) and modern pharmacology remains insufficient according to Cui 2024 comprehensive review.

## Traditional Parts Used

### Leaf with Flower (Most Studied and Approved)
- **German Commission E approval (1978):** Primary approval for "leaf with flower" combined preparation
- **European Medicines Agency:** Assessment specifically for "Crataegus spp., folium cum flore" (leaf with flower)
- **American Herbal Pharmacopoeia:** Dedicated monograph for "Hawthorn Leaf and Flower"
  - Species: Crataegus monogyna, Crataegus laevigata
  - URL: https://herbal-ahp.org/online-ordering-hawthorn-leaf-flower/
  - Note: "Most widely used botanical for supporting cardiovascular system"

### Berries (Traditional Use)
- **British Herbal Pharmacopoeia (1983):** Cited berries only
  - Regarded as standard text for professional herbalists for many years
- **American Herbal Pharmacopoeia:** Separate monograph for "Hawthorn Berry"
  - URL: https://herbal-ahp.org/online-ordering-hawthorn-berry/
- **German Commission E note:** Limited scientific support for berry alone (as of 1993)

### Individual Leaves or Flowers Alone
- **German Commission E note:** Limited scientific support for leaves or flowers used separately (as of 1993)

## Traditional Preparations

### Historical Context
The traditional preparations listed in historical monographs typically involved:
- Infusions (teas) from dried flowers, leaves, or berries
- Tinctures (alcohol extracts)
- Decoctions from berries

### Shift to Standardized Extracts
Modern clinical research has focused on standardized pharmaceutical preparations rather than traditional forms:
- **WS 1442:** Standardized to 17.3-20.1% oligomeric procyanidins
- **LI 132:** Standardized to 2.2% flavonoids
- These standardized extracts show more consistent results than traditional preparations

### Traditional vs. Modern Usage Note
- Historical herbalists used various preparations of leaves, flowers, and berries
- Modern clinical evidence primarily supports standardized leaf-with-flower extracts
- The German Commission E specifically noted limited scientific support for individual plant parts used alone

## Species Traditionally Used

Multiple Crataegus species have been used traditionally:

1. **Crataegus oxyacantha** (synonym for C. laevigata in older literature)
2. **Crataegus monogyna** (Common hawthorn)
3. **Crataegus laevigata** (Midland hawthorn)
4. **Crataegus pinnatifida** (Chinese hawthorn)

**Note:** Modern botanical nomenclature recognizes these as distinct species, though historical sources often used C. oxyacantha as a general term.

## Ethnopharmacological Context

### Comprehensive Review (Cui 2024)
- **Review scope:** 1952-2023 (71 years of literature)
- **Compounds identified:** 337 chemical compounds
- **Traditional uses documented:**
  - Cardiovascular and cerebrovascular system effects
  - Antitumor activity
  - Hepatoprotective properties
  - Antimicrobial effects
  - Immunomodulatory functions
  - Anti-inflammatory activities
- **Source:** Cui M, et al. J Ethnopharmacol. 2024;319(Pt 2):117229. PMID: 37788786

## Cultural Context

### European Tradition
Primary documentation comes from European herbal traditions:
- German phytomedicine tradition (Commission E, clinical research)
- British herbalism (British Herbal Pharmacopoeia)
- Pan-European regulatory recognition (EMA)

### Chinese Tradition
- Crataegus pinnatifida used in Traditional Chinese Medicine
- Included in comprehensive ethnopharmacological reviews
- Subject of ongoing phytochemical research

### North American Context
- Adoption of European traditional knowledge
- Standardization efforts through American Herbal Pharmacopoeia
- Integration with contemporary cardiovascular research
