# Hawthorn (Crataegus spp.) - Preparations and Dosing

## Overview

Hawthorn is available in multiple preparation forms. Modern clinical research has primarily focused on standardized extracts, while traditional herbalism uses teas, tinctures, and whole plant preparations.

## Standardized Pharmaceutical Extracts

### WS 1442 - Most Extensively Studied

#### Specification
- **Extract ratio:** 4-6.6:1 (drug-to-extract ratio)
- **Source material:** Leaves with flowers (Crataegus spp.)
- **Extraction solvent:** Ethanol 45% (w/w)
- **Standardization:** 17.3-20.1% oligomeric procyanidins (OPC)
- **Analytical method:** UHPLC with fluorimetric detection, ICH-Q2 validated
- **Procyanidin composition:** Degree of polymerization (DP) 2-10 plus polymer fraction

#### Clinical Dosing
**Heart Failure (NYHA II-III):**
- **Standard dose:** 900 mg/day (typically divided as 450 mg twice daily)
- **OPC content:** 84.3 mg oligomeric procyanidins per 900 mg dose
- **Duration:**
  - Minimum: 4-8 weeks for initial effects
  - Typical trials: 8 weeks to 6 months
  - Long-term: Up to 24 months (SPICE trial)

#### Clinical Evidence
- **SPICE trial:** 2,681 patients, 900 mg/day for 24 months
- **HERB CHF trial:** 120 patients, 450 mg twice daily for 6 months
- **Leuchtgens 1993:** 30 patients, 1 capsule twice daily for 8 weeks
- **Benefit-risk:** Positive assessment based on >1,300 patients exposed

#### Safety at Standard Dose
- Very favorable safety profile
- No drug interactions observed
- No specific adverse reactions known
- Well-tolerated up to 24 months

**Key References:**
- Holubarsch CJF, et al. Eur J Heart Fail. 2008;10(12):1255-1263. PMID: 19019730
- Holubarsch CJF, et al. Am J Cardiovasc Drugs. 2018;18(1):25-36. PMID: 29080984
- Leuchtgens H. Fortschr Med. 1993;111(20-21):352-354. PMID: 8375791
- Zick SM, et al. Eur J Heart Fail. 2009;11(10):990-999. PMID: 19789403

### LI 132

#### Specification
- **Extraction solvent:** Methanol
- **Standardization:** 2.2% flavonoids
- **Extract ratio:** Not specified in available sources

#### Clinical Dosing
- **Dose range:** 160-1800 mg daily
- **Heart failure:** 600 mg daily (3 dragées) common in trials
- **Duration:** 3 to 24 weeks in clinical trials

#### Clinical Evidence
- Multiple RCTs with variable dosing
- Part of systematic reviews and meta-analyses
- Safety data from 5,577 patients across 24 trials

#### Safety Notes
- Generally well-tolerated
- 8 severe adverse events reported (specific details not fully available)
- Higher doses may be associated with increased adverse events
- Total adverse events: 166 across 5,577 patients (mostly mild to moderate)

**Key Reference:**
- Daniele C, et al. Drug Saf. 2006;29(6):523-535. PMID: 16752934

### Comparison: WS 1442 vs LI 132

| Feature | WS 1442 | LI 132 |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Standardization | 17.3-20.1% OPC | 2.2% flavonoids |
| Extraction solvent | Ethanol 45% | Methanol |
| Typical dose | 900 mg/day | 160-1800 mg/day |
| Largest trial | 2,681 patients | Part of pooled analyses |
| Safety profile | Very favorable, no specific adverse reactions | Generally safe, 8 severe events reported |
| Evidence base | More extensive, better characterized | Multiple trials, variable protocols |

**Recommendation:** WS 1442 has the most consistent and extensive clinical evidence base.

## Fresh Plant Preparations

### Fresh Berry Extract (Crataegisan)

#### Specification
- **Source:** Fresh Crataegus berries
- **Form:** Liquid extract
- **Standardization:** Not specified in available sources

#### Clinical Dosing
- **Dose:** 3 times 30 drops daily
- **Duration:** 8 weeks in clinical trial
- **Population:** NYHA class II heart failure

#### Clinical Evidence
- **Degenring 2003 RCT:** 143 patients (69 active, 74 placebo)
- **Result:** 8.3-watt advantage over placebo (p = 0.045)
- **Effect:** Dyspnoea and fatigue occur at significantly higher wattage
- **Tolerability:** Good tolerability and patient acceptance

**Key Reference:**
- Degenring FH, et al. Phytomedicine. 2003;10(5):363-369. PMID: 12833999

## Traditional Preparations

### Tinctures (Alcohol Extracts)

#### General Preparation
Traditional herbalism typically uses:
- **Ratio:** 1:5 (herb:liquid) is common for dried herb tinctures
- **Alcohol strength:** 25-45% ethanol typically
- **Source material:** Leaf with flower, or berries separately

#### Traditional Dosing (Historical)
**Note:** These are traditional doses from herbalist practice, not from clinical trials:
- **Typical dose:** 2-5 mL, 2-3 times daily
- **Duration:** Chronic use over weeks to months
- **Onset:** Slow-acting; effects develop over 4-8 weeks

#### Modern Clinical Context
- Clinical trials use standardized extracts, not traditional tinctures
- Standardization to OPC content provides more consistent dosing
- Traditional tinctures lack the quality control of pharmaceutical preparations

### Teas (Infusions)

#### Traditional Tea Preparation
- **Source material:** Dried hawthorn flowers, leaves, or combination
- **Ratio:** 1-2 teaspoons dried herb per cup (240 mL)
- **Water temperature:** Boiling water
- **Steeping time:** 10-20 minutes, covered
- **Dosing:** 2-3 cups daily

#### Berry Decoctions
- **Source material:** Dried hawthorn berries
- **Preparation:** Simmer berries in water for 10-15 minutes
- **Traditional use:** Cardiovascular support

#### Clinical Evidence for Teas
- **No clinical trials** using tea preparations found in bibliography
- All clinical evidence based on concentrated standardized extracts
- Traditional use documented in pharmacopoeias (WHO, British Herbal)

#### Considerations
- Tea preparations contain much lower concentrations than standardized extracts
- Infusions extract water-soluble compounds; may miss some active constituents
- Inconsistent dosing compared to standardized preparations
- No data on equivalent dosing between tea and standardized extracts

### Whole Plant Material

#### Historical Use
- **British Herbal Pharmacopoeia (1983):** Cited berries only
- **Traditional preparations:** Powdered dried berries, leaves, or flowers

#### Parts Used Traditionally
1. **Leaf with flower (combined)**
   - Most supported by modern research
   - German Commission E approved form
   - Primary material for WS 1442 extract

2. **Berries alone**
   - British Herbal Pharmacopoeia primary citation
   - Fresh berry extract studied clinically (Crataegisan)
   - Traditional decoctions and preparations

3. **Individual leaves or flowers**
   - German Commission E noted limited scientific support (as of 1993)
   - Less common in traditional use

## Dosing by Condition

### Heart Failure (NYHA II-III)

#### Evidence-Based Dosing
- **WS 1442:** 900 mg/day (450 mg twice daily)
  - Contains 84.3 mg oligomeric procyanidins
  - Duration: Minimum 8 weeks; up to 24 months studied
- **LI 132:** 600 mg daily (3 dragées)
  - Standardized to 2.2% flavonoids
- **Fresh berry extract:** 3 times 30 drops daily
  - 8 weeks minimum

#### Onset of Action
- Slow-acting herb
- Initial effects: 4-8 weeks
- Full benefit: 8-12 weeks
- Requires consistent daily dosing

#### Administration
- Can be taken with or without food (not specified in trials)
- Divided doses (twice daily) common for WS 1442
- Consistent daily administration important for gradual effects

### Hypertension (Mild)

#### Evidence-Based Dosing
- **Minimum duration:** 12 weeks treatment required for blood pressure effects
- **Dose range:** Based on clinical studies (specific doses vary)
- **Szikora 2025 meta-analysis:** 6 RCTs, 428 patients showed clinically significant reduction
- **Cloud 2020 review:** Emphasizes minimum 12-week duration

#### Important Considerations
- Blood pressure effects require longer treatment than heart failure effects
- Not suitable for severe hypertension or acute blood pressure management
- Medical supervision recommended

**Key References:**
- Szikora Z, et al. Pharmaceuticals. 2025;18(7):1027
- Cloud AME, et al. Adv Integr Med. 2020;7(3):167-175

### General Cardiovascular Support

#### Traditional Dosing (Not Evidence-Based)
These are traditional herbalist recommendations, not from clinical trials:
- **Tincture (1:5):** 2-5 mL, 2-3 times daily
- **Tea:** 2-3 cups daily (1-2 tsp dried herb per cup)
- **Duration:** Chronic use over months

#### Clinical Context
- All clinical evidence uses standardized extracts
- Traditional preparations lack clinical validation for specific outcomes
- Consider standardized extracts for evidence-based cardiovascular support

## Quality Control and Standardization

### Pharmaceutical Standards

#### European Pharmacopoeia Requirements
- **Standardization:** Oligomeric procyanidins content required
- **Analytical method:** UHPLC with fluorimetric detection, ICH-Q2 validated
- **Procyanidin range:** DP 2-10 plus polymer fraction
- **Quality control:** Consistent extract ratio, solvent, and marker compounds

#### Why Standardization Matters
1. **Consistency:** Ensures each dose contains known amount of active markers
2. **Reproducibility:** Clinical trials based on standardized preparations
3. **Safety:** Better quality control reduces contamination risk
4. **Efficacy:** Consistent marker content correlates with clinical outcomes

### Extract Specifications Summary

### Selecting Quality Products

#### For Clinical/Therapeutic Use
**Prefer standardized extracts:**
- WS 1442 standardization (17.3-20.1% OPC) has most evidence
- Pharmaceutical-grade extraction and quality control
- Clear dosing based on clinical trials
- Best safety and efficacy data

#### For Traditional Use
**If using tinctures or teas:**
- Source from reputable herbalist suppliers
- Verify botanical identity (correct Crataegus species)
- Use organic or wildcrafted material when possible
- Understand limitations: no clinical trial data for these preparations

## Special Considerations

### Formulation Forms Available
1. **Capsules/tablets:** Most common for standardized extracts
2. **Liquid extracts:** Fresh berry preparations, tinctures
3. **Dried herb:** For tea preparation
4. **Powdered extracts:** May be encapsulated or in powder form

### Storage
- **Dried herb:** Cool, dry, dark location; airtight container
- **Tinctures:** Dark bottles, cool location; stable for 2-5 years
- **Standardized extracts:** Follow manufacturer recommendations
- **Fresh preparations:** Refrigerate if specified

### Product Selection Guidance

#### For Evidence-Based Use
1. Look for products standardized to oligomeric procyanidins (OPC)
2. WS 1442 specification preferred: 17.3-20.1% OPC
3. Pharmaceutical-grade manufacturing
4. Third-party testing verification
5. Clear dosing instructions based on clinical studies

#### For Traditional Use
1. Reputable herbal supplier
2. Organic/wildcrafted preferred
3. Correct botanical identification
4. Appropriate extraction method for intended use
5. Understanding of traditional vs clinical evidence

## Dose Range Across Clinical Studies

### Heart Failure
- **Minimum effective:** 450 mg twice daily (WS 1442)
- **Standard dose:** 900 mg/day
- **Range studied:** 160-1800 mg/day (various extracts)
- **Duration:** Minimum 8 weeks; up to 24 months

### Hypertension
- **Minimum duration:** 12 weeks
- **Specific doses:** Variable across studies (too heterogeneous for meta-analysis)
- **Note:** Fewer studies; less consistent dosing protocols

## Practical Dosing Recommendations

### Evidence-Based Approach
**For heart failure (NYHA II-III) with medical supervision:**
- Use standardized extract (WS 1442 specification preferred)
- Dose: 900 mg/day (450 mg twice daily)
- Duration: Minimum 8 weeks; ongoing use typical
- Monitor: Symptoms, function, under physician care

**For mild hypertension with medical supervision:**
- Use standardized extract
- Duration: Minimum 12 weeks for blood pressure effects
- Monitor: Blood pressure regularly
- Note: Gradual onset; not for acute management

### Traditional Herbalist Approach
**For general cardiovascular support (not therapeutic claims):**
- Tincture (1:5): 2-5 mL, 2-3 times daily
- Tea: 2-3 cups daily (1-2 tsp per cup)
- Duration: Chronic use over months
- Note: No clinical validation for specific outcomes

## Important Clinical Notes

1. **Slow-acting herb:** Requires 4-8 weeks minimum for effects
2. **Consistent use:** Daily dosing essential for gradual cardiovascular effects
3. **Medical supervision:** Recommended for cardiac conditions
4. **Not acute treatment:** Not suitable for acute cardiac events
5. **Standard therapy:** Modern evidence (HERB CHF) suggests limited benefit with contemporary standard therapy
6. **Subgroup effects:** SPICE trial showed benefits in specific subgroups (LVEF ≥25%)
7. **Standardization:** Clinical evidence based on standardized extracts, not traditional preparations
