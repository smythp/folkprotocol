# Hawthorn (Crataegus spp.) - Active Compounds and Bioavailability

## Phytochemical Profile

### Comprehensive Chemical Analysis (Cui 2024)
- **Total compounds identified:** 337 chemical compounds (as of 2023)
- **Review scope:** 1952-2023
- **Major compound classes:**
  1. Flavonoids
  2. Lignans
  3. Fatty acids and organic acids
  4. Monoterpenoids and sesquiterpenoids
  5. Terpenoids and steroids
- **Source:** Cui M, et al. J Ethnopharmacol. 2024;319(Pt 2):117229. PMID: 37788786

## Primary Active Markers for Standardization

### Oligomeric Procyanidins (OPCs)
**Primary standardization marker for pharmaceutical preparations**

#### WS 1442 Specification
- **Extract ratio:** 4-6.6:1 (leaves with flowers)
- **Extraction solvent:** Ethanol 45% (w/w)
- **Standardization range:** 17.3-20.1% oligomeric procyanidins
- **Procyanidin range:** Degree of polymerization (DP) 2-10 plus polymer fraction
- **Clinical use:** Most extensively studied extract; used in SPICE trial (900 mg/day = 84.3 mg OPC)

#### LI 132 Specification
- **Extraction solvent:** Methanolic extract
- **Standardization:** 2.2% flavonoids
- **Clinical dosing range:** 160-1800 mg daily across studies

#### Regulatory Standards
- **European Pharmacopoeia:** Requires oligomeric procyanidins content standardization
- **Analytical methods:** UHPLC with fluorimetric detection, ICH-Q2 validated
- **Procyanidin composition:** DP 2-10 oligomers plus polymer fraction

## Bioavailability Studies

### Epicatechin - Primary Absorbed Compound

#### Oral Absorption (Chang 2005)
**Animal model:** Male Sprague-Dawley rats (n=5 per group)

**Compounds tested:**
- (-)-Epicatechin
- Chlorogenic acid
- Hyperoside
- Isoquercitrin

**Administration routes:** Oral and intravenous

**Key findings:**
- **Only epicatechin was absorbed** from either extract or pure compound when given orally
- **Bioavailability:** 10.9-34.2% (extract vs pure compound)
  - Extract formulation: 10.9% bioavailability
  - Pure compound: 34.2% bioavailability
- Pharmacokinetics generally not significantly different between extract and pure compound for oral administration

**Intravenous administration findings:**
- Extract formulation produced higher plasma concentrations
- Larger area under curve
- Longer elimination half-life
- Smaller volume of distribution
- Lower total body clearance compared to pure compounds

**Mechanism:** Co-occurring components in hawthorn phenolic extract affect systemic behavior

**Source:** Chang Q, et al. J Clin Pharmacol. 2005;45(1):106-112. PMID: 15601812

### Limited Permeability of Flavonoids (Zuo 2006)

**Study methods:**
- In vitro: Caco-2 monolayer cell model
- In situ: Rat intestinal perfusion model
- In vivo: Pharmacokinetic studies in Sprague-Dawley rats

**Compounds studied:**
- Hyperoside
- Isoquercitrin
- Epicatechin

**Key findings:**
- **Permeability:** All three flavonoids have quite limited permeabilities
- **Oral absorption:** Epicatechin only compound detected in plasma after oral hawthorn extract
- **Metabolism:** EC and isoquercitrin showed more extensive metabolism in in situ and in vivo vs Caco-2 model
- **Extract interactions:** Co-occurring components in extract may not significantly affect intestinal absorption
- **Correlation:** Good correlation between in vitro, in situ, and in vivo absorption data

**Source:** Zuo Z, et al. Life Sci. 2006;79(26):2455-2462. PMID: 16989871

### Vitexin-2''-O-rhamnoside (Xu 2008)

**Animal model:** Rat

**Permeability classification:** High permeability class drug (per Biopharmaceutics Classification System)

**Effective permeability:**
- Value: (2.18 ± 0.48) to (2.48 ± 0.33) × 10⁻⁵ cm/s
- Location: Rat jejunal perfusion
- Concentration-dependent range tested
- **Bioavailability:** 3.57%

**Transport mechanisms:**
- **Primary mechanism:** Passive diffusion dominates absorptive transport
- **Efflux:** Absorption and secretion mediated by P-glycoprotein (P-gp)
- **Regional variation:** No significant differences across intestinal segments (duodenum, jejunum, ileum, colon)
- **Enhancement:** P-gp inhibitors (verapamil, digoxin) significantly enhanced absorption and permeability

**Source:** Xu YA, et al. Drug Dev Ind Pharm. 2008;34(2):164-170. PMID: 18302035

## Bioavailability Summary

### Absorbed Compounds
1. **(-)-Epicatechin**
   - Primary absorbed flavonoid
   - Bioavailability: 10.9% (extract) to 34.2% (pure compound)
   - Detected in plasma after oral hawthorn extract administration
   - Parent form absorption

2. **Vitexin-2''-O-rhamnoside**
   - High permeability classification
   - Bioavailability: 3.57%
   - Passive diffusion primary mechanism
   - P-glycoprotein efflux affects absorption

### Poorly Absorbed Compounds
1. **Chlorogenic acid** - Not detected in plasma after oral administration
2. **Hyperoside** - Limited permeability, not detected in plasma
3. **Isoquercitrin** - Limited permeability, not detected in plasma

### Factors Affecting Bioavailability
1. **P-glycoprotein efflux** - Active efflux mechanism reduces absorption
2. **Extensive first-pass metabolism** - Particularly for isoquercitrin
3. **Limited intestinal permeability** - Most flavonoids have poor absorption
4. **Co-occurring components** - May affect systemic behavior but not intestinal absorption significantly

## Pharmacological Activity

### Cardiovascular Effects
Primary mechanisms attributed to hawthorn compounds:
- Positive inotropic effects (increased contractility)
- Vasodilation
- Antioxidant properties
- Blood pressure reduction
- Cardioprotective effects

### Comprehensive Pharmacological Activities (Cui 2024)
Based on 337 identified compounds:
1. **Cardiovascular and cerebrovascular system effects** (primary)
2. Antitumor activity
3. Hepatoprotective properties
4. Antimicrobial effects
5. Immunomodulatory functions
6. Anti-inflammatory activities

## Clinical Relevance of Bioavailability

### Low Bioavailability Paradox
Despite low oral bioavailability of most flavonoids:
- Clinical trials show cardiovascular benefits
- Meta-analyses demonstrate significant effects
- Standardized extracts produce measurable outcomes

### Possible Explanations
1. **Epicatechin as primary active** - Better absorbed than other flavonoids
2. **Local intestinal effects** - May not require systemic absorption
3. **Metabolite activity** - Active metabolites may contribute to effects
4. **Oligomeric procyanidins** - May have better bioavailability than monomeric flavonoids
5. **Synergistic effects** - Multiple compounds working together

### Safety Implications (Yao 2008)
- Safety at recommended doses may be due to low bioavailability of some constituents when taken orally
- No reproductive toxicity found at 56x human dose in rats
- Recommendation: Pharmacokinetic studies in humans needed to verify safety interpretations
- **Source:** Yao M, et al. J Ethnopharmacol. 2008;118(1):127-132. PMID: 18485639

## Analytical Methods

### Modern Standardization
- **Method:** UHPLC with fluorimetric detection
- **Validation:** ICH-Q2 validated methods
- **Target compounds:** Oligomeric procyanidins (DP 2-10 plus polymer fraction)
- **Regulatory requirement:** European Pharmacopoeia requires OPC standardization

### Quality Control Markers
1. **Primary:** Oligomeric procyanidins (% OPC)
2. **Secondary:** Total flavonoids
3. **Extract ratio:** Drug-to-extract ratio (e.g., 4-6.6:1 for WS 1442)
4. **Extraction solvent:** Critical for consistency (ethanol vs methanol)

## Clinical Extract Specifications

### WS 1442 (Most Studied)
- **Extraction:** 4-6.6:1 from leaves with flowers
- **Solvent:** Ethanol 45% (w/w)
- **Standardization:** 17.3-20.1% oligomeric procyanidins
- **Clinical dose:** 900 mg/day (provides 84.3 mg OPC)
- **Studies:** SPICE trial (2,681 patients), multiple RCTs
- **Benefit-risk:** Positive assessment based on >1,300 patients

### LI 132
- **Extraction:** Methanolic extract
- **Standardization:** 2.2% flavonoids
- **Clinical dose range:** 160-1800 mg daily
- **Studies:** Multiple trials, some severe adverse events reported at high doses

### Fresh Berry Extract (Crataegisan)
- **Preparation:** Fresh Crataegus berry extract
- **Clinical dose:** 3 times 30 drops daily
- **Study result:** 8.3-watt improvement in 143 NYHA II patients
- **Standardization:** Not specified in available literature

## Research Implications

### Evidence Gaps
1. **Human pharmacokinetic data:** Most studies in rats; human data limited
2. **Active component identification:** Unclear which compounds are responsible for clinical effects
3. **Oligomeric procyanidin bioavailability:** Limited data on OPC absorption despite being standardization marker
4. **Metabolite identification:** Active metabolites not fully characterized
5. **Dose-response relationships:** Optimal OPC dose not established

### Future Research Needs
1. Human pharmacokinetic studies of major hawthorn compounds
2. Metabolite identification and activity testing
3. Oligomeric procyanidin absorption and distribution studies
4. Mechanism of action studies linking specific compounds to cardiovascular effects
5. Comparative bioavailability of different extracts and preparations
