# St. John's Wort (Hypericum perforatum) - Traditional Uses

## Historical Context

St. John's Wort (Hypericum perforatum L., family Clusiaceae) has been used medicinally for over 2,000 years across multiple healing traditions.

## European Traditional Medicine

### Historical Timeline

#### Ancient Greece (1st Century CE)
St. John's Wort was documented by ancient Greek physicians including Dioscorides and Hippocrates for wound healing and nerve pain.

#### Medieval Europe (5th-15th Century)
- Used as a protective herb against evil spirits and mental disorders
- Named after St. John the Baptist (flowers bloom around St. John's Day, June 24th)
- Associated with midsummer celebrations and protection rituals
- Applied to wounds, burns, and injuries

#### Renaissance Herbalism (15th-17th Century)
Documented in European herbals for:
- Melancholy (depression)
- Nervous disorders
- Wounds and bruises
- Internal injuries

### German Commission E (1984)
Official recognition of traditional uses:
- **Internal**: Depression (900 mg standardized extract per day)
- **External**: St. John's wort oil for acute injuries, myalgia (muscle pain), and first-degree burns

### Traditional European Preparations

#### St. John's Wort Oil (Oleum Hyperici)
- **Traditional Method**: Fresh flowers macerated in olive oil or sunflower oil
- **Preparation Time**: 3-6 weeks in sunlight until oil turns deep red
- **Color**: Characteristic deep red color from hypericin
- **Traditional Uses**:
  - Wounds and burns (topical application)
  - Muscle pain and inflammation
  - Nerve pain (neuralgia)
  - Bruises and contusions

#### Traditional Tea (Infusion)
- **Plant Part**: Dried flowering tops
- **Preparation**: 1-2 teaspoons dried herb per cup of boiling water
- **Steeping**: 10-15 minutes, covered
- **Traditional Dose**: 1-3 cups daily
- **Traditional Uses**:
  - Melancholy and low spirits
  - Nervous exhaustion
  - Sleep disturbances
  - Digestive complaints

#### Tincture (Folk Preparations)
- **Alcohol Extraction**: Fresh or dried flowering tops in alcohol (typically 40-60% alcohol)
- **Traditional Ratio**: 1:5 or 1:10 (herb to alcohol)
- **Maceration Time**: 2-4 weeks
- **Traditional Dose**: 20-30 drops, 2-3 times daily

## WHO Monograph Recognition (2002)

The World Health Organization officially recognized St. John's Wort in **WHO Monographs on Selected Medicinal Plants - Volume 2 (2002)**:

### Documented Traditional Uses
- **Depression and mood disorders**
- **Wound healing** (topical)
- **Anti-inflammatory** (external application)
- **Antibacterial and antiviral** properties
- **Nervous system support**

### Plant Identification
- **Latin Name**: Hypericum perforatum L.
- **Family**: Clusiaceae (formerly Hypericaceae)
- **Common Names**: St. John's Wort, Common St. John's Wort, Hypericum
- **Traditional Names**: Herba Hyperici (Latin), Johanniskraut (German)

## European Scientific Cooperative on Phytotherapy (ESCOP, 2018)

### Plant Material
- **Part Used**: Whole or cut, dried flowering tops harvested during flowering time
- **Traditional Harvest**: Peak flowering period (around St. John's Day, June 24th)
- **Quality**: Fresh flowering tops preferred for oil preparations

### Traditional Applications Documented
1. **Mental/Emotional Health**
   - Mild to moderate depression
   - Nervous tension
   - Mental exhaustion
   - Seasonal affective symptoms

2. **Topical/External Uses**
   - Minor wounds and cuts
   - Burns (first-degree)
   - Bruises and contusions
   - Muscle pain (myalgia)
   - Nerve pain (neuralgia)

## American Herbal Pharmacopoeia (1997)

The American Herbal Pharmacopoeia published quality control standards and documented traditional uses in North America:

### Traditional North American Uses
- Brought to North America by European settlers
- Used by Eclectic physicians (19th-early 20th century) for:
  - Melancholy and nervous disorders
  - Spinal injuries and nerve damage
  - Wounds and burns

## European Medicines Agency (EMA/HMPC, 2018)

### Well-Established Use
- **Indication**: Treatment of mild to moderate depressive episodes
- **Evidence**: Based on clinical trials and long-standing traditional use

### Traditional Use
- **Indication**: Relief of temporary mental exhaustion
- **Basis**: Long-standing traditional use in Europe (at least 30 years, including 15 years within EU)

## Traditional Preparation Methods

### For Internal Use (Depression, Nervous System)

#### Standardized Extract (Modern Traditional Method)
- **Form**: Tablets or capsules
- **Standardization**: 0.3% hypericin, 2-4% hyperforin
- **Dose**: 900 mg/day (300 mg three times daily)
- **Duration**: At least 4-6 weeks for initial assessment

#### Tea/Infusion (Historical Method)
- **Amount**: 2-4 grams dried herb per day
- **Preparation**: Steep 1-2 teaspoons in 1 cup boiling water for 10-15 minutes
- **Frequency**: 1-3 cups daily
- **Note**: Less concentrated than standardized extracts; traditional for mild symptoms

#### Tincture (Historical Method)
- **Ratio**: 1:5 (herb to alcohol, 40-60% alcohol)
- **Dose**: 2-4 mL (approximately 40-80 drops), 2-3 times daily
- **Traditional Use**: Nervous system support, mild depression

### For External Use (Wounds, Burns, Pain)

#### St. John's Wort Oil (Oleum Hyperici)
- **Preparation**:
  1. Harvest fresh flowers at peak bloom (late June-July)
  2. Fill a glass jar with flowers
  3. Cover with olive oil or sunflower oil
  4. Place in sunlight for 3-6 weeks (or warm location)
  5. Oil turns deep red when ready (hypericin extraction)
  6. Strain and store in dark glass bottles
- **Application**: Apply directly to affected area 2-3 times daily
- **Traditional Uses**:
  - Minor burns and sunburn
  - Wounds and cuts
  - Bruises and muscle pain
  - Nerve pain (neuralgia)

#### Poultice (Historical Method)
- **Preparation**: Crush fresh or dried herb with small amount of warm water to form paste
- **Application**: Apply directly to skin, cover with cloth
- **Traditional Use**: Wounds, bruises, inflammation

## Traditional Folklore and Cultural Significance

### Name Origin
- **Common Name**: Named after St. John the Baptist
- **Timing**: Flowers bloom around St. John's Day (June 24th, midsummer)
- **Symbolism**: Associated with protection, light, and healing

### Medieval Beliefs
- Hung over doorways to ward off evil spirits
- Used in midsummer festivals (St. John's Eve)
- Believed to have protective and purifying properties
- Associated with banishing darkness (both literal and metaphorical)

### Physical Characteristics
- **"Perforatum"**: Latin for "perforated" - refers to translucent dots on leaves
- **Traditional Identification**: Hold leaves to light to see translucent glands (appear as perforations)
- **Flower Color**: Bright yellow flowers with black dots on petal margins (hypericin glands)

## Summary: Traditional Therapeutic Applications

### Internal Uses (Historical)
1. **Mental/Emotional Disorders**
   - Melancholy (depression)
   - Nervous tension and anxiety
   - Mental exhaustion
   - Sleep disturbances

2. **Nervous System**

3. **Digestive System**

   - Stomach upset

### External Uses (Historical)
1. **Wound Healing**
   - Cuts and abrasions
   - Burns (first-degree)
   - Bruises and contusions

2. **Pain and Inflammation**
   - Muscle pain (myalgia)
   - Joint pain
   - Nerve pain (topical application)
   - Inflammation

### Traditional Harvest and Preparation
- **Harvest Time**: During flowering (late June-July, around St. John's Day)
- **Plant Part**: Flowering tops with upper leaves
- **Preparations**: Tea, tincture, oil infusion, dried herb
- **Quality Marker**: Deep red color of oil indicates proper hypericin extraction

## Modern Recognition of Traditional Uses

St. John's Wort represents a successful bridge between traditional medicine and modern clinical evidence:

- **Traditional Use**: Over 2,000 years of documented use for mood disorders and wounds
- **Modern Validation**: Multiple clinical trials confirm efficacy for mild-to-moderate depression
- **Regulatory Status**: Recognized by WHO, German Commission E, EMA, and ESCOP
- **Contemporary Use**: One of the most widely used herbal antidepressants worldwide
