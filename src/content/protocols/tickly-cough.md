## Overview

Tickly cough refers to a dry, non-productive cough characterized by persistent throat irritation without mucus production.

Demulcent herbs containing polysaccharides form protective coatings over mucous membranes [6,7]. These herbs demonstrate evidence for symptomatic relief of dry cough through mechanical and anti-inflammatory mechanisms [6,7,8].

## The Landscape

**Strong evidence:**

Honey demonstrates superior efficacy to both placebo and usual care for nocturnal cough in children. A Cochrane review of 6 RCTs (n=899) found honey reduced cough frequency more than no treatment (MD -1.05, 95% CI -1.48 to -0.62) and improved sleep quality for both children (MD -1.04, 95% CI -1.57 to -0.51) and parents (MD -0.88, 95% CI -1.23 to -0.52) [1]. The BMJ meta-analysis of 14 RCTs reported honey superior to usual care for combined symptom relief (MD -3.96, 95% CI -5.42 to -2.51) and cough frequency (SMD -0.36, 95% CI -0.50 to -0.21), with very low heterogeneity (I²=0%) indicating consistent benefit [2]. Dosing ranged from 2.5-10ml before bedtime [1].

Critical contraindication: Honey must not be given to infants under 12 months due to risk of infant botulism from Clostridium botulinum spores [3].

**Moderate evidence:**

Marshmallow root (*Althaea officinalis*) has regulatory approval for dry irritating cough from both German Commission E and the European Medicines Agency HMPC based on traditional use and limited clinical data [4,5]. A consumer survey (n=822) reported rapid onset of effects for symptomatic treatment of dry cough with marshmallow root extract [6]. The herb contains approximately 20% mucilage, predominantly polysaccharides including pectin, which form a protective film over irritated mucous membranes [6,7]. Animal studies demonstrate statistically significant decreases in cough efforts from both laryngopharyngeal and tracheobronchial stimulation [8].

Licorice root (*Glycyrrhiza glabra*) shows antitussive activity through both peripheral demulcent effects and central cough suppression mechanisms. Preclinical studies found liquiritin apioside, liquiritin, and liquiritigenin at 50 mg/kg decreased cough frequency by 30-78% in ammonia-induced cough models [9]. The EMA HMPC recognizes traditional use as expectorant in cough associated with common cold, recommending 1.5g in 150ml infusion for bronchitis or cough [10].

Safety concerns limit licorice use: as little as 100mg/day glycyrrhizic acid causes significant blood pressure increases [11]. Glycyrrhizin has mineralocorticoid-like effects causing hypokalemia, hypertension, cardiac arrhythmia, and myopathy [11]. Contraindicated in hypertension, heart failure, pulmonary hypertension, or kidney disease [11]. Recommended maximum: 100mg glycyrrhizin/day for short-term use only (4-6 weeks maximum) [11].

**Limited clinical evidence:**

Slippery elm (*Ulmus rubra*) inner bark contains abundant mucilage with demulcent and antitussive properties [12]. The FDA classifies it as GRAS (generally recognized as safe) [12]. However, clinical evidence is minimal and sustainability concerns are significant: Dutch Elm Disease has decimated wild populations, with no viable cultivated sources currently available [13,14]. Ethical sourcing requires harvesting only from naturally fallen or already-cut trees [13,14].

Herbal combination formulas containing ivy/primrose/thyme show strong evidence in meta-analysis (RR=1.40, 95% CI 1.23-1.60, p<0.001) for cough improvement [15], though effects are attributed to the combination rather than individual demulcent action.

## What We're Looking For

Clinical trials of cough remedies measure:

- **Cough frequency reduction** (number of coughing episodes per day)
- **Cough severity scores** (typically 0-6 or 0-10 scales)
- **Sleep disruption** (nighttime awakenings, sleep quality for patient and caregivers)
- **Cough duration** (days to resolution)
- **Combined symptom scores** (multi-item validated scales)

A mean difference of 1.0-1.3 points on a 7-point Likert scale represents clinically meaningful improvement [1,2]. Reduction in sleep disruption of approximately 1 point on the same scale indicates significant benefit for quality of life [1].

## Core Protocol

**Primary approach: Marshmallow root cold infusion**

Cold water extraction preserves delicate mucilaginous polysaccharides while avoiding starch extraction that occurs with heat [16,17]. Preparation: 1-2 tablespoons dried cut marshmallow root per 8 oz (240ml) cold water, steeped 4-12 hours, strained [16,17]. The resulting liquid has mildly sweet taste with characteristic mucilaginous texture [18].

German Commission E recommends 6g root or equivalent preparations daily; syrup dosing at 10ml single dose [4]. Dosing frequency: every 2-4 hours during acute symptoms to maintain protective coating, which persists approximately 2-4 hours per dose [19].

The mucilage forms a protective layer over pharyngeal and throat mucosa, physically shielding irritated tissue from further irritation while providing anti-inflammatory effects [6,7,8].

**Concentrated preparation for nighttime use:**

Marshmallow-honey syrup combines evidence for both ingredients. Method: Simmer 14g (½ oz) dried marshmallow root in 1 liter water, reduce volume by half through gentle heating, strain thoroughly, combine with equal volume raw honey (1:1 ratio) while warm but not hot (to preserve honey's bioactive compounds) [20]. Refrigerate; stable approximately 1 month [20]. Dosing: 5-10ml (1-2 teaspoons) before bedtime and as needed for nocturnal cough [1,20].

**Licorice as adjunct only:**

Due to blood pressure and cardiac concerns, licorice should be used in small amounts as flavor enhancer and anti-inflammatory adjunct rather than primary remedy [10,11]. Safe short-term dosing: 0.5-1g powdered root 1-3 times daily, with total daily glycyrrhizin intake below 100mg when accounting for all sources (teas, lozenges, candies) [9,10,11]. Maximum duration: 4-6 weeks [11].

## Expected Timeline

**Immediate relief (within minutes to 2 hours):**
Demulcent coating provides mechanical protection and soothing sensation upon contact with irritated mucosa [16,17,18].

**24-48 hours:**
Honey demonstrates measurable reduction in nocturnal cough frequency and severity within first night of use [1,2]. Marshmallow consumer survey reports "very rapid onset of effects" [6].

**3-7 days:**
Progressive improvement in cough frequency, severity, and sleep quality with consistent demulcent use. Combination herbal formulas show significant benefit by days 7-9 of treatment [15].

**Duration of coating effect:**
Individual doses provide symptomatic relief for approximately 2-4 hours, requiring frequent re-dosing (3-6 times daily) during acute symptoms [19].

## N=1 Tracking

**Quantifiable measures:**

**Cough frequency log:**
- Count coughing episodes in designated 1-hour windows (morning, afternoon, evening, night)
- Record nighttime awakenings due to cough
- Track episodes per day

**Severity rating:**
- Use 0-10 scale for cough severity (0=none, 10=worst imaginable)
- Rate at same times daily for consistency
- Track "bothersome" nature of cough (how much it disrupts activities)

**Sleep quality:**
- Hours of continuous sleep before cough awakening
- Total awakenings per night
- Partner's sleep disruption (if applicable)

**Functional impact:**
- Ability to complete sentences without interruption
- Activities that trigger coughing (talking, laughing, exercise)
- Cough-free intervals (longest period without coughing)

**Response to intervention:**
- Time from dose to symptom relief
- Duration of relief per dose
- Need for rescue dosing

**Baseline measurement:**
Track for 2-3 days before intervention to establish pattern, then continue tracking during treatment. Meaningful improvement: reduction of ≥1 point on severity scale, ≥30% reduction in frequency, or ≥1 hour increase in continuous sleep [1,2].
