## What The Evidence Says

Oil of oregano has a surprising clinical story: its strongest emerging evidence isn't for the antimicrobial and antifungal uses it's marketed for, but for **chronic sinus congestion**.

**Strongest evidence:**
- **Chronic rhinosinusitis (sinusitis without nasal polyps):** A 75-person RCT found oregano oil nasal spray reduced sinus symptoms by 51.52 points on a validated scale (SNOT-22) — compared to 21.60 points for fluticasone nasal steroid and 11.84 for placebo [1]. Separately, a network meta-analysis of 47 RCTs ranked *O. vulgare* first among 18 herbal treatments for chronic sinusitis without polyps [2]. The two findings are independent and point the same direction. Low certainty by GRADE; independent replication is still needed.
- **Respiratory inflammation via carvacrol:** Five RCTs using purified carvacrol — the main active compound in high-quality oregano oil — at 1.2 mg/kg/day for 2 months consistently improved lung function (FEV1, FVC, peak flow), reduced inflammatory cytokines (IL-4, IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α), and decreased oxidative stress markers in asthmatic patients [3, 4] and in veterans with chronic chemical-induced lung injury [5]. A 2020 systematic review synthesized these findings across 5 human studies [6]. All 5 efficacy RCTs came from the same Iranian research group — independent replication is still absent.

**Moderate signals:**
- **Antiparasitic:** In 14 adults with confirmed parasitic infection, 600 mg/day emulsified oregano oil for 6 weeks cleared Entamoeba in all 4 cases (100%) and Blastocystis in 8/11 cases (73%) [7]. Uncontrolled — no comparison group — but it's the only published human trial and a notable effect size.
- **Enteric antimicrobial:** In 106 patients with metabolic syndrome and confirmed enteric bacterial infection, oregano essential oil showed antibacterial activity comparable to antibiotic treatment — with no gut microbiome disruption [8]. Not randomized or placebo-controlled.
- **Exercise recovery:** A single 500 mg dose of oregano powder post-exercise reduced creatine kinase and malondialdehyde (muscle damage markers) and increased antioxidant enzyme activity in 24 young soldiers within 120 minutes [9]. Single acute dose; not replicable to chronic supplementation yet.

**What hasn't been proven (despite the hype):**
H. pylori eradication, Candida overgrowth, antiviral activity, and immune system support all have compelling in vitro data or anecdotal reports. Controlled human trials are absent or inadequate. One 15-person uncontrolled case series reported 93% H. pylori clearance using a multi-herb blend — compelling signal, not clinical evidence [12].

**The carvacrol issue:** Much of the respiratory evidence uses purified carvacrol, not whole oregano oil. At the studied dose of 1.2 mg/kg/day, a 70 kg person needs ~84 mg carvacrol/day — equivalent to roughly 112–168 mg of oregano oil standardized to 50–75% carvacrol. This dose conversion makes the research directly applicable to high-carvacrol products, but irrelevant to products with unverified or low carvacrol content.

## Traditional Use

**The herb has millennia of use. The oil supplement is a 1990s invention.**

**Ancient Greek medicine (Dioscorides, 1st century CE):**
- Cough, bronchitis, sinus congestion — steam inhalation and decoctions
- Indigestion, bloating, intestinal spasms — infusions
- Wound antisepsis and skin infections — poultice
- Gastrointestinal parasite expulsion — internal preparations

**European phytotherapy (19th–20th century):**
- Cough, bronchitis, respiratory congestion (herbal tea, steam inhalation)
- Digestive spasms, flatulence (tincture, tea)
- Related species (*O. majorana*, *O. dictamnus*) formally recognized by the European Medicines Agency for mild GI complaints and skin irritation around nasal passages — establishing the genus pattern, even without an official *O. vulgare* monograph

**Unani and Arab medicine (9th–16th century):**
- Respiratory complaints, digestive disorders, and antiseptic use under the name *za'atar*

**Mexican folk medicine:**
- Digestive remedy, cough suppressant, emmenagogue (menstrual regulator)
- Abortifacient use documented — the historical basis of the modern pregnancy contraindication

**The gap between tradition and supplement:** Traditional medicine used dried herb as infusions, decoctions, steam, and tinctures. The concentrated steam-distilled essential oil sold today never existed in these traditions. Modern oregano oil supplements inherit the traditional reputation by extrapolation — the two preparations have different compound profiles and different delivery mechanisms, and should not be conflated.

## How To Try It

### Choose Your Preparation

**This is not optional.** Which product you choose determines whether you're taking the studied compound or essentially a cooking spice.

Common culinary oregano (*O. vulgare* subsp. *vulgare*) often contains less than 20% carvacrol. Greek oregano (*O. vulgare* subsp. *hirtum*) contains 60–86% [14]. A product made from the wrong subspecies may contain essentially no therapeutic carvacrol.

**Required label information:**
- Species: *Origanum vulgare* subsp. *hirtum* — or "Greek oregano" or "Mediterranean oregano with ≥60% carvacrol"
- Carvacrol percentage: at least 60% (European Pharmacopoeia minimum); prefer 70–80% for therapeutic use
- Certificate of Analysis (CoA) confirming carvacrol by gas chromatography — ask the company if not posted

| Form | Best For | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| **Softgel capsules** | Sinusitis, respiratory, antiparasitic | Closest to studied preparations; measurable dose; no burn |
| **Liquid oil in carrier** | Flexible dosing; topical combination | Intense mucosal irritation if under-diluted; harder to dose |
| **Herbal tea** | Digestive support, tradition | Minimal carvacrol; different active compounds (phenolics) |
| **Tincture** | Full-spectrum herb support | Less standardized; more traditional |

### Dosing

**Capsules (evidence-referenced starting points):**
- Start: 150–300 mg/day with food
- Antiparasitic (Force 2000): 600 mg/day × 6 weeks
- Carvacrol-referenced respiratory dose for 70 kg adult: ~84 mg carvacrol/day

**Calculate your dose:** Divide target carvacrol (mg) by product carvacrol fraction.
- Example: 84 mg carvacrol target ÷ 0.75 (75% product) = 112 mg product/day
- A 150 mg capsule at 75% carvacrol delivers ~113 mg carvacrol — one capsule covers the target dose
- A 500 mg capsule at 20% carvacrol delivers only 100 mg carvacrol at triple the product amount

**Liquid drops:** Minimum 1:4 dilution in carrier oil or juice. Never undiluted — essential oil burns mucosal tissue. 3–5 drops 1–3 times daily is the commercial convention; dose varies widely by product carvacrol concentration.

**Herbal tea:** 1–2 teaspoons dried herb per cup, covered steep 10–15 minutes, 1–3 cups daily. Does not deliver therapeutic carvacrol; indicated for traditional digestive and respiratory herb support.

### Timeline

- **Digestive and acute antimicrobial:** Days to 2 weeks
- **Sinusitis:** Trial the 4-week RCT duration before evaluating
- **Respiratory/inflammatory:** 2 months is the studied duration for carvacrol effects

## What To Track

**Baseline (one week before starting):**
- Symptom score for your target indication (sinus congestion 1–10, respiratory symptoms, digestive complaints)
- GI baseline — existing heartburn, bowel habits — to distinguish side effects from benefits

**During trial:**
- GI tolerance in week 1 (heartburn, nausea are most common; take with food)
- Target symptom changes at 2 weeks, 4 weeks
- Any skin changes, hives, worsening breathing or nasal symptoms (allergy signal)

**STOP immediately if:**
- Hives, facial swelling, increased breathing difficulty — anaphylactic allergy (Lamiaceae)
- Worsening GI symptoms that persist after reducing dose
- Rash from topical use

## Who This Is/Isn't For

### Most likely to benefit:
- **Chronic sinusitis** — the strongest and most consistent indication across two independent studies
- **Recurrent respiratory infections** — convergent antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory evidence supports this use
- **Confirmed intestinal parasites** — 600 mg/day × 6 weeks is the only published human protocol (n=14); reasonable to try alongside, not instead of, medical evaluation
- **Asthma as adjunct** — carvacrol data is promising; do not replace prescribed medications
- **Bloating, digestive cramping, mild bacterial GI issues** — traditional use with some clinical signals

### Insufficient evidence for:
- **H. pylori** — strong in vitro activity; one uncontrolled 15-person case series with a multi-herb blend; no RCT
- **Candida/fungal infections** — compelling in vitro; zero human trial data
- **Antiviral protection / cold prevention** — in vitro only
- **General immune strengthening** — no human data

### Should not use:
- **Pregnant or trying to conceive** — contraindicated; abortifacient traditional use documented across multiple cultures; non-negotiable
- **Breastfeeding** — avoid medicinal doses; insufficient infant exposure data
- **Lamiaceae allergy** (mint, thyme, basil, sage, rosemary) — known cross-reactivity; test carefully if family allergy history
- **On warfarin or antiplatelet drugs** — theoretical potentiation; disclose to prescriber
- **On insulin or oral hypoglycemics** — theoretical additive blood glucose lowering; monitor
- **Children** — no pediatric safety data established

## The Burn

Liquid oil of oregano drops have a reputation — earned. Undiluted or poorly diluted, the essential oil produces an intense, sustained burning sensation in the mouth, throat, and esophagus. This isn't a sign it's working. It's carvacrol acting as a chemical irritant on mucous membranes at high concentration — the same mechanism that makes it antimicrobial in vitro also irritates tissue.

If using liquid drops: always dilute in carrier oil first, or take in juice. Capsules avoid the problem entirely and are better studied for clinical indications. The same principle applies topically — always patch test on the inner arm first, dilute to 1–2% in carrier oil (1–2 drops per teaspoon), and keep away from eyes, face, and broken skin.

## Quality Matters

**The species problem is real.** "Oil of oregano" supplements vary from ≥80% carvacrol therapeutic preparations to products made from common culinary oregano containing less than 20% carvacrol. The label alone won't tell you unless subspecies and carvacrol percentage are stated.

**Non-negotiable requirements:**
- Subspecies stated: *O. vulgare* subsp. *hirtum*, "Greek oregano," or "Mediterranean oregano"
- Carvacrol percentage on label: ≥60% (minimum); prefer 70–80% for therapeutic use
- Certificate of Analysis (CoA) from third-party GC testing — this is a document, not a claim; find it on the company website or request it
- Emulsified softgel form preferred for oral bioavailability over oil dissolved in carrier lipid

**Avoid:**
- Products without subspecies specification
- No carvacrol percentage disclosed
- No CoA available
- Undiluted essential oil for any internal use

**Regulatory context:** FDA classifies oregano as GRAS (generally recognized as safe) as a food flavoring — this covers culinary amounts, not supplement doses. Health Canada accepts it as a natural health product for antioxidant and digestive use. The EFSA established a no-adverse-effect level (NOAEL) of 200 mg/kg/day in 90-day rat studies — a safety margin of roughly 4,000x over typical dietary exposure, providing meaningful reassurance for standard supplement doses (150–600 mg/day) [14]. LiverTox rates it "unlikely to cause liver injury" — no cases documented in clinical registries [13].

## The Bottom Line

Oil of oregano's popular reputation centers on being a powerful natural antibiotic and antifungal. That reputation is partly supported by strong in vitro data — carvacrol does disrupt bacterial cell membranes and kill Candida in a dish. The human evidence for those uses is thinner than the marketing suggests.

What's better documented is chronic sinusitis, where oregano outperformed a prescription nasal steroid in one trial and ranked first in a network meta-analysis. And for respiratory inflammation broadly, the carvacrol constituent RCT series provides consistent, if not yet independently replicated, evidence for benefit in asthma.

The biggest variable is product quality. Without *O. vulgare* subsp. *hirtum* and a verified carvacrol percentage ≥60–70%, you may be taking an herb with minimal therapeutic content — the bottle matters as much as the herb.

**Worth trying if:** You have chronic sinusitis that hasn't responded fully to standard care, recurrent respiratory infections, or a confirmed parasitic gut infection, and you want a botanical option with emerging evidence behind it.

**Be cautious if:** You're on anticoagulants, blood sugar medications, or have ever reacted to mint family herbs. Tell your prescriber.

**Do not use if:** You are pregnant or planning to become pregnant. This is the one firm line.

Start at 150–300 mg/day with food. Give it 4–8 weeks for the indication you're targeting. If nothing is shifting after 8 weeks, it may not be the right tool — stop and reassess rather than escalating dose.
