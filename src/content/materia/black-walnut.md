## What The Evidence Says

Black walnut sits at an unusual position in herbal medicine: **no human clinical trials exist for any therapeutic use**, yet it holds a regulatory-approved traditional use claim in Canada. That distinction matters.

Health Canada's 2022 NHPID monograph approved black walnut hull as a traditional vermifuge — to help expel intestinal worms. This wasn't based on clinical trials; traditional use designations don't require them. It's regulatory recognition that the tradition is real and plausible, not proof that it works. No other regulatory body worldwide (not WHO, Commission E, EMA, or USP) has issued a monograph for *Juglans nigra*.

**What lab work shows:**
- Juglone, the primary active compound, kills bacteria, fungi, and cancer cells in culture [7]
- It induces ferroptosis (iron-dependent cell death) in pancreatic carcinoma cells in vitro [6]
- It inhibits Pin1, an enzyme implicated in cancer and Alzheimer's — in a test tube [7]
- A 2024 UHPLC-HR-MS/MS study identified 161 compounds and showed juglone can cross GI epithelial membranes and potentially the blood-brain barrier in an artificial membrane model [3]

**The critical caveat**: none of this has been tested in humans. We don't know what plasma concentrations of juglone are achievable after oral dosing. We don't know if the anthelmintic, antifungal, or any other effect observed in the lab translates to a useful clinical outcome.

**The safety signal you need to know**: juglone is Ames-positive — mutagenic in a standard bacterial assay — when metabolically activated by CYP450 enzymes, at concentrations of 1.25–2.5 μmol/L [2]. This is the scientific basis for the universal 14-day use limit. We don't know whether therapeutic oral doses produce mutagenic juglone concentrations in human tissue. We don't have human pharmacokinetic data. The risk is theoretical but real enough that caution is the only defensible position.

## Traditional Use

Black walnut's traditional record is North American and concentrated — not the multi-thousand-year, multi-civilization story you get with some Ayurvedic herbs. Its depth is regional and genuine.

**Indigenous North America:**
The anthelmintic use is the most consistent thread across pre-contact and 19th-century Indigenous traditions. Unripe green hulls were prepared as teas or infusions to expel roundworms, tapeworms, and pinworms. The timing of harvest — green hulls in early autumn — aligned with what practitioners called the "wormy season." Fresh hull juice was applied topically for ringworm and skin parasites; bark decoctions served as antiseptic gargles.

**Eclectic Physicians (1820s–1930s):**
The 19th-century American Eclectic school adopted and systematized these uses. Black walnut appeared in their materia medica for intestinal worms, skin diseases (impetigo, herpetic eruptions, favus), and as an "alterative" — a category roughly equivalent to "blood purifier" — for syphilis and scrofula, reflecting pre-antibiotic practice. Their preparation of choice was green hull extract in dilute alcohol, often combined with other alteratives like yellow dock and sarsaparilla.

**20th-century folk herbalism:**
The tradition continued and narrowed toward Candida overgrowth and "parasite cleansing," particularly after Hulda Clark's 1990s protocols popularized black walnut alongside wormwood and cloves. There's no clinical evidence for these combination protocols. The topical use for athlete's foot and nail fungus persists in folk practice based on juglone's documented antifungal activity in vitro.

The single traditional use that earned regulatory recognition — anthelmintic — is also the most historically consistent one, documented independently across Indigenous practice, Eclectic medicine, and folk herbalism.

## How To Try It

Everything centers on the **unripe green hull** (pericarp) — not the nut, not the leaves, not the bark for internal use. Ripe, blackened hulls have substantially less juglone.

### Forms and Dosing

**Dried hull powder or capsules (most accessible):**
- Health Canada approved dose: **0.65–2.75 g/day**
- Commercial capsules are typically 250–500 mg; that's 2–5 capsules/day
- For anthelmintic use, most practitioners use the higher end of the range
- Take with food, never on empty stomach

**Liquid extract 1:2 (naturopathic standard):**
- 1.5–5.5 mL/day
- This aligns with the Health Canada dose when you convert at the 1:2 ratio
- Ethanolic extracts preserve juglone better than aqueous preparations — juglone degrades in acidic water

**Tincture 1:4 at 25% ethanol (traditional folk form):**
- 5–10 drops, 3× daily
- Very low hull equivalent per dose — this is the cautious end of the spectrum
- Typically cycled: 2 weeks on, 2 weeks off

### Timing and Protocol

- **Never within 1–2 hours of other medications** in either direction — tannins bind drugs and reduce absorption
- Take with a meal
- 14 days maximum, then stop
- If there's no response or benefit in 14 days, more days won't help

### What Not To Do

Don't make a strong tea or decoction for internal use at traditional bark-decoction ratios — those traditional preparations were often topical or intended for very short-term use, and the doses can far exceed the Health Canada safety threshold. Aqueous preparations also have poor juglone bioavailability since juglone is relatively insoluble in water and degrades in gastric acid.

## What To Track

**Before starting:**
- Your reason for use (specific: anthelmintic, skin condition, topical fungal)
- Baseline GI symptoms (any existing nausea, cramping, loose stools — black walnut can cause all of these)
- Any current medications and their timing

**During the 14 days:**

For anthelmintic use:
- Stool changes — passage of worm segments, changes in GI symptoms
- Energy levels (heavy parasite burden can cause fatigue; resolution is sometimes noticed)
- GI tolerance: nausea, cramping, and diarrhea are dose-dependent side effects, not signs of "die-off"

For skin/topical use:
- Photograph the affected area before starting
- Track lesion size, itching, scaling weekly
- Improvement typically appears in week 1–3 for topical fungal conditions

**Stop immediately if:**
- Any sign of allergic reaction: hives, throat tightening, swelling
- Severe GI distress that doesn't improve with dose reduction
- Jaundice, dark urine, or unusual fatigue (theoretical hepatotoxicity signal)
- Symptoms worsen

## Who This Is/Isn't For

### Reasonable candidates:

- Adults with confirmed or suspected intestinal parasites seeking a traditional approach, particularly where pharmaceutical anthelmintics are declined or unavailable
- People with topical fungal conditions (athlete's foot, ringworm) as an adjunct to conventional treatment — the in vitro antifungal evidence is at least mechanistically plausible
- Folk herbalists working within an established clinical tradition who understand the evidence ceiling

What they typically report: GI activity (sometimes dramatic — this is expected with anthelmintic herbs), stool changes, and with topical use, clearing of fungal patches over 2–3 weeks.

### Not appropriate for:

- **Anyone with a walnut allergy** — full stop; IgE cross-reactivity between *J. nigra* and *J. regia* is established, with Jug n 4 binding IgE in 32% of confirmed allergy patients [4]
- **Pregnant or breastfeeding people** — absolute contraindication
- **Anyone on multiple oral medications** — the tannin-drug binding interaction is clinically significant and affects too many drug classes to manage safely without pharmacist oversight
- **People wanting evidence-based treatment for Candida or systemic fungal infection** — the clinical evidence doesn't exist; consider proven antifungals
- **People wanting a long-term daily supplement** — the mutagenicity data forecloses this; black walnut hull is not for continuous use

## The Stain

Juglone is the same compound that makes walnut husks permanently stain everything they touch — wood, concrete, skin, teeth. If you're using liquid preparations, expect brown-black discoloration of your lips, mouth, and teeth during use. Urine and stools typically darken. This is cosmetically annoying but not a health concern.

If you ever handle fresh green hulls directly: use nitrile gloves. Juglone penetrates latex, and the fresh-hull stain on skin takes weeks to fade. Outdoor processing is recommended — the allelopathic juice will kill nearby plants.

## Quality Matters

**The problem unique to black walnut**: no standardization exists anywhere. The primary active compound (juglone) is chemically unstable — it oxidizes during processing, drying, and storage, which is why Health Canada explicitly requires only "non-standardized" extracts. No pharmacopoeial monograph exists to verify against. The quality range across commercial products is wide and largely unverifiable.

**What to look for:**
- Products specifying "unripe" or "green" hull — not mature or dried black hulls
- Ethanolic extracts over aqueous preparations (juglone requires ethanol to remain stable)
- Nitrogen-purged or refrigerated storage for tinctures and liquid extracts
- Products that don't show heavy browning or blackening of the preparation (indicates juglone oxidation)
- Third-party testing for heavy metals and microbial contamination

**Avoid**: products that specify just "walnut hull" without indicating ripeness state, and any preparation claiming standardized juglone content — the instability of juglone makes this claim dubious or the product potentially unsafe.

Because no regulatory standard exists beyond Health Canada's traditional use designation, the manufacturer's quality controls are your only safeguard.

## The Bottom Line

Black walnut hull is a genuine North American folk medicine with real phytochemistry and a consistent traditional use record for intestinal parasites. It also has zero human clinical trials, a mutagenic primary compound, a hard 14-day use limit, and significant drug interaction concerns.

**What it might do**: expel intestinal worms and help with topical fungal conditions — both plausible based on juglone's in vitro activity and centuries of traditional use.

**What it won't do**: serve as a long-term supplement, treat systemic Candida with certainty, or substitute for evidence-based treatments where those are available and appropriate.

**The honest position**: if you have a legitimate anthelmintic need, black walnut hull is the only herb with any regulatory recognition for that purpose, however modest. Use it at the approved dose, for the approved duration, with full respect for the contraindications and the drug interaction risk. Then stop.

For anything beyond that narrow traditional use, the evidence hasn't arrived yet.
