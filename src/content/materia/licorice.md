## What The Evidence Says

Licorice has **genuine, replicable clinical evidence** concentrated in two areas: gastrointestinal conditions and liver support. The evidence base is more specific than most herbs — not "general tonic," but actual RCT-level evidence for defined conditions.

**Strong evidence:**
- **Functional dyspepsia**: 30-day trial with 50 adults, GutGard DGL 150mg/day — 56% marked improvement vs 0% placebo [Raveendra 2012]
- **GERD/heartburn**: Phase III RCT with 200 adults, 28 days — heartburn significantly resolved by day 14 (p=0.017) and day 28 (p=0.005); regurgitation improved from day 7 [Raj 2025]
- **H. pylori eradication**: 60-day trial with 100 adults — 56% stool antigen negative vs 4% placebo; 3.73× more effective than placebo at clearing gastric H. pylori [Puram 2013]
- **H. pylori adjunct**: Adding licorice to standard triple therapy raised eradication from 62.5% to 83.3% in 120 patients — a meaningful clinical upgrade [Hajiaghamohammadi 2016]
- **Aphthous ulcers (topical)**: 6 clinical trials with 314 participants — pain reduced within 4 days; ulcer size reduced by day 8; epidermal growth factor elevated vs control [Dorsareh 2023]
- **Liver enzymes**: Meta-analysis of 15 RCTs with 1,367 patients — ALT reduced 15.63 U/L vs control; AST reduced 7.37 U/L [Giangrandi 2024]

**Moderate evidence:**
- **Menopausal hot flashes**: 90 women, 8 weeks — reduced hot flash frequency and severity vs placebo; effects returned 2 weeks after stopping [Nahidi 2012]
- **PCOS metabolic support**: 66 overweight women, 8 weeks — improved BMI, lipid profile (TG, TC, LDL, HDL), and insulin resistance markers vs diet alone [Hooshmandi 2024]

**Doesn't work for:**
- COVID-19: 60 patients, 7 days — no benefit on oxygen saturation, temperature, respiratory rate, mortality, or hospital stay [Ameri 2023]

**Critical nuance that changes everything**: The same compound that drives the clinical benefits (glycyrrhetinic acid, GA) is the one that raises blood pressure. A 2024 RCT showed that the WHO "safe" limit of exactly 100mg GA/day still raised systolic BP by 3.1 mmHg and suppressed renin and aldosterone by 30–45% in healthy young adults [Af Geijerstam 2024]. This is a strong signal that current guidelines underestimate the threshold for cardiovascular effects.

The solution is the DGL split: **deglycyrrhizinated licorice** retains the GI-protective flavonoids (glabridin, liquiritigenin, isoliquiritigenin) while removing the GA. All three GutGard GI trials used DGL and reported safety comparable to placebo with no BP or electrolyte effects.

## Traditional Use

Three civilizations independently arrived at the same plant, for the same conditions, with extraordinary depth of documentation.

**TCM (millennia):**
- *Gan Cao* (甘草) — "sweet herb" — appears in virtually every formula category as a *harmonizing* herb. Its role is unusual: not to treat a single condition but to moderate the actions of other herbs, reduce toxicity, and smooth formula interactions.
- Tonifies Spleen Qi, moistens Lungs, stops cough, clears heat, relieves spasms and pain
- *Zhi Gan Cao* (honey-fried form) used specifically for tonifying Qi and nourishing the heart
- Species: primarily G. uralensis

**Ayurveda (Charaka Samhita, Sushruta Samhita):**
- *Yashtimadhu* (यष्टिमधु) — "sweet stick"
- Classified as *Rasayana* (rejuvenating), *Sita* (cooling), *Madhura* (sweet taste and effect)
- Used for cough, bronchitis, hoarseness, gastric ulcers, hyperacidity, general tonic, skin healing
- Preparations include decoctions, herbal ghee (ghrita), powders, medicated oils

**European herbalism (3,000+ years):**
- Licorice extracts found in Tutankhamun's tomb (~1300 BCE) — one of the oldest documented herbal preparations in existence
- Theophrastus (~300 BCE) described uses for thirst and respiratory conditions
- Hildegard von Bingen (12th century) documented use for stomach and liver
- Traditional European apothecary noted licorice as not suitable for those with "excess heat or high blood pressure constitutions" — an empirical observation that maps exactly to what we now understand as pseudoaldosteronism

**Regulatory recognition**: German Commission E positive monograph for respiratory catarrh and gastric/duodenal ulcers. WHO positive monograph. EMA well-established use designation. This level of convergent traditional + regulatory validation is not common.

## How To Try It

The single most important decision is **DGL vs whole-root**, not dose or timing.

### Choose Your Preparation Based on Your Goal

**For GI conditions (dyspepsia, GERD, H. pylori, gastric inflammation) — use DGL:**

DGL is the evidence-based choice for GI use. It removes the cardiovascular-risk compound while retaining the GI-protective flavonoids.

| Product | Dose | Duration | Indication |
|---------|------|----------|------------|
| **GutGard** (glabridin ≥3.5%, flavonoids ≥10%) | 75–150 mg/day | 30–60 days in trials | Dyspepsia, GERD, H. pylori |
| **DGL chewable tablets** | 400–500 mg, 2–4 tablets before meals | As needed | Heartburn, gastric discomfort |

- Take DGL 15 minutes before meals — direct mucosal contact enhances local effect
- Chewing (rather than swallowing whole) is recommended for chewable tablets

**For respiratory, adrenal, or traditional tonic use — use whole root with awareness:**

The dried root decoction is the WHO-referenced standard preparation:

- **Dose**: 1–2 g cut root per cup of water, simmered 10–15 minutes, up to 3× daily
- At 1g per cup with 4% GA, you're at ~40mg GA/day — within the WHO limit, but effects begin below 100mg
- **Maximum duration: 4 weeks without medical supervision** (EMA/HMPC; Commission E)
- Check blood pressure before starting; recheck at 2 weeks

**For mouth ulcers — use topical:**
- 1–5% licorice paste, mouthwash, or dissolving oral patch applied directly to the ulcer
- 3–4 times daily; expect pain reduction by day 4, ulcer resolution by day 8
- Negligible systemic GA exposure at topical doses

### Taste Note

Licorice is intensely, unmistakably sweet. Glycyrrhizin is 30–50× sweeter than sucrose. The decoction tastes sweet, slightly earthy, with anise-like undertones. Unlike most medicinal herbs, there's nothing to mask — the sweetness is the active compound.

## What To Track

**Before starting:**
- Resting blood pressure (two readings, sitting, morning)
- Baseline GI symptom score: heartburn frequency/severity, bloating, pain (daily 1–10)
- If using whole root: serum potassium if available

**Week 1–2:**
- GI symptom changes — trials show effects by day 7–14: GERD regurgitation from day 7, heartburn resolution by day 14 [Raj 2025]; dyspepsia improvement by week 4 [Raveendra 2012]
- Blood pressure (especially if using whole-root preparations)
- Any ankle swelling, unusual thirst, or muscle weakness (early pseudoaldosteronism signs)

**Week 4 assessment:**
- Compare GI symptoms to baseline
- Blood pressure — should not have risen significantly on DGL; if on whole root, expect some effect
- Decide on continuation or stopping

**RED FLAGS — Stop immediately:**
- Headache with elevated blood pressure reading
- Ankle or hand swelling not previously present
- Muscle weakness or cramping (hypokalemia sign)
- Any significant blood pressure rise above your baseline

## Who This Is/Isn't For

### Good candidates for DGL:
- Chronic heartburn or GERD (especially if wanting to reduce PPI dependence)
- Functional dyspepsia, bloating, upper GI discomfort
- H. pylori treatment (as adjunct to or alternative starting point)
- Recurrent mouth ulcers
- Anyone wanting the GI benefits without cardiovascular risk

### Good candidates for whole root (with appropriate monitoring):
- Healthy adults without hypertension, hypokalemia, or renal/hepatic issues
- Adrenal support or cortisol augmentation (legitimate use in Addison's disease, Methlie 2011)
- Respiratory cough and bronchial catarrh (traditional; supported by WHO/Commission E)
- Short-course use (≤4 weeks), low dose (1–2g dried root)

### People who should not use whole-root licorice (GA-containing preparations):
- **Hypertension** — absolute contraindication; licorice will worsen it
- **Hypokalemia** — licorice will worsen it; can progress to muscle weakness or paralysis
- **Pregnancy** — risk of premature labor (EMA/HMPC contraindication)
- **On diuretics** (loop or thiazide) — additive hypokalemia; potentially severe
- **Liver cirrhosis** — impaired metabolism; severe electrolyte risk
- **Severe renal insufficiency** — reduced clearance; accumulation risk
- **On antihypertensives** — licorice counteracts blood pressure medication

DGL preparations do not share these contraindications based on available clinical data, but caution is still reasonable during pregnancy and for anyone with severe systemic illness.

## The Sweet Paradox

Licorice is rare among medicinal herbs: it tastes good. Intensely, distinctly sweet. The glycyrrhizin that drives the therapeutic effects is the same compound that gives it this unmistakable flavor. In the traditional world this was a feature — licorice was used to make bitter formulas more palatable across TCM, Ayurveda, and European apothecary alike.

This also means that accidental overconsumption is easy. Licorice candy, licorice-flavored teas, and licorice herbal products can stack GA intake without the user realizing it. If you're adding licorice tea to your routine, account for any licorice confectionery you eat. At doses people find modest and pleasant, you can exceed the threshold for cardiovascular effects.

## Quality Matters

**For DGL products:**
- Look for standardization markers: glabridin ≥3.5% w/w, total flavonoids ≥10% w/w (GutGard standard)
- GutGard is the only DGL extract with three published Phase II/III RCTs; other DGL products vary in quality and have less direct evidence
- Confirm "deglycyrrhizinated" or "DGL" is explicitly stated; some products use this term loosely
- Third-party testing for GA content to confirm deglycyrrhizination

**For whole-root preparations:**
- European Pharmacopoeia standard: ≥4.0% glycyrrhizic acid (dry basis) — minimum quality bar
- GA content varies 4–24% in practice; the actual GA per capsule matters for dosing safely
- Look for stated GA% per dose; avoid products with no glycyrrhizin quantification
- Third-party testing for heavy metals
- Certified organic reduces contamination risk

**Avoid:** Licorice products with no standardization information, no GA content disclosure, and no third-party testing.

## The Bottom Line

Licorice has **real clinical evidence** for GI conditions — not theoretical, not animal data, but Phase III RCTs with defined populations, controlled designs, and meaningful effect sizes. The 56% marked improvement rate for dyspepsia vs 0% placebo, the 83.3% H. pylori eradication rate when added to triple therapy, the liver enzyme reductions across 1,367 patients — these are genuine signals.

**The split is everything:**
- **DGL**: Use it. Strong GI evidence, excellent safety profile, no cardiovascular risk. If you have digestive issues, this is a well-supported option.
- **Whole root**: Use it carefully, briefly, and only if you don't have contraindications. Genuine traditional and regulatory support for respiratory and GI use — but the cardiovascular effect is real, measurable, and occurs at doses below what current guidelines define as safe.

**When it works**: Meaningful GI symptom relief, H. pylori clearance support, faster mouth ulcer healing, liver enzyme normalization, temporary reduction in hot flash frequency.

**When to be cautious**: Any hypertension, pregnancy, potassium issues, diuretic use, or renal/hepatic impairment — avoid GA-containing preparations entirely. For everyone else using whole root: 4 weeks maximum, monitor blood pressure, stay at low-end doses.

The traditional world's warnings about licorice in people with edema and "high heat" constitutions weren't superstition. They were correct clinical observation, centuries before anyone understood 11β-HSD2 inhibition.
