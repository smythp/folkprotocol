> **Strong evidence:**
>
> Honey demonstrates superior efficacy to both placebo and usual care for nocturnal cough in children. A Cochrane review of 6 RCTs (n=899) found honey reduced cough frequency more than no treatment (MD -1.05, 95% CI -1.48 to -0.62) and improved sleep quality for both children (MD -1.04, 95% CI -1.57 to -0.51) and parents (MD -0.88, 95% CI -1.23 to -0.52) [1]. ## After: Accessible Wikipedia-Level Tone

A BMJ review of 14 clinical trials found honey superior to usual care for both symptom relief and cough frequency, with all studies showing similar benefits [2]. Dosing in these studies ranged from 2.5-10ml before bedtime [1].

A consumer survey with 822 participants reported rapid symptom relief from marshmallow root extract for dry cough [6]. Animal studies found several active compounds in licorice reduced cough frequency by 30-78% [9]. The European Medicines Agency recognizes licorice's traditional use for cough associated with the common cold, recommending 1.5g in 150ml tea for bronchitis or cough [10].

## Key Principles Demonstrated

### 1. Statistical Translation
- Always convert MD/SMD/CI to plain language meaning
- Keep the magnitude ("one episode per day", "30-78%")
- Can include technical notation in parentheses if needed, but lead with plain language

### 2. Medical Terminology
- Define technical terms inline on first use
- Use common anatomical names (throat, airways vs. laryngopharyngeal)
- Break down complex mechanisms into understandable steps
- Group related conditions when listing contraindications

### 3. Study Language
- "RCT" → "clinical trial" or "study"
- "Preclinical" → "animal studies" or "laboratory studies"
- "n=899" → "899 participants" (first use), can use (n=899) after
- "Meta-analysis" → "review" (still accurate)

### 4. What NEVER Changes
- Citation numbers [n]
- Specific numbers (sample sizes, dosages, percentages)
- Scientific accuracy of claims
- Evidence hierarchy labels
- Safety warning severity

### 5. The "Educated Dinner Guest" Test
Read each transformation aloud:
- Would you say "mineralocorticoid-like effects" to a friend? No.
- Would you say "affects hormones that control blood pressure"? Yes.

This is the calibration point: accessible to non-medical readers while maintaining scientific precision.
