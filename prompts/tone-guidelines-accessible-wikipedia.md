# Tone Guidelines: Accessible Wikipedia-Level Writing

## Target Audience & Reading Level

**Goal:** 8th-grade reading level (educated lay reader)
**Model:** Wikipedia medical articles, not PubMed abstracts
**Balance:** Scientific accuracy + accessible language

## Core Principles

1. **Keep all citations and numbers** - Don't sacrifice rigor for accessibility
2. **Explain technical terms on first use** - Don't assume medical vocabulary
3. **Translate statistics into plain language** - Keep the numbers, add meaning
4. **Use common names first, Latin in parentheses** - Make it scannable
5. **Define mechanisms simply but accurately** - No hand-waving

## Statistical Language Transformations

### Confidence Intervals & Effect Sizes

❌ **Too Academic:**
"A Cochrane review of 6 RCTs (n=899) found honey reduced cough frequency more than no treatment (MD -1.05, 95% CI -1.48 to -0.62)"

✅ **Accessible:**
"A major Cochrane review of 6 clinical trials with 899 participants found honey reduced cough frequency by about one episode per day compared to no treatment [1]"

**Rule:** Translate MD/SMD into what it means in practice. Keep CI in parentheses if precision matters, otherwise omit.

---

❌ **Too Academic:**
"The BMJ meta-analysis of 14 RCTs reported honey superior to usual care for combined symptom relief (MD -3.96, 95% CI -5.42 to -2.51) and cough frequency (SMD -0.36, 95% CI -0.50 to -0.21), with very low heterogeneity (I²=0%)"

✅ **Accessible:**
"A BMJ review of 14 clinical trials found honey more effective than usual care for symptom relief and cough frequency, with all studies showing similar benefits [2]"

**Rule:**
- "RCT" → "clinical trial" or "study"
- "n=899" → "899 participants" (first time), can use "n=899" in parentheses after
- "MD/SMD [number]" → describe the actual effect in plain language
- "I²=0%" → "very consistent results" or "all studies showed similar benefits"
- Keep the specific numbers in brackets, simplify the statistics

---

### Study Design Language

❌ **Too Academic:**
"Preclinical studies found liquiritin apioside, liquiritin, and liquiritigenin at 50 mg/kg decreased cough frequency by 30-78% in ammonia-induced cough models [9]"

✅ **Accessible:**
"Animal studies found several compounds in licorice reduced cough frequency by 30-78% [9]"

**Rule:**
- "Preclinical" → "animal studies" or "laboratory studies"
- Simplify chemical names unless they're commonly known
- Keep the percentage effects

---

## Medical Terminology Transformations

### Mechanisms of Action

❌ **Too Academic:**
"Demulcent herbs containing polysaccharides form protective coatings over mucous membranes [6,7]"

✅ **Accessible:**
"Demulcent herbs contain natural compounds that create a protective coating in the throat and airways [6,7]"

**Rule:** Define the mechanism in terms anyone can visualize. "Mucous membranes" → "throat and airways" or "throat lining"

---

❌ **Too Academic:**
"Glycyrrhizin has mineralocorticoid-like effects causing hypokalemia, hypertension, cardiac arrhythmia, and myopathy [11]"

✅ **Accessible:**
"Glycyrrhizin (licorice's active compound) affects hormones that control blood pressure and electrolyte balance, potentially causing low potassium, high blood pressure, irregular heart rhythm, and muscle problems [11]"

**Rule:**
- Define technical terms inline on first use
- Break up medical term lists into understandable language
- "Mineralocorticoid-like effects" → "affects hormones that control blood pressure"
- "Hypokalemia" → "low potassium"
- "Cardiac arrhythmia" → "irregular heart rhythm"

---

### Contraindications & Safety

❌ **Too Academic:**
"Contraindicated in hypertension, heart failure, pulmonary hypertension, or kidney disease [11]"

✅ **Accessible:**
"Not recommended for people with high blood pressure, heart conditions, or kidney disease [11]"

**Rule:**
- "Contraindicated in" → "not recommended for people with" or "should not be used by people with"
- Simplify medical conditions to common names where possible
- Group related conditions: "heart failure, pulmonary hypertension" → "heart conditions"

---

❌ **Too Academic:**
"Critical contraindication: Honey must not be given to infants under 12 months due to risk of infant botulism from Clostridium botulinum spores [3]"

✅ **Accessible:**
"Important safety note: Never give honey to infants under 12 months old due to botulism risk from bacterial spores [3]"

**Rule:**
- Keep safety warnings strong but accessible
- "Critical contraindication" → "Important safety note" or "Do not use"
- Scientific names can be omitted if they don't add clarity
- Keep the specific age/dosage constraints

---

## Anatomical & Physiological Terms

❌ **Too Academic:**
"Animal studies demonstrate statistically significant decreases in cough efforts from both laryngopharyngeal and tracheobronchial stimulation [8]"

✅ **Accessible:**
"Animal studies found significant reductions in coughing from both throat and lung irritation [8]"

**Rule:**
- "Laryngopharyngeal" → "throat"
- "Tracheobronchial" → "lung" or "airway"
- "Stimulation" → "irritation" (in context of cough)
- "Statistically significant" → "significant" (the statistics are implied by the citation)

---

## Abbreviations & Jargon

### Common Simplifications

| Academic | Accessible |
|----------|-----------|
| RCT | clinical trial, study |
| n=899 | 899 participants (first use), (n=899) thereafter |
| MD -1.05 | reduced by about 1 point/episode |
| 95% CI | (can often omit, or "with high confidence") |
| SMD -0.36 | small/moderate/large effect |
| I²=0% | consistent results across studies |
| p<0.001 | highly significant (or omit) |
| vs. | compared to |
| GRAS | generally recognized as safe |
| HMPC | European medicines authority |

### Keep These (Common Enough)

- FDA
- BMJ, Cochrane (but explain on first use)
- mg, ml, g (dosing units)
- % (percentages)

---

## Evidence Hierarchy Language

❌ **Too Academic:**
"The EMA HMPC recognizes traditional use as expectorant in cough associated with common cold, recommending 1.5g in 150ml infusion for bronchitis or cough [10]"

✅ **Accessible:**
"The European Medicines Agency recognizes licorice's traditional use for cough, recommending 1.5g in 150ml tea for bronchitis or cough [10]"

**Rule:**
- Spell out acronyms on first use, can abbreviate after
- "Infusion" → "tea" (more accessible, still accurate)
- "Expectorant" can stay if defined earlier, otherwise → "helps loosen mucus"

## Before/After Examples from Tickly Cough

### Example 1: Overview Section

❌ **Before:**
"Demulcent herbs containing polysaccharides form protective coatings over mucous membranes [6,7]. These herbs demonstrate evidence for symptomatic relief of dry cough through mechanical and anti-inflammatory mechanisms [6,7,8]."

✅ **After:**
"Demulcent herbs contain natural compounds (polysaccharides) that create a protective coating over the throat lining [6,7]. These herbs show evidence for relieving dry cough by physically protecting irritated tissue and reducing inflammation [6,7,8]."

---

### Example 2: Strong Evidence Section

❌ **Before:**
"A Cochrane review of 6 RCTs (n=899) found honey reduced cough frequency more than no treatment (MD -1.05, 95% CI -1.48 to -0.62) and improved sleep quality for both children (MD -1.04, 95% CI -1.57 to -0.51) and parents (MD -0.88, 95% CI -1.23 to -0.52) [1]."

✅ **After:**
"A Cochrane review of 6 clinical trials with 899 participants found honey reduced cough frequency by about one episode per day compared to no treatment, and improved sleep quality for both children and parents by approximately one point on a seven-point scale [1]."

---

### Example 3: Moderate Evidence Section

❌ **Before:**
"A consumer survey (n=822) reported rapid onset of effects for symptomatic treatment of dry cough with marshmallow root extract [6]. The herb contains approximately 20% mucilage, predominantly polysaccharides including pectin, which form a protective film over irritated mucous membranes [6,7]."

✅ **After:**
"A consumer survey with 822 participants reported rapid symptom relief from marshmallow root extract for dry cough [6]. The herb contains about 20% mucilage (primarily polysaccharides including pectin) that forms a protective coating over irritated throat tissue [6,7]."

---

### Example 4: Safety Information

❌ **Before:**
"Safety concerns limit licorice use: as little as 100mg/day glycyrrhizic acid causes significant blood pressure increases [11]. Glycyrrhizin has mineralocorticoid-like effects causing hypokalemia, hypertension, cardiac arrhythmia, and myopathy [11]."

✅ **After:**
"Licorice has significant safety concerns: as little as 100mg daily of its active compound (glycyrrhizic acid) can raise blood pressure [11]. This compound affects hormones that regulate blood pressure and electrolytes, potentially causing low potassium, high blood pressure, irregular heart rhythm, and muscle problems [11]."

## Practical Rules for Transformation

### The Translation Pattern

For every technical claim, use this pattern:
1. **State the finding in plain language**
2. **Include the specific numbers**
3. **Add the citation**
4. **Define technical terms inline (in parentheses or brief clause)**

Example:
"Animal studies found significant reductions in coughing (30-78% decrease) from licorice compounds [9]"

### The "Educated Dinner Guest" Test

Would you say this sentence to an educated friend over dinner who isn't a medical professional?

- If YES → Good accessible tone
- If NO → Needs simplification

### The Readability Checklist

Before: Read your sentence
Ask:
1. ✓ Does it have technical jargon?
2. ✓ Can I define it inline?
3. ✓ Can I use a simpler synonym?
4. ✓ Can I describe it instead of naming it?

After: Rewrite applying simplifications
Check: Did I keep the citation and numbers?

---

## What NOT to Sacrifice

### Always Keep:

1. **Citation numbers** - Every factual claim needs [n]
2. **Specific numbers** - 899 participants, 6 studies, 30-78% reduction
3. **Dosage precision** - 1.5g, 100mg/day, 2-4ml
4. **Study sizes** - n=822, 6 RCTs (can write "6 clinical trials (n=899)")
5. **Effect sizes** - Translate to plain language but keep magnitude
6. **Safety warnings** - Critical information, just make accessible
7. **Scientific accuracy** - Simplify language, not facts

### Often Omit:

1. **Statistical notation** - CI ranges, p-values (unless central to claim)
2. **Complex chemical names** - Unless commonly known
3. **Latin anatomical terms** - Use common names
4. **Abbreviations** - Spell out on first use
5. **Statistical heterogeneity** - Translate to "consistent/varied results"

```markdown
## TONE: Accessible Wikipedia-Level

Write for an educated lay reader (8th-grade reading level):

**Statistical translations:**
- "RCTs (n=899)" → "clinical trials with 899 participants"
- "MD -1.05, 95% CI -1.48 to -0.62" → "reduced by about one episode per day"
- "SMD -0.36" → "small but consistent benefit"
- "I²=0%" → "very consistent results across studies"

**Medical term simplifications:**
- "Mineralocorticoid-like effects" → "affects hormones that control blood pressure"
- "Laryngopharyngeal stimulation" → "throat irritation"
- "Hypokalemia" → "low potassium"
- "Contraindicated in" → "not recommended for people with"

**General rules:**
- Define technical terms inline on first use: "glycyrrhizin (licorice's active compound)"
- Use common anatomical names: "throat" not "laryngopharyngeal", "airways" not "tracheobronchial"
- Translate statistics to meaning: keep the numbers, explain what they mean
- Spell out acronyms on first use: "European Medicines Agency (EMA)"
- Keep all citations, dosages, study sizes, and percentages
- Prefer "clinical trial" or "study" over "RCT"
- Prefer "animal studies" over "preclinical studies"

**The test:** Would you say this to an educated friend over dinner? If not, simplify.
```

## Examples of Good Balance

### Good: Keeps Numbers, Adds Meaning

"A Cochrane review of 6 clinical trials with 899 participants found honey reduced cough frequency by about one episode per day compared to no treatment [1]"

✓ Has specific numbers (6, 899, one per day)
✓ Has citation [1]
✓ Plain language (clinical trials, not RCTs)
✓ Meaningful translation (one episode per day vs. MD -1.05)

### Good: Simplifies Mechanism, Stays Accurate

"Demulcent herbs contain natural compounds (polysaccharides) that create a protective coating over the throat lining [6,7]"

✓ Defines technical term inline (polysaccharides)
✓ Uses common anatomy (throat lining vs. mucous membranes)
✓ Keeps citations [6,7]
✓ Scientifically accurate

### Good: Safety Warning, Accessible

"Never give honey to infants under 12 months old due to botulism risk from bacterial spores [3]"

✓ Clear, direct safety warning
✓ Keeps specific age constraint (12 months)
✓ Simplified but accurate (bacterial spores vs. Clostridium botulinum)
✓ Has citation [3]

## Red Flags to Fix

If you see these in draft content, revise:

🚩 **"MD -1.05, 95% CI -1.48 to -0.62"** without plain language translation
🚩 **"RCT (n=899)"** instead of "clinical trial with 899 participants"
🚩 **"Mineralocorticoid-like effects"** undefined
🚩 **"Laryngopharyngeal stimulation"** instead of "throat irritation"
🚩 **"Preclinical studies"** instead of "animal studies"
🚩 **"I²=0%"** instead of "consistent results"
🚩 **Undefined acronyms** (EMA, HMPC without explanation)
🚩 **Chemical names** without context when simpler term works
🚩 **"Contraindicated in hypertension"** instead of "not recommended for people with high blood pressure"

---

## Success Criteria

A successfully calibrated piece will:

1. ✅ Read like a Wikipedia medical article (encyclopedic but accessible)
2. ✅ Contain all original citations and numbers
3. ✅ Define all technical terms on first use
4. ✅ Translate statistics to practical meaning
5. ✅ Use 8th-grade vocabulary while maintaining precision
6. ✅ Never condescend or oversimplify to the point of inaccuracy
7. ✅ Balance rigor with readability

**The gold standard:** An educated lay reader with no medical background can understand the claims and evaluate the evidence, while a medical professional finds no loss of scientific accuracy.
